***Cognitive psychology***
- **Basics:**
	- Definition: branch of psychology concerned with scientific study of mind
	- Cognition: neural processes like perception, attention, and memory 
		- Ways of studying cognition:
			- **Experimental psychology:** running experiments to observe participants’ cognitive processes
			- Cognitive neuroscience: using biological measurements to understand neural processes
			- **Cognitive neuropsychology:** studying lesion/case-based research on patients with brain damage
			- Computer modeling: running code to model neural processes
- **History:**
	- **F.C. Donders (1868**) measured reaction time of decision making
		- Designed two experiments: 
			- Simple reaction time task: participants pushed a button when a light appeared
			- Choice reaction time task: participants pushed separate buttons relative to the placement of the light
		- Subtracted *the simple reaction time* from *the choice reaction time* to get *the duration of decision making* (1/10th of a second)
	- **Wilhelm Wundt (1879)** established first psychology lab
		- Lab was established in Germany
		- Wundt is considered the father of psychology
		- Invented an approach known as **structuralism and believed that psychology needed to be studied through sensations
		- **Analytic introspection:** participants were trained to **describe experiences/thoughts in response to stimuli**
		- His methods were subjective
	- **Hermann Ebbinghaus (1885)** measured the time course of forgetting
		- Read a list of nonsense syllables aloud (to himself) to determine the number of repetitions necessary to repeat the list without errors
		- He took breaks and relearned the list at varying intervals
		- **Savings:** *original time to learn the list* - *time to relearn the list after a delay*
			- Savings decrease as delays (retention intervals) get longer
			- **Savings curve:** savings as a function of retention interval 
			- Forgetting occurs rapidly over the first two days, but slows as time goes on
	- **William James (1890)** published the first psychology textbook (Principles of Psychology)
		- Early American psychologist who taught the first psychology course at Harvard
		- Considered the father of American psychology
		- His work lead to the rise of behaviorism
	- **James Watson (1913)** proposed behaviorism, believing that psychology was too subjective
		- Two issues with analytic introspection: 
			- Low inter-rater reliability
			- Results were difficult/impossible to verify
		- Behaviorism:
			- Eliminated the mind as a possible topic
			- Advocated research on observable behavior
		- Little Albert (?????) experiment:
			- Classical conditioning with UCS, UCR, CS, CR
			- Inspired by Pavlov’s dog experiment (“what happens when you do that to a person?” never a question you should be asking)
			- Used infant named Albert as a participant
			- White rat was paired with a loud noise until the appearance of the rat alone would make the baby cry
			- To this day, the poor guy is still afraid of white fuzzy things
	- **B.F. Skinner (1938)** pioneered operant conditioning (Skinner box)
		- Interested in the relationship between stimuli and responses
		- Operant conditioning uses reinforcement and punishment to modify behavior
	- **E.C. Tolman (1948)** trained two groups of rats with a four-armed maze
		- Group 1: rats would always turn right for the reward, no matter where it was (the rat’s starting position was changed to accommodate this)
		- Group 2: rats would always have the food in a specific location
		- Results: group 2 learned faster because they created a cognitive map of the maze
	- **Noah Chomsky**, a famous linguist, argued with Skinner
		- Skinner (1957) published a book arguing that children learned language through operant conditioning from their parents/guardians
		- Chomsky (1959) wrote a response arguing that children had innate language separate of operant conditioning 
			- Universal language?
	- **Ulric Neisser (1967)** publishes first cognitive psychology textbook
		- Considered the father of cognitive psychology
	- Rise of behaviorism lead to the **cognitive revolution**
		- Begins in the 1950s
		- Sharp decline of behaviorism parallels rise in computer science
			- Introduction of digital computer (1954)
			- Information-processing approach:
				- Way to study the mind based on insights associated with computers
				- Argues that operations of the mind occur in stages
			- Attention and flow diagrams:
				- Cherry (1953) modified James’ attention-based research and created the dichotic listening task (participants are exposed to one message in each each)
				- Broadbent (1958) developed the first flow diagram to show the linear processes of attention
			- Memory research:
				- Atkinson and Shiffrin (1968) developed a three-stage model of memory:
					- Sensory memory (lasts less than one second)
					- Short-term memory (lasts several seconds, but has limited capacity)
				- Tulving (1972) divided long-term memory into three components:
					- Episodic memory (explicit, life events)
					- Semantic memory (explicit, facts)
					- Procedural memory (implicit, physical processes)
		- Theories of the brain as an information processor

## **Chapter 2: Cognitive Neuroscience**

***Key concepts:***
- Levels of analysis: topics can be studied in many different ways (usage-based, fact-based, experience-based)
- Within psychology, this can provide structural understanding:
	- Chemical processes → activation of neurons → activation of brain regions → perception
	- Chemical processes → activation of neurons → activation of neural storage → memory

***Neurons:***
- First studied in the 19th century as part of nerve nets (cf. neural networks)
- Golgi developed staining techniques to isolate specific cells
- Ramon y Cajal used Golgi stains to observe the first individual neurons (and created hand-drawn diagrams)
- **Neuron doctrine:** individual cells transmit signals in the nervous system - the cells are not continuous or physically connected
- **Neural circuits:** groups of interconnected neurons
- Structure of a neuron:
	- Cell body: functions as the metabolic centre of the neuron
	- Dendrites: receive neurochemical signals from other cells
	- Axons: fluid-filled tubes that transmit signals to other cells, sometimes myelinated
		- Myelin: a layer of insulation, produced by oligodendrocytes, that wraps around axons and speeds up potentiation 
		- Nodes of Ranvier: gaps in the myelination where potentials can be retriggered
	- Synapse: gap between a dendrite and axon where transmission occurs
- **Receptors:** neurons specialized to receive information
- Edgar Adrian (1928, 1932) recorded electrical signals from neurons, and noted that action potentials that were spatiotemporally close produced more intense “feelings”

***Neurochemical signals:***
- Resting potential (-70mv): charge within a neuron
	- Abundance of Na outside the cell vs abundance of K inside
- Action potential (40mv): charge that polarizes the neuron and produces an impulse that travels down the axon
	- **Steps of an action potential:**
		- Neurons receive a signal 
		- Na channels open, causing passive Na influx through the dendrites and cell body
		- K channels open, causing K efflux
		- At peak of action potential, Na channels close
		- After hyperpolarization, K channels close
		- During the refractory period, the cell returns to its resting potential
		- Potential travels down the axon and the next neuron’s synapse is triggered, and so on
	- Neuron receives a signal from the environment (receptor/sensory cells receive physical signals, while interneurons receive chemical signals), and the information travels down the axon to the dendrites of another neuron
	- Action potentials can fire as fast as 100m/s
	- Signals are transmitted without decay or loss (all-or-none law)
		- Action potentials either happen or they do not
		- Action potentials typically do not vary in strength or speed
- Neurotransmitters: chemicals released at synapses, triggered by action potentials, that communicate with post-synaptic cells

***Neural transmission:***
- Initial research on transmission focused on the visual cortex
	- Researchers recorded single neurons
	- Easy to manipulate visual stimuli and record response
	- Many neurons at different levels will fire to V1
	- Visual signals will fire throughout the cortex
- Neural representation: idea that all experiences are based on representations in the nervous system
	- Stronger stimuli will produce more firing
	- Patterns of firing could represent specific stimuli
- Feature detectors: neurons that respond to specific features of stimuli
	- Some neurons in the temporal lobe respond to complex stimuli
	- Columns of cells in the cortex identify orientations of lines
	- Hubel & Wiesel’s weird cat isolation experiments demonstrated the development of feature detectors
- Experience-dependent plasticity: the structure of the brain adapts based on experience
	- Phantom limb syndrome:
		- Condition where patients experience sensations in a limb that does not exist
		- Occurs in amputees and those born without limbs
			- For most amputees, the sensations are painful
			- The limb is removed but the sensory afferents remain
- Hierarchical processing: processing that progresses from lower to higher areas of the brain

***Sensory coding:***
- Difficult to determine the neural representation of the senses
- Coding: the way stimuli are represented in the mind
- Sensory code: the way neural processes represent the environment
- Specificity coding: activation of a specialized neuron in response to a particular stimulus
- Population coding: activation of a group of neurons in response to a particular object 
- Sparse coding: activation of a very small group of neurons in response to a particular object

***Localization of function:***
- Specific functions are performed by specific brain regions
- Cerebral cortex is responsible for many cognitive functions
- Neuropsychology (lesion studies) provided evidence for localization of function
	- Cortical equipotentiality: early theory that conceptualized the brain as a whole
	- Neuropsychology focuses on how cognition and behavior are related to the physical brain and nervous system, studying how injuries or illness affect neural function
	- Issues: ethics, extremely low external validity
	- Famous examples: Phineas Gage, Patient H.M., tan guy
	- Aphasia:
		- Broca studied patients who suffered linguistic issues (inability to produce language; Broca’s aphasia) after lesioning to a specific area (Broca’s area)
		- Wernicke studied patients who suffered different linguistic issues (inability to understand language; Wernicke’s aphasia) after lesioning to another specific area (Wernicke’s area)
			- Wernicke correctly proposed that language processing and production involves pathways that spread across the brain, not just isolated areas
	- Wartime studies showed that damage to the occipital lobe resulted in blindness
	- Damage to the parietal lobe results in issues with sensory processing
	- Damage to the temporal lobe can result in prosopagnosia (inability to recognize faces)
- Double dissociation: evidence that two processes/areas are totally separate

***Distributed representation in the brain:***
- Central principle of cognition: most of our experience is multidimensional 
- Functions are processed by/in many different areas of the brain
	- This theory complements localization of function

***Research methods:***
- **Direct recording of neural processes:**
	- **Single-cell recording:**
		- Using microelectrodes to measure the voltage change when an action potential occurs
		- Rate of firing is measured
		- Commonly done on animals, but not humans, because of invasiveness 
		- High spatiotemporal accuracy
		- Difficult to pinpoint specific cells - and not always helpful to do so
	- **Electroencephalography (EEG/ERP):**
		- Using electrode caps to measure neural activity through the scalp
		- Provides information about general neural state
		- Event-related potentials time-link specific EEG patterns to events/stimuli
		- High temporal accuracy, but low spatial accuracy
		- Only measures cortical activity
	- **Magnetoencephalography (MEG):**
		- Records the magnetic fields generated by neural activity
		- Higher spatial accuracy than EEG
		- The tube
	- **Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI):**
		- Measures the density of hydrogen atoms in different areas, clearly showing the different structures of the brain
		- Advantages: clear, detailed picture of the structure of the brain
		- Disadvantages: no measure of activity or function
	- **Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI):**
		- Measures neural activity based on levels of oxygenated hemoglobin (positively correlated with higher activity)
		- Advantages: high spatial accuracy for localizing neural function
		- Disadvantages: images are much less clear, low temporal accuracy
		- Resting-state fMRI: data recorded while participant is at rest
		- Voxels: 3D pixels that represent activation areas in fMRI data
		- Classifier: computer program designed to recognize patterns of voxels
	- **Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS):**
		- Measures changes in blood oxygenation by projecting near-infrared light through the skull
		- Commonly used in pediatrics, since it is not invasive
	- **Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS):**
		- Uses magnetic coils to produce temporary lesions in specific brain areas
	- **Diffusion tensor imaging (DTI):**
		- Measures diffusion of water in the brain
		- Maps white matter paths throughout the brain
- **Cognitive neuropsychology:**
	- Using patients with brain damage to study functions of brain regions
	- TMS, lesion studies, etc

***Neuroanatomy:***
- **Basic brain regions:** 
	- Brain stem: breathing, blood pressure, and heart rate
	- Cerebellum: balance, coordination of fine movements, and learning
	- Cerebral cortex: general cognition, perception, and higher thinking
		- Frontal lobe: cognitive control, motor control, language production
		- Parietal lobe: somatosensory cortex
		- Occipital lobe: visual processing
		- Temporal lobe: auditory processing, memory
- Visual processing in the brain:
	- Fusiform facial area: region in the temporal lobe that appears to respond exclusively to faces
		- Lecture 4: Greeble study
			- After training, the FFA responded better to the greebles
			- Is the FFA a visual expertise area instead?
	- Parahippocampal place area: region in the temporal lobe that responds exclusively to pictures of places
	- Extrastriate body area: region in the temporal cortex that responds to pictures of bodies and body parts (not faces)
	- Lateral occipital area: objects
	- Visual word form area: visual word recognition 

***Neural networks:***
- Interconnected regions of the brain that communicate to facilitate cognition
- **Structural connectivity:**
	- Neural writing diagram created by axons that connect brain regions
	- Structural pathways form the brain’s information highway
	- Within the structural pathways, there are functional pathways that serve different functions
- Track-weighted imaging (TWI): technique that measures brain connectivity based on water diffusion down axons
- Connectome: map of the brain showing neural networks
- Functional connectivity: how neural activity in separate regions is connected
	- Regions that are connected do not necessarily communicate via neural networks
	- Brain networks observed with resting-state fMRI:
		- Executive control network (higher cognition)
		- Sensorimotor network (sensory and motor processes)
		- Dorsal attention network (attention to visuospatial surroundings)
		- Visual network (visual processing)
		- **Default mode network** (at rest)
			- Discovered by Marcus Raichle (1997)
			- Brain function that increases when the fMRI task is not occurring
			- ROIs: frontal lobe, parietal lobe
			- Damage to the DMN can prevent mind wandering
		- Salience network (attention to survival-relevant stimuli in the environment)

## **Chapter 3: Perception**

***Key takeaways:***
- Experience resulting from stimulation of the senses
	- Sensation: ability to detect the senses
	- Perception: method of the brain processing and communicating senses with the rest of the body and brain
- Perception is malleable and **occurs concurrently with actions**
- Process of perception:
	- Stimulus → reception → transduction → processing → perception → recognition → action
- Reasoning plays a large part in perception

***Computer perception:***
- Computer vision began in the 1950s
	- MIT (1966) attached a camera to a computer, which showed them how rudimentary computer vision was
- Machine & deep learning are improving computer capabilities
- Inverse projection problem: task of determining the object that projected an image onto the retina
- Occlusion: hiding or obscuring something from view
- Viewpoint invariance: the ability to recognize an object regardless of the perspective

***Types of visual processing:***
- **Bottom-up processing:** 
	- Stimulus → brain processing
	- When we see something, the information travels from our retina to our visual cortex, then to the associational cortex, where we assign meaning to the information
	- Example: seeing a series of white lines on a grey background and realizing that it is a crosswalk 
	- Example: touching a hot surface and quickly pulling your hand away
	- **With bottom-up processing, your experience is based on your senses**
- **Top-down processing:**
	- Brain → stimulus processing
	- Contextual clues, involving associations in our brain, allow us to better categorize information
	- Example: completing a CAPTCHA that asks you to mark crosswalks, and determining that a blurred white shape is a crosswalk based on context clues
	- Example: recognizing a pan is hot because it has just been on a hot stove
	- **With top-down processing, your experience is based on cognition**

***Auditory processing:***
- Speech segmentation: the ability to tell when one sentence ends and another begins regardless of language (top-down processing)
- Transitional probabilities: the likelihood that one sound will follow another within a word
- Statistical learning: process of learning about language patterns
	- Occurs as early as 8 months 

***Object processing:***
- **Helmholtz’s unconscious inference:**
	- Unconscious inference: our perceptions are the result of unconscious inferences about our environment
	- Likelihood principle: idea that we perceive the object most likely to have caused a certain stimulus 
- **Gestalt psychologists:**
	- Key ideas:
		- The mind groups patterns according to intrinsic laws of organization 
		- The whole is different than the sum of its parts
		- Experience is not necessarily a result of sensation
			- Cf. structuralism: experience is a result of basic blocks of sensation
	- Principles:
		- Law of simplicity: 
			- People tend to interpret stimuli as being as simple as possible
		- Law of good continuation: 
			- Lines tend to be seen as following the smoothest path
			- Things that appear connected are assumed to be continuous
		- Law of similarity: similar things appear to be grouped together
		- Law of proximity: objects that are close to each other are grouped together
		- Law of common fate: objects that move in the same direction are grouped together
		- Law of common region: objects within the same region are grouped together
- **Environmental regularities:** 
	- **Physical regularities:** regularly occurring physical properties 
		- Oblique effect: horizontal and vertical objects are perceived more easily because of their abundance
		- There are more neurons that respond to horizontal and vertical objects - could be a result of evolution
	- **Semantic regularities:** characteristics associated with functions performed in given scenes
		- **Scene schema:** knowledge of what a given scene usually contains
		- **Script:** knowledge of the actions that typically occur at a given scene
- **Bayesian inference: estimate of probability is based on prior probability**
	- Estimate of the probability of a given outcome is influenced by two factors:
		- Prior probability
		- Likelihood of a given outcome

***What vs. where:***
- Brain ablation: controlled removal of brain areas
- **What pathway:** 
	- Temporal lobe 
	- Ventral pathway
	- “Perception” pathway
	- Monkey experiments: difficulty with the object discrimination problem
		- Food was under one of two objects
	- Damage to ventral pathway causes **agnosia** (inability to recognize objects)
		- Patient D.F.
			- TBI: anoxia (oxygen deficit) from carbon monoxide poisoning 
			- Pathology: visual agnosia
			- Could not identify people or objects unless she had a non-visual sensory cue
- **Where pathway:**
	- Parietal lobe (receiving from V1)
	- Dorsal pathway
	- “Action” pathway
	- Monkey experiments: landmark discrimination problem
		- Food was closer to an object
	- Damage to dorsal pathway causes **optic ataxia** (inability to locate objects)
		- Patient R.V.
			- TBI: bilateral damage to parietal lobe
			- Pathology: optic ataxia

## **Chapter 10: Visual imagery**

***Types of imagery:***
- **Visual imagery:** seeing something without a visual stimulus
- **Mental imagery:** ability to recreate sensory experiences without stimuli 
- **Spatial imagery:** ability to imagine spatial relations
- **Object imagery:** ability to imagine details or objects
- **Imagination:** the mind’s ability to generate sensory experiences without stimuli
- **Early theories:**
	- Wundt proposed that there were three basic elements of consciousness:
		- Imagery
		- Sensations 
		- Feelings
	- Imageless thought debate: debate on whether thought is possible without images
	- **Imagery debate: debate on whether imagery is spatial or propositional** 
- Aspects of mental information:
	- Propositional information: information processed using language
	- Spatial information: visual information processed relative to its location
		- Dorsal/where pathway
	- Depictive information: visual information processed relative to the qualities of the stimulus
		- Ventral/what pathway
- **Aphantasia: complete lack of mental imagery**
	- Compensated with language skills?
- Mental images like peg systems or mind palaces can help people to remember information

***Imagery & perception:***
- Do imagery and perception share the same mechanisms?
- Both seeing visual stimuli and imaging them activates V1
- **Perky (1910):**
	- Asked participants to create a visual image on the wall
	- Picture was projected onto the wall
	- Participants did not notice the presence of the picture
	- Overlap between perception and imagery?
- **Shepard and Metzler (1971):** 
	- Imagery is spatial, and perception and imagery share mechanisms
	- **Mental chronometry:** amount of time needed to perform cognitive tasks
	- Participants mentally rotated an object to see if it matched another object (in a different orientation)
	- The more rotations needed, the longer the task took
- **Kosslyn (1973):**
	- Spatial correspondence between imagery and perception
	- Mental scanning: creating and scanning mental images 
		- Memorize picture and create a mental image
		- Then move one element of the image to another area
- **Pylyshyn (1973):**
	- Spatial representation is an epiphenomenon
		- Phenomenon that accompanies, but is not part of, a mechanism
	- Proposed that imagery is propositional
	- Sparked the imagery debate (see above)
- **Lea (1975):**
	- Alternative explanations against visual imagery
	- More distractions during larger scanning tasks
	- Low internal validity, difficult to judge what is actually happening cognitively
- **Kosslyn (1990s):**
	- **Mental walk tasks: tasks that involve mentally moving around an imaginary stimulus**
	- Spatial/depictive representations: different parts of an image are described relative to their locations
	- Propositional representations: different parts of an image are related by language
- **Ganis et al (2004):**
	- fMRI data showed overlap between perception and imagery in the brain
- **Farah (2000):**
	- Patient with damage to occipital lobe 
	- Damage to actual visual field resulted in damage to the field of visual imagery

***Imagery & cognition:***
- Cognitive revolution focused on the internal processes behind imagery
- **Paivio (1960s):**
	- Conceptual peg hypothesis: concrete nouns create mental images
		- In paired associate learning, a word that evokes a mental image is paired with another word that does not; the mental image helps to remember the other word
	- Dual coding theory: sensory imagery and verbal information are used together in the brain
- **Kreiman (2000):**
	- Imagery neurons: neurons that fire rapidly during perception and imagery

## **Chapter 4: Attention**

***Key takeaways:***
- **Attention:** ability to focus on specific stimuli
	- **History of attention:**
		- William James first investigated it in Principles of Psychology
	- **Types of attention:**
		- **Selective attention**: attending to one stimulus while ignoring others
		- **Divided attention:** attending to more than one stimulus at a time
		- **Endogenous attention:** voluntarily focusing on a stimulus to achieve a goal
			- Also known as voluntary attention
			- Goal-driven, effortful, and long lasting
			- Top-down process
			- Tested with central cues (i.e., arrows)
			- Can be directly covertly and overtly
			- Determinants of eye movements:
				- Scene schemas guide fixations in certain environments
				- Meaningful stimuli 
				- Tasks in progress (eye movements precede motor movements)
		- **Exogenous attention:** involuntarily focusing on a stimulus in a brief manner
			- Also known as involuntary attention
			- Automatic and short lasting
			- Bottom-up process
			- Tested with a peripheral cue (i.e., sudden flash)
			- Can be directly covertly and overtly
			- Determinants of eye movements:
				- Stimulus salience
				- Faces
				- Addictive stimuli
				- Scene schemas with inconsistent items
		- **Overt attention:** focus of attention that follows the movement of the eyes
		- **Covert attention:** shifting of attention without moving the eyes
			- Helmholtz discovered covert attention by seeing if he could remember stimuli that he was not actively looking at 
			- Posner (1978) cued participants to direct their attention to a stimulus before it appeared
				- Valid cues led to faster responses
		- **Attentional capture:** rapid shift of attention to a relevant stimulus
		- **Attentional warping:** map of categories in the brain changes to make more space for categories that are being searched for
	- **Modes of attention:**
		- **Inattentional blindness:** unawareness of visual stimuli that are not being attended to (cf. inattentional deafness for auditory stimuli)
			- **Change blindness:** inability to notice changes that are not being attended to
		- **Task switching:** switching of attention and cognition resources from one task to another
			- **Multitasking:** attempting to consciously divide attention between multiple tasks, while also attempting to switch between those tasks
			- **Productivity illusion:** false belief that multitasking increases productivity
		- **Automatic processing:** processing that occurs without intention and uses few cognitive resources
		- **Choking:** decline in performance following increased conscious awareness
		- **Visual scanning:** movements of the eye from one stimulus to another
			- Fixation: pausing of the eyes on places of interest
			- Saccade: eye movements from one fixation point to another
		- **Stimulus salience:**
			- Bottom-up perception
			- Factors that determine attention to stimuli
			- Saliency map: map of a scene that includes stimulus salience
		- **Scene schemas:**
			- Top-down perception
			- Knowledge about what is likely to be present in a given scene
			- Can guide attention towards relevant stimuli
- **Distraction:** a stimulus interfering with the processing of another stimulus
	- **Mind wandering:** thoughts that unintentionally or unconsciously arise
	- Multitasking (texting, calling, talking) while driving can be as dangerous as driving while drunk or fatigued
- **Schneider and Shiffrin (1977):**
	- Divided attention between remembering a target and monitoring rapidly presented stimuli, including distractors
	- With practice (600+ trials), participants improved
- **Broadbent (1958):**
	- Filter model of attention: a filter system lets some stimuli through and blocks others, before meaning is analyze, to make attention more effective and efficient
	- Early selection model: explains selective attention by early filtering of unattended stimuli
	- Supported by Cherry’s dichotic listening experiment (1953)
		- Cocktail party effect: ability to focus on one stimulus while filtering out others
		- Shadowing experiment: participants listened to two different messages, one in each ear, and had to repeat one of the messages
	- Filter identifies the attended stimulus based on its characteristics
	- Detector processes information from the attended stimulus to determine its meaning
	- Diagram:![[Screenshot 2025-05-22 at 12.09.29 AM.png]]
- **Treisman (1964):**
	- Intermediate selection model
	- Attenuation model of attention:
		- Proposed an attenuator instead of a filter, analyzing incoming messages in terms of physical characteristics, language, and meaning
		- All stimuli passed through, but relevant stimuli passed through at a greater strength
		- Dictionary unit: contains words that can be activated at a certain threshold
	- Diagram:![[Screenshot 2025-05-22 at 12.15.19 AM.png]]
- **MacKay (1973):**
	- Late selection models of attention: selection of relevant stimuli does not occur until after the information has been processed for meaning
		- Based on priming
	- MacKay’s experiment:
		- Dichotic listening with ambiguous phrases in one ear and either “river” or “money” in the other
		- Participants then had to choose a phrase closest to what they heard
		- Meaning of the other word biased the participants’ choices

***Load & capacity:***
- **Processing capacity:** 
	- Amount of information people can handle
	- Limits the ability to process new stimuli
- **Perceptual load:** related to task difficulty
	- High-load tasks use more processing capacity
	- Low-load tasks use less processing capacity
- **Stroop task:**
	- Color words appear on a screen, but the color of the font is different from the color indicated by the word
	- This interferes with participants’ ability to name the color of the font
- **Attentional blink task:**
	- RSVP (rapid serial visual presentation) task: distractor letters/numbers are interspersed with target letters/numbers
	- When participants have to attend to two different targets, performance worsens
- **Lavie (2009):**
	- Load theory of attention: ability to ignore task-relevant stimuli depends on the load of the current task

## **Chapters 5-8: Memory**

***Memory:***
- Processes involved in storing and retrieving information after the original stimuli are no longer present

***Modal model of memory:***
- Atkinson and Shiffrin (1968) proposed that memory processes information through stages (i.e., short-term and long-term memory)
- Proposed during cognitive revolution and inspired by Broadbent’s flow diagram
- Control processes: active methods of remembering that differ from task to task

***Sensory memory:*** 
- **Brief retention of sensory stimuli**
- Shortest structural feature (type of memory) in the modal model
- Sensory memory decays (is lost) within seconds (or even less than a second)
- **Echoic memory:** brief auditory sensory memory that persists for a few seconds after the original stimulus has faded
	- MEG study: participants hear a repeating tone, interspersed with deviant tones
	- Results showed Mismatch Negativity - reflects comparison between targets and distractors
	- Suggests that echoic memory lasts roughly 10 seconds
- **Iconic memory:** brief visual sensory memory of a *visual icon*, lasting for only a fraction of a second after the original stimulus has faded
	- Persistence of vision: continued perception of light after the original stimulus has faded
	- **Sperling’s experiments (1960):**
		- Whole report method: after a brief presentation, participants reported all stimuli
		- Partial report method: after a brief presentation, participants reported some of the stimuli (indicated by cues)
		- Delayed partial report method: after a brief presentation, participants reported some of the stimuli (indicated by delayed cues)

***Short-term memory:***
- **Middle structural feature in the modal model**
- Memory mechanism that holds limited (7 ± 2) information for a brief period (15-20 seconds)
- **Measures of short-term memory:**
	- Digit span: number of digits a person can keep in their short-term memory
	- Change detection: identifying differences between displays (Luck & Vogel)
- **Control processes:**
	- Rehearsal: repeating a stimulus over and over to keep it active in short-term memory
	- Chunking: combining smaller units in larger ones (chunks), which can increase the capacity of short-term memory 
		- Story mnemonic: putting random items into a meaningful order within a story 
- **Proactive interference:** previously-learned information hinders subsequent learning
	- Release: conditions that eliminate the decrease in performance caused by proactive interference 
- Debate: items vs information?

***Long-term memory:***
- **Memory mechanism that holds large quantities of information for long periods of time**
- Longest structural feature in the modal model
- Recognition memory: correct identification of a previously-occurring stimulus
- **Murdoch (1962):**
	- Serial position curve: 
		- Plotting how well participants recalled an ordered list of words
		- Primacy effect: when earlier words are better remembered
		- Recency effect: when later words are better remembered
- **Neuropsychology:**
	- **Patient H.M.:**
		- Bilateral hippocampal removal for treatment of epilepsy in 1953
		- Resulted in debilitating anterograde amnesia (inability to form new memories) and retrograde amnesia for just the 3 years preceding the surgery
	- **Patient K.F.:**
		- Accident resulted in damaged parietal lobe
		- Short-term memory was impaired, but long-term memory was intact
	- **Jill Price:**
		- Hyperthymesia: 
			- Incredibly detailed autobiographical memory and enhanced recall skills
			- Very rare - only a few cases have been identified

***Types of long-term memory:***
- **Explicit/declarative memory:** memories that can be consciously recalled or explained
	- **Semantic memory:**
		- Memory of facts
		- Mental time travel does not occur
		- Proposed by Tulving (1985)
		- K.C.:
			- Hippocampus was damaged
			- Loss of episodic memory, but semantic memory was intact
	- **Episodic memory:**
		- Memory of events
		- Mental time travel (reliving episodic memories) occurs
		- Proposed by Tulving (1985)
		- L.P.:
			- Encephalitis resulted in hippocampus damage
			- Loss of semantic memory, but episodic memory was intact
	- **Autobiographical memory:**
		- Memory of personal experiences
		- Involves aspects of both episodic and semantic memory
		- Semantic memory can retroactively affect episodic memory 
		- **Personal semantic memories:** factual memories of personal experiences
	- **Prospective memory:**
		- Memory used to remember, rehearse, and perform planned actions
		- Constructive episodic simulation hypothesis (really rolls off the tongue): theory that episodic memories are extracted and recombined to simulate future events
- **Implicit/nondeclarative memory:** memories that affect behavior, but cannot be consciously recalled 
- Related to basal ganglia and cerebellum
	- **Procedural/skill memory:**
		- Knowledge of how to perform actions or skills - it’s like riding a bike…
		- Expert-induced amnesia: unawareness of actions due to strong procedural memory
	- **Priming:**
		- Presentation of priming stimulus changes response to test stimulus
			- In-class example: word-generation task following presentation of word list
		- Propaganda effect: phenomenon of people reporting recently seen facts as true
	- **Conditioning:**
		- **Classical conditioning:**
			- Pairing of a neutral stimulus with a salient stimulus that elicits a response from the neutral stimulus
			- Unconditioned stimulus (UCS), which automatically causes response (UCR), is paired with conditioned stimulus (CS) to produce a new response (CR) to the CS
		- **Operant/instrumental conditioning:**
			- Shaping of future behavior with different consequences
			- Participant’s behavior leads to a reinforcement or punishment
				- Reinforcement: increases probability of repetition of behavior
				- Punishment: decreases probability of repetition of behavior
				- Negative contingency: result that takes something away from the participant
				- Positive contingency: result that gives something to the participant
			- Different operant contingencies: ![[Screenshot 2025-06-01 at 10.56.35 PM.png]]
	- **Non-associative learning:**
		- Habituation: repeated exposure to a stimulus results in progressive decrease in response
		- Sensitization: repeated exposure to a stimulus results in progressive increase in response

***Encoding of memories:***
- **Coding:** 
	- Visual coding
	- Auditory coding
	- Semantic coding (meaning-based)
- **Fluency:** the ease with which an item can be remembered
- Verbatim recall: the ability to recall information exactly as presented
- Metacognition (thinking about thinking) is involved
- **Emotion and memory:**
	- Emotional memories are more easily and vividly remembered
	- Emotion improves memory over time
	- Activity in amygdala improves memory ?
- **Levels of processing:**
	- Memory depends on the depth of processing of information
	- Deep processing: involves close attention to meaning and elaborative rehearsal
	- Shallow processing: involves maintenance rehearsal
- **Retrieval methods:**
	- Maintenance rehearsal: repetition that does not involve meaning or connections
	- Elaborative rehearsal: repetition that involves meaning or connections
	- Paired-associate learning: word recall skills are tested based on semantically logical vs. non-logical word pairs 
	- Self-reference effect: memory is improved if you relate a word to yourself
	- Generation effect: generating information based on context is better for memory formation than memorization
	- Retrieval practice effect: memory retrieval increases elaboration, which improves memory
	- Testing effect: being tested on information improves memory
	- Spacing effect/distributed practice: increase in performance from short study sessions separated by breaks
	- State-dependent learning: memory is best when a person is in the same state for encoding and retrieval
	- Transfer-appropriate processing: memory is improved if types of processing are matched from encoding to retrieval (Morris, 1977)
- **Retrieval cues:**
	- Free recall: participants freely retrieve information
	- Cued recall: participants retrieve information with the aid of cues
		- Greatly improves recall - see Tulving & Pearlstone (1966), Mantyla (1986)
- **Consolidation:**
	- Donald Hebb (1948) proposed consolidation as the neural representation of learning and memory
		- When we learn something, it changes thousands of our synaptic patterns and connections - actually so fucking cool
	- **Models of consolidation:**
		- Standard model of consolidation: memory retrieval depends on the hippocampus only during consolidation
		- Multiple trace model of consolidation: memory retrieval, especially of episodic memories, always depends on the hippocampus
	- **Types of consolidation:**
		- Synaptic consolidation: rapid structural changes occurring at synapses
		- Systems consolidation: gradual structural changes occurring at circuits in various brain regions
			- Based on Gilboa (2004), who identified that the hippocampus is always somewhat involved in activation and consolidation
		- **Long-term potentiation:** increased firing of a neuron due to activity at the prior synapse
	- Reactivation: previously encoded memories are retrieved, and memory is strengthened
	- **Time**, **sleep**, and **awareness** of testing are all critical for consolidation
		- Take a nap - it will help (a long one, too)

***Working memory:***
- Introduced by Baddeley and Hitch (1974) as a replacement for short-term memory
- Limited-capacity system for temporary storage of information relating to comprehension, learning, and reasoning
- Related to **prefrontal cortex**, which processes visual and auditory information
	- Delayed-response task: information is provided, then memory is tested after a delay
	- Monkeys without PFC performed poorly on the delayed-response task
	- Without PFC, monkeys cannot hold information in their working memory
	- Single-cell recordings show that neurons remain active while the monkey holds information in working memory
- **Phonological loop:** mechanism that holds and processes verbal and auditory information
	- Components:
		- Phonological store: holds a limited about of verbal and auditory information for a few seconds
		- Articulatory rehearsal process: keeps items in the phonological store from decaying
	- Phonological similarity effect: occurs when similar letters or words are confused
	- Word length effect: the longer the list of words, the more difficult it is to remember
	- Articulatory suppression: interference with the operation of the phonological loop that occurs when an irrelevant word is repeated instead of a relevant word
- **Visuospatial sketchpad:** holds and processes visual and spatial information
	- Visual imagery: creation of mental images in the absence of a visual stimulus
- **Central executive:** part of working memory that coordinates the activity of the phonological loop and visuospatial sketchpad
	- Perseveration: difficulty in switching from one behavior or process to another
		- Often occurs in patients with frontal lobe damage
- **Episodic buffer:** backup store that communicates with long-term and working memory stores
	- Holds more information for longer than the components of working memory stores

***Memory loss:***
- **Amnesia:**
	- **Retrograde amnesia:** inability to retain past memories, especially prior to TBI
	- **Anterograde amnesia:** inability to create and store new memories
	- **Graded amnesia:** amnesia that is most severe for events that occurred directly before TBI
	- **Infantile amnesia:**
		- Inability to recall memories from infancy or early childhood
		- Normal part of development
		- Repressed childhood memories: memories that have been “excluded” from consciousness
			- Controversial due to ambiguity and instances of false implantation of child memories
	- **Dementia:** broad diagnosis used to categorize intense cognitive decline, especially related to memory and reasoning
- **Remembering vs knowing procedure:** 
	- Participants are presented with a stimulus, then asked to indicate *remember* if they vividly remember the stimulus presentation, or *know* if the stimulus seems familiar but they do not remember the presentation
- **Semanticization of remote memories:** loss of episodic detail for distant memories

***Memory errors:***
- Source monitoring: attributing the origin of memories
- Source monitoring error/misattribution: misidentifying sources of memories
	- When memories are encoded, the original context can sometimes be lost
	- Scripts and schemas (see **Perception notes**) can affect memories - your expectations bias you
	- **Jacoby (1989)** experiment:
		- Participants read random names
		- Later, participants had to identify the famous people in a given list of names
		- The longer participants waited, the less accurate they became
- Cryptomnesia: unconscious plagiarism of others’ work
- Illusory truth effect: repeated presentation leads to increased probability of evaluating a statement as true
	- Related to propaganda effect 
- Pragmatic inference: inferring something implicit from a statement after multiple exposures
- Misinformation effect: information (misleading post-event information) presented after an event changes how the event is perceived 
	- **Loftus (1974)** experiment:
		- Participants viewed footage of a car accident
		- When researchers used the word “smashed” instead of “hit,” participants reported a much more violent accident
	- **Loftus (1975)** experiment:
		- Participants viewed footage of a car accident
		- When asked misleading questions about the road signage, participants would often falsely report their memories of the accident
	- **Lindsey (1990)** experiment:
		- Participants heard a story narrated by either a woman, a man, or both
		- Misleading information would be presented
			- When it was presented by the same narrator, participants accepted that information as true
			- When the narrator switched, participants recognized the information as false
- **Flashbulb memories:** 
	- Memories of shocking, collective, and highly publicized events (9/11, Challenger, JFK, January 6, pandemic)
	- Flashbulb memories can be shaped over time
	- Recall errors can occur after a delay
- **False memories:**
	- Deese-Roediger-McDermott (DRM) paradigm:
		- Participants hear a list of words related to a concept
		- When tested on the list, participants will often recall the concept, though it was not originally included in the list
		- Sleep demo in class
	- Wade (2002):
		- After viewing photoshopped pictures from their childhood, participants would report that they remembered the events in the pictures
- **Memory errors** can become extremely relevant (and potentially dangerous) in **criminal justice** settings
	- Over time, reproductions of memories can become shorter and contain omissions or inaccuracies
	- **Errors in eyewitness testimonies:**
		- Witnesses can be easily influenced, which is a problem since witness statements are often the main source 
		- Errors can occur due to familiarity - witnesses are more likely to accuse someone they have seen
		- Witnesses almost always identify the suspect in a lineup, even when the real criminal is not present
			- Sequential viewing can help, but it is controversial
			- Blind lineup procedures: the detective running the test should not known who the suspect is
		- Weapon focus effect: fired gun distracts witnesses from all other actions taking place
		- Post-identification feedback effect: increase in confidence when identification is validated 
		- Cognitive interview reduces interruption, validation, or potential manipulation of recall
		- Research done by Gary Wells in the 1990s

## **Chapter 11: Language**

***Overview:***
- **Language:** system of communication using sounds or symbols to facilitate expression
	- Communication: system by which information is exchanged
	- Alphabet: standardized set of letters or symbols representing individual sounds
	- Hierarchical nature of language: idea that language consists of a series of small units that are combined to form larger units
	- Rule-based nature of language: idea that rules in language specify the ways to arrange words and phrases
- **Hierarchy of language:**
	- Phoneme → morpheme → word → phrase → sentence → narrative
		- Phonemes:
			- Smallest unit of sound
			- No meaning on its own
- **Universality:**
	- All humans develop and learn a language
		- Even deaf children will invent their own form of sign language
	- Language is universal across cultures, and development is similar across cultures
- **Cognitive psychology & language:**
	- Psycholinguistics: psychology study of language
	- Chomsky (1957) argued with Skinner (1957) over whether language developed out of operant conditioning (it does not)
		- Chomsky stated that language is genetically coded and universal
- **Lexicality:**
	- Lexicon: knowledge of what words mean, how they sound, and how they’re used
	- Lexical semantics: meanings of words
		- Semantics (meaning) is separate from syntax (grammar)
	- Lexical decision task: procedure in which participant is asked to decide as quickly as possible whether a particular stimulus is a word or not
		- All possible meanings for a word are activated during initial word recognition
	- Lexical ambiguity: words that have more than one meaning
	- Meaning dominance: when different meanings of a word compete for relevance
		- Some meanings of words are used more frequently than used
		- Balanced dominance: words with two meanings that have the same frequency
		- Biased dominance: words with two meanings that have different frequencies 
		- Can be influenced by context
- **Frequency:**
	- Word frequency: relative usage of words in a particular language
	- Word frequency effect: phenomenon of faster reading times for high-frequency words than low-frequency words
	- Speech segmentation: perception of individual words regardless of pauses
- **Perception:**
	- Language perception is multi-modal
	- It involves both top-down and bottom-up processing
	- Perception of words is easier than perception of single sounds
	- Phonemic restoration effect: occurs when missing sounds in a phrase are filled in by our brain
- **Conversations:**
	- Rapid, shared speech involving multiple people
		- Shared information increases as conversations continue
		- Common ground: knowledge shared among conversationalists
		- Entrainment: synchronization between conversationalists
			- Picking up words, phrases, and mannerisms from others
- **Neuropsychology of semantics:**
	- **Broca’s aphasia:**
		- Use of subject words without grammatical structure
			- Telegraphical speech: use of “content” words (nouns or verbs)
			- Agrammatism: issues with grammar structure
			- Issues with rules and combinatorial processes
		- Comprehension is intact, but speech is not fluent
	- **Wernicke’s aphasia:**
		- Use of nonsensical words within grammatical structure
			- Anomia: issues with remembering and selecting words
			- Issues with lexical access in associative memory
		- Comprehension is not intact, but speech is fluent

## **Chapter 13: Judgement**

***Overview:***
- **Judgement:** making a decision or drawing a conclusion about something or someone
	- Confirmation bias: tendency to accept or reject information based on whether it confirms our beliefs
	- Myside bias: confirmation bias specific towards one’s own opinions and attitudes
	- Bias bias: tendency to recognize biases in others, but not in oneself
	- Belief bias: tendency to believe syllogisms are valid if their conclusions are believable
- **Reasoning:** cognitive processes that make conclusions beyond given information
	- **Inductive reasoning:** conclusions made based on information that is evidenced to be true
		- Argument depends on representativeness and the number of observations
		- **Heuristics:** rules that provide likely solutions to problems
			- Availability heuristic: events that are more easily remembered are perceived as being more likely
			- Representativeness heuristic: probability that A is part of B can be determined by how well A resembles B
			- Conjunction rule: the probability of the conjunction of two events cannot be higher than the probability of each event
		- **Correlation:** connection between events
			- Illusory/spurious correlation: correlation between two events that appears true, but is not actually true
				- Stereotypes: oversimplified, often negative, generalizations about groups of people 
	- **Deductive reasoning:** reasoning that involves logic
		- Syllogism: basic form of logical statement that involves two premises and a conclusion
			- Categorical syllogisms: syllogisms that categorize with “all, none, or some” 
			- Conditional syllogisms: syllogisms based on if-then statements
			- Mental model approach: determining whether syllogisms are valid by creating models of situations based on premises
- **Decision:** making choices between alternatives
	- **Utility:** outcomes that achieve goals
		- Expected utility theory: theory that assumes that people are basically **rational**, so they will make the most beneficial decision for themselves
	- **Emotions:**
		- Expected emotions: predicted emotions evoked by decision outcomes
		- Incidental emotions: emotions that are **not** caused by decision-making but that affect decision making
	- **Choice presentation:**
		- Framing effect: decisions are influenced by how the choices are presented
			- Opt-in procedure: procedure that requires people to take active steps to choose a course of action
			- Opt-out procedure: procedure that requires people to take active steps to avoid a course of action
			- Status quo bias: tendency to do nothing when faced with a decision
		- Risk aversion: tendency to make decisions that avoid risk
		- Risk-taking strategy: decision-making strategy governed by risks
	- **Neuroeconomics:**
		- Neuroscience of decision making
