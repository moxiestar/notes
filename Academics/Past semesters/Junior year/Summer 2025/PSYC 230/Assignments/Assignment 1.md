## Draft
For my first faculty member, I’m focusing on Dr. Joseph Hopfinger, a professor of Cognitive Psychology whose research focuses on attention, perception, and working memory (WEBSITE). My interest in his research was first piqued when I took his electroencephalography laboratory course last fall. It was only the second neuroscience course that I’d taken since switching to psychology as a sophomore, and I was still extremely overwhelmed. Dr. Hopfinger’s course made the complex concepts of electroencephalography more accessible for me, as well as providing me with the skills to analyze electroencephalographic data on my own. This fall, I’ll be returning to the class as a learning assistant — my first teaching experience, and one that I’m very excited for. 
Currently, I work as an undergraduate research assistant in Dr. Hopfinger’s lab, where we run experiments measuring mechanisms of attention. In particular, one of my supervisors, Sepideh Hedayati, is examining the overlap between attentional hold and capture, and how that overlap is represented in electroencephalographic waveforms. She expects to see that images of human faces, compared to non-facial stimuli, capture participants’ attention. My other supervisor, Saleena Lynch, is designing an experiment on visual working memory for her master’s thesis. Using OpenSesame, we’ve created an experiment that tasks participants with remembering strings of letters while responding to visual probes. This experiment is based on one that Dr. Hopfinger and an old graduate student used in previous research on distraction (PARKS & HOP). Dr. Hopfinger’s earlier research, seen in Nature Neuroscience, used fMRI to evaluate levels of attention within the brain (NAT ARTICLE). He has also conducted experiments with transcranial magnetic stimulation, though most of his recent work centers on electroencephalography. 
My second faculty member is Dr. Sami Yousif, who researches topology and spatial cognition (WEBSITE). Though I don’t know Dr. Yousif personally, I had the privilege of listening to his talk at the NC Conference on Cognition this April. Dr. Yousif was an engaging and kind speaker, and his slides were clear and thoughtful. His talk caught my attention, particularly the more visual aspects of his research. He presented several papers (ARTICLES) that examined how participants perceived sets of topologies (small linear maps, similar to the famous map of the London Underground). His studies had found that people have a seemingly innate ability to recognize an outlier in a set of topologies, suggesting that we have strong natural understandings of spatiality. His lab conducted a modified version of the experiment among children (KIDS ARTICLE), and they found the same results. Past research in Dr. Yousif’s lab has focused on visual adaptation, visuospatial representation, and the temporal aspect of memory. Also, as a side note, his website is the best lab website I have ever seen.
