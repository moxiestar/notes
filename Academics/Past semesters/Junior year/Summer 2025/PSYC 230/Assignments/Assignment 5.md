**Clive Wearing:**

The first interview in this assignment focuses on Clive Wearing, an elderly English man who, after suffering from encephalitis, presented one of the most severe cases of anterograde amnesia ever documented. Anterograde amnesia, the inability to form new long-term memories, keeps Clive eternally stuck in the present moment. Clive’s memory only lasts for about two minutes at most, and because of his amnesia, he cannot transfer those short-term memories to his long-term memory store. In his journal, Clive records that he is finally awake, but because his short-term memory — and by extension, his conscious awareness — is constantly being refreshed, this awakening does not last. His journal is filled with entries upon entries that are crossed out, with Clive insisting in each new entry that this time, it’s the real thing. Later, when his wife asks him about the entries, he believes that he wrote them while unconscious. He acknowledges the handwriting is his, but does not remember ever seeing the book and has no idea how his writing got there. When pressed, he becomes angry, demands that his wife “use her intelligence,” and even throws the book aside. 
Out of everything, the thing he remembers most vividly may be his wife. It is clear, even from a short video, that he loves her deeply. He is able to remember that he is married and knows his wife’s name, but when he sees her, he expresses that he is “conscious for the first time.” Clive tells his wife that his condition is “like death,” a statement that’s stuck with me since I watched the interview. Despite his debilitating amnesia, however, some aspects of his memory have remained intact. His procedural memory has not been affected; his motor functions are fine, and as a former musician, he can still play music well, though his wife admits that he might be “a bit rusty.” When his wife asks what year it is, he is able to correctly identify the decade, and remembers that his birthday is “next month,” though he is unable to say how old he is. He can use contextual clues — the weather and the landscape outside — to guess what season it is. His intelligence, conversational and speech skills, as well as his sense of humor, are still intact. I’ve seen a couple interviews with Clive, and his case has always struck me as being particularly tragic. 
Jill Price, the subject of the second interview in this assignment, has the opposite problem: she has an extraordinarily powerful autobiographical memory. She can remember an astounding number of details, both semantic and episodic, about every day of her life since she was fourteen years old. Jill is able to accurately describe meals she had years ago, and her memory is just as sharp when it comes to facts — she can remember when the LA riots started, or when Ted Kaczynski was arrested. When speaking with the interviewer, Jill states that she has a “split screen” in her mind, with one half attending to her present surroundings and the other half playing a constant tape of memories. Interestingly, only her personal semantic memory is so accurate, and her general semantic memory is not especially strong. Jill says that she struggled in school, and found it impossible to memorize facts or recite poems. Emotionally, her condition has its ups and downs: her brain is a scientific wonder, but she says that it is “horrendous” not being able to forget anything.
