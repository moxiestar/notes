## Draft
For this assignment, we watched a series of snippets from the show Brain Games, where witnesses at the scene of a crime were quizzed about their recollection of the events. The first question asked how many people were involved in the crime — I correctly answered four, but probably only got this right because the video had just called attention to the fourth accomplice, the man in the red jacket. For the second question, witnessed were presented with a line-up of suspects, with the majority identifying #5 as the suspect. I’m not completely sure how, since the men in the line-up all looked pretty similar, but I managed to get this one right too — the suspect was actually #3. The witnesses chose #5 because he was also present at the crime scene, standing behind the magician, though he was not actually involved in the crime. 
Overall, I thought the witnesses performed pretty poorly. I know I had an advantage, since I got to view the crime several times, but their inconsistencies were glaring. On top of that, their confidence in their version of events shocked me. One woman in particular, a young white woman with curly blonde hair, was extremely overconfident about what had taken place, very adamant that she knew what had happened. In the courtroom sequence, many of the witnesses stated that they would testify before a jury, despite the fact that most of them were wrong about what had occurred at the crime scene. This experiment really changed the way I think about the justice system — like many, I’ve always had faith in witness statements, and thought of them as a powerful form of proof, but watching this showed me how unreliable human memory really is. I thought the detective in the video did a good job shedding light on the issues in the justice system and recommending improvements, but it’s clear that we have a long way to go.
