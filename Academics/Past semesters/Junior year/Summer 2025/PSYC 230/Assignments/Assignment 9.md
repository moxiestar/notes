## Draft

I have to admit that this was one of the hardest assignments, since we learned so much so quickly that it was hard to narrow down to just ten concepts. These are the ones that I ended up choosing as the most memorable. 

1. Eyewitness testimonies. While watching the Brain Games episode, I was shocked by how inaccurate the witnesses were (and by how little I could recall). I have always been, for a variety of reasons, a proponent of the death penalty, but this made me really question my beliefs. The story of Ronald Cotton was incredibly moving and I hope I can find the time to read his book. 
2. Aphantasia. One of my best friends once mentioned that she has little to no visual imagery, but I just couldn’t imagine what that was like. This section of the course was fascinating for me, since I have very intense visual imagery. 
3. Broca’s “tan” patient. Even though I’ve seen neurosurgery up close, the image of the patient’s brain was so shocking that it’s become seared in my memory. I’m interested in both aphasia and brain damage, so this sparked a bit of morbid curiosity.
4. Cognitive revolution and computer development. Though I do a lot of work in cognitive psychology, I actually didn’t know the history of the cognitive revolution. In particular, I didn’t know that the revolution was spurred by the development of the early computer, and I found this really interesting. 
5. Little Albert experiment. This experiment just struck me as particularly messed up, albeit in an amusing way. Whenever I think of classical conditioning, I think of grown-up Albert, panicking when he sees Santa Claus. They could really get away with anything back then. 
6. Steve Carell neuron. My biopsychology professor briefly covered localization of function, but the example of the Steve Carell neuron was such a perfect mental image that I don’t think I’ll ever forget this concept. Do I now have a neuron that responds specifically to the idea of the Steve Carell neuron?
7. Perky (1910). This experiment blew my mind — the idea that your visual imagery could be so vivid that you wouldn’t notice an image projected in front of you is, for lack of a better word, kind of insane. I’d really like to see a demo of this one. 
8. Jill Price. As someone with a pretty terrible memory, I always assumed having “photographic memory” would be a gift. But Price made it sound odd at best and miserable at worst. 
9. Meaning dominance. I never really thought about how we choose the right meaning for a given word, but this unit was interesting. Because of the example we saw in class, I now have a strong association between meaning dominance and the word “cardinal.” 
10. Dory’s memory loss. I saw Finding Dory in theaters, and cried, loudly, but never realized that her issue was with the formation of long-term memories, not so much with short-term memory. This whole unit was a lot of fun — I loved watching the different trailers and trying to identify the types of amnesia.
