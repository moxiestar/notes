**Theories of attention:**
In the video, the narrator discusses three competing theories of selective attention: the early, late, and attenuation theories. She argues that the late selection theory could be too restrictive, since processing of sensory stimuli occurs quickly and efficiently. Our brains are designed to identify relevant stimuli within a short time span, but the late selection theory posits that we wait until we have categorized stimuli based on meaning to select relevant stimuli. This would be a waste of time and resources, and the theory goes against the fact that we can react to relevant stimuli while still processing others. 

Treisman’s Attenuation Theory (1964) is an intermediate selection model, proposing an attenuator in place of the filters seen in the early and late attention models. The attenuator takes in stimuli from both ears, both attended and unattended, and analyzes incoming messages in terms of physical characteristics, language, and meaning. In Treisman’s model, all stimuli are processed for meaning. Attended stimuli are given priority within the brain, but the attenuator can make unattended stimuli available if they become important later. 
   
Though the debate about attentional mechanisms continues, Treisman’s Attenuation Theory is supported by the process of language acquisition in infants. Some of our language abilities are universal, but others are learned from nearby adults. From an early age, infants must be able to filter out background noises, categorizing them as unimportant so that they can focus on human voices. Without isolating human voices, infants would be unable to pick up speech from surrounding adults. The fact that infants have an innate ability to categorize certain stimuli as important corroborates the Attenuation Theory. 

**Posner Cuing Demo:**
The Posner cueing task, developed by Michael Posner in the late-twentieth century, measures mechanisms of spatial attention. Cues, which appear before stimuli, direct participants’ gaze either towards or away from target stimuli. These are known as valid and invalid cues, respectively. Using cueing to manipulate visual attention can illuminate how attentional processes occur in the brain. In this particular experiment, we are presented with a cue (a green X), followed by a stimulus (a green circle), and asked to respond with keypresses based on the location of the stimulus. Valid cues appear on the same side of the screen as the stimuli, while invalid stimuli appear opposite. 

I completed the experiment with an average reaction time of 369 ms for valid cues and 486 ms for invalid cues. These results aligned with my expectations, since during the experiment, I could feel myself lagging behind after invalid cues, especially invalid cues that occurred after a string of valid cues. Also, I am currently extremely exhausted, since my dog no longer sleeps through the night, so I did not expect my focus to be particularly sharp. This lack of focus could have contributed to my slower reaction times following invalid cues.
