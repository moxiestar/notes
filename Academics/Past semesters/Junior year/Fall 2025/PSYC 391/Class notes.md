## Week 1: Learning Myths
**Students are not accurate judges of their own knowledge**
- High-performing students are more accurate - possibly because they better understand their cognition 
- High-performing students also overestimated others’ performances, taking their knowledge for granted
- “Students who knew more …. were much more capable of evaluating how much they knew” (p. 2)
- Bad students overestimate themselves
- “Incompetence … causes poor performance but also the inability to recognize that one’s performance is poor” (p. 4)
- Hindsight bias negatively influences feedback after bad performances - answers seem obvious when we have access to them

**Teaching methods do not have an effect on students’ learning**
- Studies on different teaching methods did not show any significant effect on learning
- Some students performed better when taught with their least favorite methods
- Customized learning could even harm performance as compared to control groups

**Neuroscience & education**
- Myths about neuroscience are surprisingly prevalent in education 
	- In the UK over 90% of educators believe in preferred teaching methods and over 40% of educators believe we only use 10% of our brains 
- Though many myths are grounded in truth, they are often overblown in the world of popular science - which makes sense since most people get scientific information through a secondary source (ie social media)
- Communicating neuroscientific findings in accessible language is difficult and misunderstandings are common

## Week 2: Academic Productivity
**Optimizing Learning in College**
- Students come into college underprepared and overwhelmed
	- Introductory college courses don’t teach students how to study 
	- Many students only study by re-reading textbooks or notes
- Active, purposeful thinking aids learning
	- Starting school: organizing your schedule, finding times and places to study, buying course materials before the semester starts
	- Before class: review comprehension questions before each chapter, ask questions about key concepts
	- During class: attend class, write notes on paper, follow slides on paper
	- After class: prepare for tests, use spaced repetition, quiz yourself
- Incorporating other healthy habits will improve learning too
- Overconfidence harms students: students think they know more than they do

**Popular Study Strategies**
- **Rereading**
	- By far the most common strategy, but also the least active
	- Can give students false confidence
	- Most effective when first and later readings are spaced out
	- Not helpful when cramming
- **Highlighting**
	- Also common and relatively passive
	- Generally effective for most exams
	- Students are not always able to select the most important information to highlight
- **Note-taking**
	- Relatively less common, but more active
	- Shows beneficial effects on encoding
	- Organizing information helps to retain it (lol I know)
	- Reviewing notes is also effective over time
	- Copying notes verbatim is not helpful for learning
- **Outlining**
	- Least common of the five strategies
	- Again, organizing information is beneficial for retention
	- Outlines provided by professors are more helpful than outlines created by students
	- Skeletal outlines, filled out after lectures, are helpful, especially when the skeletal is created before class
- **Flashcards**
	- More common and also somewhat active
	- Most helpful for simple memorization
	- Like rereading, can give students a false sense of knowledge
	- Psychologists believe that flashcards help with retention, while students use flashcards to check their knowledge
	- Learning suffers when students choose to drop flashcards

**SMART goals**
- Time management has a huge effect on academic success
- Working backwards helps to plan more effectively
- **S**pecific, **M**easurable, **A**ction-orientated, **R**ealistic, **T**ime-bound goals
