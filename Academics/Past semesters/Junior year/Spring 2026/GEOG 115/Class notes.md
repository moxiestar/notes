## Lecture 1
**Space**
- **Space:** the arrangement of things and relation between them
- **Spatial thinking:** how people understand and organize space
	- Spatial thinking originates in the vestibular system 
	- Grid and place cells in the hippocampus help us locate ourselves in our mental map
	- Culture and evolution have shaped our spatial cognition
- **Spatial frameworks:** ways that maps have taught us how to understand space

-------------------------------------------------------------------
## Lecture 2
**Gesture-first hypothesis**
- Strong evidence that gesturing was the first human “language”
	- Non-verbal way of communicating spatial relationships
	- Also a way of processing spatial information
	- Spoken language may have developed from gesturing
		- Communication pathways are based on spatial processing
		- Left side of hippocampus relates to spatial understanding, while right side of hippocampus relates to sequential communication

**More spatial thinking**
- Spatial thinking is internal, external, and inherent to how we move through the world
	- Externalizing spatial thinking allows for mutual understanding, navigating, record keeping, and ownership

**What are maps?**
- Maps are visual depictions of relationships between things in a space
- Maps are also externalized arguments for how space should be represented
- Basically, maps show the things that exist, how they’re related, and how they should be represented

## Lecture 3
**Scientific cartography**
- Cartographers are objective scientists, measuring the land
	- This led to the idea that maps are inherently true

**Critical cartography**
- Launched by historian J. B. Harley
- Based on two earlier theories
	- Foucault: knowledge is intertwined with power
	- Derrida: maps are rhetorical and can be deconstructed
- “Deconstructing Maps” by Harley
	- Maps construct geographic knowledge through selection, omission, symbolization, scale, orientation, design, data
- Mapmaking has always been linked to power
	- As a result, some maps are seen as more correct than others
- Mapmakers create maps based on status and culture
	- Their authority is not necessarily earned and may be biased
- **Two key takeaways**
	- Maps shape our understanding of space
	- Maps influence political, legal, and social decisions about land

## Lecture 5
**Spatial logic**
- Space encompasses both the objects in space and their arrangement
- **Early examples**
	- **Qibla maps**
		- Circular maps depicting the world
		- Focus on layout of religion and morality
		- Spatial relationships are unidirectional
			- Direction matters, but not distance
	-  **T-O maps**
		- Space is organized relative to Jerusalem 
		- Orientation towards Eden
		- T represents the cross 
	- **Babylonian World Map**
		- Rudimentary geography
		- Babylon is the center of the world

## Lecture 6
**Spatial hierarchy**
- Space is a reflection of political-social hierarchy
	- Hierarchical relationships matter most
	- Maps should be arranged to signal important & hierarchy
- **Early examples**
	- **Japanese Buddhist World Map**
		- Created in 1710
		- Extremely geographically inaccurate - on purpose?
		- Centered on a lake in Tibet where Buddha was conceived 
	- **Gyoki-style maps**
		- Japan is represented in the shape of Vajra (Buddhist spiritual object)
		- Regions are represented as abstract ovals 
			- Representation is based on geopolitics, not on actual geography
	- **Da Ming Hunyi Tu**
		- Created during the Ming Dynasty (1300s)
		- Resurgence of Confucian ideology
		- Only China is drawn accurately, using the Pei Xiu’s grid system
	- **Kangido Map**
		- Created in Korea (1400s)
		- Reflects Korea’s status as a new empire
		- Situates Korea within a global network
		- Confirms knowledge of Japanese Gyoki map

## Lecture 7
**Space as time**
- Our experience of space is inseparable from time
	- Space is produced over time
	- Relationships and patterns in space emerge over time
	- Movement and change shape how space exists over time
	- Space is a dynamic and layered product of past and present
- Maps (which are temporally static) give the impression that space is fixed
- Ideally, space would be represented sequentially, showing change instead of static
- **Early examples**
	- **Peutinger Map**
		- Created in the 4th century
		- 22 feet long, showing 53,000 miles of roads
		- Illustrated itinerarium
			- Itinerary guiding travel through the Roman Empire
			- Ancient equivalent of a subway map
		- Simplified topography 
	- **Matthew Paris Itinerary** 
		- Created in the 13th century
		- Used by a cloistered monk
		- The temporal sequence on the map represents their spiritual journey
	- **Mesoamerican cartography**
		- Developed before colonization
		- Space and time were never separate
		- Maps simultaneously included calendars, historical records, and geography
		- **Codex Mendoza (1541)**
			- Produced for Spanish consumption
			- Space and time move out from the center
		- **Lienzo of Zacatepec (1540)**
			- Pairs territorial boundaries with historical narratives
			- Small pictographs on the map tell stories
		- **Plano en Papel de Maguey (post-colonization)**
			- Measured geographical maps
			- Accepted by Spanish authorities as legal documents

## Lecture 8
**Space as propaganda**
- Space as a political resource that can be taken or lost
- Maps should be made in relation to power
- Maps should be future-orientated
- Geopolitical maps originated in fascist countries (Germany, Italy)
- **Examples**
	- **Grundsignaturen (1935)**
		- Style guide for geopolitical mapping
		- Developed in Germany
		- Renders space as dynamic and power-based 
	- **Geopolitik (1934)**
		- Not created by a professional cartographer
		- Produced in Germany
		- States are depicted as organisms (lebensraum)
	- **L’equilibrio politico mediterraneo (1939)**
		- Depicts the Roman idea of Mare Nostrum
		- Represents a hunger for power that is “rightfully theirs”
		- Anger after the Mutilated Victory in WWI
		- Goal of reclaiming the Roman Empire
	- **Germans in Europe (1934)**
		- Produced in Germany
		- Transition from Grundsignaturen-style maps to the use of color maps
		- Proves the need to redraw borders based on German occupation
	- **The New Europe Is Unbeatable (1942)**
		- Produced in Germany
		- Use of arrows and color for propaganda purposes
		- Layout presents German expansion as inevitable 
- **Characteristics of propaganda maps**
	- Deliberate distortion
	- Symbolism
	- Selective emphasis
	- Emotional manipulation
	- Simplification 
	- Narrative

## Lecture 9
**Standardizing space**
- Up until now, space has been imbued with external meaning
- Driven by Ancient Greek philosophy
	- Questioning life and matter
	- **Two main concepts**
		- Space is structured according to geometric principles
		- Those geometric principles can be defined mathematically
- **Ptolemaic maps**
	- Ptolemy’s Geographia provided a template with
		- Latitude and longitude
		- Grid-based mapping
		- Projection methods
	- Idea that the earth could be mapped with a coordinate system

## Lecture 10
**Western Scientific Cartography**
- **Relies on universal measured geography**
	- Space shown as a measurable object that can be quantified
	- Maps correspond to standardized locations in the world
- **Pioneered during the Renaissance**
	- Cultural shift from religion to science
	- Revival of classical (Greek) texts
	- Technological change
	- Practical demands
- **Characteristics of scientific cartography**
	- Coordinate systems
	- Geometric projection
	- Measurement and scale
	- Standardization and universality 
	- Functional utility 
- **Functions of scientific cartography**
	- Facilitated navigation
	- Allowed leaders to claim territory 
	- Defined trade routes
	- Provided shared knowledge
- **Early examples**

## Lecture 11
**What is territory?**
- Bounded unit of space whose edges are legally enforceable
	- Boundaries have typically been socially generated
	- Space must be actively maintained
- **Territorialization:** process of creating a territory
- **History of territories**
	- Emerged in response to military, economic, and political pressures
- **State cartography**
	- Formal, scientific means of mapping territories (and power)
- **Early examples**
	- **Polder maps**
		- Created in the Netherlands from 1600-1700
		- Used for administrative purposes
	- **Catastro of Ensenada**
		- Created in Spain in 1749
		- Used for taxation and revenue 
	- **Saxon maps**
		- Created in Germany from 1600-1700
		- Used in legal land ownership disputes

## Lecture 12
**Governance of space**
- **Theory of spatial governance**
	- Governing space is easier and simpler than governing people
		- Space can be bounded, measured, and standardized
	- Once space is governed, authorities can then apply that governance to people in the space
	- Basically, space governed → people governed
- **Model of governing space**
	- **Problematization:** governing the space must solve an existing problem
		- Space can be classified as problematic through moral and social implications
	- **Authorization:** the space must be accessible for governance
		- Certain figures have authority or expertise over space
	- **Intervention:** action must be taken to govern the space
		- Space is reorganized to achieve governance
		- This can be physical, regulatory, economic, etc
- **Examples**
	- **Panopticon**
		- Guard tower in the center of a circular prison
		- Windows in the guard tower are blacked out, meaning the prisoners are always worried a guard might be watching them

## Lecture 13
**Space from above**
- **Remote sensing:** process of gathering information about an area from a distance
	- **Examples:** aerial imagery, LIDAR, satellite imagery
	- **History**
		- Remote sensing emerged as part of military surveillance during WWII
		- **CORONA Program:** first large-scale orbital reconnaissance program, collecting information on China and the Soviet Union
		- **LANDSAT 1:** first civilian-led resource management mission
		- **Land Remote Sensing Policy Act:** law allowing private companies to sell remote data

## Lecture 14
**Digital mapping**
- **Era 1: pre-print cartography (antiquity - 1450)**
	- **Production:** artists; scholars; court cartographers; navigators
	- **Reproduction:** fully manual and hand-drawn
	- **Spatial data:** surveys; personal reports; astronomical observations
	- **Authority:** patrons
- **Era 2: print cartography (1450 - 1990)**
	- **Production:** trained surveyors; field crews; cartographers 
	- **Reproduction:** mechanized
	- **Spatial data:** triangulation; cadastral, topographic, and hydrographic surveys; aerial photography; administrative datasets
	- **Authority:** bureaucracies; nation-states
- **Era 3: digital cartography (1990 - present)**
	- **Production:** engineers; data scientists
	- **Reproduction:** completely digital; distribution via web or mobile
	- **Spatial data:** satellites; GPS; imagery; machine learning
	- **Authority:** corporations; algorithms

## Lecture 15
**Projection**
- **Latitude & longitude** 
	- Provides a mathematical system to identify locations on Earth’s surface
	- Works because we assume the Earth is smooth and spherical
		- But the Earth is NOT smooth or spherical 
- **Geographic coordinate system**
	- Combines the actual shape of the Earth (geoid) with an ellipse to produce a datum
	- **Geodetic datum:** datum that aligns a specific area of the geoid with the ellipse
	- **Geocentric datum:** datum that aligns the center of the geoid with the center of the ellipse
- **Projection distortion**
	- Shape 
	- Area 
	- Distance
	- Direction

## Lecture 17
**Types of map designs**
- **Reference** 
	- Designed to show the overall location of geographic features
	- Broad and detailed view 
	- Meant for navigation and orientation
- **Thematic**
	- Designed to focus on a specific theme or pattern
	- Less detailed, but more specific
	- Not meant for navigation

**Design elements**
- Selection
- Symbology
- Context
- Visual hierarchy
- Design principles

**Visual variables**
- **Categorical:** hue (color) & shape
- **Numeric:** value & size
