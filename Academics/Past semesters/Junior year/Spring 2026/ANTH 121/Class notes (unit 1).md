## The Americas before 1492
**Anthropological struggles**
- Difficult to know anything about extinct cultures without writing systems, artifacts, etc
- Excavation is often not possible 
- Plus, history is written by the victors, and we may not have unbiased writings about ancient cultures
	- In this case, Spanish accounts are studied, but they are not objective

**Naming conventions**
- Place names convey ownership and power
	- Naming a new area implies conquest or discovery
	- Think of Columbus “discovering” America 
	- It also allows ownership to be publicly recorded and commemorated
- “Many historical controversies boil down to who has the power to name what” (Michel Rolph-Trouillot)
- **The new world**
	- Newness implies that the place is uninhabited 
	- This of course was incorrect
	- The name dismisses those native to the Americas
- **Columbus’ discovery**
	- His discovery was closer to an invasion or raid
	- By naming this event as a discovery, we cement it as a discovery instead of an invasion 
		- Positive reframing of a violent event
- **Why America?**
	- Waldseemüller map (1507) was the first map to show America as a land mass
	- He suggested the name **Amerigo**, for explorer Amerigo Vespucci 
	- **Amerigo Vespucci** was born in Italy and later joined a merchant port in Spain
		- His port was operating when Columbus first returned from America
	- Vespucci was a good friend of Columbus
		- First to point out that America may not be China
	- Vespucci took four (or maybe two) voyages to the Americas 
		- There’s evidence for at least one of his voyages, but the first and last seem not to have happened
	- His accounts were widely published and well known
		- They were much more popular than Columbus’ accounts
		- They were also grossly exaggerated, sensationalistic, and very negative towards natives
	- Essentially, America got its name because of Vespucci’s popularity, and by extension because of his lies

## What is a city?
**Definitions**
- For the first time, there are more people living in cities than living rurally
- How do we define a city, and what is the purpose of the definition?
	- **Louis Wirth’s qualifications for cities**
		- Permanence 
		- Large population
		- Dense population
		- Heterogeneous population
	- **Functional qualifications for cities**
		- Central places (markets, pilgrimage, power)
- **Space:** geographic entities that are produced by and influence human action
	- **Example:** Franklin Street (as a location)
- **Place:** spaces that have been given meaning by humans
	- **Example:** Franklin (as a destination/experience)
- **Cities are both spaces and places**
	- Identity (belonging, language, culture)
	- Community (social connections to others)
	- Security (????)

**The Urban Revolution**
- In the 1000s, humans began to develop cities
	- Development was centered in the Middle East
	- Humanity has existed for 200,000 years, but cities only appeared 9000 years ago
	- Most of our evolution occurred rurally
- **Earliest known city**
	- Çatalhöyük
	- Located in Southern Turkey near a water source
	- Existed 9000 years ago
	- Simple buildings & structures
		- No streets
		- People entered their houses via the roof

**Childe’s Urban Revolution (1950)**
- One of the most citied archaeological articles of all time 
- However, it is outdated and often inaccurate 
- Debate if crops or cities came first
- **Vere Gordon Childe**
	- Australian academic
	- Known as the Great Synthesizer (me too)
	- Work focused on Europe & the Middle East
	- Involved in Marxism and socialism
	- Ideas rooted in his time (often racist)
- **Childe’s assumptions**
	- Urbanism required climbing the social ladder
	- Lack of diversity is normal, since cities could only develop in a few places
	- Civilization is synonymous with urbanism
	- Urbanism represents beneficial progress

**Were cities so good for people?**
- People had to work harder to earn a living
- People had less freedom
- People had poorer health and often died younger
- People experienced more violence and chaos

## Archaeologies of Space and Place
**Theories of space**
- Spatial relationships are inherently intertwined with social relationships
	- “If it is social, it is spatial”
- Remember the earlier definitions of space (physical area) and place (conceptual area)
	- Dialectical relationship between spaces, places, and humans
	- So humans build cities, but cities build humans too
- **Study of space and place**
	- Overlap between archaeology and geography
		- Archaeology provides narratives of places through time
		- Geography defines spatial form and social processes

**Techniques for spatial data sourcing**
- **Remote sensing**
	- Satellites, LIDAR
- **Surveys**
	- Pre-defined documentation of historical materials
	- Mapping, photography, artifact patterns (how and where artifacts are found)
- **Artifact analysis**
	- Production-distribution exchange
- **Oral histories and archives**
	- Narratives created from space
- **GIS (Geographic Information Systems)**
	- Software for analyzing geographic data
	- **Site location**
		- Creates statistical models of sites, including environmental data
		- Saves money and makes collaboration easier
	- **Movement**
		- Modeling movement patterns based on data and probability
		- Can confirm or disprove hypotheses about geographical data
	- **Viewshed**
		- Modeling sight lines and visual perspectives 
		- Can provide archaeologists with a historical lay of the land

**Malata**
- Small town once located in south central Peruvian islands
- Colonized twice, once by the Incas (1400s) and again by the Spaniards (1540s)
- Archaeological site
	- No evidence of reconstruction of residential buildings
	- However, there was evidence of reconstruction of patio walls, mostly by colonizers

**Andean Plazas**
- **Moore 1996**
	- Investigation of three plazas within Andean settlements
		- Inka (1500s)
			- Clear central plaza (large open space)
			- Theater of power (raised structure in plaza)
			- Empire with one leader
		- Chimu (1200s)
			- No formal plaza
			- Restricted access and interactions
		- Tiwanaku (500s)
			- Clear central plaza 
			- Restricted access
			- Empire with several leaders
	- **Plaza:** arena for encounters
		- Proxemics are important for understanding interactive spaces

**Proxemics**
- Language of personal space developed by Edward Hall
- Reshaped our understanding of space

## Clovis points
**Artifact**
- Large spear point created to hunt large herbivores (mammoths, etc)
- Typically attached to a stick to form a javelin
	- Distinct flute at attachment point
- **First identified at Blackwater Draw, New Mexico** 
	- Identified in the 1900s
	- Produced for about 300 years 
	- Provided evidence that humans were in America 10,500 years ago

**Beringia model**
- Theory that Clovis points represent the arrival of humanity in America
- Native Americans traveled to America via Beringia
	- Beringia was a land bridge that connected Asia and America roughly 30,000-11,000 years ago
	- Largely covered by ice 
	- When the ice melted (roughly 13,000 years ago), Clovis points appeared and megafauna began going extinct
- Evidence fit archaeologists’ expectations of masculine, hunting based societies
- Model was widely dominant
	- Pre-Clovis sites (predating 13,000 years ago) were largely ignored
	- Prominent archaeologists had the power to slander those who ? pre-Clovis sites

**Monte Verde**
- Site in Southern Chile
- Discovered in the 1970s
- Excavated by Vanderbilt archaeologists in the 1990s
- Evidence of settlements 18,500 years ago
	- Ten different types of seaweed found
	- Clear evidence of advanced development 
- Inspired the Two-phase model

**Two-phase model**
- **Phase 1:** early migrations along the pacific coast (Kelp Highway)
- **Phase 2:** Clovis migration after the ice-age corridor over Beringia melts 

**Two lessons from American archaeology**
- There are always more people
	- More pre-Clovis sites are continually being discovered
- They’ve been here for longer than we thought
	- Likely around 20,000 years ago

## Overview of early American cultures
**Reasons for development**
- **Politics** (militaristic states)
	- **Key traits:** barracks, administrative centers, fortresses, etc
- **Demographics** (growing populations)
	- **Key traits:** crowded spaces, quickly developing architecture
- **Ideological** (shared religious beliefs)
	- **Key traits:** religion-based architecture/art

**Mesoamerica**
- **Mesoamerican culture**
	- Similar cosmology/mythology 
	- Similar calendars
	- Similar ecology/crops
		- Three sisters: corn, squash, beans
	- Linguistically and ethnically diverse
- **Olmec**
	- Located in the Gulf of Mexico
	- Aztec word for “rubber people”
	- Anthropomorphic art, intense trade, cities
	- Mother of Mesoamerica
		- Foundation for much of Mesoamerican culture
		- Created the ball game, calendar, writing, etc
		- This theory has been challenged
	- **Layout of Olmec cities**
		- Cities based on tiers of labor
			- Large (monumental buildings, carvings)
			- Medium (???????)
		- Cities were placed to control trade networks
		- Cities may have been workshops
			- Import of raw materials → export of finished goods

**South America**
- **Andean culture**
	- Likely developed for ideological (religious) reasons
	- Monumental, non-utilitarian architecture 
- **Chavin de Huantar**
	- Early farming city in Peru
		- Intensive maize agriculture
	- Inhabited by the Chavin people 
		- Known as a pilgrimage destination 
	- Strong presence of San Pedro cactus among elites
		- Hallucinogen producing visual and auditory illusions
		- Elites would travel through an underground complex where priest would whisper voices

**North America**
- **Moundbuilders**
	- Mounds built at Native American sites across the midwest and southeast
		- Functioned as political and social centers
	- **Cahokia**
		- Settlement on the Mississippi River
		- Large and influential 
	- **Watson Brake**
		- Over 5500 years old
			- Older than Stonehenge and the Pyramids
		- **Mound A**
			- 25 feet tall, 230 feet in diameter
			- Concentration of fire cracked rock
			- Wild animals, fish, shells
			- No agriculture (noteworthy)
	- **Poverty Point Culture**
		- 3600-3100 years ago
		- Lower Mississippi
		- Shared traits: artifacts, trade, jewelry, hunter-gatherer culture
		- **Poverty Point Site**
			- Stone age site located far from any stone deposits
			- Second largest mound (72 feet x 700 feet) after Cahokia
			- Possibly a ritual or ceremonial site

## Planning
**Why plan?**
- Standardization, power, money, safety, ideology
- **L’Eixample**
	- Barcelona faced excess expansion
	- New developments divided buildings into blocks
		- High modernist planners took inspiration from French city planners
	- Planning allowed for modernization 
- Planned cities are not always organized on grids
- Studying ancient city planning is difficult since there are often no records

**Three definitions of planning**
1. **The deliberate actions of builders**
	- More traditional definition
	- Issues
		- We don’t know the builders’ intentions
		- We can only see the end result
2. **The ways a city follows an urban design**
	- Emphasis on standardization 
	- Reflected in patterns, inventories, and orientation
3. **The ways buildings are coordinated**
	- Emphasis on coordination (obviously)
	- Reflected in accessibility, monumentality, and formality

**Planning & power**
- What does city planning say about the city?
- A well-planned city projects power and confidence 

**Inka Empire (Tawantinsuyu)**
- **Timeline**
	- Formed in Cuzco between 1200-1400 AD
	- Rapid expansion across most of South America from 1400-1572 AD
- Renowned for their **planning** and **organization** 
	- **Political Economy:** distribution of resources within a society
		- **Wealth finance:** controlling the flow of luxury goods
		- **Staple finance:** controlling the flow of agricultural goods
	- To achieve control, the Inkas constructed an elaborate road system
	- Use of labor as currency

## Water
**Water as power**
- Controlling water supply is key for leaders retaining power over their citizens
- Revolutions often occur more during droughts
- **Hydraulic societies**
	- Emergence of absolute rule in societies where political power is closely connected to water supply
- **Evolutionary questions**
	- Originally believed that abundance of water led to faster and more advanced development
	- However, advanced cities existed without abundance of water
		- **Cahokia** is a notable example 
			- Built on the Mississippi, but did not employ irrigation techniques
			- Without irrigation, there is no water-based labor, so there is no water-based power
			- There were flood mitigation measures, however
			- Manmade lake behind the most important structures
- **Political economy of water**
	- **Hierarchy:** vertical links between levels of power
	- **Heterarchy:** horizontal and vertical links between levels of power
- **Water in Maya**
	- The Maya understood that water had its own agency
		- Creation myths describe the world as a turtle on a sea
	- They saw water as a resource of control and performance
	- **Mayan hydraulic technologies**
		- **Aguadas**
			- Limestone pits periodically lined with clay
			- Found in **Nakun**
				- Small Mayan city on the Guatemala-Belize border
				- Intense fluctuation between wet and dry seasons
		- **Chultunob**
			- Smaller domestic storage units for water, food, and household goods
	- **Case study: Aventura**
		- Small Mayan city developed between 750-1150 CE
		- Flourished during a time of chaos and drought (Maya “collapsed” in 900 CE)
		- Site contained 5 temples and 63 residential compounds
		- Evidence of inequality between housing size and masonry
			- **However, water was accessible to all regardless of status**

**Studying water**
- **Infrastructure**
	- Canals, dams, aqueducts, reservoirs
- **Remote sensing**
	- Satellites, LIDAR, drones
- **Soil/botanical analysis**
	- Wetlands, season-based changes 

**Landscape archaeology**
- Study of how cultural and environmental variables influence human interactions with landscape/surroundings

**Cenote**
- Naturally occurring limestone depressions common in the Yucatán peninsula 
- Formed in the craters of the asteroid that killed the dinosaurs
- **Chichen Itza** 
	- Mayan city built atop a cenote

## Domestication 
**Eras**
- **Paleolithic:** era characterized by hunting and gathering with stone tools
- **Neolithic:** era characterized by farming with stone tools
	- Occurred 10,000 - 12,000 years ago, all over the world
	- Development of farming corn and wheat

**What is domestication?**
- Intentional reproduction of animals or plants to benefit humanity
- Domestication is usually not intentional or forward-looking
	- Often, there is more animal agency than we think 

**Why does domestication happen?**
- **Behavioral reasons**
	- Reproduction frequency
	- Sociality
	- Tameness
	- **Neotony**
		- Transfer of juvenile traits to adulthood
		- i.e., an adult dog acts like a wolf puppy
		- Cuteness encourages protection and care 

**Dogs**
- First domesticated species
- Helped humans domesticate other species
- Beginnings are unclear
	- Reciprocity and companionship
	- For cats, domestication began when grain storage emerged

## Study of food
**Foodway**
- Chain of acquiring, preparing, and consuming food
- Culture-specific and meaningful
- Significant role of power
	- Push to make food producers invisible and consumers visible
	- Essentially erasing the labor that creates food

**Food remains at archaeological sites**
- Botanical remains
- Faunal remains
- Dietary stable isotopes
- Paleofeces
- Tools
- Texts/iconography

**Foodway evolution in the Eastern Woodlands**
- **Consistent practices:** mast-based foods, wetland-based foods, hunting, foraging
- **Eastern Agricultural Complex**
	- Organized agriculture that appeared over thousands of years
	- No longer exists
- **Mesoamerican imports**
	- **Maize:** gradual introduction
	- **Beans:** late introduction 
	- **Pumpkins:** who fucking knows
- Introduction of corn led to more authoritarian societies 
- **Gender roles**
	- Women often did more foraging
	- Men often did more hunting, especially of large animals

## Religion
**What is religion?**
- **Edward Tylor (1871):** religion is “an attempt to understand the world”
- Religion is connected to power within society
- It also serves as a bridge for social connection
- Who fucking knows though

**How do archaeologists study religion?**
- Archaeologists often study rituals instead of actual religions
- **Religion:** Belief → Behavior → Ritual

**Andean religions**
- **Folk religions**
	- Beliefs of everyday people
	- Durable, adaptable, difficult to change
- **State religions**
	- The ways states and empires draw upon folk religions to maximize power
- **Hybrid religions**
	- **Inka religions**
		- Pantheon of gods and goddesses
		- Emperor was seen as divine figure responsible for maintaining order
		- **Wak’a:** sacred place imbued with spiritual powers
		- **Ceque:** walking pilgrimage to the Inka capital

**Mesoamerican cosmology**
- **Axis Mundi**
	- Three-tiered cosmos (sky-earth-marine underworld)
	- Sacred places or points on the landscape
	- Cities planned around these beliefs
- **Mayan religions**
	- Mayan customs saw rulers as being god-like
	- But rulers were required to make gruesome offerings (penis blood-letting??)
	- Elites had to perform ritual blood-lettings 
	- SOUNDS FAMILIAR
- **Copan**
	- Southeast periphery of Mayan territory
	- Relatively small 
	- One of Maya’s “flower worlds” 
		- Spiritually blessed places were associated with flowers
		- Temples at Copan were analyzed and found to contain traces of flowers

**Shamanism**
- Original Siberian indigenous ritual specialists
- No connection to the indigenous Americas
	- Peruvian leaders have more in common with priests than with shamans
