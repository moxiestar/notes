**History of Cahokia**
- Settlement on the Mississippi River
- Moundbuilding society
- Large and influential 
- **Watson Brake**
	- Over 5500 years old
		- Older than Stonehenge and the Pyramids
	- **Mound A**
		- 25 feet tall, 230 feet in diameter
		- Concentration of fire cracked rock
		- Wild animals, fish, shells
		- No agriculture (noteworthy)
- **Poverty Point Culture**
	- 3600-3100 years ago
	- Lower Mississippi
	- Shared traits: artifacts, trade, jewelry, hunter-gatherer culture
	- **Poverty Point Site**
		- Stone age site located far from any stone deposits
		- Second largest mound (72 feet x 700 feet) after Cahokia
		- Possibly a ritual or ceremonial site

**Water as power**
- Controlling water supply is key for leaders retaining power over their citizens
- Revolutions often occur more during droughts
- **Hydraulic societies**
	- Emergence of absolute rule in societies where political power is closely connected to water supply
- **Evolutionary questions**
	- Originally believed that abundance of water led to faster and more advanced development
	- However, advanced cities existed without abundance of water
		- **Cahokia** is a notable example 
			- Built on the Mississippi, but did not employ irrigation techniques
			- Without irrigation, there is no water-based labor, so there is no water-based power
			- There were flood mitigation measures, however
			- Manmade lake behind the most important structures

**Collapse in Cahokia**
- Built in the floodplains of the Mississippi River
- Deforested by 450
- Urbanized by 900
- **Reaching its apex by 1100**
	- Homogenous material structure
	- Population of roughly 30,000
	- Pilgrimage center
	- Network of earthen mounds
- **Collapse by 1275**
	- Significant depopulation of the American bottom
	- Abrupt shift away from centralized power 
	- Falloff of building mounds
	- Less diversification of food
	- Increased wall construction 
	- Little evidence of actual violent conflict
- **Collapse theories**
	- Ecological erosion led to collapse
		- Deforestation had been going on for a long time
	- Floods wiped out the power structures
		- Not probable
