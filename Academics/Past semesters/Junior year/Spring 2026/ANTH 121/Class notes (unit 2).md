## Ancient economies
**New York City**
- Linape natives sold Manhattan Island to Dutch explorers for beads/trinkets
	- Beads were a medium of exchange between natives and explorers
	- Their value was 60 guilders or roughly 24 dollars (now worth billions of dollars)
	- They also received other goods, clothes, etc
- In Linape culture, land was not something to be owned or sold
	- Essentially, they were selling use rights

**Social life of things**
- Way of understanding early economy/trade
- Archaeologists look for objects that are out of place at a site
- **Objects out of place indicate…** 
	- Trade of things
	- Diffusion of ideas
	- Migration of people
- **Importance of trade**
	- Trade is correlated with the advancement and success of a society
	- Trade is power 

**Classic economic forms**
- **Redistribution:** redivision of central collection of goods  
- **Reciprocity:** exchange of goods between individuals for social purposes
- **Market exchange:** trade of goods based on supply and demand 

**Hau**
- Maori philosophy of gift giving
- Duty to impress upon the gift-giver the effect of the gift on others
- Basically a debt of gratitude

## Sports
**What makes a sport?**
- Athleticism 
- Rules
- Competition 
- Entertainment (maybe)

**What is the significance of sport?**
- Reveals what cultures value
- Reveals how cultures play

**Perspectives on sport**
- **Sport as play**
	- Emphasizes the spontaneous creativity of sport
	- Sees sport as a positive outlet
- **Sport as ritual**
	- Highlights the symbolic and cultural significance of sport 
	- Sees sport as an identity or a shared experience
- **Sport as game**
- **Sport as society**
	- Emphasis on the economic aspect of sport

**Issues with studying ancient sports**
- **Ivory tower mindset:** tendency for academics to think sports are frivolous
- Vast difference between modern capitalistic sports and ancient sports

**Mesoamerican ball game (ulama)**
- **Rules of the game**
	- Players passed a rubber ball back and forth with their hips
	- Players would aim for hoops, rings, or lines
	- Played in a large stone building
	- Spectator sport
		- Large audience at games
	- Scores were kept 
		- 88-pound stone scoreboard was found in Teotihuacan 
- Contrary to popular belief, human sacrifice was not involved
	- **Popul Vuh**
		- Mayan origin myth
		- Two brothers traveled to hell to play ball with gods
		- One brother was decapitated, and the other continued playing, with his head
- Evidence of trading occurring during ball games

**Sites of ulama**
- **Etlatongo**
	- Allegedly the earliest known ball court in the highlands
	- Associated artifacts are coastal in origin
	- So where does the sport come from? Who knows
- **Pasa la Amada**
	- Large village that became increasingly centralized over time
	- Development of hierarchy?
- **Mound 6**
	- Ball court built over 1375 days

**Chunkey**
- Two-person game involving sticks and discs
- Played at Cahokia
- Early form of lacrosse
- At least 1500 years old
- **Gameplay**
	- Disc is rolled across the court
	- Players throw their sticks and try to hit the disc 
- **Social aspects**
	- Trading
	- Gambling

**Stickball**
- Two-person game involving sticks and balls (no way)
- Played in the Southeast US
- **Early form of lacrosse**
	- Lacrosse developed from Iroquois stickball
	- Observed by French-Canadian Jesuits in the 1600s
	- Stickball lost its spiritual aspects 
	- William George Beers made the first rulebook for lacrosse
		- Believed that lacrosse would teach masculinity and Christianity to young men
- **Social aspects**
	- Often played to build morale during times of war
	- Community gathering

## Collapse
**What is collapse?**
- **Textbook definition:** rapid loss of an established level of sociopolitical or economic complexity
- **Demographic collapse:** loss of population
	- Popular notion of collapse
		- Fundamental part of every society’s journey
	- Sometimes, it overlaps with true societal collapse
- **Political collapse:** loss of political structure
- **Sociocultural collapse:** loss of sociocultural coherence 

**Edward Gibbon**
- First study of societal collapse
- “History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire”
- Early focus on moral decay and external pressures
- Relevant because of unrest in the British Empire around time of publication 

**Joseph Tainter**
- Theory that societies collapse when they get too complex

**Study of collapse**
- Settlement patterns
- Stratigraphy
- Paleodemography
- Resource management analysis
- Warfare and conflict evidence
- Architectural decline

**Collapse in Rapa Nui (Easter Island)**
- Remote volcanic island in the southeastern Pacific Ocean known for moai
	- Big ass mogging statues
	- Over 900 present on the island
	- Colonizers could not understand how the people could have built the statues
- Apex between 1200-1500
- **Evidence of collapse**
	- Dramatic decline in population and societal complexity
	- Internal conflict and possible warfare
	- Deforestation and ecological decay
	- Disappearance of the moai tradition
- **Old theories**
	- Destruction of ecosystem led to societal decline
	- Clan warfare broke out over limited resources
- **Current theories**
	- Emphasis on the resilience of the Rapa Nui people
	- Resampling of the environmental data
		- Paleobotanical studies
		- Stable isotope analysis
		- Analysis of obsidian tools
		- Radiocarbon dating
		- Evaluation of oral history
- **Current chronology**
	- **1200:** small group first arrives and begins deforesting and building moai
	- **1250:** population continues to grow and rat population prevents reforesting
	- **1350:** maximum population reached and deforestation continues
	- **1680:** population remains static with low environmental resources
	- **1720:** first Europeans arrive and find that current population 

**Collapse in Cahokia**
- Built in the floodplains of the Mississippi River
- Deforested by 450
- Urbanized by 900
- **Reaching its apex by 1100**
	- Homogenous material structure
	- Population of roughly 30,000
	- Pilgrimage center
	- Network of earthen mounds
- **Collapse by 1275**
	- Significant depopulation of the American bottom
	- Abrupt shift away from centralized power 
	- Falloff of building mounds
	- Less diversification of food
	- Increased wall construction 
	- Little evidence of actual violent conflict
- **Collapse theories**
	- Ecological erosion led to collapse
		- Deforestation had been going on for a long time
	- Floods wiped out the power structures
		- Not probable

**Collapse in Mayan Empire**
- **Original timeline of the Empire**
	- **Preclassic period:** early development and agriculture
	- **Classic period:** major cities, art, and writing
	- **Postclassic period:** decline and collapse
		- Decline of major cities (Tikal, Palenque, Calakmul)
		- Decentralization of power (emergence of smaller cities)
		- Less emphasis on royalty in art
		- Increased conflict
- **Original theories**
	- **Warfare**
		- Increased warfare led to central urban decline
		- **Evidence:** fortifications, war-related imagery, forensic evidence of violent deaths
		- **Critique:** warfare not unique to the late Classic period
	- **Environmental erosion** 
		- Drought created a sustainability issue
		- **Evidence:** lower rainfall, reduced agriculture production
		- **Critique:** drought occurred several times across Mayan history
	- **Political instability** 
	- **Cultural exchange**
	- **Migration**
- **Current understanding**
	- No one theory explains the decentralization that occurred
	- Evidence of unrest, but also evidence of development

**American Apocalypse**
- **Collapse in the Inka Empire**
	- Spanish conquerers invaded the Inka Empire in the early 1500s
		- 7000 Inka warriors died
		- Inka Emperor was executed
	- **Seven myths of Spanish conquest**
		1. The myth of exceptional men
		2. The myth of the king’s army
		3. The myth of the white conquistador
		4. The myth of completion
		5. The myth of miscommunication 
		6. The myth of native desolation
		7. The myth of superiority 
	- **Cusi Huarcay**
		- Queen of the Inka Empire
		- Niece of the Emperor (Atahualpa)

**Archaeologies of rebellion**
- **Puruchuco-Huaquerones** 
	- Inka administrative center and cemetery
	- Human burials with evidence of gunshot wounds and rapid burials
- **Ollantaytambo**
	- Manco Capac’s last stand after the Spanish siege on Cusco
	- Cusi Huarcay spent her time here and in Vilcabamba 
	- Reservoir core on the land

**Three important takeaways for collapse**
- The term collapse has inherently biased connotations about civilization/culture
- There are always huge chronological issues with collapse theories
- Understanding the available data is difficult but critical

## Legacy
**Archaeological past vs indigenous present**
- **Heritage:** what is the role of the past in the present?
- **Tourism:** how is the past communicated?
- **Repatriation:** who owns the past? 

**World Heritage System**
- Created by UNESCO in 1972
- Effort to protect and preserve natural areas and historic sites
- **World Heritage Sites**
	- Historic locations with protected status
	- Locations are provided with state support, status, and branding
- Members of UNESCO (“States Parties”) are required to systematically review and nominate locations 
	- Once locations are chosen, members are required to protect the “Outstanding Universal Value” of sites
- Criteria can be biased and overly specific 

**Bramley-Moore Dock**
- Built in 1846 in Liverpool
- Revolutionized global trade
	- Fireproof warehouses 
	- Cranes
- UNESCO status
	- World heritage center status granted in 2004
	- Status revoked in 2024

**Benin Bronzes**
- Example repatriation of African artifacts to British Museum
- **Benin**
	- Kingdom in West Africa
	- Colonized by British Empire
	- Known for using bronze plates to depict their history 
		- Each plate was a record of major events
- **Punitive Expedition of 1897**
	- Scramble for Africa between colonizers
	- Search for palm oil and ivory
	- Brits plundered Benin’s arts and treasures to cover their invasion costs
		- British Consul General tried to get permission to invade 
		- Total number casualties unknown
	- **1000 Benin Bronzes are taken**
		- 40% go to the British Museum 
		- Nigeria tried to take them back in 1940, but was unsuccessful 
		- Loan agreement was formed in 2018

**NAGPRA**
- **Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (1990)**
	- Protects Native American graves and remains 
	- Regulates control of archaeological excavations on reservations
	- Mandates the return of artifacts/remains to Native Americans
- Museums, federal agencies, and universities must comply
- **Objects covered**
	- Human remains
	- Funerary objects
	- Sacred objects
	- Objects of cultural patrimony 

**The Ancient One**
- Human remains discovered in Kennewick, WA
	- Historical area of the Umatilla tribe
	- Remains were so old (9000 years) that they were deemed not part of the tribe
	- Facial reconstruction had white features, promoting anti-Native American sentiments
- Many lawsuits emerged over the remains
	- Originally not linked to the Umatilla tribe
	- Genetic link was finally proven and remains were repatriated 

**Recent changes (2024)**
- Museums are now given five years to finish their inventories
- Museums must identify artifacts (making repatriation easier)
- More consultations are needed for research and display
- Range of protected objects widened
