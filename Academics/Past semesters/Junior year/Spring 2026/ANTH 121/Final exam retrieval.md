**Three classic economic forms** 
Redistribution: distribution-based economy
Reciprocity: gift-based economy

**Markets and Inka economy** 
Inka: expansive empire
Economy: capitalist bureaucratic system with no central marketplaces, labor controlled via state-organized feasts, maximizing power through control of ordinary goods and treasures

**Markets and Maya economy** 
Maya: city-state empire
Economy: gift-based economy controlled by elites, with markets integrated throughout the empire

**Markets and Aztec economy** 
Aztec: expansive empire
Economy: not capitalist, trade of commodities within central marketplaces

**Defining collapse** 
Collapse is traditionally defined via what’s interpretable in popular culture - a sudden demographic/cultural decline 
In popular culture, collapse = apocalypse (loss of population, structure, resources)
Collapse often takes many different forms, typically not in the ways that people expect
Defining and understanding collapse, particularly with ancient societies, can be extremely difficult 

**Traditional vs. Current views on collapse at Rapa Nui** 
Rapa Nui (Easter Island): island nation home to Moai (huge mogging statues)
When British settlers arrived to find a small population with evidence of deforestation, they assumed the natives had fought over limited resources, leading to depopulation
Now, the theory is that the deforestation was more gradual and the population was never that big

**Stickball and Mississippian society (in the past)** 
Stickball, precursor to lacrosse, was popularized in Mississippian society
Wide variety of rules but generally involved a large number of players using sticks with hoops to throw a rubber ball around a court 
Adapted by Canadian dentist to form the early basis of lacrosse (“just like we took their land, now we take their game” or whatever)

**Mesoamerican ballgame functions** 
Mesoamerican ballgame involved players hitting a rubber ball back and forth with their hips, with the goal of scoring in a hoop
Played in an ⌶ shaped court
Urban myth of human sacrifice or beheading - reflective of an origin myth where the hero plays ball with his brother’s severed head, not the actual game
Scoreboards kept on large stones 

**Spectators at the ballgame (_Totocapan_ example)** 
Mesoamerican ballgame was a huge spectator sport, with as many as 18,000 in the audience
GIS analysis of Totocapan shows a stadium with a capacity for tens of thousands of spectators

**Earliest known Mesoamerican ballgames** 
Etlatongo is the site of the earliest ballgame artifacts, but the artifacts are coastal and Etlatongo is in the highlands
So the origin of ballgame is unknown

**NAGPRA (origins, who and what is covered)** 
Native American Grave Protection and Repatriation Act
Law ordering the identification and return of all Native American gravesites/remains to their appropriate tribes 

**“Cultural Affiliation” in NAGPRA** 
Tribal identity of the remains

**Responsibilities of museums for NAGPRA** 
Identification of artifacts; if the artifacts are identified, return to the rightful parties

**Critiques of NAGPRA**

Politics can get in the way

**Cahokia collapse theories** 
Depopulation, deforestation, decentralization within Cahokia, which had previously been a huge cultural center
Political unrest, environmental resource draught - neither are particularly strong theories 

**Maya collapse theories** 
Old theories involved political unrest, war, or environmental drought after the classical period of the Mayan empire
None of these theories are fully supported or fully explain Mayan decentralization
