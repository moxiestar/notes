**Etymology of America**
America was named after Amerigo, an infamous Spanish explorer. A contemporary and friend of Columbus, he was known for fabricating or exaggerating accounts of his travels. 

**Clovis-First/Beringia Model**
The Clovis model theorizes that humans first appeared in America at the same time as Clovis points (stone arrowheads), roughly 12,000 years ago. The theory holds that early Americans travelled down from Asia via the Bering Strait during a time when the ice bridge over the strait had melted. The theory was so prevalent amongst anthropology academics that it took decades for archaeologists to accept evidence that humans had been in America for at least 18,000 years.

**Kelp highway**
Pathway for travel along the pacific coast for early humans migrating to America. Called the Kelp Highway because of the evidence of seaweed being moved down the pacific, consistent with its use in cuisine.

**Catalhoyuk**

**Definition of a city**
A city is typically a large, organized settlement, often one that travelers visit for religious or political reasons. Its population is large, dense, relatively permanent, and heterogenous. 

**Neolithic revolution**
Era characterized by the use of more advanced stone tools and the development of agriculture. Preceded by the Paleolithic Revolution, when basic stone tools were used in hunting and gathering. 

**Origins of agriculture**
Agriculture began in the Neolithic era, with an early farming culture appearing in southeastern North America.

**Dog domestication**
Early domestication of wolves into dogs was likely fueled by both wolves and humans, since each could provide for the other. Wolves likely saw an option to be protected and fed, while humans saw an option to have a warm, strong, totally badass companion.

**Physical/molecular/behavioral domestication**
Theory that domestication occurs on physical, molecular, and behavioral levels, with domesticated species changing in a variety of ways. Many species are domesticated based on behavior (sociability, trainability, frequency of reproduction, etc).

**Consequences of domestication**
Often, new species are created as the result of domestication. Dogs have genetic overlap with wolves, but behaviorally and physically, they are more similar to wolf puppies than to full grown wolves. 

**Critiques of the Shaman concept**
Shaman is now used as a catchall term for a spiritual, often indigenous, leader. However, the term originated in Siberia, used to describe a specific type of holy figure within a Siberian tribe. The religious leaders in ancient North and South American societies were likely more similar to priests than Siberian Shamans. 

**Flower worlds**
Sacred areas in Mayan civilization, where flowers were cultivated and spread as a sign of spiritual significance. 

**Difficulties of studying religion**
Religion means something different to everyone; some focus on power, some focus on spirituality, some focus on mythology, etc. On a more practical level, it is impossible to know the beliefs of ancient people, so we are always drawing conclusions based on incomplete evidence.

**Hierarchy vs. heterarchy**
A hierarchical society has a top-down structure, with interaction being almost exclusively vertical. A heterarchical society, on the other hand, has a more complex structure, with interaction occurring both horizontally and vertically. 

**Yucatan geology**
In the Yucatan peninsula, cenotes (limestone deposits) are concentrated around craters of the asteroid that wiped out the dinosaurs. Yes, actually.

**Cenotes**
Limestone deposits found in the Yucatan peninsula of South America. Filled in by Yucatan civilizations to create water stores, since the natural limestone was too porous to hold water. 

**Olmec**
Named “rubber people” by the Aztec, the Olmec have been regarded the forefathers of Mesoamerica, with a highly-structured, labor- and trade-based society, though this theory has been challenged. Olmec civilization was located in the Gulf of Mexico. They had a strong flow of imports and exports, and were known for a number of advancements, both political, artistic, and cultural. Their cities were structured around labor and trade. 

**Aventura**
Small Mayan city that was founded and that flourished during a time of chaos and collapse for the Mayan empire. Though inequality existed in the city, water was accessible to all. 

**Nakum**
Small Mayan city notable for the presence of aguadas (limestone pits lined with clay, used for water storage). On the border between modern-day Guatemala and Belize. Weather fluctuated intensely between wet and dry seasons.

**The Moundbuilders**
Early hunter-gatherer culture in eastern and southeastern North America, known for their distinctive mounds built at the centers of their settlements. Many Moundbuilder settlements were near the Mississippi River.

**Cahokia (and its watery landscape)**
Cahokia was the largest Moundbuilder civilization, characterized by its vast mounds and techniques for mitigating flooding from the nearby Mississippi River. A manmade lake in the settlement served as a symbol of the leaders’ power. 

**Poverty Point**
Poverty Point was the second largest Moundbuilder civilization, lying on the lower Mississippi. Poverty Point culture refers to the culture shared based the Moundbuilder settlements, including similar art, similar cuisine, and obviously similar architecture. 

**Watson Brake**
Watson Brake was another large Moundbuilder civilization, and one of the oldest, predating both Stonehenge and the Egyptian Pyramids. Its most famous mound is the vast Mound A. 

**Ceques**
Web of travel routes that branched out from Cuzco, connecting the Inka Empire. Many were used for pilgrimages to Qorikancha. 

**Qorikancha**
The Inka Empire’s central temple, once gilded and lavishly decorated. The temple was dedicated to the sun god Inti, and it was home to the Inti Raymi festival. As with most Inka architecture, the masonry is precise and vast. Stones are perfectly placed to form huge walls with almost no gaps or errors. Later, when Cuzco was conquered by Spain, they built over Qorikancha to create their own temple. 

**Sacsayhuaman**
Settlement near Cuzco. As with Qorikancha, the masonry in Sacsayhuaman is remarkable and far ahead of its time. It likely served as an astronomical observatory or ceremonial center.  

**Proxemics of ritual in plazas**
Proxemics, the study of personal space and physical interactions, can influence and be influenced by public spaces like plazas.

**Inka/Chimu/Tiwanaku plazas**
In Inka plazas, people were encouraged to gather and interact. A central raised platform served to elevate its singular leader.
Chimu had few central spaces, with its citizens being much more restricted. 
Tiwanaku was in between: it had some central spaces, but access was still restricted.
