## Module 7: Group Influence
**Influence of groups on behavior**
- **Social group:** set of individuals who share a purpose and identity 
- **Risky shift:** theory that people make riskier decisions in groups than they would alone
- **Group polarization:** theory that people have more extreme attitudes when in groups
	- In a cohesive group, attitudes get more and more extreme over time
- **Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979)**
	- Theory that two main factors influence our self-worth
		- **Personal identity:** influence of our personal achievements and status
		- **Social identity:** prestige of our in-groups
			- In-groups can be bolstered, thereby increasing our self-worth
	- When self-worth increases, we Bask In Reflected Glory (okay)
- Groups incite extreme behavior, since they both a) directly influence our self-worth and b) polarize our attitudes

**Influence of groups on performance**
- **Social facilitation effect (Markus, 1978)**
	- Participants performed either easy or difficult tasks
	- Participants were either alone or watched by a group
	- For the easy task, being watched made participants faster
	- For the difficult task, being watched made participants much slower
- **Task characteristics**
	- **Divisible task:** task that can be divided into smaller subtasks
	- **Unitary task:** task that must be done all at once
	- **Additive task:** task that combines individual efforts
	- **Averaging task:** task that averages individual efforts
- **Online social facilitation effect (Park & Catrambone, 2007)**
	- Participants performed either easy or difficult tasks
	- Participants were either alone, watched by another person, or watched by a virtual avatar
	- Same effect was seen regardless of whether the observer was virtual
		- For the easy task, being watched made participants faster
		- For the difficult task, being watched made participants much slower
- **Social loafing effect (Ringelmann, 1913)**
	- Ringelmann observed that oxen could perform more work on their own than in a group
	- He then measured the same effect in people
		- When alone, participants performed at 100% capacity
		- In a group of two, participants performed at 95% capacity
		- In a group of three, participants performed at 85% capacity
		- In a group of eight, participants performed at 37% capacity
	- **Factors that cause social loafing**
		- Coordination loss
		- Motivation loss
	- **Techniques to reduce social loafing**
		- Make tasks relevant and important
		- Make goals specific and difficult
		- Establish group identity
		- Diversify the group

**Group Think**
- Suboptimal decision making that occurs in a group setting
	- **Factors**
		- Strong group identity
		- Isolation from outsiders
		- Forceful leadership
		- **Mindguards:** members of the group who help increase conformity
	- **Prevention**
		- Playing devil’s advocate

**Bystander effect**
- Phenomenon of people ignoring others in need
	- People assume someone else will help
	- Result of Group Think?
- To avoid the bystander effect, the victim must NIRK:
	- **Notice:** get others’ attention
	- **Interpret:** break through pluralistic ignorance (tell others what’s going on)
	- **Responsibility:** avoid diffusion responsibility (pick out someone in particular)
	- **Knowledge:** tell others how to help

## Module 8: Stereotyping, Prejudice, & Discrimination
**Cognitive categorization**
- Our brains naturally group similar things in schemas
- **Advantages of schemas**
	- Helping us navigate the environment
	- Speeding up information processing through association 
	- Storing more information
	- Conserving cognitive resources
- **Disadvantages of schemas**
	- Lower accuracy in ambiguous situations
	- Biases can emerge based on what information we see
	- People are reluctant to update or change their schemas

**Social categorization**
- **Definition:** cognitive process of placing people into social groups 
- Each person is associated with multiple schemas
- Activation of those schemas depends on:
	- Salience of cues
	- Relatedness of schemas
	- Relevance of schemas

**Stereotypes**
- **Stereotype:** strong positive or negative beliefs about the characteristics of a social group
- **Dissociation model of stereotypes (Devine, 1989)**
	- **When understanding new people, we…**
		1. Automatically activate social schemas
		2. Perform controlled individuation of the person 
	- **Our understanding can be influenced by…**
		- Implicit attitudes based on social schemas
		- Implicit attitudes based on environments/exposures
		- Simplicity of social schemas
		- Bias of social schemas
		- Motivation to individuate a person
- **Hiring Managers Study (Pager, 2003)**
	- Researchers sent identical applications to hiring managers across the country
	- **Manipulations**
		- Applications either had white or black names
		- Applications either had a prison record or no prison record
	- **Results**
		- White applications, with or without prison record, were more successful than black applications
		- Racial bias was the same whether the hiring manager was white or black
			- Implicit bias likely played a larger role than explicit bias or environment

**Stereotype threat** 
- Situations can activate negative stereotypes about our in-group
- These activations worsen our performance and increase our anxiety
- **Case study (Steele & Aronson, 1995)**
	- Participants, either white or black, had to take a test, which was either described as diagnostic or not at all
	- Goal was to activate a negative stereotype about black participants’ academic performance
	- When test had been described as diagnostic, black students performed much worse than white students
- **Case study (Canning et al., 2019)**
	- Researchers surveyed faculty beliefs (either fixed or growth) about intelligence and performance
	- With fixed faculty, white/Asian students performed far better than under-represented minorities
	- With growth faculty, the overall performance was much better, and the difference between race groups was much smaller 

**Prejudice & discrimination**
- **Prejudice:** strong negative attitude towards an out group and its members
- **Discrimination:** strong negative behaviors towards an out group and its members
- **Realistic conflict theory (Sherif, 1966)**
	- When participants perceived that they had limited resources in the past, they believed that they still had limited resources, and were more inclined to fight for those resources
- **Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979)**
	- **In group:** favoritism and heterogeneity
	- **Out group:** derogation and homogeneity
- **Case study (Amira, Wright, & Goya-Tocchetto, 2019)**
	- We typically default to helping our in group
	- In times of threat or unrest, we tend to hurt the out group
- **Intergroup contact theory (Allport, 1954; Pettigrew, 1998)**
	- In situations where groups have…
		- Equal status
		- Common goals
		- Intergroup cooperation
		- Support from authorities
		- Mutual interdependence
		- Informal conditions
	- Conflict between clashing groups can be reduced

## Module 9: Interpersonal relationships
**Physical attractiveness**
- **Universal/genetic factors:** evolution has shaped physical attraction
	- Symmetry 
	- Youthfulness
	- Averageness
	- Physique
- **Cultural factors:** social learning also shapes attraction through classical & operant conditioning
	- Mass media
	- Social groups
- **Situational:** current goals/situations will influence who you find attractive
	- Perceptual contrast

**Psychological attractiveness**
- **Personal factors**
	- **Proximity**
		- Shared experiences
	- **Familiarity**
		- Exposure effect (Zajonc, 1968)
	- **Similarity**
	- **Positive affect**
- **Situation factors**
	- **Mortality salience**
	- **Social ostracism**

**Research deep dive**
- **The social functions of the emotion of gratitude via expression (Algoe, Fredrickson, & Gable, 2013)**
	- Participants engaged in two-way conversations, then filled out a relationship satisfaction scale
	- Then they returned six months later to fill it out again
	- Relationship satisfaction and gratitude responsiveness were both strong predictors of later relationship satisfaction

**Relationship theory**
- **Communal relationships (Clark & Mills, 1979)**
	- People provide services/benefits to fulfill partner’s needs
- **Reciprocal self-disclosure (Reis & Shaver, 1988)**
	- Relationship quality and closeness can be improved when both partners are disclosing their emotions
		- Ew
- **Relational mobility across 39 nations (Thomson et al., 2018)**
	- Researchers measured relationship intimacy across individualistic and collectivistic countries
	- Individualistic countries showed higher expression and intimacy 
- **Triarchic theory of love (Sternberg, 1986)**
	- Triangle of intimacy, commitment, and passion 
- **Moderators of marriage satisfaction**
	- Maturity
	- Homogamy
	- Equality
	- Commitment
- **Relationship dissolution**
	- **Four horsemen of the relationship apocalypse (Gottman)**
		- **Criticism:** verbally attacking partner’s traits
		- **Defensiveness:** always seeing yourself as the victim
		- **Stonewalling:** withdrawing from relationship (giving the cold shoulder)
		- **Contempt:** insulting and hating partner
