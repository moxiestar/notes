## Module 1: Introduction to social psychology
**Key terms**
- **Conceptual terms**
	- **Social psychology:** study of behaviors, thoughts, and feelings
	- **Social cognition:** how social knowledge develops and influences our cognition
	- **Social neuroscience:** how social behavior influences and is influenced by neural activity
	- **Social situation:** the groups and individuals we regularly interact with
	- **Social influence:** the processes by which we change our behaviors 
- **ABCs of social psychology**
	- **Affect:** moods and emotions
	- **Behavior:** actions and expressions
	- **Cognition:** intellectual thinking
- **Environmental factors**
	- **Situationism:** influence of a situation on behavior
	- **Subjectivism:** individual experience of the world
	- **Multidimensionality:** seemingly simple things affected by many different factors

**History of social psychology**
- Social psychology experiments began in the 1900s
- **Kurt Lewin** and **Leon Festinger** pioneered social psychology in the 1940s and 50s
	- Lewin is known as the father of social psychology
	- Lewin created the **person-situation interaction equation:** Behavior = f(person, social situation)
	- Festinger edited Research Methods in the Behavioral Sciences (1954)
- **Trends in social psychology research** 
	- Social psychology first focused on violence and power, particularly that seen in WWII
	- It then expanded to inter-group relations and advertisting
	- In the 1970s and 1980s **social cognition** became a focus
	- By the 21st century psychologists turned to **social neuroscience**

**ABCs of social psychology**
- **Affect:** moods and emotions
	- **Mood:** feelings that occur in the background of daily life
	- **Emotions:** brief mental and physiological states
- **Behavior:** actions, expressions, and interactions
	- **Social exchange:** the act of interaction
	- **Reciprocal altruism:** mutual exchange of benefits between friends
- **Cognition:** intellectual thinking
	- **Social cognition:** cognitive processes related to social interactions
	- **Schema:** expectations for a given person or scene
	- **Attitude:** feeling about a person, group, or thing

**Social evolution**
- **Evolutionary adaptation:** the idea that much of human nature is determined by evolution
- **Fitness:** influence of positive hereditary characteristics on survival and reproduction
- Traits that arose due to evolution include self-concern and other-concern
	- **Self-concern:** desire and ability to protect those close to us
		- **Kin selection:** favoring the reproductive success of our relatives above all else
		- **In group:** people who are similar and close to us, regardless of genetic relations
	- **Other-concern:** desire to connect with others (not a part of our groups)
	- Many interactions involve both self-concern and other-concern

**Social situations**
- Our social groups have a huge effect on our behavior
	- Close friends influence us, as do people we’ve never met
- Strong social networks greatly improve health and happiness, while ostracism is intensely harmful
- **Social norms:** behaviors or feelings that are shared and valued by a group
	- Social norms instruct members on the general rules of the group
	- Norms also serve to enforce socially acceptable behavior
	- Many social norms can be observed or acquired passively, through observation or influence
- **Cultures:** groups of people living in a specific area who share history, values, and norms
	- **Individualism:** cultural norms that value independence and self-betterment
		- Typically seen in Western cultures
		- High emphasis on self-concern
	- **Collectivism:** cultural norms that value interdependence and connection
		- Typically seen in Eastern Asian cultures
		- High emphasis on other-concern

## Module 2: Methods in social psychology
**Research in social psychology**
- Psychological research is typically empirical (based on observational data)
	- Research builds on itself, so variables < hypotheses < theories < laws
		- **Variables:** basic building blocks of science
		- **Hypothesis:** putting together variables to make a prediction
			- **Research hypothesis:** specific prediction about the relationship between the variables of interest
		- **Theories:** putting together hypotheses to make bigger, better-informed predictions
		- **Scientific laws:** putting together theories to define known rules

**Variables**
- **Conceptual variables:** abstract, underlying concepts
	- Conceptual variables are often internal and must be inferred
	- We use operationalization to understand and report these variables
- **Operational variables:** quantifications of conceptual variables
	- Operational variables are external and can be quantified 
	- Often physiological or self-reported
	- **Validity:** operationalization reflects only the target variable as much as possible
		- However, you cannot just measure one variable
		- When we collapse a conceptual variable into a numerical measure, we oversimplify physiological data
	- **Measure sensitivity:** operationalization should reflect the amount of the variable
	- **Measurement error:** ineffective or inaccurate operationalization of conceptual variables
- **Independent variable:** variable to be manipulated
	- **Condition:** different manipulations of the IV
		- Participants must have equivalence across all conditions
		- **Equivalence:** equality across as many demographics as possible
			- i.e., participants should have similar physiological features, backgrounds, cultures, etc
		- That way, the only variable influencing the DV is the IV
- **Dependent variable:** variable to be measured
- **Moderating/confounding variable:** extraneous variable that influences the DV
- **Random assignment:** randomization of participants so that each individual has an equal chance of being assigned to either condition 
- **Law of large numbers:** with big enough samples, the variance decreases

**Experimental designs**
- **Three major goals**
	- Describing phenomena as they are in the world
	- Predicting phenomena as they are in the world
	- Ascribing causality between phenomena
- **Three major designs**
	- **Descriptive studies**
		- Measures the current state of given variables in a defined population
		- Produces a time-locked snapshot 
		- Does not generalize over time
		- **Threats to validity:** measurement error
		- **Types**
			- **Observational:** variables operationalized before observation
			- **Archival:** variables taken from databases or historical records
			- **Survey:** variables are polled from participants
	- **Correlational studies**
		- Measures the relationship between two or more variables
		- Often more naturalistic than other studies
		- Good option when randomization is not available
		- **Results:** positive or negative correlation (or no correlation)
			- **Correlation coefficient:** provides the strength and direction of the relationship
		- **Threats to validity:** measurement error, spurious relationships (confounding)
	- **Experimental studies**
		- Measures how an independent variable effects a dependent variable
		- In experimental studies, participants are often randomly assigned to different conditions
		- **Results:** statistics showing the relationship (if one is present) between IV and DV
		- **Threats to validity:** spurious relationships, operationalization 
- **Factorial design:** experiments with two or more independent variables

**Sampling**
- Psychological samples are often inaccurate to the larger population
	- WEIRD: white, educated, industrial, rich, democratic
- **Example: Expectancy Violations Theory**
	- **Experiment setup**
		- Social expectation: people should stand roughly 3ft away from each other
		- If a positively perceived person stands closer, they will be more persuasive
		- If a negatively perceived person stands closer, they will be less persuasive
	- Because this experiment only measured a specific population, results were skewed
		- Results would vary across different cultures and situations 
- With every study, we should be thinking about the chosen sample and how the results might differ with another sample

## Module 3: Attitudes
**Social cognition**
- **Lewin’s Field Theory (1936):** Behavior = $f$(person, environment)
	- Also denoted as $B = f(P*E)$
	- Supports multidimensional explanations for social psychology

**Schemas & representations**
- **Schema:** unit of interconnected knowledge
	- **Changes to schematic structure**
		- Occurs when we encounter new information that does not match our schematic structure
		- **Assimilation:** changing stimulus to fit our schematic structure
		- **Accommodation:** changing schematic structure to fit the stimulus
		- Including exceptions into your schematic structures makes them more complex and realistic
			- Uniform schematic structures are not realistic
		- **Assimilation is easier and more common than accommodation**
- **Mental representation:** neural symbol for an abstract memory/concept
	- More accessible mental representations appear more frequently in your cognition
	- Higher accessibility leads to higher mental fluidity
		- Ease of activation
		- Speed of activation
		- Greater influence on decision-making
	- Less accessible mental representations have to be purposefully searched for
- **Mental accessibility:** availability of neural knowledge
- **Spreading activation:** when the activation of one mental representation leads to the activation of other connected mental representations

**Learning and conditioning**
- **Conditioning:** learning to associate stimuli with responsive behaviors
	- **Operant conditioning:** association based on specific reinforcement 
		- Pioneered by Skinner 
		- Experiences followed by positive emotions are more likely to be repeated, while experiences followed by negative emotions are less likely to be repeated
	- **Associational learning:** association of an event or object with a natural response (i.e., involuntary behaviors or emotions)
		- Pioneered by Pavlov
		- Also known as classical conditioning
	- **Observational learning:** association by observing the behavior of others
		- Pioneered by Bandura

**Information processing**
- Information is processed on a continuum from **automatic** to **controlled** 
	- **Automatic:** high efficiency; low intention, awareness, and control
		- Conserves cognitive resources
		- Often produces heuristics and biases
	- **Controlled:** low efficiency; high intention, awareness, and control
		- Drains cognitive resources
		- Can lead to learning and expansion
- **Cognitive bloat:** overload of cognitive resources
- **Four factors that influence processing**
	- Efficiency
	- Intention
	- Awareness
	- Control

**Attitude** 
- Mental representation with an evaluative component
- **Qualities of attitudes**
	- **Valence:** Positive to negative
		- **Magnitude:** Slight to extreme
		- **Explicitness**
			- **Implicit attitudes**
				- Automatic
				- Situationally activated
			- **Explicit attitudes**
				- Controlled
				- Consciously activated
			- Implicit and explicit attitudes are typically aligned, but not always
			- **Affect Misattribution Procedure:** method of measuring implicit attitudes
- **Attitude-behavior inconsistency**
	- Cognitive dissonance resulting from inconsistency between attitudes and behaviors
		- **Conflict:** performing a behavior that does not align with your attitudes
		- **Negative arousal:** cognitive dissonance state caused by conflict
			- Magnitude is modulated by how important the attitude is to you
		- **Reduction:** removal of the conflict
			- Typically done by the easiest means possible, often by justifying our behavior
	- **Insufficient Justification Study (Festinger & Carlsmith, 1959)**
		- Participants were engaged in incredibly boring tasks 
		- Participants were then told that an experimenter had called out sick, and they were asked to run the next participant, with compensation of either $0, $1, or $20
		- When running the next participant, the original participant lied and told them they were about to perform a series of very interesting tasks
		- The original participant then completed a survey about the experiment
			- The $1 group rated the experiment most positively, likely because they were compensated so little and had to justify their experiences and behaviors to themselves
	- **Effort Justification Study (Aronson & Mills, 1959)**
		- Participants were told they were joining a social group, but had to complete initiation tasks
			- In the mild initiation, participants read a boring book to themselves 
			- In the severe initiation, participants read erotica out loud
		- They were then played boring recordings from the social group
		- Participants were then asked how excited they were to join the group
			- The severe initiation group was by far the most excited, likely because they were convincing themselves that what they had done was worthwhile
		- This is why hazing works

**Persuasion**
- Unconscious change in attitude in response to rhetoric
- **Dual Process Model of Persuasion**
	- **Spontaneous/superficial message processing**
		- Shallow, sometimes subconscious processing of rhetoric
		- Occurs when motivation **or** cognitive resources are low
		- Easily influenced by source arguments, cues, and heuristics
		- Typically triggered by weak arguments, low personal relevance, or perceived lack of expertise 
	- **Thoughtful message processing**
		- Thorough, deliberate processing of rhetoric
		- Occurs when **both** motivation and cognitive resources are high
		- Best influenced by message arguments (information and data)
		- Typically triggered by strong arguments, personal relevance, or perceived expertise 
- **Source arguments**
	- Attractiveness
	- Relatability
	- Likability 
	- Perceived expertise
- **Persuasive tactics**
	- **Foot-in-the-door technique:** attempting a small persuasion first, then building to a larger persuasion
	- **Bait-and-switch technique:** persuading with false information, then revealing the truth once the audience is persuaded
	- **Door-in-the-face technique:** attempting an outrageously persuasion first, then presenting a smaller persuasion as a compromise
	- **Pre-giving technique:** persuading by giving the audience a gift and guilting them into reciprocating 
- **Preventing persuasion** 
	- **Forewarning:** priming audiences with information before they receive persuasive messages about that information
	- **Inoculation:** priming audiences by gradually criticizing the persuasive argument 

**Cognitive biases & heuristics**
- Once situationally activated, cognitive biases will automatically power our cognition 
- **Availability heuristic:** tendency to overestimate the likelihood of highly accessible mental representations
	- **Example:** we are more likely to die in a car crash, but we believe we are more likely to die in a plane crash
- **Representativeness heuristic:** tendency to make judgements based on our own expectations/schema (stereotypes)
- **Anchoring heuristic:** tendency to be influenced by initial/suggested information
	- Study by Jung et al. (2016)
		- Donuts for sale in a university quad
		- Different groups saw different suggested prices 
			- **Group 1:** pay what you want for the donuts
			- **Group 2:** pay what you want (suggested amount of $0.25)
			- **Group 3:** pay what you want (suggested amount of $1.75)
		- Groups always paid close to the suggested amount (anchor)
- **Confirmation bias:** tendency to gravitate towards information that reinforces our beliefs and schemas
- **False consensus bias:** tendency to overestimate the prevalence of our own beliefs
- **Sleeper effect:** tendency to forget the source, but remember the information
- **Self-reception:** influence of our beliefs on our understanding of ourselves
