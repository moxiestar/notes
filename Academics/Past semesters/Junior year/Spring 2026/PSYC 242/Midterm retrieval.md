### **CHAPTER 4 – Psychological Assessment: Science and Practice**
**Questions**
- What is assessment? (*General definition and broad purpose*)
	- Evaluation that measures an individual’s behaviors, abilities, or mental state. Aims to understand where the patient is psychologically, why they’re there, and how their problems can be fixed
- What is the sequence of the six steps of the assessment process? (*This would likely show up in a multiple choice question where I could provide information about a step in the assessment process and ask you to select which step would come next*)
	1. Determine the reason for referral and presenting problem
	2. Decide what issues to test for
	3. Decide what test(s) to use
	4. Interview the client
	5. Draw conclusions
	6. Report conclusions
- What are the differences between reason for referral, referral source, and presenting problem? (*Be able to identify them from a case vignette*)
	- Reason for referral: quantifiable problem that has brought the client to therapy
	- Presenting problem: client’s subjective description of their problem
	- Referral source: party who referred the client to therapy 
- On what considerations might a psychologist base their hypothesis generation about the referral question?
	- Client’s psychological history (medical, social, etc), client’s behavior, client’s circumstances
- What things might one consider when selecting the method of assessment?
	- Applicability/specificity of test, time/money considerations (can the client afford this test)
- What are the psychometric properties of an assessment?
	- Reliability, validity, accuracy, specificity 
- **What is reliability? Name and describe the 3 types of reliability**
	- Inter-rater reliability: consistency of scores with different diagnosticians
	- Re-test reliability: consistency of scores over time
	- Internal reliability: consistency of questions within test
- **What is validity? Name and describe the 5 types of validity**
	- Construct validity: ability of the test to accurately measure the target variable
	- Convergent validity: similarity between tests measuring overlapping variables 
	- Discriminant validity: dissimilarity between tests measuring different variables
	- Predictive validity: ability of the test to predict future scores/outcomes
	- **Incremental validity: ability of the test to provide unique results**
- What is standardization and why is it important for assessments?
	- Standardized tests use pre-determined sets of similar questions, reducing subjectivity of clinical assessments
- What is the ideal approach to choosing sources and format of collecting info (i.e., from whom one collects information and in which format they collect it)? (*Don’t need to know clinical judgement vs actuarial prediction*)
	- Information should, whenever possible, be gathered from relevant and reliable sources (medical professionals, scientists, etc)
- What does assessment research involve? (*This is a topic from textbook that you are responsible for knowing*)
	- Epidemiology (the study of the incidence and prevalence of medical/mental conditions)
	- Incidence: rate of instances of the condition
	- Prevalence: percentage of people affected by the condition
- What are correlations and experiments and how do they differ?
	- Correlations measure the non-causal relationship between two variables
		- Correlational studies often involve control groups
	- Experiments manipulative independent variables to measure the effects on dependent variables
		- Experimental studies often involve random assignment 
		- Experiments are usually conducted in lab environments
- What are the problems with the Serotonin Imbalance theory?
	- Some psychologists assume that the correlation between low serotonin and depression implies that there is a causative relationship between serotonin and depression
	- We can’t draw this conclusion without performing experimental research, which isn’t possible or ethical in this case
- **What are some of the criticisms/limitations of neuroimaging research?**
	- Oversimplification
	- Lack of accuracy about true cognitive processes
	- Lack of causative results
	- **Poor reliability**

### **CHAPTER 5 – The Clinical Interview**
**General notes**
- Don’t need to know types of clinical interviews (e.g., diagnostic vs psychosocial, etc.)

**Questions**
- What is a clinical interview and why do we do them?
	- A clinical interview assesses a client’s psychological history and current psychological state, and allows a psychologist to develop an accurate and effective treatment plan
- What is rapport and why is it important?
	- Rapport is the connection and understanding between a psychologist and their patient
	- A strong rapport can lead to more effective and informative therapy, with the client feeling more comfortable to share more about themselves
- What are the forms of non-verbal communication?
	- Body language (nodding, laughing, smiling), posture (relaxed, stiff), personal space (proximity to client), sounds with no linguistic meaning ()
- What is the difference between open-ended vs closed-ended questions? (*You don’t need to know other types of questions*)
	- Open-ended questions have no particular answer, so they allow the client to lead the conversation and give as much information as they want
	- Closed-ended questions have a particular answer (yes/no, a lot/not at all), so they keep the client’s answers short, specific, and relevant
- What is active listening and why is it important?
	- Active listening involves giving the client your full attention, repeating their statements for better understanding, and communicating via non-verbal means
- **What are content reflections & affect/meaning reflections? (*Be able to identify the difference between them*)**
	- Content reflections involve simply repeating the client’s statements
	- Affect/meaning reflections involve distilling the client’s meaning and repeating it in your own words
- What purpose does use of intentional silence have during an interview?
	- Silence makes us uncomfortable, so intentionally being silent can draw information out of a client
	- Silence can also give the client time/space to reflect
- Why is it important to ask clients about their strengths?
	- It can help the client feel more positive about themselves
	- It can also help the psychologist develop a treatment plan tailored to the client’s strengths
	- It can also help the psychologist understand how the client copes with their issues/conditions
- **What is the structure of a clinical interview? (*Know the three different structures and pros/cons of each*)**
	- Structured
		- Pre-determined list of questions
		- Great for standardization, poor for personalization
		- Easier to report and use for conclusions
	- Semi-structured
		- Some pre-determined questions, some open-ended questions
		- Poorer standardization, but better personalization
		- Harder to report
	- Unstructured
		- No pre-determined questions, all open-ended questions
		- Completely led by the client
		- Bad for standardization, but great for personalization
		- Very difficult to report or use for conclusions/treatment
- **What are the Key Questions about a presenting problem?**
	- How long has it been happening?
	- How often does it happen?
	- How long does it last when it does happen?
	- How bad is it?
	- What makes it better/worse?
	- How does it affect everything else?

- Do not need to know Cognitive-behavioral assessment

**Questions**
- What is the general principle of behavioral theory/assessment (i.e., black box mentality; emphasis on observable and quantifiable behaviors)?
	- Behavioral theory focuses on things that can be observed, and treats internal processes as unknowable and unquantifiable 
	- Basically, you can only understand what you can see
- **What is typically the key referral question for behavioral assessment?**
	- What behavior is occurring, and what is causing it?
- **What is interval coding? What is event recording? (*Be able to identify which one is being used in a case vignette*)**
	- Interval coding determines whether the behavior occurs within a certain time frame
		- Better when behaviors are continuous or unclear
	- Event recording observes how many distinct instances of the behavior occur overall
		- Better when behaviors are discrete and clear 
- What is naturalistic observation? What is controlled observation? (*Be able to identify which one is being used in a case vignette*)
	- Naturalistic observation involves viewing the patient in a natural/default/home setting and seeing whether the behavior occurs
	- Controlled observation involves viewing the patient in a laboratory setting specifically designed to elicit the behavior
- **What is a behavioral interview and when would you want to conduct one?**
	- A behavioral interview quantifies the target behavior and tries to identify its causes and consequences
	- However, unlike observation, the psychologist interviews the client instead of watching them 
	- Useful when the client’s behavior is disrupting their life
- What are the four functions of behavior? (*Be able to identify the function of a given behavior in a case example*)
	- Escape: behavior aims to end a situation
	- Sensory: behavior aims to elicit one of the five senses
	- Tangibility: behavior aims to get something 
	- Attention: behavior aims to attract attention
- What is functional analysis? (*Know the ABC components, and be able to identify and label them in vignettes*)
	- Antecedent: event that triggers behavior
	- Behavior: you know this one
	- Consequence: events caused by behavior
- Why is self-monitoring helpful? (*We touched on this briefly in class, but see textbook*)
	- Self-monitoring can be useful to provide a daily baseline of behavior, giving the psychologist access to more data
	- Ecological momentary assessment: client records a brief datapoint on their device, typically their phone

	- A trait is a building block of someone’s personality
	- Trait Theory believes that personality can be broken down into and explained by these blocks

	- Framework of understanding personality through five traits (OCEAN)
		- Openness
		- Conscientiousness
		- Extraversion
		- Agreeableness
		- Neuroticism
- What are projective tests? What are objective tests? (*Know the main idea, philosophy, and structure of each*)
	- Objective tests measure the client’s answers to direct and honest questions
		- Based on the theory that it is better to directly measure the client’s personality
		- Can sometimes be clinically useful/relevant
	- Projective tests measure the client’s answers to ambiguous or strange questions
		- Based on the theory that it is better to bring the client’s subconscious out and allow them to answer questions intuitively
		- Rarely clinically useful or relevant
- What are Rorschach tests? (*Know generally what it is and how it is used*)
	- Rorschach created a series of ten strange, ambiguous inkblots and recorded what participants saw in the inkblots
	- He recommended against its use in clinical settings, but it was adopted after his death

	- What is factor analysis?
		- Similarity between questions, and relevance of the questions to the target variables 
	- What is construct validity?
		- Performance of the test compared to other well-established tests that measure the same target variables

### **CHAPTER 8 – The Assessment of Intelligence**
**General notes**
- Know that intelligence is hierarchical in nature, meaning that g is comprised of smaller individual components of intelligence
- Don’t need to know history of intelligence theory
- Don’t need to know the sequence of events that led to the CHC model, including contributions of Spearman, Thurstone, Cattell, Horn, and Carroll
- Don’t need to know modern theories (e.g., Gardner & Sternberg)
- Don’t need to know heritability of IQ, behavioral genetics, or stability over time
- Don’t need to know psychometrics (e.g., internal consistency, split-half reliability)

**Questions**
- **What are the three core components of intelligence commonly included in definitions?**
	- Ability to learn, ability to adapt, ability to think critically
- What is *g*?
	- Spearman’s *g* is a representation of general intelligence, a baseline for intelligence that he believed influenced performance and overall smarts
- What is IQ?
	- An intelligence quotient is a representation of intelligence and development based on age
	- IQ equation = (mental age / chronological age) * 100
- What is the definition of mental age (and what are the problems with it)?
	- Mental age is the relative age of our brain/intellect, calculated based on academic aptitude assessments
- What is deviation IQ? (*Know that it is the currently used method, and that it allows for comparison across age groups, so an IQ of 100 means the person is in the 50th percentile for intelligence among people of the same age as them.*)
	- Deviation IQ compares an aptitude score to the common scores in that age group
	- It is the currently used method
- **According to research, what does IQ predict?**
	- Academic success, career success, income, physical health
- What are racial/ethnic and gender differences among IQ? Who does worse and what are some hypotheses as to why? 
	- Men and women perform differently across different categories (men are typically better at spatial reasoning, women are typically better with languages and emotional intelligence)
	- Black and Hispanic people typically score lower than Asian or White people
		- This could be because of true differences, or because of test biases, due to how and why the tests were created
- What was the case of Larry P? What was the problem before the case and what was the problem after the case?
	- Larry P was a black student in California in the 1970s who had been wrongly placed in special education. After he had an IQ test performed, the school boards rejected the result. At trial, California courts banned black students from taking IQ tests
- What are intelligence tests?
	- Intelligence tests are standardized, quantitative scales designed to measure cognitive aptitude
- What are Wechsler Scales? What are their historical origins?
	- The Wechsler Intelligence Scales (WPPSI, WISC, WAIS) were developed by David Wechsler for use in quantifying intelligence at the hospital where he worked
	- They take the average of intelligence across four domains 
- **What are the four index scores for the WAIS?**
	- Verbal reasoning, perceptual reasoning, working memory, processing speed

**General notes**
- For this chapter, I am not as much concerned with specific details of the DSM and RDoC as I am with your critical thinking about the categorical and dimensional approaches. Be able to compare and contrast the approaches on a conceptual level and discuss the strengths and weaknesses of each. This would make a good short answer question topic

**Questions**
- What is the difference between assessment and diagnosis?
	- Assessments gather information and provide data about the client 
	- Diagnoses classify clients and can be used for treatment
- What is the categorical approach to diagnosis? What is the dimensional approach to diagnosis?
	- Categorical diagnoses
		- Pre-determined, standardized list of criteria that the client is measured against
		- Clients are tested for specific disorders
		- With the DSM-V, clients cannot receive diagnoses unless they meet every single criterion 
		- Great for universality and research 
		- Poor for ambiguous conditions (more black-and-white than grey)
		- Can be biased, outdated, subjective, and even stigmatizing 
	- Dimensional diagnoses
		- Nuanced assessment of clients based on a variety of symptom scales
		- The RDoC assesses clients across seven key mental systems 
		- Clients are measured across the board, not tested for specific conditions
		- Great for ambiguous conditions (more grey than black-and-white)
		- Poor for research, universality, and treatment
		- Has never come to fruition (the RDoC does not actually exist)
- According to Cuthbert & Insel (2013), what are the advantages of the RDoC?
	- It is more empirical and scientific than the DSM-IV or ICD
	- It performs better in ambiguous situations
	- It can be more personalized to different clients

### **Essay question topics**
- **Functional analysis**
	- Functional analysis is the study of the factors that influence behaviors and the consequences that result from behaviors 
		- ABC: Antecedent → Behavior → Consequences
			- Antecedent: situational factors that cause behaviors
			- Behavior: observable action 
			- Consequences: situational factors resulting from behaviors
	- Behaviors have four possible causes
		- Escape: behavior is performed to end an unpleasant stimulus or situation
		- Tangibility: behavior is performed to gain a tangible reward
		- Sensory-based: behavior is performed because it just feels good
		- Attention: behavior is performed to receive attention
- **Racial and gender differences among IQ scores**
	- Men and women score differently on IQ tests
		- Men are typically higher in spatial processing, while women are typically higher in verbal reasoning
	- Hispanic and black people often score lower on IQ tests than white people
		- This could reflect a real difference
		- It could reflect biases in the tests
			- The tests were created by white men for white men
		- It could reflect societal disparities or stereotypes
			- Black people are often discriminated against and kept from resources
			- Many grow up with less wealth and less education than their white peers
	- Harmful outcomes?
		- Ramifications of Larry P’s case (IQ tests banned in CA for black students)
		- IQ tests used for forcible sterilization or institutionalization
- **Categorical vs. dimensional diagnoses**
	- Categorical diagnoses view mental disorders as lists to be checked off
		- Focus on specific criteria and guidelines
		- Essentially, putting patients into applicable boxes
		- The question is, do they have this disorder?
		- Great for standardization, and treatment planning 
		- Poor for personalization, nuance, and ambiguity
		- Example: DSM-V
			- Big book of crazy
			- Issues
				- Subjectivity of criteria
				- All-or-none approach
				- Stigmatization of disorders
	- Dimensional diagnoses view mental disorders as individualized spectrums
		- Focus on holistic overview of mental health
		- The question is, how bad are their symptoms?
		- Great for research, nuance, personalization, and ambiguity
		- Poor for diagnoses, standardization, and treatment planning
		- Example: RDoC
