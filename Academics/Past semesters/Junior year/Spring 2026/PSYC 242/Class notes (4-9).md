## Psychological assessments
- **Psychological assessment:** collecting and synthesizing client data to provide effective diagnoses and treatment
	- Speciality of many clinical psychologists
	- **Steps**
		1. **Determine the presenting problem**
			- **Referral source:** who referred the client?
			- **Reason for referral:** what question is the assessment supposed to answer?
			- **Presenting problem:** what is the client’s description of their problem?
		2. **Choose what problems to assess for**
			- How do we address the reason for referral?
			- What diagnoses are likely?
		3. **Select the assessment method**
		4. **Gather data**
		5. **Draw conclusions and classify the problem**
		6. **Report conclusions**
		7. **Develop an intervention plan**
	- **Methods**
		- **Multi-method assessment:** technique of assessing a patient by conducting a variety of different tests
- **Psychometric evaluation:** tests that measure the quality of psychological assessments
	- **Evidence-based assessments** have passed these tests
- **Reliability**
	- **Test-retest reliability:** consistency of scores over time
	- **Inter-rater reliability:** consistency of scores between different diagnosticians
	- **Internal reliability:** consistency of questions within the test
- **Validity**
	- **Construct validity:** applicability and accuracy of the test
	- **Convergent validity:** similarity between tests of the same things
	- **Discriminant validity:** dissimilarity between tests of different things
	- **Predictive validity:** predictive power of the test
	- **Incremental validity:** ability of the test to provide unique and useful results
- **Clinical vs Actuarial** 
	- **Clinical judgement:** decisions based on training and expertise
	- **Actuarial prediction:** decisions based on statistics and probability
- **Assessment research**
	- **Epidemiology:** study of the prevalence of a medical/mental condition
		- **Incidence:** rate of new instances of the condition
		- **Prevalence:** percentage of the population affected by the condition
	- **Correlational research:** research that examines the non-causal relationship between two variables
		- **Correlation:** measure of the strength of the relationship between two variables
		- **Examples**
			- **Serotonin imbalance theory**
				- There is a correlational between depression and low serotonin, but this is not a causal relationship
			- **Limitations of neuroimaging research**
				- Lack of accuracy about true cognitive processes
				- Oversimplification
				- Poor reliability
				- Correlational results, not causative results
	- **Cross-sectional designs**
		- Studies that examine individuals at a single point in time
		- A control group is typically included for comparison
	- **Longitudinal designs**
		- Studies that examine individuals over a given frame of time
		- High rates of attrition are often seen
	- **Experimental research**
		- Studies that manipulate independent variable(s) to measure the effect on dependent variable(s)
		- Participants are typically assigned to groups randomly
		- In clinical psychology, experiments are used to gather data and measure the efficacy of treatment 
		- Experiments are typically conducted in a simulated laboratory setting

## Clinical interviews
**Interview process**
- Interviewing a client for assessment purposes
	- **Rapport:** alliance between client and clinician
		- Be accepting and understanding of the client
		- Show expertise and credibility
	- **Active listening** is important to demonstrating attention and understanding
		- Responding to the client to make them feel heard and understood
		- Repeating the client’s words/sentiments
			- **Content/summary reflection**
				- Paraphrasing and repeating the client’s words
			- **Affect/meaning reflection**
				- Inferring the deeper meaning of the client’s words
	- Psychologists should emphasize the client’s strengths wherever possible 
		- The client’s strengths provide information about
			- How they cope with problems
			- What they’re proud of
			- What they’ve accomplished
			- What’s important to them
	- Psychologists should have **stimulus value** (self-awareness)
	- Silence is powerful, prompting the client to reflect and give more information
- **Types of interview questions**
	- **Open-ended** 
		- Less specific, unstructured questions
		- Questions that prompt a longer answer
		- Allows the client more autonomy in their answer
	- **Closed-ended questions**
		- More specific, structured questions
		- Questions that prompt a shorter answer
		- Allows the client less autonomy in their answer
- **Types of interview structures**
	- **Structured**
		- Specific questions/script with no deviations
		- **Pros:** provides reliable psychometrics
		- **Cons:** lack of flexibility or rapport
	- **Semistructured**
		- Some questions are verbatim, but deviations are allowed
		- **Pros:** compromise between psychometrics and flexibility
		- **Cons:** possibility for interview bias or poor statistics 
	- **Unstructured**
		- No specific questions/script, so conversation is led by the client
		- **Pros:** offers more flexibility
		- **Cons:** provides poor psychometrics
- **Investigating presenting problems**
	- **Key questions**
		- **Duration:** how long does it last?
		- **Frequency:** how often does it happen?
		- **Intensity:** how bad is it when it happens?
		- **Onset:** when did it start?
		- **Contributing/mitigating factors:** what makes it better/worse?
		- **Impairment:** how is it affecting their life?
- **Types of interviews**
	- **Intake-admission interview**
		- **Purposes**
			- Understanding reasons for admission
			- Formulating treatment 
	- **Mental status examination**
		- **Purposes**
			- Observing client presentation
	- **Psychosocial history interview**
		- **Purposes**
			- Documenting the client’s personal history
			- Obtaining background context
	- **Diagnostic interview**
		- **Purposes**
			- Diagnosing conditions with standardized questions

## Behavioral assessments
**Definitions**
- **Behavioral assessment:** assessment that measures a patient’s behavior based on learning theory (operant and classical conditioning)
	- Behavioral psychologists focus on observable phenomena, not unconscious processes
	- Behavioral psychologists view a patient’s behavior during an assessment as a **sample** of their overall behavior
		- Clinical assessments focus on diagnosable **signs** and symptoms
- **Target behavior:** behavior of interest in an assessment
- **Methods**
	- **Behavioral interview:** asking the patient about the behavior
	- **Behavioral observation:** directly observing the target behavior
		- **Settings**
			- **Naturalistic observation** 
				- Observing the patient’s behaviors within their natural context
				- Ideal when behaviors cannot be reproduced in a lab
				- Higher ecological validity, lower precision 
			- **Controlled observation** 
				- Putting the patient in a situation that elicits the target behavior
				- Lower ecological validity, higher precision
		- **Interval coding**
			- Observing whether the behavior occurs within a specific period
			- Ideal when behaviors are harder to distinguish or longer-lasting
		- **Event recording**
			- Observing each distinct occurrence of the behavior
			- Frequency-based
			- Ideal when behaviors are discrete and countable 
	- **Self-monitoring:** process by which the patient observes their own behaviors
		- **Ecological momentary assessment:** short, prompted self-report survey conducted via device
- **Functional analysis**
	- Investigating the relationship between the situational factors that influence the target behavior
	- Every volitional behavior serves a function, so it’s our job to find that function 
	- **Flow of functional behavior:** **antecedent** → **behavior** → **consequence** 
		- **Antecedents:** situational factors that precede the behavior
		- **Consequences:** situational factors that follow the behavior
- Behavior often occurs in cycles, mutually influenced by people 
	- This can lead to “vicious cycles” of behaviors
	- Can be self-reinforcing or mutually-reinforcing
	- Can be upwards (positive) or downwards (negative) influence

| **Function**    | **Description**                                                               | **Examples**                                    |
| --------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **Escape**      | Engaging in a behavior to end <br>or avoid something unpleasant               | Taking a new route<br>home to avoid traffic     |
| **Attention**   | Engaging in a behavior to <br>receive attention                               | Raising a hand to get<br>called on by a teacher |
| **Tangibility** | Engaging in a behavior to get<br>access to an item or activity                | Completing homework<br>to earn play time        |
| **Sensory**     | Engaging in a behavior because<br>it feels good or relieves <br>something bad | Scratching an itch                              |

## Personality assessments
**Definitions**
- **Personality:** patterns in behavior and emotion over time that shape perceptions, motivations, beliefs, and interactions
	- Some psychologists believe in a situational perspective, emphasizing the influence of the situation
	- Others believe in a personal perspective, emphasizing the influence of internal factors
	- Essentially, personality is a collection of traits that appear relatively consistently across situations 
		- However, a person may act against their personality (a courageous person is not always courageous)
- **Personality trait:** a single aspect of personality
	- Building block of personality
	- Influenced by both biological (genetics) and environmental factors (conditioning)
- **Five-Factor Model**
	- Theory that personality is comprised of five main traits
	- **Traits** 
		- **Neuroticism** (vs. emotional stability)
			- Facets: anxiety, hostility, depression, self-consciousness, impulsiveness, vulnerability
		- **Extraversion** (vs. introversion)
			- Facets: warmth, gregariousness, assertiveness, activity, tendency to seek excitement, prevalence of positive emotion
		- **Openness** (vs. closedness)
			- Facets: fantasy, aesthetics, feelings, actions, ideas, values
		- **Agreeableness** (vs. antagonism)
			- Facets: trust, straightforwardness, altruism, compliance, modesty, tender-mindedness
		- **Conscientiousness** (vs. lack of direction)
			- Facets: competence, order, dutifulness, motivation, self-discipline, deliberation
- **Types of personality tests**
	- **Projective**
		- Derived from psychoanalytic philosophy
		- Tests consist of open-ended, ambiguous questions
		- **Issues:** lack of face validity (inability to tell what the test is testing)
		- **Projective hypothesis:** people will reveal aspects of their personality when trying to figure out ambiguous questions
		- **Rorschach tests**
			- 10 cards with symmetrical inkblots created by Hermann Rorschach
			- Rorschach himself created it as a diagnostic for schizophrenia and did not believe in its efficacy as a personality test… and yet
			- Major issues with scoring and standardization
				- Scoring typically focuses on how patients interpret the inkblots
	- **Objective**
		- Tests consist of standardized questions with scale-based answers
		- **Empirical methods of objective test construction**
			- **Empirical criterion keying**
				- Items should not directly assess any specific content
				- Response patterns should be grouped by personality profiles
			- **Factor analysis**
				- Describes how well test items relate to a specific construct
			- **Construct validity**
				- Compares scores on new test to other, established tests
				- Using item analysis to evaluate performance of individual test items
- **Uses of personality tests**
	- Forensic settings
	- Treatment planning
	- Determining client weaknesses/strengths 
- **Note:** personality assessments are **not diagnostic**
- **Personality tests still in clinical use**
	- **Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)**
		- 335 true or false questions
		- Measures 52 aspects of personality
		- Easy to check answers for validity 
		- Problems with norming sometimes arise

## Intelligence assessments
**What is intelligence?**
- No universally accepted definition
- **Common components of definitions**
	- Ability to acquire new information
	- Ability to adapt to new situations
	- Ability to think critically

**Theories of intelligence**
- **General intelligence factor (g)**
	- Developed by Spearman (1904)
	- Theory that people have a general, baseline level of intelligence
- **Primary mental abilities**
	- Developed by Thurstone (1938)
	- Theory that multiple elements make up intelligence
		- i.e., verbal comprehension, word fluency, number facility, memory, visualization, perceptual speed, induction, speed of judgement…
- **Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) model**
	- Developed by Carroll (1993)
	- Theory of complex levels of intelligence
		- General intelligence (g) governs 8 broad abilities that govern 69 (nice) smaller abilities
	- Considered the best current model of intelligence
- **Theory of multiple intelligences**
	- Developed by Gardner (1999)
	- Modes of intelligence that overlap to influence our problem-solving skills
		- Naturalistic, musical, kinesthetic, mathematical, interpersonal, intra-personal linguistic, spatial
- **Triarchic theory of intelligence**
	- Developed by Sternberg (2005)
	- Theory that three types of intelligence (analytic, creative, and practical) inform our intellect

**Intelligence quotient (IQ)**
- **Intelligence quotient:** means of quantifying intelligence, diving mental age by chronological age
	- **IQ equation:** $IQ = MA/CA * 100$
	- **Mental age:** age-based evaluation of mental capabilities
		- First proposed by Alfred Binet
- **Deviation IQ**
	- Quantifying IQ based on the difference between the test taker’s results and the typical results for their age group
	- This removes age-based biases present in the original IQ equation

**Types of tests**
- **Intelligence tests**
	- Measures facets of intelligence
	- **Examples:** WISC, WAIS
- **Achievement tests**
	- Measures academic achievement
	- **Examples:** standardized testing in grade school
- **Aptitude tests**
	- Predicts likelihood of success in a particular environment
	- **Examples:** ACT, SAT, MCAT

**What can we predict with IQ scores?**
- Achievement test scores
- School performance (to an extent)
- Occupational outcomes (in specialized fields)
- Income
- Physical health

**What can’t we predict with IQ scores?**
- Creativity
- Mental health
- Emotional intelligence
- Morality

**How can we use IQ tests clinically?**
- Testing for giftedness
- Testing for intellectual disability
- Testing for learning disability
- Testing for specific cognitive impairments
- Determining cognitive strengths and weaknesses
- Observing cognitive processes
- Predicting academic success

**IQ test biases**
- Differences observed between racial/ethnic groups
- **Possible explanations for differences**
	- Test biases
	- Societal disadvantages 
	- Stereotype threat

**Intelligence tests**
- **Wechsler Intelligence Scales**
	- David Wechsler put forth the Wechsler-Bellevue Intelligence Scale in (1939)
	- Designed to be clinically useful rather than theoretical 
	- **Hierarchically structured**
		- Set of four subtests make up composite IQ score (FSIQ)
			- **Verbal Comprehension:** grasp and knowledge of language
			- **Perceptual Reasoning:** spatial processing, pattern recognition
			- **Working Memory:** ability to store information
			- **Processing Speed:** rapidity of processing without making errors
	- **Different tests for different age ranges**
		- **WPPSI:** preschoolers (2-7)
		- **WISC:** children (6-16)
		- **WAIS:** adults (16-90)

## Diagnosis & treatment planning
**Classification of mental disorders**
- Diagnosis happens based on a number of descriptive criteria
	- **Signs:** observable phenomena 
	- **Symptoms:** subjective experiences
	- **Associated features:** prevalence, common co-morbidities 
- **Cultural syndromes:** cultural concepts of distress
- **Essentialism:** view that mentally ill people are fundamentally different from others

**Categorical models of diagnosis**
- Classical approach to diagnosis
- Based on medical models
- Client’s symptoms are compared against a list
- **Main question:** do they have a given disorder?
- **Strengths**
	- Facilitates clear and easy communication
	- Allows for specific research
	- Provides guidance for treatment planning
- **Weaknesses**
	- Reliance on descriptive criteria
	- Subjectivity
	- Ambiguity 
	- Arbitrary cutoffs
	- Stigmatization of mental illness
- **Example:** DSM-V
	- **Weaknesses**
		- Terrible inter-rater reliability (20-70%)
		- Copyrighted (not free)
		- Diagnoses often occur in a vacuum

**Dimensional models of diagnosis**
- Method that measures all mental disorders on a spectrum
- **Main question:** how intense are their symptoms?
- **Strengths**
	- Less arbitrary delineation of disorders
	- More detailed and specific information
	- Destigmatization of mental illness
	- (Possibly) more empirical 
- **Weaknesses**
	- Largely theoretical (no working framework)
	- Difficult to interpret and communicate in clinical settings
	- Difficult to determine who “deserves” treatments
	- Difficult to develop and test treatments
- **Example:** Research Domain Criteria (Cuthbert & Insel, 2013)
	- **History of RDoC**
		- NIMH’s approach to classifying psychopathology
		- Based on a dimensional framework for psychopathology
		- Focus on neurobiological processes across disorders
		- Aims to identify biomarkers of psychopathology
	- **Aims**
		- Identifying overlap between various mental disorders
		- Understanding the full range from abnormal to normal psychology
		- Developing reliable measures for symptoms of mental disorders
		- Understanding the complete etiology of mental disorders
	- **Criteria**
		- Negative valence domain
		- Positive valence domain
		- Cognitive systems
		- Social systems
		- Arousal systems
	- **Seven pillars**
		- Emphasis on research and science
		- Multidimensional approach to psychopathology
		- Better and more consistent scales for mental disorders
		- Better frameworks for psychological research
		- Equal importance given to behavior and neurobiology
		- Recognition of mental disorders based on scientific evidence
		- Opportunities for the diagnosis criteria to be updated
	- **Weaknesses**
		- No diagnostic system based on the RDoC exists
		- Understands all mental illnesses through a biological/medical lens
		- Overemphasizing the importance of neuroimaging
		- De-emphasizing the importance of psychosocial factors 
	- **Example**
		- Anhedonia (loss of pleasure) is found across a number of mental illnesses
		- RDoC researchers would use Positive Valence Domain to measure a patient’s reward processing
			- **Methods:** neuroimaging, behavioral tasks, self-report measures
