## What is a clinical psychologist?
**Key terms**
- **Mental illness**: pattern of behavior, thinking, or feeling that causes significant personal distress or interference with daily life
- **Psychological treatment:** specific intervention to treat a mental illness
- **Counseling:** talk sessions to support patients through less severe mental illnesses

**Clinical psychologists**
- **Clinical psychology:** field of psychology specializing in assessment, diagnosis, treatment, and research of mental illnesses
	- Clinical psychologists believe mental illnesses are determined by biological, cognitive, emotional, and environmental factors
	- **Focus on talk therapy and CBT (see below)**
	- **Biopsychosocial model:** understanding of mental illness through biological, psychological, and social factors
		- **Biological factors:** genetics, neurobiology
		- **Psychological factors:** affect, behavior, cognition
		- **Social factors:** society, culture
- **Education:** doctoral degree (typically PhD, sometimes PsyD)
	- Training focuses on practicum, science-based coursework, and research
	- Internships in clinical settings are often recommended
- **Activities**
	- **Intervention:** using specific treatments to target psychological problems
		- **Psychotherapy:** treatment involving a close conversation-based relationship between therapist and patient
		- **Cognitive behavioral therapy:** changing thoughts is the path to changing behaviors and feelings
			- Bi-directional influences between affect, behavior, and cognition
			- **History of CBT**
				- **Wave 1:** Behavior therapy
				- **Wave 2:** Behavior therapy + cognitive therapy
				- **Wave 3:** Behavior therapy + cognitive therapy + emotion-focused therapy
	- **Diagnosis:** gathering evidence and drawing conclusions about a patient to determine their current mental conditions
		- Goal is to form an effective treatment plan
	- **Research:** experimentation that refines understanding of mental illness
	- **Teaching:** instructing young or potential psychologists
	- **Clinical supervision:** guiding or supervising trainees in clinical or treatment settings
	- **Consultation:** offering expertise to assist others (colleagues, etc)

**Mental health professions**
- **Medical focus**
	- **Psychiatrists**
		- Psychiatrists are physicians and exist within the framework of medicine
		- Psychiatrists may be better equipped to diagnose and treat, but their perspective is exclusively medical 
			- Mental illnesses are seen as biological neural disease
			- Appointments are short and focus on medical management
		- Psychologists traditionally have less authority and must cede to psychiatrists in medical decisions
	- **Psychiatric nurses**
		- Nurses that specialize in mental illness
		- Like psychiatrists, they have a medical/biological perspective
		- They often work in medical centers
		- They can prescribe medication, but often fulfill a medication management role in a treatment team
	- **Health psychologists**
		- Aren’t all psychologists health psychologists??
		- Health psychologists manage holistic treatment plans for mental health patients
- **Counseling focus**
	- **Psychotherapists**
		- Umbrella term for a mental health professional
		- Does not refer to any profession or training in particular
	- **Counseling psychologist**
		- Academic mental health professionals (PhD or PsyD in psychology)
		- They often research therapy methods and processes
		- Step up from licensed counselors 
			- Counseling psychologists provide the same services, but have more expertise
	- **Licensed counselors**
		- Counselors work with individuals who have minor or short-term mental issues
			- For more serious issues, they defer to psychiatrists or clinical psychologists
		- They conduct interviews to assess patients
		- They often work in educational or organizational settings
		- They take a positive counseling approach, focusing on the patient’s strengths 
	- **Social workers**
		- Much more common profession
		- They often visit patients at home or work
		- Recently, social workers have turned towards more psychotherapy
	- **School psychologists**
		- Psychologists (PhD or PsyD) who work with students in an educational setting with the goal of improving their academic success
		- They often focus on students with special needs (disabled students, gifted students, etc)
		- Could be a cool career :-)
	- **Marriage/family therapists**
		- Mental health professionals who diagnose and treat problems in marriage or family settings
	- **Life coaches**
		- Unlicensed professionals who give life advice

## Guest Lecture: Multiculturalism
**Clinical multiculturalism**
- **Multiculturalism:** promotion or celebration of cultural diversity within a society
- Culture plays a huge role in clinical practice and understanding of mental illness
- History of racism in psychology
	- **Scientific racism:** use of data to justify a biologically-based hierarchy
	- Clinical psychology has often been used to promote hateful ideology
		- Has harmed the image of psychology in the present day
- **Cultural competency:** ability to understand and respect other cultures
	- Foundation of many ethical psychological principles
- **Cultural humility:** self-reflective openness to diversity 
	- Involves being curious, aware, and willing to learn 
	- In clinical practice, using neutral materials is often best

## Detailed history of clinical psychology
**History of clinical psychology: diagnostics**
- **Early contributors**
	- **William Tuke:** English psychologist who developed humane asylums
	- **Eli Todd:** American psychologist who founded Institute of Living (Scoville)
	- **Philippe Pinel:** French physician who reformed early asylums
	- **Dorothea Dix:** American who campaigned for humane asylums in New Jersey
- **Early diagnostics**
	- **Francis Galton**
		- Eugenicist who pushed for quantitative psychology measures
	- **James McKeen Cattell**
		- Pioneer of “mental tests” for intelligence
	- **Lightner Witmer**
		- His first name was David. I looked it up
		- Gave the field its name
		- Opened the first psychological clinic (1896)
		- Founded the first psychological journal (The Psychological Clinic)
		- He focused on children with special needs
	- **Emil Kraepelin**
		- Diagnostic categories developed in 1913
			- **Curable illnesses:** mental illnesses caused by external factors
			- **Incurable illnesses:** mental illnesses caused by internal factors
- **Beginning of the modern era**
	- **Alfred Binet**
		- French psychologist who studied children with special needs
		- Believed that clinical psychology lay in the deviation from norms
		- **1908 Binet-Simon scale:** early IQ test created by Binet and Theodore Simon
			- Huge global influence on the field
			- Revised as Stanford-Binet test
	- **Personality testing**
		- Pioneered by Carl Jung
		- **Kent-Rosanoff Free Association Test (1910):** early personality test
	- **American Psychological Association (APA)**
		- Founded after WWI by the Army
		- Produced the Army Alpha and Army Beta tests
	- **David Wechsler**
		- **Wechsler-Bellevue test (1939):** foundational IQ test
			- Revised in 1955 as the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale
		- **Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (1949)**
	- **Projective testing**
		- Diagnostic materials that involved linking images and text
		- **Example:** Rorschach tests
- **Modern era**
	- **Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (1943):** self-report personality test
		- First test that did not require subjective interpretation of results
	- **Radical behaviorism (1950s):** shift toward quantifiable behaviors
		- Lead to a rise in behavioral assessments
	- **Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-I)**
		- Diagnostic dictionary published by the APA
		- First appeared in 1952

**History of clinical psychology: interventions**
- **Early contributors**
	- **Jean Charcot:** early investigator of patients with unexplained physiological mental issues
	- **Josef Breuer:** worked with hysteric patient Anna O
		- Published Studies on Hysteria (1895) with Freud
	- **Sigmund Freud:** we know this one
- **Beginning of the modern era**
	- **Clifford Beers:** manic depressive patient who wrote an exposé on asylums
	- **William Healy:** psychologist who established a guidance clinic for troubled/violent children
	- **Early talk therapy**
		- Joseph Pratt and Elwood Worcester collaborated to pioneer discussion-based therapy in 1905
		- Group therapy became more popular by the early 1930s
	- **Alfred Adler:** psychologist who focused on interconnected family relationships
	- **Psychoanalysis evolves?**
		- Carl Rogers, John Dollard, and Neal Miller all focused their work around psychoanalysis
	- Newer forms of therapy appeared in the 1950s, including Gestalt therapy, family therapy, and early forms of CBT
	- **Behaviorism**
		- Behavioral psychologists pioneered operant conditioning (B.F. Skinner), conditioned reflex therapy (Andrew Salter), and systematic desensitization (Joseph Wolpe)
		- Behavioral therapy became more popular than psychoanalysis 
- **Modern era**
	- Psychotherapy often tends to follow trends (“inner-child therapy” etc)
	- Time-effective therapy is increasingly popular (though maybe not effective)
	- Therapy has become more standardized and now relies on treatment manuals

**History of clinical psychology: research**
- **Early contributors**
	- **Wilhelm Wundt**
		- German father of psychology
		- Founder of the first psychology lab (1879)
	- **William James**
		- American father of psychology
		- Author of Principles of Psychology (1890)
- **Beginning of the modern era**
	- **Ivan Pavlov:** pioneer of classic conditioning
	- **Carl Rogers:** pioneer of therapy research in the 1950s
	- Julian Rotter: author of Social Learning and Clinical Psychology (1954)
- **Timeline of psychological research**![[Screenshot 2026-01-22 at 8.37.57 PM.png]]

**History of clinical psychology: practice**
- **Beginning of the modern era**
	- The APA backed much of early professional clinical psychology
	- During WWII, the VA expanded clinical psychology 
	- After WWII, PhD degrees in clinical psychology became more common
	- **Boulder Conference (1949):** conference that provided the current training model for graduate education in clinical psychology
- **Modern era**
	- Cognitive-behavioral studies began in the 1970s
	- Growth of the profession continued through the 1980s and 1990s
	- **1988 Schism**
		- Disagreements over the balance between logistics and academics
		- American Psychological Society was formed by members who left the APA

## Clinical psychology training models
**Clinical Scientist training**
- **Pioneered by McFall (1991)**
	- Believed that clinical psychology had moved too far from science
- **Research-focused**
	- **Three pillars of clinical science training**
		1. Clinical psychology must always be scientific
		2. Treatment should only be used when
			- Clearly described
			- Benefits are clearly stated and validated
			- Side effects overshadow benefits
		3. Psychological training should produce clinical scientists
- **Strengths**
	- Emphasis on empirically-supported treatments
	- Advances current research
	- Often better funding
- **Weaknesses**
	- Excessive focus on research
	- Slow to adopt new interventions
	- Long training
- Accredited by the PCSAS
- **Degree:** PhD

**Scientist Practitioner training**
- **Pioneered at the Boulder Conference (1949)**
- **Balance of research and practice**
	- Equal training in science and practice
	- Training happens at universities
	- Coursework in statistics and research
- **Strengths**
	- Balanced training
	- Understanding of both science and practice
	- Flexible career outcomes
- **Weaknesses**
	- Less emphasis on clinical training
	- Programs are small and competitive
- Accredited by the APA
- **Degree:** PhD

**Practitioner Scholar training**
- **Pioneered at the Vail Conference (1973)**
- **Practice-focused**
	- First two years provide a theory foundation 
	- Then pivots to a focus on clinical practice
	- Dissertation is usually a lit review or case study
- **Strengths**
	- Strong clinical training
	- More “real world” experiences
	- More students trained than in PhD programs
	- Admissions are more holistic 
- **Weaknesses**
	- Training is more narrow
	- Less science-based
	- Less career flexibility
	- Stigma of receiving a PsyD
- Accredited by the APA
- **Degree:** PsyD

**Similarities between all three**
- Broad foundation in psych theory
- Training in assessment and treatment
- Applied experience in external treatment settings
- Familiarity with clinical research
- Multiculturalism and ethics
- Year-long internship (residency?) after coursework

**PhD in clinical psychology**
- **Length:** 5-7 years
- **Funded:** yes
- **Acceptance rate:** 1-5%
- **Emphasis:** research
- Better licensing exam scores
- Often focused on CBT
- Schools are more reputable

**PsyD in clinical psychology**
- **Length:** 4-6 years
- **Funded:** no
- **Acceptance rate:** 40%
- **Emphasis:** clinical work
- Worse licensing exam scores
- Focused on CBT, psychodynamic, and family therapy
- Schools are less reputable

## Ethics of clinical psychology
**Standards of practice**
- **Competence**
	- Psychologists should practice within the bounds of their abilities
	- This refers both to academic training and background (culture, personal experience)
- **Informed consent**
	- Don’t do stuff to the patient if the patient didn’t say you could do it
	- I think it’s a little scary that we need hardcoded laws for this shit
- **Confidentiality**
	- Psychologists should honor the privacy of patients whenever possible 
	- Some third parties can access patient information in certain circumstances (financial, imminent harm, etc)
		- Psychologists have a duty to report instances where a patient is being harmed by others, is harming others, or is harming themselves
		- Psychologists may also have to report information to patients’ parents or guardians in child psychology settings
- **Relationships**
	- Psychologists may not have dual relationships with patients
	- Relationships between psychologists and patients must be strictly clinical
	- No funny business <3

**Standards of research**
- **Informed consent** 
	- You know this one
- **Confidentiality** 
	- Information shared for research purposes must not have any identifiers 
- **Deception**
	- Psychologists may withhold the true purpose of an experiment, but only when it does not harm the participants
	- Participants must not feel exploited by deception 
- **Debriefing** 
	- Psychologists should provide the participant clear and thorough information about the experiment
- **Don’t falsify your data**
	- Except if funny

**Handling ethical dilemmas**
- **Consent** 
	- Informed consent when you begin working with a patient
	- Making sure they know what information is protected
- **Consult**
	- Discuss the situation with a colleague or peer
- **Document**
	- Record every decision and step
	- Provide rationales 

**Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct**
- APA code of ethics 
- First published in 1953
- Revised in 2017 after psychologists help CIA torture inmates at Gitmo
- **Two sections**
	- **Aspirational**
		- **General Principles:** ideal standards for psychological practice 
		- 5 principles
			- **Beneficence and non-maleficence:** psychologists should help their patients
			- **Fidelity and responsibility:** psychologists should be trustworthy and professional
			- **Integrity:** psychologists should be honest and… integritous
			- **Justice:** psychologists should aim to help all patients equally
			- **Respect for people’s rights and dignity:** psychologists should respect the rights and dignity of patients
	- **Enforceable** 
		- **Ethical Standards:** rules of conduct that can be specifically violated
		- 10 standards (four most important in this class)
			- **Competence**
				- Obligation to accurately represent services/training
				- Responsibility to continue education
			- **Human relationships**
				- Obligation to do no harm
				- Obligation to avoid dual relationships wherever possible
				- Obligation to remain confidential to maintain trust
					- Limits to confidentiality: harm to or by self or others
						- Baker Act (involuntary psych hold)
						- Tarasoff (duty to warn threat of harm to others)
						- Minors have no right to confidentiality
			- **Therapy** 
				- Don’t have sex with your patients
				- **Informed consent**
					- Always give consent
