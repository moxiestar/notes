## **Chapter 10: Evidence-Based Treatment**
**What does it mean for a treatment to be “evidence-based”? Why does it matter?**
Evidence-based treatment is scientifically sound, based on empirical research, and evaluated by independent assessments. It is proven to be effective and have good outcomes, so is often more helpful for patients.

***What is the Barlow (2004) criteria for “psychological treatment”?***
Treatment must be effective, relevant, and scientific.

**How do we know if a treatment actually works? Who do we ask? When do we ask? What do we ask?**
Test the treatment with rigorous, specific assessments. Ask the patient, ask a third party. Ask before, during, and after treatment. Ask about differences in symptoms.

***What is the difference between efficacy and effectiveness? What type of validity is prioritized by each? What is the tradeoff of prioritizing one vs the other?***
Efficacy is how well a treatment works in theory, effectiveness is how well it works in practice. Efficacy: internal validity. Effectiveness: external validity. 

***What is a randomized controlled trial (RCT)? What are the key features of RCTs (Table 10.2)?***
Randomized controlled trials are tests of a given treatment’s effectiveness for a certain population. A group of homogenous patients with the same pathology is randomly assigned to either a control group or a treatment group. Trained therapists assess patients via blinded assessment, so they don’t know whether clients are receiving the treatment or not. 

**What is meant by “common factors” vs “specific factors”?**
Common factors is the theory that most psychological disorders can be treated by overlapping therapies. Specific factors is the theory that each psychological disorder must be treated by its own tailored form of therapy. 

**What is evidence-based treatment? What is evidence-based practice? What are the components of EBP?**
I just answered this. Evidence-based practice involves choosing the best empirical treatment for each patient. This means using clinical expertise, experience, rapport, and scientific knowledge. 

## **Chapter 11: Behavioral treatments**
*Be comfortable conducting the “enhanced functional analysis” introduced in this chapter*
**What is the goal of behavior therapy?**
Changing, reducing, or replacing a specific maladaptive target behavior. 

**What are we targeting in behavior therapy (i.e., what are we trying to change)?**
Situations where the target behavior is performed. Goal is to reduce and change response to behavioral urges. 

***What are the stages of the behavior therapy process?***
Identify target behavior. Perform functional analysis. Implement treatment. Assess outcomes. Reformulate treatment as needed.

**Explain the applications of behavior therapy:** 
Skills training (assertiveness training): teaching patients to implement certain social strategies (like assertion)
Behavioral parent training (BPT): teaching parents to effectively respond to their child’s behaviors
Habit reversal training: teaching patients to be aware of their habit urges and actively combat them by performing other behaviors
Behavioral activation (BA): teaching patients, typically with depressive or isolating symptoms, to purposefully perform healthy, adaptive behaviors

***What are the strengths and criticisms of behavior therapy?***
Strengths: widely applicable across different patient populations, efficient, effective
Weaknesses: dismissive of deeper health problems
