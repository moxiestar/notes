## Case formulation
**General definitions**
- Idiographic (individualistic) approach to characterizing psychological problems 
- Goal of explaining client’s specific problems, rather than labelling them
	- Problems are described in cognitive, emotional, and behavioral terms instead of diagnostic terms

**Diathesis stress model**
- Theory that some are more likely than others to develop psychopathologies
	- The less predisposed you are, the more stress you can handle before you develop pathologies
	- The more predisposed you are, the less stress you can handle before you develop pathologies

**Components of case formulation**
- **Problem list:** collection of presenting problems
- **Predisposing factors:** hypotheses about causes and predispositions
	- **Diatheses**
		- Genetics or biological factors
		- Life experiences
- **Precipitants:** events that worsen the problems
	- **Stressors**
		- Single acute events
		- Major life changes
		- Illnesses
	- Not exclusively daily or short-term
- **Perpetuating factors:** things that allow the problem to continue
- **Protective factors:** strengths and positives that can help the client

**Strengths and weaknesses**
- **Strengths**
	- Rooted in empirical science
	- Personalized and individualistic 
	- Socioculturally nuanced
	- Dimensional
	- Iterative
- **Weaknesses**
	- Relatively new
	- Unclear psychometrics
	- Difficult to adopt

## Psychological treatment
**Types of treatment**
- **Psychological treatment:** use of scientific/empirical interventions to understand and solve a patient’s problems
	- **Framework (Barlow, 2004)**
		- Treatment must be derived from psychological science
		- Treatment must be tailored to the processes causing problems
		- Treatment must have been proven effective in controlled studies
	- **Assessing efficacy & effectiveness of treatments**
		- **Efficacy:** how well the treatment works in theory
			- Focuses on internal validity
			- Tested via randomized controlled trials 
		- **Effectiveness:** how well the treatment works in practice
			- Focuses on external validity
			- Highly lacking in clinical psychological science
		- **Key questions**
			- **Who** do we assess?
				- Client
				- Clinician
				- Independent evaluator: unbiased third-party clinician
			- **What** do we assess?
				- Reduction in symptoms
				- Improved functioning
				- Improved quality of life
			- **How** do we assess?
				- Standardized, valid assessments
			- **When** do we assess?
				- Before treatment
				- During treatment
				- Immediately after treatment
				- Long after treatment
	- **Choosing an effective treatment**
		- **Evidence-based practice:** selecting a treatment based on efficacy, clinical expertise, and patient profile
		- Importance of flexibility and client input
	- **Models of psychological treatments**
		- **Common factors:** idea that all treatments are similar/interchangeable
			- It doesn’t matter what treatment the patient uses, it just matters that they’re being treated 
			- Good rapport, good general health, and high client expectations make the biggest difference in treatment
		- **Specific factors:** idea that there is little overlap between treatments 
			- It matters that patients are given specific treatments
			- Treatments are designed to target specific disorders
			- This theory is often supported by “dismantling studies”
				- Studies that dismantle a treatment plan to find the most powerful element
		- **Interactional perspective:** philosophy that falls between common and specific
- **Psychotherapy:** methods of changing a patient’s behaviors, thoughts, and/or feelings to improve their mental health and functioning
	- **Behavioral:** aims to modify problematic behaviors
	- **Cognitive-behavioral:** aims to modify problematic thoughts and behaviors
	- **Dialectical:** aims to develop skills for coping with stress, etc
	- **Mindfulness/acceptance:** promotes calm by reframing and accepting thoughts
	- **Psychodynamic:** aims to gain insight into unconscious cognitive processes
	- **Client-centered/humanistic:** promotes trust through positive psychology

## Behavioral interventions
**Basics of interventions**
- **Goals**
	- Reduce maladaptive behaviors
	- Increase healthy behaviors
	- Reduce distress and/or impairment
- **Guiding principles**
	- Behavior is understood in terms of learning theory
	- Behavior is seen as the key problem, not a sign of a deeper problem
	- Origins of the behavior are largely not important
	- Any maladaptive behavior can be replaced with adaptive behavior
	- Treatment is specific to each client
- **Philosophy**
	- Based on learning theory principles (classical/operant conditioning)
	- Action-orientated 
	- Pragmatic

**Steps of behavioral interventions**
1. **Define and assess target behavior**
	- Identify maladaptive behavior(s)
	- Assess behaviors for frequency, intensity, and duration
2. **Perform functional analysis**
	- Identify factors maintaining target behavior (ABC)
	- A & C are not based on observation
		- Antecedents are always in the vein of classical conditioning
		- Consequences are always in the vein of operant conditioning
3. **Implement treatment**
	- Collaborate with client to find best options
	- Start with the most important behavior…
		- Snowball method: start with the easiest/least distressing behavior
		- Diffusion method: start with the most difficult/distressing behavior
	- Reinforce desired behavior
		- Frame goals positively
		- Practice in-session and between sessions
	- Problem-solve barriers and resistance
		- Meet clients where they are
		- Focus on how things will improve for clients
		- Build commitment to change
	- Specific techniques
		- Contingency clarification: helping client understand antecedents and consequences
		- Stimulus control: helping client remove antecedents
		- Contingency management: punishing maladaptive behavior and rewarding adaptive behavior 
4. **Assess outcomes**
	- Regularly monitor changes in behavior
	- Measure effect of intervention on target behavior
	- If nothing has changed, reconsider identification of target behavior 
5. **Reformulate intervention as needed**

**Strengths of behavioral intervention**
- Efficacy and effectiveness well established
- High efficiency 
	- Course of treatment is usually short
- Wide breadth of application
	- Can be applied to virtually every voluntary behavior 
	- General principles apply to almost every situation

**Weaknesses of behavioral intervention**
- Not applicable to every problem
- Seen as dehumanizing because it reduces human behavior to behavioral principles
- Some see it as inhumane/unethical
	- Techniques like exposure therapy and aversive conditioning are often intense and distressing

**Applications of behavioral interventions**
- **Skills training**
	- Teaching patients to improve their social functioning
- **Assertiveness training**
	- Teaching patients to stand up for themselves
	- Guiding principle: everyone has a right to express their thoughts and feelings
- **Behavioral parent training**
	- Reward-based system of externalizing behavior in children
	- **Shaping:** encouraging desired behaviors by rewarding any similar behaviors
	- **Time-out:** removing a child from a situation that causes problematic behaviors
	- **Contracting:** agreeing to punish/reward certain behaviors
	- **Grandma’s rule:** requiring the child to work before they play
- **Token economies**
	- Allowing controlled access to maladaptive behavior via vouchers/tokens
	- For example, doing certain chores would allow a child to accumulate tokens to play video games
- **Exposure therapy**
	- Repeated exposure to the fearful stimulus until the fear response is extinguished 
- **Progressive muscle relaxation**
	- Technique where patients tense and then relax various muscle groups
	- Effective for reducing stress or falling asleep
- **Behavioral activation**
	- Treating depression and depressive patterns via specific behaviors
		- Patients are encouraged to engage in positive behaviors and disengage in negative behaviors
	- This was the technique covered in Reflection 7
- **Habit reversal training**
	- Technique to break habits via three components
		1. **Awareness training:** patient logs the occurrences of the habit
		2. **Stimulus control:** patient uses various techniques to reduce triggering stimuli
		3. **Competing response practice:** patient replaces the habit with a behavior that prevents it
			- i.e., a patient who wants to bite their nails might put on gloves and wash the dishes
- **Cue exposure**
	- Exposing the patient to triggering stimuli while keeping them from performing the triggered behavior
- **Aversive conditioning**
	- Association of unpleasant stimuli/feelings with the behavior
	- i.e., nausea or vomiting become associated with alcohol

## Cognitive-behavioral interventions
**History of cognitive-behavioral treatment**
- Inspired by the cognitive revolution of the 1960s
	- **Cognitive revolution:** shift away from classical conditioning and operant conditioning
	- **Cognitive perspective:** philosophy that our thoughts, feelings, and behaviors, all influence each other
	- **Cognitive specificity:** differing effects of situations based on our emotional states
	- **Cognitive distortions:** maladaptive thinking patterns
		- All-or-nothing thinking: having black-and-white view of the world
		- Mind reading: assuming what others are thinking
		- Fortune telling: predicting future outcomes
		- Catastrophizing: anticipating the worst-case scenario
		- Should/must: having inflexible rules about the world

**Three waves of CBT**
- **Wave 1:** behavior therapy
- **Wave 2:** behavior therapy + cognitive therapy
- **Wave 3:** behavior therapy + cognitive therapy + emotion-focused/experimental therapy
	- i.e., ACT, DBT

**Basic framework of CBT**
- **Stimulus → thoughts → feelings → behaviors**
- **Activating event:** situation that influences your beliefs
	- Yes, this is basically the same thing as an antecedent
- **Beliefs:** feelings or thoughts that arise because of the situation
- **Consequences:** emotional and behavioral results 

**Key philosophies of CBT**
- Understanding that we can always change our behavior, if nothing else
- **Rational thinking:** realistic, grounded framework for understanding the world
	- CBT does not emphasize an overly positive perspective 
	- Goal is to have thoughts be flexible but reasonable 
- **Constructive negative emotions:** negative emotions that can help us work through/understand situations
	- Negative emotions are essential for survival, but they can be maladaptive
	- When based on rational thinking, they can be extremely useful 
- **Unconditional self-acceptance:** ability to see oneself as worthy of love regardless of success or failure

**Steps of CBT**
1. **Defining the target problem**
	- Focus on how a client responds to a given situation
	- Figure out what the client can and cannot control
2. **Explaining the ABC model**
	- Bring attention to the beliefs piece
3. **Identifying A, B, and C**
	- Start with consequences
	- Work back to the activating event
	- Then assess beliefs
	- Use beliefs to identify patterns of cognitive distortions
- Implementation 
	- Have clients self-monitor their ABC patterns
	- Have clients practice **cognitive restructuring** 
		- Identifying and changing maladaptive thoughts
- Outcome assessment 

**Practice of cognitive restructuring**
- **Evaluating thoughts via the following framework**
	1. **Evidence:** what evidence do you have that this thought is true?
	2. **Alternative explanation:** could something else have caused this?
	3. **Outcome:** what’s the worst possible outcome?
	4. **Effect:** what are the effects of believing these thoughts?
	5. **What should I do? What can I do?**
	6. **If a friend was in this situation, what advice would I give?**

**Main models of constructive restructuring**
- **Cognitive Model**
	- Developed by Beck
	- Views thoughts as testable hypotheses
	- **Framework**
		- Automatic thoughts → intermediate beliefs → core beliefs
- **Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy**
	- Developed by Albert Ellis
	- **Four cognitive distortions**
		- Demandingness
		- Awfulizing
		- Low frustration tolerance
		- Conditional acceptance

## Exposure therapy
**Anxiety**
- The body’s response to perceptions of threats
	- Physiological, cognitive, and behavioral components
- Typically a healthy response, unless it becomes excessive and maladaptive
- **Patterns**
	- Fear cues that trigger physiological responses
	- Safety behaviors to mitigate the anxiety 
	- Maladaptive beliefs about the world

**Exposure therapy**
- Treatment that counteracts anxious patterns by exposing the patient to feared stimuli
- Generally more effective than psychotherapy or medications
- **Basics**
	- Patient practices confronting their fears without using safety behaviors
- **Types of exposure**
	- **In vivo exposure:** confronting the fear in real life
	- **Imaginal exposure:** imagining the fear
	- **Virtual reality exposure:** confronting the fear via VR
	- **Interoceptive exposure:** confronting triggering physical sensations
- **Assessments**
	- What triggers/situations are causing the fear?
	- What does the patient believe will happen?
	- What are the patient’s safety behaviors?
- **Education**
	- Important to teach the patient about anxiety
- **Exposure planning**
	- Give the patient homework to tackle outside of therapy sessions
		- List of situations to be addressed
	- Build commitment and plan for adversity
		- Make sure the patient has a plan for roadblocks 
- **Therapist’s role**
	- Advise patient on choosing exposure
	- Push them away from safety behaviors
	- Avoid reassuring the patient
	- Don’t model fear
- **Measuring success**
	- Patients are assumed to improve if…
		- Fear declines during each session
		- Fear is lower at the beginning of each subsequent session
		- Patient can tackle more difficult exposures
- **Theories behind exposure therapy**
	- **Habituation** 
		- **Theories**
			- Repeatedly confronting fears lowers the fear response
			- Fear extinction occurs on a curve over time
			- Fear is lowered during and between exposure sessions
		- **Issues with the theory**
			- Teaches patients that anxiety is a bad thing
			- Goal of controlling fear, not lessening fear
			- Fear is viewed as a failure or threat
			- Not a good predictor of outcomes
			- Doesn’t generalize well across different situations
			- Not necessary for long-term improvement
		- **Issues with fear extinction**
			- Learned associations cannot necessarily be extinguished 
			- External fear responses are not always linked to internal fear responses
			- Fears can return spontaneously 
	- **Inhibitory learning**
		- **Theories**
			- Inaccurate fears are proven wrong
			- New pathways are built alongside the old, maladaptive fear pathways
				- Updated drafts don’t replace older drafts
			- Goal of activating the new pathways in fearful situations 
		- **Exposure methods**
			- Asking the patient to make predictions about what will happen during fearful situations 
			- Maximizing activation of new relationship and minimizing activation of old relationship
			- Using exposure to test maladaptive beliefs
			- Asking patients to explain what they learned after the fact
			- Explain the importance of exposure
			- Teach patients to tolerate anxiety

## Acceptance-based treatments
**Philosophies**
- **Dialectical thinking:** practice of accepting and understanding contradicting facts
- **Mindfulness:** noticing situations and feelings without passing positive or negative judgements
- **Acceptance:** embracing unpleasant experiences

**Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)**
- Developed by Steven Hayes based on the following frameworks…
	- **Relational frame theory:** theory that the ability to relate concepts and ideas is the basis of human understanding
		- Learning via association
	- **Experiential avoidance:** unwillingness to endure difficult feelings or situations
		- Desire to get rid of these difficult feelings
	- **Psychological flexibility:** ability to live in the moment despite difficult situations
		- Psychologist aims to encourage client to stop obsessing over controlling/reducing anxiety 
- Focuses on teaching patients to cope with symptoms and situations
- **Core principles of ACT**
	- **Acceptance:** allowing unpleasant feelings or experiences to come and go without reacting
	- **Cognitive defusion:** stepping back and observing our thoughts without engaging; reframing our experiences of the world as subjective, not objective
	- **Contact with the present moment:** bringing full awareness and engagement to the present moment
	- **Observing self:** understanding that thoughts and feelings do not define us as people
		- Theory that there is a central, essential part of yourself, regardless of your thoughts and feelings
	- **Values:** clarifying what things are most important in our lives
		- Intertwined with committed action 
		- Helps patients understand what drives their motivation
		- Healthy goals should overlap with values
	- **Committed action:** setting goals and taking action to achieve them
		- Working towards value-driven goals, despite unwanted thoughts or feelings
- **Efficacy?**
	- ACT seems to be better than no treatment
	- Works for a wide range of patients with a wide range of issues
		- Particularly those who struggle with emotional regulation
- **Criticisms**
	- Too heady for certain patient populations
	- Huge overlap with CBT 
		- Same concepts and strategies, different terms

**Dialectical behavior therapy (DBT)**
- Aimed at patients who struggle with emotional regulation and intensity 
- Based on the following frameworks…
	- **Biosocial theory of personality:** idea that personality is a combination of environmental and biological factors
		- Patients may have a biological tendency to emotional reactivity
		- Patients may also have been chronically invalidated (i.e., told they’re overreacting)
	- **Wise mind:** way of understanding the mind as a combination of rational thoughts and emotions
		- **Reasonable mind:** practical, pragmatic side of the brain
		- **Emotion mind:** feeling-based, illogical side of the brain
		- Wise mind combines and accepts both sides of the brain
	- **Distress tolerance skills:** set of methods that teach patients to cope with unpleasant situations and feelings
- **Founded by Marsha Linehan** 
	- Created for suicidal BPD patients in the 1980s
	- Aims to balance acceptance-based strategies and change-based strategies 
- DBT is usually administered across different formats
	- **Individual therapy:** assessing maladaptive behaviors one-on-one 
	- **Group therapy:** learning how to develop skills 
	- **Coaching:** in-the-moment interventions with the patient
		- Patient is allowed to call or text the therapist if needed
- **Balances acceptance and change**
	- **Acceptance-based strategies**
		- **Mindfulness:** being aware of the present moment
		- **Distress tolerance:** managing a crisis or unpleasant situation without making it worse
			- Encouraging patients to practice coping skills to tolerate unbearable situations
			- Promoting alternatives to self-destructive behaviors
	- **Change-based strategies**
		- **Emotion regulation:** reducing reactivity to emotions
			- Encouraging patients to understand, identify, and label their emotions
			- Discouraging quick, intense reactions 
			- Teaching that emotions are helpful and adaptive 
			- Leaning into the opposite action
				- Act opposite to your urges, counteracting unhealthy behaviors 
		- **Interpersonal effectiveness:** maintaining healthy, mutually beneficial relationships
			- Teaching patients conflict resolution, active listening, etc
			- Prioritizing self-respect and needs
- **Problem-solving options**
	- **Solving the problem**
		- Using interpersonal effectiveness skills
	- **Feel better about the problem**
		- Using emotional regulation skills
	- **Tolerate the problem**
		- Using tolerance and mindfulness skills
	- **Stay miserable!!!**
		- Using NO skills <3

## Early psychotherapy
**Psychoanalysis**
- Developed by Sigmund Freud
- Grew out of an interest in hysteria (catatonia) and hypnosis
- **Psychoanalytic theory**
	- **Psychic determinism:** theory that every action has an unconscious meaning and purpose
	- **Instincts:** idea that life goals were driven by either life instincts or death instincts
	- **Personality structures:** theory that personality was composed of id, ego, and superego
		- **Id:** subconscious, inaccessible aspects of personality
		- **Ego:** rational, executive aspects of personality
		- **Superego:** set of ideals and goals inherited from parents

**Client-center therapy (CCT)**
- **Developed by Carl Rogers**
	- Arose after psychoanalysis and behaviorism became popular 
	- Rogers disliked Freud’s emphasis on therapist expertise and client destructiveness
	- He also disliked the black box approach of behaviorism
	- He aligned with a humanistic perspective instead
		- He believed that humans have an innate drive towards wholesome, meaningful self-actualization
- Aimed to help patients reach their full human potential
- **Theoretical basis**
	- **Phenomenological theory:** theory that humans have an urge to preserve the phenomenal self
		- **Phenomenal self:** fundamental understanding of oneself
		- **Growth potential:** strategy of encouraging a patient’s existing positive traits 
	- **Empathy:** mechanism of making others feel understood and respected
	- **Unconditional positive regard:** fundamental respect for patients as human beings
	- **Congruence:** practicing honesty and openness towards patients, even when difficult 
- **Process of CCT**
	- Therapists provide reflection statements to communicate with clients
	- Clients engage in self-exploration with minimal input from therapist
	- Therapists also provide encouragement and reassurance 
- **Issues**
	- Little empirical evidence for efficacy of CCT
	- Not typically used in clinical settings
	- Lack of multicultural perspective
