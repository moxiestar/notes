Question 1: Broadly speaking, what questions can we answer with a t-test?  
When the standard deviation for a population is not available, a t-test allows us to use a sample standard deviation to mathematically determine statistical significance. Essentially, a t-test provides the same information as a Z-test, but can be used when there is less access to information. T-tests produce three outputs: a p-value, which indicates statistical significance, a t-value, which indicates distance from the null hypothesis, and the degrees of freedom, the total number of independent variables. 

Question 2: How do we know if two samples are statistically different from each other? In other  
words, what statistics will tell us if our results are statistically significant?
If a t-test is performed and the resulting p-value is less than 0.05, the results are statistically significant. Performing t-tests has an effect on the degrees of freedom, with a degree being lost with each calculation. 

Question 3: What does it mean if two samples are significantly different from each other? What  
does it mean if they are not significantly different?  
If two samples are significantly different from each other, it indicates that they came from different populations. If they are not significantly different, they likely originated from the same population.
