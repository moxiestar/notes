Question 1: Broadly speaking, what kinds of questions can we test with a factorial ANOVA?  
When evaluating three or more statistical groups, ANOVAs can measure the differences between those groups. While one-way ANOVAs are useful when measuring the effects of a factor with three or more groups on the dependent variable, factorial ANOVAs allow us to compare the effects of two factors with three or more groups on the dependent variable. Factorial ANOVAs tell us not just whether the factors had an effect on the dependent variable, but also how large that effect was. Additionally, factorial ANOVAs can also answer questions about the interactions between factors and how those interactions affect the dependent variable. 

Question 2: How do you know if more than two samples are statistically different from each  
other? In other words, what statistics will tell you if your results are statistically significant?  
The p-value, seen below in the results, indicates statistical significance. If the p-value falls below .05, the effect seen is likely statistically significant (i.e., not due to chance). ANOVAs also involve F-tests, ratios of multiple variance values, which provide a way to compare means across multiple groups.

Question 3: Are there significant main effects of primary usage (content creation versus passive  
browsing) and sex (male versus female)?  
Yes, we see significant main effects of both primary usage (p = <.001, F = 12.96) and sex (<.001, F = 17.58).

Question 4: Is there an interaction between primary usage (content creation versus passive  
browsing) and sex (male versus female) on social media usage?  
Yes, there is an interaction effect (p = .026, F = 5.00). We can determine this from the p-value, but we can also observe this pattern by looking at the graph. There, we see that the two factors are not parallel, and if the lines continued along the x-axis, they would eventually cross to form an X. This shows that they are not mirroring each other in their behaviors.
