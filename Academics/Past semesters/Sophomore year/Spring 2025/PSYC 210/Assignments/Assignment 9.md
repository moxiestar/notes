Question 1: Broadly speaking, what kinds of questions can we answer with an ANOVA? 
When evaluating three or more statistical groups, ANOVAs can measure the differences between those groups. One-way ANOVAs, in particular, are useful when comparing a single dependent variable with three or more groups of a factor. By performing a one-way ANOVA, researchers can determine whether the groups, as well as the interactions between the groups, have an effect on the dependent variable. 

Question 2: How do you know if more than two samples are statistically different from each 
other? In other words, what statistics will tell you if your results are statistically significant? 
In the results of the one-way ANOVA (image below), a p-value is seen, indicating that a t-test was performed. As usual, if the p-value falls below .05, this indicates that the results are statistically significant. ANOVAs involve F-tests, ratios of multiple variance values, which provide a way to compare means across multiple groups.

Question 3: Are there differences between the three ethnic groups on drinking behavior? 
Yes. Within the descriptives, there is an obvious difference between the three ethnic groups. The mean for African Americans is 3.32 (SD = 2.53), and the mean for Asian Americans is 3.80 (SD = 1.70), while the mean for European Americans is 7.90 (SD = 4.62).

Question 4: Which group(s) drink more than the others? 
As stated above, the descriptives above show that European Americans (mean = 7.90) drink more than the other two groups. The p-value for the one-way ANOVA (p = .001) indicates that this result is significant, as does the Post-Hoc Test, which shows p-values of <.001 between European American and both Asian and African American.
