Question 1: Broadly speaking, what questions can we answer with a Z test? 
Z tests allow us to compare data across different distributions and measure standard deviations. By performing a Z test, we can see how different a given score is from the mean, and we can definitively tell whether or not a sample is significantly significant.

Question 2: How do we know if a sample is significant different from the population mean? In other words, what statistics will tell us if our results are statistically significant? 
If we perform a two-tailed Z test and find that our sample falls outside of (-1.96, 1.96), we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude that our data is statistically significant. In a one-tailed test, our sample must fall above 1.65.

Question 3: What does “statistically significant” mean? 
When a sample is statistically significant, it is different enough from the mean to fall outside the standard deviation, indicating that the results are not just due to chance.

Question 4: Is a randomly selected university’s mean ACT score of 20 (N = 10) really that different than the population mean (18.6) with a standard deviation of 5.9? 
No, it is not that different from the population mean - the score of 20 is not statistically significant, since the Z test statistic of 0.75 falls between (-1.96, 1.96). This score falls within the null distribution.

Question 5: Is UNC’s mean university ACT score of 30 (N = 10) really that different than the population mean (18.6) with a standard deviation of 5.9? 
Yes, UNC's score of 30 is statistically significant! The Z test statistic (6.11) falls outside of (-1.96, 1.96) and outside of the null distribution.
