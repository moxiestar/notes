Question 1: Broadly speaking, what does multiple regression and correlation analysis tell us?  
Multiple regression and correlation analysis can both examine and make predictions about relationships between variables. Using multiple regression allows researchers to compare the relationships between multiple independent variables and one dependent variable. Multiple regression shows which variables have an impact, as well as the magnitude of that impact. With correlation analysis, researchers can calculate the direction and strength of the relationship between variables. The correlational coefficient shows degree of association between the variables. Positive and negative correlation are associated with values going up or down related to variables. 

Question 2: How do you know multiple variables predict another? In other words, what statistics  
will tell you if your results are statistically significant? 
As usual, a t-test is being performed, which provides a p-value. If a p-value falls below 0.05, this indicates that the results are statistically significant, and the effect being seen is not due to chance. 

Question 3: Do age and IQ predict the reading ability?
Yes, both age and IQ predict reading ability. For both variables, the p-values of their slopes (0.006 for IQ and <0.001 for age) are significant. Notably, the standard metric of age (0.0671) is larger than that of IQ (0.0114). The adjusted R-squared value (0.80) indicates that 80% of differences in reading ability can be explained by differences in age and IQ. 

Question 4: Which of the predictors (age, IQ) is the stronger predictor of reading ability?
Age is the stronger predictor. This is reflected in its larger standardized slope (0.5842), which indicates a strong positive linear relationship between age and reading ability. Here, this value is labelled as the estimate.
