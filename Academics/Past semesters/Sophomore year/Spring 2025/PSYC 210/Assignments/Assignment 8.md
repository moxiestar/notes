Question 1: Broadly, what do confidence intervals tell us? 
Confidence intervals allow statisticians to estimate the bounds of a certain population parameter, such as mean or mode. They provide an upper and lower bound for the data points, as well as giving a percentage that indicates how much of the data will fall within the same range upon retesting. 

Question 2: Broadly, what do effect sizes tell us?  
Effect sizes provide context for the standardized size of a relationship. Cohen’s d, for example, is an effect size that measures the differences between two means, while an r-squared value represents the amount of variance in the dependent variable caused by the independent variable. 

Question 3: What do effect sizes do that hypothesis tests and the raw difference in means don't  
do?  
Raw differences, hypothesis testing, and effect sizes are all measures of the differences between data points. Out of the three, raw differences are the most superficial: they answer the question “Do these values look different?” Hypothesis tests, meanwhile, can tell researchers whether values are actually different. Effect sizes are the only measures that can identify the size of the difference.

Question 4: How do confidence intervals tell you whether your results are statistically  
significant?  
A confidence interval of 95% indicates that 95% of the population likely falls within certain bounds. If results outside of that 95% reliably occur, statistically significance is likely, and the null hypothesis can be rejected. Additionally, because an independent samples t-test is being performed here, the p-values will indicate significance.

For social comparison, Cohen’s d effect size is small (0.386), indicating that the difference between men and women is minor.
