1: Broadly speaking, what is the relation between population, sampling, and sampling distributions?
A population is a group of similar items, typically people, on which data are being gathered and statistical tests are being run. Sampling is the process of taking a subset of a population and analyzing those select datapoints. Every time a population is sampled, a mean is produced; when creating a sampling distribution, each one of the sample means is averaged to form a broader mean. 

2: How does the center (i.e., mean) of the sampling distribution of sample means compare with the population mean? Does this vary depending on the shape of the population distribution or the size of the samples? 
Interestingly, the population (16.71) mean lies right between the two sampling distributions means (16.72 for N=5 and 16.70 for N=25). It seems that a larger sample size produces a smaller mean. 

3: How does the shape of the sampling distribution compare with the shape of the population distribution? Does this vary depending on the shape of the population distribution or the size of the samples? 
The population distribution is messy, without anything resembling a normal distribution. The sampling distributions, on the other hand, are both normal, though the distribution for N=5 is much broader than the distribution for N=25; there is more deviation from the mean. 

4: How does the variability (i.e., standard deviation) of the sampling distribution compare with the variability of the population distribution? How does changing the size of the samples change the variability of the sampling distribution? What does this say about the effect of sample size on how far the mean of a single sample will be from the population mean? 
The standard deviation of the population distribution is much higher (8.97) than the SDs for either sampling distribution (4.02 for N=5 and 1.78 for N=25). A smaller sample size produces a lower standard deviation, with a leptokurtic curve. This indicates that, in a smaller sample, any given single sample mean will be much closer to the overall mean.
