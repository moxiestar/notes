Question 1: Broadly speaking, what does regression analysis do? What information does it give  
us? 
Regression analysis provides another way to compare the relationships between variables. In particular, it can predict the effects of a given variable on another variable. Linear regression calculates the line of best fit for a data set, indicating the overall pattern or trend of the data. 

Question 2: How do you know if one variable is associated with another? In other words, what  
statistics will tell you if your results are statistically significant?  
Since a t-test is being performed here, the p-value will indicate statistical significance. Between drinks and stress level, the two variables of interest, the p-value is 0.002, less than 0.05, confirming statistical significance. 

Question 3: Does stress level predict drinking?  
There is a positive linear relationship between stress level and drinking, as indicated by coefficient B, labelled here as the estimate. However, the inverse of the adjusted R squared, which represents variability, is high (88%), which means that stress likely does not predict drinking. 

Question 4: How strong is this relationship?  
The R squared value represents the percent overlap between the two variables; it shows how much variability in the dependent variable was caused by the independent variable. Here, the R squared is between 12-13%. Since the value is so low, it can be reasonably inferred that the relationship between stress and drinking is weak.
