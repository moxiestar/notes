**27.1.2025**
Research question design
- Issues to consider:
	- Operationalization
	- Confounding variables
	- Inter-rater reliability
	- Test-retest reliability
- Terms:
	- Variable: anything that can differ from person to person
	- Construct: abstract concept we are trying to measure
	- Operational definition: how we measure a concept
- Measurement types:
	- Self-report measures: asking the participant about their behaviors
		- Examples: surveys, interviews
		- Advantages: easy to use, questions can be asked directly, internal reliability can be checked by asking the same question different ways
		- Disadvantages: everybody lies, questionnaires are boring, self-reporting accurately is hard
	- Behavioral measures: observing the participant
		- Examples: actions, behaviors, behavioroid measures (willingness to perform a given behavior)
		- Advantages: less-biased than self-reporting, IRL as opposed to self-reporting
		- Disadvantages: difficult to design and conduct, difficult to specify the underlying psychological process, difficult to generalize
	- Physiological measures: measuring bodily processes
		- Examples: neuroimaging, heart rate
		- Advantages: none of the issues that come along with self-reporting
		- Disadvantages: expensive, mobility restrictions (natural behavior is altered), problems of reverse inference (difficult to specify original processes)
- Measurement scales: nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio
	- Nominal: categorical groups, not ranked
	- Ordinal: ranked categories, values are not equally spaced out
	- Interval: numerical categories, values are equally spaced out
	- Ratio: numerical categories, values are equally spaced out, true zero point, no negative values
- Types of variables: 
	- Independent variable
		- Variable that is manipulated by the researcher
		- Already known - predictor variable
	- Dependent variable
		- Variable that is measured by the researcher
		- Unknown - outcome variable
		- Expected to be influenced by the independent variable

**5.2.2025**
- Types of validity:
	- Construct validity: are the operational definitions good approximations for the conceptual variables?
		- Is our operational variable measuring what we think it is?
		- The operationalization should be based on our definition of the conceptual variables
	- Internal validity: does the independent variable actually cause the dependent variable?
		- Correlation between IV and DV ≠ causation, unless we have strong internal validity
		- 3 requirements to test internal validity:
			- Covariation: changes in one variable must correspond to changes in the other variable
			- Temporal sequence: the IV should occur before the DV
			- Lack of confounds: no alternative explanations for the results with the DV
				- Confound: something that covaries with the IV
	- External validity: is our result generalizable?
		- Do our results provide an accurate depiction of the real world?
		- Do the results apply across populations, or in real-world situations?
		- Greater internal validity -> lower external validity
			- The study becomes too specific
	- Statistical validity: did we use the correct statistics?
		- Are the statistics we used correct in our measurements?
- Validity = accuracy, reliability = precision

**17.2.2025**
Non-experimental designs
- No causal relationships can be proved
- No manipulation of variables
- No manipulation of participants' behavior
- No random assignment of participants
- Seeks to analyze behaviors but cannot explain them
- Can analyze correlations or overlaps between variables - so long as they are not causal
- Ideal for situations when the participants or variables cannot be manipulated
- Ideal for surveying a broader theme

Correlation DNE causation
- A relationship between two variables does not imply causation
- Spurious correlations: significant correlations between variables that have no true relationship

Types of non-experimental research
- Correlational research
	- Gathering information about a sample and testing for association between variables
	- Indication of strength of association between two variables
		- -1 (negative - one decreases as the other increases) to 1 (positive - both increase or decrease simultaneously)
		- 0 (no relationship)
- Observational research
	- Researchers observe and record subjects' behavior
	- Data collection without manipulation or interference
		- Data can be quantitative or qualitative
	- Examining data to find patterns
	- Types of observational research
		- Naturalistic study - observing subjects in their natural environments
		- Participant study - becoming part of the group of interest 
			- The researcher can either be disguised or undisguised
		- Structured observation - controlling the environment to study specific behaviors
- Single variable research
	- Designed to study a specific property within a large sample
- Case study
	- In-depth analyses of a particular person or group
	- Ideal for unusual events or medical conditions
	- Often used to study brain damage across subjects
- Archival research
	- Examining existing research or data to test a hypothesis
	- Missing data is common - leads to holes in research

Quasi-experimental designs
- Research designs where researchers only have partial control over the IV and cannot randomly assign groups
- In between a true experiment and a non-experimental design
- Higher internal validity than non-experimental designs
- Useful when:
	- Variables cannot be manipulated
	- Participants cannot be randomly assigned
- Benefits: still manipulating IV enough to affect DV
- Problems: introduce confounding variables due to lack of manipulation

Types of quasi-experimental designs:
- One group designs:
	- Used when it’s not possible to have a control group
	- The only group is the experimental group
	- Types of one group designs:
		- One-group post-test only design: only tests DV after the manipulation
			- Issues: 
				- Unclear what effect the manipulation actually had
				- Weakest/worst type of quasi-experiment
		- One-group pre-test-post-test design: tests DV before and after manipulation
			- Not an experimental design because there’s no control group
		- Interrupted time-series design: manipulation that occurs between measurements on a group
			- Time-series: measurements taken over a period of time
- Nonequivalent group designs:
	- Used when random assignment isn’t possible
	- Types of nonequivalent group designs:
		- Post-test only nonequivalent groups design: only tests after manipulation
		- Pre-test-post-test nonequivalent groups design: tests before and after manipulation
		- Interrupted time-series design with nonequivalent groups: multiple pre- and post-tests occur
	- Can be improved by switching or randomizing the order of the testing 

-------------------------------------------------------------------

Factorial designs
- Experiments with multiple independent variables
- Each level of one IV is combined with the other IV levels in all possible ways
- Number of total conditions is the product of all the levels (ie, 2 x 2  = 4)
- Between-subjects and within-subjects designs must be assigned for each variable
	- Between-subjects factorial design: 4 groups, 4 conditions
	- Within-subjects factorial design: 1 group, 4 conditions
	- Mixed factorial design: some variables are within, some are between
- Benefits:
	- Allows researchers to test each IV in different environments
	- Isolates the effects of IVs
- Types of factorial research designs
	- Factorial experiment with all manipulated variables
	- Factorial experiment with some manipulated variables and some not
	- Factorial non-experimental study with no manipulated variables
- Graphing factorial designs
	- Bar graphs or line graphs
	- X-axis: one of the IVs
	- Y-axis: DV
	- Other IVs are shown as bars or lines
- Interpreting factorial designs
	- Main effect
		- Overall effect of one IV, averaging across all IVs
	- Interaction effect
		- Effect of one IV depends on the levels of another IV
		- Spreading interaction: effects of an IV exists at one level of the other IV but not at the other IV
		- Crossover interaction: effects of each IV are opposite are different levels of the other IV

**17.3.2025**
Hypothesis testing
- Research hypotheses - prediction based on previous literature
- Statistical hypotheses - mathematical prediction based on analyses of results
- Steps:
	- 1. Define the null and alternative hypotheses
		- Alternative hypotheses are based off of research hypotheses
		- Prediction of a relationship between behaviors/variables
		- Null hypothesis indicates that results have been caused by chance
		- No directionality in alternative/null testing
	- 2. Choose a value for distinguishing the two hypotheses
		- Choosing a cut-off point for the chance of Type I error to be small enough
		- Standards in psych are 0.05, 0.01, and 0.001
	- 3. Design the experiment and choose statistical tests
		- Considering the operationalizations of the variables
	- 4. Collect the data and run analyses
	- 5. Make a decision, using the previously chosen value
		- If the p-value is below the chosen point, reject the null hypothesis
		- If the p-value is greater than or equal to the chosen point, fail to reject the null hypothesis

Statistical testing
- Sampling error - variability of means from sample to sample
- Effects seen in the sample should reflect the population and not be caused by sampling error
- Null hypothesis ($H_0$): results are caused by chance
- Alternative hypothesis ($H_1$): results are caused by a true relationship/variable
- Null hypothesis testing: 
	- Data is “innocent until proven guilty” - we assume the null hypothesis is true
	- We are testing to see if the null hypothesis can be rejected
	- Terminology: cannot “accept” the null hypothesis - either reject, or fail to reject
- Four options:
![[Screenshot 2025-02-05 at 3.12.22 PM.png]]
- Inferential statistics:
	- Mechanism to minimize errors
	- Tests provide p-values, which represent statistical significance
	- Chances of getting a Type I error are less than or equal to the p-value
		- p < 0.05 = significance
		- p > 0.05 = sampling error
	- Below 0.05, a lower p-value does not indicate greater significance
	- Significant results do not prove the alternative hypothesis
	- Replication must be performed, and even then, **nothing is ever proven**

**7.4.2025**
Validity
- Sampling bias
	- An unrepresentative sample is used in research
	- Caused by faulty sampling methods
	- Can be fixed with probability sampling
- Non-response bias
	- Bias caused by a difference between those who participated in a survey vs those who did not 
	- Only an issue if the two groups differ in the variables of interest
- Measurement effect
	- Studying people will change their behaviors
	- Results will not be reflective of true behavior
- General concerns
	- Attrition
		- Number of subjects who failed to complete the study
		- Large concern for longer studies with multiple segments
	- Homogeneous attrition 
		- Equal level of attrition across all conditions
		- Concern if the groups that dropped out were part of an important population
	- Heterogeneous attrition
		- Attrition rates differ across two groups
		- Concern if the benefits of random assignment are erased
- Statistical validity
	- Do the tests performed line up with the experiment?
	- Having too few participants can cause issues
	- Poorly designed questions

**14.4.2025**
Peer-reviewing
- Steps:
	1) Paper is submitted to a journal
	2) Internal review by the editor
	3) External review by other researchers
	4) External reviewers send comments to the editor
	5) The editor makes a final decision
- Issues:
	- Process is slow and often biased
	- Some reviewers are better than others
	- Comments are not always helpful
	- Errors in the process can and do occur

Research issues:
- P-hacking: manipulating data to get a significant p-value
	- Examples: 
		- Limiting participant pool size to get a significant result
		- Testing a variety of variables and only reporting those that are significant
		- Excluding participants that skew the results
		- Explicitly changing data to achieve significance
- HARKing: hypothesizing after results are known

*Reproducibility is KEY*
- Replications can be direct (exact replications) or conceptual (methods differ)
	- Direct replication does not provide new knowledge, while conceptual replication cannot definitively prove or disprove the original results
- File drawer problem: many null results remained unpublished
