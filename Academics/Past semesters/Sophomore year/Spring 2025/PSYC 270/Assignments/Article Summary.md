1. Citation
   Spence, A., Beasley, K., Gravenkemper, H., Hoefler, A., Ngo, A., Ortiz, D., & Campisi, J. (2020). Social media use while listening to new material negatively affects short-term memory in college students. _Physiology & Behavior, 227_(2020), 113-172. [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physbeh.2020.113172](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physbeh.2020.113172)

2. Full summary
   This study sought to determine whether exposure to social media would affect retention rates in university students studying course material. All participants (n = 45) were aged 18-24 and completed the Logical Memory Immediate Recall (LM 1) section of the Weschler Memory Scale IV. Participants were instructed not to eat, drink, or exercise for an hour before evaluation. During testing, new information was presented in an auditory format, and students were split into three groups: a control group that took the LM 1 without any distractions, a group that took the LM 1 while viewing Instagram, and a group that took the LM 1 after viewing Instagram. Statistical analyses indicated that Instagram had a negative effect on memory, particularly in the group that viewed Instagram while being presented with new material. The study confirmed the theory that students should avoid using social media in academic settings. 

3. Brief summary
   In this study, researchers used the Logical Memory Immediate recall section of the Weschler Memory Scale IV to test participants' recall of new material during and after exposure to social media. Results clearly showed the detrimental effect of using Instagram while trying to learn new information.

   Before reading the article, I assumed any exposure to social media would have negative effects on retention. However, I learned that viewing social media after studying has minimal effects on academic performance. 
   b. Do you agree with the claims the researchers are making? 
   Yes, I know from experience that their claims are totally founded.
   c. What major limitations exist? What unanswered questions remain within this area of research? Do you have any ideas for a specific research question/s that follow up on one or more of these limitations or unanswered questions? 
   The study size is small (only 45 participants), and only 9 of these participants were men. Additionally, many studies examining social media and academics focus on college students; few look into other age groups, such as high school or graduate students. Personally, I'd be curious about a potential difference in the effects of social media across different departments: for example, do pre-medical students struggle with Instagram-induced issues as much as English majors?
