**Overview**
Basic research question: how does social media use affect college students?
Hypothesis: exposure to videos on Instagram and Tiktok will lead to lower performance on a memory evaluation
Full research question: how does exposure to short-form social media content affect short-term memory retention and recall in college students, compared to no exposure?
Title: Just Keep Scrolling: How Exposure to Short-Form Social Media Content Affects Retention and Recall in University Students
Design: survey - multiple choice questions about a short story
- They are then asked to recall both the story and the videos
- Control: pts read a short story, then take a two minute break
- They are then asked to recall the story
- All pts report screen time
Idea: 
As shown in several studies (Chossi et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2024; Spence et al., 2020), viewing short-form social media content while learning new information is highly detrimental to retention. 

**Articles**
*Short-Form Videos Degrade Our Capacity to Retain Intentions: Effect of Context Switching On Prospective Memory*
- Hypotheses: does use of social media while completing a prospective memory task impair students' ability to perform the task
- Methods:
	- 60 participants
	- Measures: lexical decision task, prospective memory task
	- Experimental groups: participants were assigned to one of three social media groups (or the control group) based on the app they spent the most time on 
		- TikTok group: participants completed round 1 of tasks, took a 10 minute rest break and viewed their TikTok feed, then completed round 2 of tasks
		- Twitter group: participants completed round 1 of tasks, took a 10 minute rest break and viewed their Twitter feed, then completed round 2 of tasks
		- YouTube group: participants completed round 1 of tasks, took a 10 minute rest break and watched a 10 minute video from a prepared playlist, then completed round 2 of tasks
	- Control group: participants completed round 1 of tasks, took a 10 minute rest break with no distractions, then completed round 2 of tasks
- Main findings:
	- Use of TikTok significantly impairs students' ability to perform short-term procedural memory tasks
	- Twitter and YouTube did not have the same effect
	- **Viewing short-form social media in between completing tasks is detrimental to the student's ability to complete the task**
- Citation: Chiossi, F., Haliburton, L., Ou, C., Butz, A. M., & Schmidt, A. (2023). Short-Form Videos Degrade Our Capacity to Retain Intentions: Effect of Context Switching On Prospective Memory. _Proceedings of the 2023 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems_, 1–15. https://doi.org/10.1145/3544548.3580778

*Association between problematic social media use and memory performance in a sample of Lebanese adults: The mediating effect of anxiety, depression, stress and insomnia*
- Hypotheses: do psychiatric disorders and social media addication have a negative effect on memory
- Methods:
	- 466 participants
	- Measures: Memory Performance Scale of the Memory Awareness Rating Scale, Social Media Use Disorder Scale, Hamilton Anxiety Scale, Beirut Distress Scale, Lebanese Insomnia Scale
	- Groups: all groups were screened with the same measurements
- Main findings:
	- Excessive social media use was correlated with lower performance on memory evaluations and higher anxiety levels
- Citation: Dagher, M., Farchakh, Y., Barbar, S., Haddad, C., Akel, M., Hallit, S., & Obeid, S. (2021). Association between problematic social media use and memory performance in a sample of Lebanese adults: The mediating effect of anxiety, depression, stress and insomnia. _Head & Face Medicine_, _17_(1), 6. https://doi.org/10.1186/s13005-021-00260-8

*Effect of Social Networking Addiction on Memory Functioning and Aggression Among University Students: Mediating Role of Sleep Quality*
- Hypotheses: is social media addiction correlated with worse sleep and higher aggression; are male students more addicted to social media than female students
- Methods: 
	- 405 participants
	- Four measures: Social Networking Addiction Scale, Aggression Questionnaire, Sleep Quality Scale, Multifactorial Memory Questionnaire
	- Groups: all groups were screened with the same measurements
- Main findings: 
	- Male and female students were equally addicted
	- Negative correlation between high quality of memory and poor quality of sleep
	- Positive correlation between poor quality of sleep and aggression
	- Lower social media use indicates higher quality of memory
	- **Use of social media during or after learning new information leads to worse memory of the information**
- Citation: Khan, M. J., Khan, B., Ismail, F., Khan, A., Ahmad, A., & Sagheer, S. (2024). Effect of Social Networking Addiction on Memory Functioning and Aggression Among University Students: Mediating Role of Sleep Quality. _Pakistan Journal of Psychological Research_, _39_(4), 813–831. https://doi.org/10.33824/PJPR.2024.39.4.44

*Hyperconnected: Children and adolescents on social media. The TikTok phenomenon*
- Main findings:
	- Survey of effects of social media on youth
		- Social media has changed the way we communicate
		- On average, babies first appear online at 6 months of age
		- Excessive social media use has significant side effects: physical lethargy, obesity, insomnia, worsened academic performance, as well as a variety of cognitive issues
		- Minors on TikTok often register as being 16 or older, despite their real age, as this gives them access to TikTok's For You Page - this also often exposes them to dangerous or shocking content
		- A variety of studies have highlighted the negative effects TikTok has on adolescents
	- Recommendations for TikTok use
		- Restricting the content that adolescents can see, as well as ensuring that children below 13 do not use TikTok — though how they would enforce this is unclear
		- Providing social media and internet education for children, so they can better understand the decisions they make online
		- Controlling how long children view TikTok (up to 2 hours), as well as when they view it
- Citation: Pedrouzo, S. B., & Krynski, L. (2023). Hyperconnected: Children and adolescents on social media. The TikTok phenomenon. _Archivos Argentinos De Pediatria_, _121_(4), e202202674. https://doi.org/10.5546/aap.2022-02674.eng

*Research on Adolescents Regarding the Indirect Effect of Depression, Anxiety, and Stress between TikTok Use Disorder and Memory Loss*
- Hypotheses: is there a mediating effect on memory loss between excess TikTok use and depression or anxiety
- Methods: 
	- 3036 participants
	- Measures: Smartphone Addiction Scale, Depression Anxiety Stress Scale 21, digit spans
	- Groups: all groups were screened with the same measurements
- Main findings: 
	- Excessive TikTok use is correlated with with cognitive impairments such as memory loss, depression, and anxiety
	- Female students showed higher use of TikTok
	- Male students showed more cognitive impairments
- Citation: Sha, P., & Dong, X. (2021). Research on Adolescents Regarding the Indirect Effect of Depression, Anxiety, and Stress between TikTok Use Disorder and Memory Loss. _International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health_, _18_(16), Article 16. https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph18168820

*Social media use while listening to new material negatively affects short-term memory in college students*
- Hypotheses: does exposure to social media affect retention rates in university students studying new course material
- Methods: 
	- 45 participants
	- Measures: Weschler Memory Scale IV 
	- Experimental groups: new information is presented to both groups in auditory format
		- Group 1 takes the Weschler while viewing Instagram
		- Group 2 takes the Weschler after viewing Instagram
	- Control group: new information is presented to the group in auditory format
		- Group takes the Weschler without any distractions
- Main findings:
	- Statistical analyses indicated that Instagram had a negative effect on memory, **particularly in the group that viewed Instagram while being presented with new material**
	- Study confirmed the theory that students should avoid using social media in academic settings
- Citation: Spence, A., Beasley, K., Gravenkemper, H., Hoefler, A., Ngo, A., Ortiz, D., & Campisi, J. (2020). Social media use while listening to new material negatively affects short-term memory in college students. _Physiology & Behavior_, _227_, 113172. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physbeh.2020.113172

*How did you hear the news? The role of traditional media, social media, and personal communication in flashbulb memory*
- Hypotheses: do people remember news they learned from social media as well as news they learned from traditional media
- Methods:
	- 314 participants
	- Definition: flashbulb memories are intense, long-term memories of important or shocking events
	- Measures: in person test, online questionnaire that measured vividness of memory and confidence in memory (i.e., how much the participants trusted the source)
	- Groups: participants were asked about the assassination of Osama bin Laden right after his death
		- Participants were then interviewed again online, at the following intervals:
			- 7 days
			- 42 days
			- 224 days
			- 365 days
		- Participants were sorted into three groups based on their source media:
			- Traditional
			- Social
			- Personal
- Main findings:
	- Source (traditional or social media) does not affect long-term memory retention
	- Initial reports showed that traditional media showed the highest vividness and trust, but over time, the results levelled out
	- Social media was associated with the lowest level of vividness
- Citation: Talarico, J. M., Kraha, A., Self, H., & Boals, A. (2019). How did you hear the news? The role of traditional media, social media, and personal communication in flashbulb memory. _Memory Studies_, _12_(4), 359–376. https://doi.org/10.1177/1750698017714835

*Mental workload variations during different cognitive office tasks with social media interruptions*
- Main findings:
	- 33 studies were surveyed
	- Unsurprisingly, interruptions that took place during critical moments, especially in medical settings, slowed participants down and impaired their ability to refocus on the task at hand
	- Interruptions had a particularly negative effect on students
	- The effect of an interruption depends on when it occurs
- Citation: Zahmat Doost, E., & Zhang, W. (2023). Mental workload variations during different cognitive office tasks with social media interruptions. _Ergonomics_, _66_(5), 592–608. https://doi.org/10.1080/00140139.2022.2104381
