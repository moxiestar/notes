## **Are You Still Watching? 
How Exposure to Short-Form Social Media Content Affects Recall in University Students**
## **Abstract (NEW PAGE)**
In modern life, social media is almost ubiquitous. Recent research (Chossi et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2024; Sha & Dong, 2021; Spence et al., 2020) shows that use of social media may contribute to a number of cognitive issues, including depression, anxiety, and worsened recall. In our study, we investigated how exposure to short-form video content on social media affects university students. Participants read an AI-generated story based on true events; after completing the story, the experimental group viewed either Instagram Reels or TikTok for three minutes, while the control group took a break free of distractions. To measure the effects of social media usage, we had participants take a short, multiple-choice quiz, and used their scores as a marker of retention. We expected to see worsened retention in the experimental group, but our results were inconclusive, with no clear effects of social media usage on recall. Further research will be necessary to determine how memory and exposure to social media are correlated. 
## **Introduction (NEW PAGE)**
Since the dawn of the internet in the late 1960s (Leiner et al., 2009), digital media has reshaped society. It has restructured the way people work, the way they think, the way they study, and, perhaps more than anything else, the way they communicate. Six Degrees, released in 1997, was the first modern social media platform that allowed users to have the entire social world at their fingertips (Pedrouzo & Krynski 2023). In the decades following the creation of Six Degrees, a host of other social media platforms have appeared online, including Facebook, Myspace, Reddit, YouTube, Twitter, and Instagram. Since then, social media has become the de facto mode of communication for the younger generations: recent reports (“Global,” 2024) have shown that daily social media use has gone up from 90 minutes in 2012 to 143 minutes in 2024. 

Though digital communication has many benefits, it also has significant downsides. Shannon et al. (2022) found a correlation between excessive social media use and mental health issues such as depression and anxiety, as did several other similar studies (Khan et al., 2024; Sha & Dong, 2021). Social media is harmful in the classroom, too. As demonstrated in several studies (Chossi et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2024; Spence et al., 2020), viewing short-form social media content while learning new information is highly detrimental to retention. Due to the damaging nature of social media and its prevalence among young adults, understanding its effect on students is crucial. This study seeks to examine whether viewing short-form, video-based social media content after learning new information has a negative impact on recall. We hypothesize that we will find a correlation between social media use and poorer performance on memory evaluations. 

**Effects of Social Media on Cognition and Mental Health**
The detriments of social media use, both psychological and physical, have been studied across a variety of settings. Adverse effects include physical lethargy, obesity, insomnia, cognitive issues, and worsened academic performance (Pedruozo & Krynski, 2023). A recent survey of Chinese high school students (_n_ = 3036) focused on the effects of TikTok, reporting a mediating effect on memory loss between excessive TikTok use and psychiatric issues such as depression and anxiety (Sha & Dong, 2021). Similar results were observed in a study of Lebanese adults (Dagher et al., 2021), where social media use was strongly correlated with higher levels of anxiety. Social media use has also been proven to be associated with increased aggression (Khan et al., 2024). Although there is a clear negative correlation between social media use and mental wellbeing, social media is so ubiquitous, with almost 5 billion users (Montag et al., 2024), that reducing exposure is difficult. Additionally, there is no precedent to the internet age, and many of the long-term dangers of social media exposure for children and adolescents remain unknown.

**Memory Loss Associated With Social Media Use**
Further studies have narrowed their focus to examine the effects of social media in academic settings. In their article “Short-Form Videos Degrade Our Capacity to Retain Intentions: Effect of Context Switching On Prospective Memory,” Chiossi et al. (2023) predicted that the use of social media would impair students’ ability to recall and perform cognitive tasks. Participants were assigned to one of three groups — a TikTok group, a Twitter group, and a YouTube group — each of which completed a round of tasks, took a ten-minute break to view their app of choice, and then completed another round of tasks. A fourth group, the control, completed the first round of tasks, took a ten-minute break with no distractions, and then continued to the second round. Chiossi et al. found that the TikTok group performed significantly worse on the procedural memory task than the other three groups, with a dramatic uptick in the rate of incorrect answers (2023). We sought to implement a similar design, choosing to analyze only one app instead of three. 

Spence et al. (2020) designed a similar experiment, hypothesizing that exposure to social media would lower retention rates in university students studying new course material. Participants were asked to listen to a short story before being tested on the details. One experimental group listened to the story while viewing Instagram, another did so after viewing Instagram, and a control group was exposed without any distractions. The groups were then evaluated with the Wechsler Memory Scale IV. Out of the two experimental groups, both showed lower retention than the control group, in particular the group that viewed Instagram while listening to the new material (2020). The study confirmed that browsing Instagram harmed memory and indicated that students should limit their use of social media in academic settings. A review of 33 studies focusing on social media interruptions in the workplace reinforced these findings, reporting that the effect of an interruption depends on when it occurs (Zahmat & Zhang, 2023). Additionally, Zahmat and Zhang’s review showed that, compared to other participants, cognitive disruptions had a particularly negative effect on students (2023). We hope to replicate these results. 

## **Current Study Design and Hypotheses**
As previously stated, our study hopes to evaluate the effects of short-form social media content on retention rates in university students. Other studies, several of which are outlined above, have focused on TikTok and its negative psychological impact on its users, from increased rates of depression and anxiety to worsened academic performance and recall ability. However, due to the recent, though short-lived, TikTok ban and subsequent issues with accessibility, we initially planned to center our study on Instagram Reels. In the final stages of planning, we chose to let participants use either Instagram or TikTok, since some students do not have access to Instagram or may use it only rarely. A foundational study on social media usage (Sha & Dong, 2021) focused on high school students, while several other recent articles (Chiossi et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2024; Spence et al., 2020) used college students as their participants. We aimed to build on that research by making our study specific to short-form video content and university students.

In our study, participants in an experimental group will read a short story, and then scroll through their feed on Instagram Reels for three minutes. A control group will read the same story, but take a three-minute break with no distractions. Both groups will then be asked to recall details about the story in a short survey composed of multiple-choice questions. As an added data point, participants will be asked to report their screen time, as well as their daily Instagram use. 

Prior research demonstrates that viewing social media during or after exposure to new material harms students’ retention. We expect to see these findings replicated in our study. We hypothesize that the experimental group will not be able to fully remember the short story, as the three minutes of exposure to Instagram Reels will have damaged their ability to form new memories. We predict that the control group, on the other hand, will be able to accurately recall details about the story. If our hypothesis is correct, our study will offer further proof that social media use is harmful in academic environments and should be restricted for better scholastic performance. Given the current research on this topic, one could reasonably claim that the harms of social media outweigh the benefits; we hope that our study will help to prove this claim.

## **Methods
Participants**
We recruited a sample of convenience (N  = 50) from undergraduate students at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. The original sample size was N = 72, but 12 participants were excluded for not completing the entire survey or incorrectly answering the attention check. The participants were primarily female (74.0%), with the remaining participants identifying as male (26.0%) and within the ages of 18 to 23 (M = 19.6, SD = 1.13). Our participants were also asked about their racial identity (White = 2.0%, Asian = 14.0%, Asian and White = 4.0%, Black or African American = 8.0%, White = 64.0%, White and Hispanic/Latino = 4.0%, and Hispanic/Latino = 4.0%). Additionally, our study included other demographic information, such as academic year (first-year = 30.0%, sophomore = 40.0%, junior = 22.0%, senior = 8.0%) and their majors. The primary majors of our participants were Psychology (B.A. and B.S.), Neuroscience, and Biology, though majors widely varied across our sample. Lastly, participants recorded their overall screen time based on the screen time reports on their device (M = 5.74, SD = 2.54), and their screen time specific to social media (M = 2.96, SD = 2.16). 

The participants were recruited in person, as well as via class forums, social media, and other messaging apps. No incentives were provided to complete the story, but potential participants were given information about the premise of the study (conducted for a research methods course for psychology majors), and the time it would take (about 10 to 15 minutes) before beginning the survey. The participants gave consent for taking part in the study and were able to withdraw their participation at any point, free of consequences. All undergraduate students at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill were eligible, and the only requirement was completing the survey in its entirety, including the attention check.  

## **Materials** 
**Questionnaire**
To measure the effects of social media use on memory, we designed a short, three-part experiment in Qualtrics. The survey first gathered demographic information about participants, such as their age, academic year, course of study, race, and gender, as well as statistics about their daily screen time and social media usage (see Appendix B). Participants were then instructed to read an ΑΙ-generated, non-fiction story titled “The Bizarre Case of the Exploding Toads” (see Appendix C). AI was used to generate the story, ensuring that it was entirely original and minimizing the risk that participants had encountered the material before. After reading the story, participants were randomly assigned, using Qualtrics’ software, to one of two groups. The control group was instructed to take a three-minute, distraction-free break, facilitated by a timer embedded in the survey, while the experimental group received the same timer but was instructed to scroll on TikTok or Instagram Reels using their own devices. We included the three-minute break in alignment with prior research, which similarly used short snippets of time between information exposure and memory assessment (Chiossi et al. 2023; Spence et al. 2020). 

Following the three-minute break, participants completed an 11-item AI-generated multiple-choice quiz assessing factual details from the story to measure short-term memory (see Appendix D). After data collection, participants’ quiz responses were evaluated using an accuracy score, which served as our dependent variable. This score reflected memory quality and each participant’s ability to accurately recall information from the story. Following data cleaning, all results were then analyzed using Jamovi to examine the effects of social media use and other variables on memory recall.

## **Design and Procedure** 
**Design** 
This experiment sought to test the effects of social media on retention, and we predicted that higher social media use would lead to lower performance on memory evaluations. To test our hypothesis, we used an experimental, between-subjects design, with participants being randomly assigned to either a control or an experimental group. During our experiment, we had the participants read a story, take a three-minute break, and then complete a multiple-choice quiz about the story, testing their memory of salient details. Our predictor variable was social media use, and we had two levels of the variable in the experiment: one where participants used social media and one where participants did not. This variable was operationalized by having the experimental group scroll through social media for a set amount of time after reading the story. The control group took a distraction-free break. Our dependent variable was retention rate; this was operationalized as the number of correct answers on the quiz. To ensure that all participants were fully reading the questions before answering, we had an “attention check” question, which instructed participants to answer “Do not agree.” Data from any participants who answered otherwise was discarded. 

**Procedure** 
This experiment was executed using Qualtrics, an online digital software that allows for questionnaire randomization and the ability to manipulate various survey designs. Participants were encouraged to complete the survey using a computer for relative ease of viewing. The first section of the survey provided a brief synopsis of the purpose, benefits, and risks of the experiment, followed by a request for consent (see Appendix A). This required individuals to type the word “yes” if they gave consent, and otherwise were instructed to close out the window. Afterward, we collected demographics, which included age, gender, race/ethnicity, academic year, majors/minors, and average daily screen time. 

Subsequently, we provided all participants with an AI-generated short story that was based on true events. We asked the participants to read the story thoroughly. After reading the story, we implemented the experimental design. With the use of Qualtrics’ randomization feature, the participants were randomly assigned to one of two scenarios. Half of the participants were placed in the control group, in which they were directed to engage in a three-minute break with no distractions after reading the story (N  = 25). A 3-minute timer was embedded in the survey and remained visible to the participants. The other half of the participants were placed in the experimental group and were told to scroll on social media for 3 minutes after reading the story, particularly TikTok or Instagram (N  = 25). Despite the assigned experimental condition, both groups were instructed to return to the survey immediately once the timer went off and proceeded to the next page of the survey. 

The next page of the survey asked the participants to complete an 11-question survey regarding the short story they had read just 3 minutes prior. 10 of the 11 questions were based directly on the story and were used to ascertain their level of retention. The remaining question was an attention check that asked participants to respond with a particular answer. This question was placed in the middle of the quiz, and was used to identify any inattentive participants whose answers may skew our results. After the quiz, participants were all provided with a debrief that concisely explained the purpose of our research (see Appendix E). 

## **Results**
**Primary Analysis** 
Our primary hypothesis was that social media use would impair the participants’ ability to accurately recall the information they were presented earlier in the short story, before scrolling on social media. Before conducting our primary analysis, we analyzed the descriptive statistics and frequencies of the participants’ accuracy scores. The scores were generated by listing the number of correct answers out of the total 11 questions, including all participants answering the attention check correctly (3 = 2.0%, 4 = 2.0%, 5 = 10.0%, 6 = 2.0%, 7 = 4.0%, 8 = 10.0%, 9 = 10.0%, 10 = 26.0%, 11 = 34.0%; see Figure 1.) To test whether the mean levels of the accuracy score differed between the social media and break groups, we conducted an independent-samples t-test. Results of the two-tailed t-test indicated no significant difference between the social media (M = 9.04, SD = 2.32) and no distractions groups (M = 9.13, SD = 2.17), t(df) = 48.0, p = .893. 

**Other Inferential Statistics**
As part of our analysis, we asked the participants to report their overall and social media screen time. To determine if the levels of social media screen time predicted the accuracy of the comprehension test, we conducted a regression analysis. Results of the regression analysis indicated that social media screen time was not a significant predictor of accuracy (b = -.1204, SE = .18, t(df) = -.67, p = .508, R2 = .122). 

In addition to analyzing the difference between the social media use and no distractions groups, we also asked the participants to report their academic year to understand whether there was a relationship between year and accuracy. To determine whether the effect of group (social media versus no distractions) on accuracy score differed as a function of academic year, we conducted a 2 (group: social media, no distractions) x 4 (academic year: freshman, sophomore, junior, senior) two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). We did not find a significant main effect of academic year such as that those in the social media condition had the same level of quiz accuracy (M = 9.04, SD = 2.32) as those in the no distractions group (M = 9.13, SD = 2.17), F(1, 48) = .018, p = .893. Finally, there was a significant interaction between group and year on accuracy, F(1, 3) = 4.06, p = .013, η2 = .209. When participants were in the junior group, there was higher quiz score accuracy in the no distractions condition than in the social media condition. However, when participants were in the senior group, there was higher quiz accuracy in the social media condition than in the no distractions condition. When participants were in the freshman or sophomore group, there were no differences (see **Figure 2**). 

Secondly, to determine whether the effect of group (social media versus no distractions) on accuracy score differed as a function of gender, we conducted a 2 (group: social media, no distractions) x 2 (gender: female, male) two-way ANOVA. We did not find a significant main effect of gender such that those in the social media condition reported the same level of accuracy (M = 9.04, SD = 2.32) as those in the no distractions group (M = 9.13, SD = 2.17), F(1, 1) = 3.75, p = .059, though this p-value approached significance. Finally, there was no significant interaction between group and gender on accuracy, F(1,1) = 1.10, p = .300. 

## **Discussion**
In this study, we measured the effects of short-form social media on retention in university students. We hoped to see the results of previous research replicated in our study, but we did not see any clear effects, either negative or positive, of social media on short-term retention. Our main hypothesis was that our experimental group, who viewed Instagram after exposure to new information, would perform more poorly on a quiz about that information. However, we found no significant effect of social media usage on accuracy rates among participants. We did see an interaction between group and year on accuracy, such that participants who reported themselves as juniors performed better in the no distractions condition than in the social media condition. Participants who reported as seniors, on the other hand, scored higher in the social media condition than in the no distractions condition. These results revealed a link between academic year and retention rates, which we had not expected. 

Prior research (Almarzouki et al., 2022; Chiossi et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2024; Sha & Dong, 2021; Spence et al., 2020) outlined the harmful effects of social media in academic settings. In particular, many explored the overlap between the psychological and memory detriments experienced by those who overuse social media (Khan et al., 2024; Sha & Dong, 2021). Others (Almarzouki et al., 2022; Chiossi et al., 2023; Sharifian et al., 2021; Spence et al., 2020; Tamir et al., 2018) focused more exclusively on memory. Since we were investigating participants in an academic context, we chose to examine only the effects of social media on memory. Chiossi et al. (2023) conducted an experiment where students viewed social media in between task completion before performing a memory evaluation. Spence et al. (2020) published a similar study where university students listened to a short story either while or after browsing Instagram; they were then quizzed on details about the story. In both studies, social media usage significantly worsened participants’ retention rates. We were unable to replicate their results in our own research, but this may have been due to the limitations discussed below. 

Several conclusions can be drawn from our findings. First, the fact that we were unable to replicate prior results raises questions about the reproducibility of social media research. Within Chiossi et al. (2023), the Twitter and TikTok groups scrolled through their native feeds, while the YouTube group viewed a pre-compiled selection of videos. Spence et al. (2020) allowed participants in the experimental groups to scroll through their own Instagram feeds, but others (Dagher et al., 2021; Khan et al., 2024) questioned participants about their social media use via surveys. Different social media feeds, pre-compiled or organic, will have different effects on participants. Second, though we were unable to find a link between social media usage and retention, this topic will continue to be relevant, as the internet continues to be a part of daily life, especially with the popularity of technology like artificial intelligence. It is critical to further investigate the effects of social media on cognitive and psychological health. 

Our study had a number of limitations, most notably that our internal validity was low. Since we used an online survey to administer our experiment, we had little control over participants’ behavior. Though we implemented an attention check (see Appendix D, Question 5), without being able to monitor participants in person, we cannot know whether they fully followed the instructions. As a result, we could not guarantee that there were no confounds or that there was a direct temporal sequence from social media usage to memory evaluation. Additionally, we allowed participants to browse either TikTok or Instagram; this variability could have impacted the results. Ideally, in future research, we would limit browsing to a specific app or selection of content in order to reduce variability. Lastly, the multiple-choice quiz presented to participants at the end of the survey may have been too easy, since the majority of participants performed extremely well (see Figure 1). Designing a more complex or challenging quiz would ensure that participants were practicing recall as opposed to simply relying on common sense. 

As further research on internet usage continues, it will be essential to pinpoint how the cognitive issues caused by social media arise. The critical limitation of our study was its low internal validity; in future research, we hope to focus more specifically on the effects of a single app or software on a student population. Additionally, though the psychological issues correlated with social media usage have been explored, further studies investigating those psychological issues would be beneficial for healthcare providers seeking to find the best treatment for those negatively impacted by social media. This in turn would also be beneficial for instructors whose students’ academic performance has deteriorated due to social media. Though the internet has become an integral part of modern education, research (Almarzouki et al., 2022; Spence et al., 2020; Zahmat & Zhang, 2023) has shown that using social media while studying is problematic. 

Social media is currently used by more than half of the population (Montag et al., 2024). For many of those users, it is their default mode of communication; for others, it is their way of sharing their lives with the world. The internet, social media in particular, has many benefits, quickening communication and allowing people to connect across borders and time zones. However, as this paper discussed, social media also has the potential to cause substantial harm, socially, psychologically, and cognitively. Though we were unable to find a clear effect of social media on memory loss in our own research, many other studies have shown how memory is damaged by social media use, especially in academic settings. Without a better knowledge of how the internet impacts academic performance, the education system will be unable to adapt to better support students who regularly use social media while learning. Understanding the detrimental aspects of social media is crucial to improve global well-being.

**Appendix C. Short Story: The Bizarre Case of the Exploding Toads**
In the spring of 2005, something strange started happening in a small pond in Hamburg, Germany. Hundreds of toads were found mysteriously exploding, their bodies bursting apart in gruesome fashion. The pond soon earned the chilling nickname “The Pond of Death.” Scientists, wildlife experts, and concerned locals gathered to investigate. Initially, theories ran wild—was it a virus? A toxic chemical leak? A new environmental disaster? No one could explain why the toads were inflating like balloons and violently bursting.

However, a key clue emerged after close examination of the exploded toads: all of them were missing their livers. This bizarre pattern led researchers to suspect that a predator was involved. But what kind of animal could eat a toad’s liver with such surgical precision, leaving the rest of the body intact? After days of observation, the answer finally came—crows. These highly intelligent birds had discovered that toads’ livers were a rich, nutritious source of food. The crows had learned to peck through the toads’ skin, remove the liver, and leave the rest behind. Normally, a toad’s main defense is to puff up its body when threatened, making it harder to swallow. However, with a missing liver, the toads continued inflating uncontrollably, and without the necessary internal structure to regulate the pressure, they simply exploded. This discovery not only solved the mystery but also provided insight into the remarkable intelligence of crows. They weren’t just attacking randomly—they were demonstrating observational learning. If one crow figured out how to extract the liver, others could quickly pick up the technique. Crows are known for their ability to recognize human faces, solve puzzles, and even use tools, but this gruesome incident revealed yet another layer to their complex behavior. 

Scientists also noted that this wasn't an entirely unique phenomenon—similar liver-targeting behavior had been observed in other animals, such as dolphins, who sometimes remove the livers of sharks. The toad incident was simply one of the most dramatic and visually shocking examples of how predators can adapt their hunting techniques in unexpected ways. But the implications of the exploding toads extended beyond just the gruesome spectacle. Toads play a crucial role in ecosystems by controlling insect populations, particularly mosquitoes and agricultural pests. With a sudden and drastic decrease in the toad population, researchers warned that insect numbers could surge, leading to potential ecological imbalances. Some studies suggest that without amphibians regulating insect populations, nearby plant life could also suffer due to an increase in herbivorous insects. While no immediate ecological disaster was recorded, the event served as a reminder that even small disturbances in predator-prey relationships can have unexpected ripple effects. 

Even today, the Exploding Toad Mystery remains one of the most bizarre cases in modern zoology. It is a reminder that nature is full of surprises, and sometimes the most unbelievable explanations turn out to be true.
