*Action Potentials*
- Major topics:
	- Resting potential
	- Cycle of K and Na ions
	- All-or-none law
- Information transmission
	- Nerve impulse: action potential
		- Occurs in axon
		- Generated in axon hillock
	- Synaptic transmission: neurotransmitters, postsynaptic potentials
		- NTs are present in synapse
		- PSPs are present in dendrites and soma
- Membrane potential: electrical potential difference between the inside and outside of the cell
	- Membrane is composed of lipid bilayer and protein channels
	- Selectively permeable
		- Non-gated protein channels: generally open
		- Gated protein channels: open and closed
			- Voltage-gated ion channels
			- Ligand-gated channels
			- Mechanically-gated channels
	- Charge is generally more negative inside of neurons (typically -70 millivolts)
		- Charge is determined by Na & K and membrane permeability
- Sodium-potassium pump
	- Uses active transport - requires energy
		- Electro-chemical gradient across membrane is passive
	- Pumps 3 Na out and 2 K in
	- Concentration gradient acts to move Na inside but K outside
	- Electrical gradient acts to move both Na and K inside
- Nerve impulse (action potential)
	- Electrical signal generated at axon hillock
	- Speed of a nerve impulse ranges from 1m/s to 100m/s
	- Involves opening of voltage-gated ion channels
	- Once generated, an axon potential will be conveyed bar none, and its magnitude will not decrease over distance
	- Phases
		- 1: Resting potential -> stimulus (typically NT). Voltage-gated Na and K channels are closed
		- 2: Stimulus opens some Na channels, causing depolarization past the threshold
		- 3: Full depolarization. Start of action potential, reaching a peak around 40-60mv. Na channels are open, but K are closed
		- 4: Repolarization following closing of Na channels. Na channels close, while K channels open
		- 5: Hyperpolarization during refractory period. Na channels are closed, while some K channels are still open
		- 6: Return to resting potential. All channels are closed
		- Threshold: amount of depolarization required to fire an action potential
	- Refractory periods
		- Absolute: time when Na channels cannot open again and new action potentials cannot be fired
		- Relative: time when Na channels can open again, but producing a new action potential will require a much stronger stimulus

*Postsynaptic potentials*
- Excitatory postsynaptic potentials (EPSP) - depolarizes
	- Opens Na+ channels -> Na+ enters cell -> makes cell more positive -> AP is generated
- Inhibitory postsynaptic potentials (IPSP) - hyperpolarizes
	- Opens Cl- channels -> Cl- enters cell -> makes cell more negative -> AP is suppressed

*Summation of graded potentials*
- Temporal summation: repeated stimuli over a short period of time (200ms) produced a stronger response
- Spatial summation: several small stimuli in a general location produced a stronger reflex
- Order and location matters - if an initial stimulus is not strong enough to depolarize the cell, several following stimuli may sum to cause depolarization. But if the following stimuli are further up from the initial, they'll have no effect. Stimuli are summed as they travel down the cell
- Temporal and spatial summation typically occur together

- DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) codes for proteins
- Broken down into four bases: adenine, thymine, cytosine, guanine
- Chromosomes result from DNA being warpped around histones
	- Histones - four proteins with tails
	- Epigenetic markers are located on the tails
- Alleles
	- Two forms of the same gene
	- One found on each chromosome - inherited from each parent
	- Either dominant or recessive
		- Dominant genes act in both recessive and dominant conditions
		- Recessive genes only act in the heterozygous condition
- Homozygous: two alleles of the same type (both dominant, both recessive)
- Heterozygous: one dominant and one recessive allele
- Genotype: allele formation (EE, Ee, ee)
- Phenotype: physical traits or appearance caused by genes
	- Intermediate gene occurs in a phenotype when there is incomplete dominance in the heterzygous condition - results in a mixture of the two properties
- Mendelian genetics
	- Mendel demonstrated that inheritance occurs through discrete units of heredity (genes)
	- Law of segregation
		- Organisms contain two alleles for each gene, and these alleles separate in gametes
	- Law of independent assortment
		- Alleles for different genes are passed to the offspring independent of each other - basic probability 
- DNA -> RNA -> proteins
	- Transcription (DNA -> RNA): each base is copied over into one strand of RNA, with T being replaced by U
	- Translation (RNA -> proteins): The triplet bases in RNA determine codons and amino acids in proteins
- Nature vs Nuture
	- Nature
		- Physical and mentral traits are rooted in genetics
		- Ultimate cause of behavior: genes, evolution, adaptation
	- Nurture
		- Physical and mental traits are rooted in experience
		- Proximal cause of behavior: learning, experiences, socialization
	- Nature and nurture?
	- Influence of nurture - PKU (phenylketonuria)
		- Genetic disease caused by one mutation 
		- Lack of enzyme to convert phenylalanine into tyrosine (which can be converted into dopamine)
		- Phenylalanine builds up and leads to severe mental disabilities
- Genetic change
	- Mutation: heritable changes in DNA molecules
	- Microduplication/microdeletion: part of a chromosome is deleted or duplicated
		- Some researchers believe schizophrenia is a result of microduplications and microdeletions of neurological genes
- Epigenetics
	- Study of gene expression (with modification of the DNA sequence)
	- Some genes are only active at a certain point or time in one's life
	- Changes in gene expression are critical for learning and memory
	- Epigenetic differences are likely behind the differences between identical twins
	- Modifications to gene expression
		- Methyl groups
			- Tag DNA transcription
			- Can cause the DNA to be wound too tightly on histones, preventing transcription
		- Acetyl groups
			- Can loosen DNA, allowing transcription
	- Epigenetics can be affected by 
		- Environmental chemicals
		- Development
		- Drugs
		- Aging
		- Diet
	- Mouse licking study
		- Attentive mouse mothers help affect methyl groups near the GR gene
		- The GR gene affects stress hormones
		- Mice with attentive mothers handle stress better as adult mice
	- Överkalix study
		- Kept excellent records of health, birth, and death
		- Cycles of famine were observed in the records
		- Descendants of children who had experienced famine had less heart disease and diabetes
- Behavioral genetics
	- Research on the genetic influences of behavior
	- Genome-wide association studies, twin studies
	- Fast-growing field in psych and bio
- Heritability
	- Statistic looking at the proportion of variation that is due to genetics or the environment
	- Can be difficult to assess
	- Twin studies, correlation between physical and behavioral measures
- Concordances
	- Research of probabilities of whether relatives will develop neuropsychiatric disorders

*Anatomy of the eye*
- Path of light
	- Images enter the eye from the pupil 
	- It then hits the retina, where it is inverted
		- Within the retina, it goes from ganglion -> bipolar -> photoreceptors
		- It then travels back to the ganglion cells
		- The ganglion cells transmit to the optic nerve
		- The optic nerve transmits to the visual cortex
- Cranial nerves 
	- II. Optic (vision)
	- III. Oculomotor (eye movements)
	- IV. Trochlear (eye movements - superior oblique)
	- VI. Abducens (eye movements - lateral rectus)
	- III. Oculomotor (all eye muscles except for superior oblique and lateral rectus)
- Retinal anatomy
	- Blind spots occur due to concentration of axons in an area - optic disk
	- Five primary layers
		- *Receptors*
			- Rods - scotopic vision
				- Specialized for dim light, night vision
				- Slow recovery time
				- Multiple rods transmit with one bipolar cell
				- More dense (120 million)
				- Located in the periphery
				- Inhibitory cells - light decreases their activity
			- Cones - photopic vision
				- Specialized for daylight, color
				- Fast recovery time
				- One cone transmits to one bipolar cell
				- Less dense (6 million)
				- Located in the fovea
		- Horizontal cells
			- Responsible for **lateral inhibition**
				- Horizontal cells inhibit their neighbors, producing contrast in vision
				- Receptors excite both horizontal cells and bipolar cells
		- *Bipolar cells*
		- Amacrine cells
			- Refine signal transmission
		- *Ganglion cells* (give rise to the optic nerve)
			- **Only cells in the retina that can fire an action potential**
	- Fovea
		- Depression in the central portion of the retina
		- Allows for acute and detailed vision
		- Dense with receptors, especially cones
			- Each receptor in the fovea attaches to a single bipolar cell and a single ganglion cell (midget ganglion cell)
			- Small receptive field: one-to-one connection

*Optic nerve anatomy*
- Optic chiasm: where the left visual field and right visual field meet
- Optic pathways:
	- Left visual fields from both eyes go to the right side of the brain
	- Right visual fields from both eyes go to the left side of the brain
- Visual information is sent to the lateral geniculate nucleus in the thalamus
- From there it communicates to the primary visual cortex (striate cortex)

*Lateral geniculate nucleus*
- LGN ganglion cells
- Information from the contralateral eye goes to LGN layers 1, 4, 6
- Information from the ipsilateral eye goes to LGN layers 2, 3, 5
- Magnocellular pathway: layers 1, 2
	- Encodes the "what"
	- Information from midget ganglion cells
	- Encodes color
	- Only communicates with LGN
	- Small receptive fields
	- Connects to the dorsal pathway
- Parvocellular pathway: layers 3, 4, 5, 6
	- Encodes the "where"
	- Information from parasol retinal ganglion cells
	- Encodes movement and overall patterns
	- Communicates with LGN and other thalamic areas
	- Large receptive fields
	- Connects to the ventral pathway

*Visual cortex*
- Located in occipital lobe
- Communicates with frontal lobe in about 1/5 of a second
- Primary visual cortex (striate cortex, V1)
	- Input from LGN
	- Output to all other layers of the visual system
	- Each hemisphere represents the contralateral visual field
	- Layers 1, 2, 3, 5, and 6 - pyramidal neurons
	- Layer 4 - interneurons
		- Primarly receives LGN inputs
		- Ocular dominance columns
			- Eye inputs are segregated
			- Each eye has a dominant column
			- Alternating columns of ocular dominance throughout layer IV
- Extrastriate visual cortical areas (V2-5)
- Orientation columns
	- Cells have preferred orientation
	- Cells with the same orientation preferences are organized into columns
	- Cause of course they are
	- Preference for neighboring columns are not random
- Receptive fields
	- The point in space where light enters the retina; and from there strikes a rod/cone
	- Other visual cells derive their receptive fields from the connections they receive
- Types of cells
	- Simple cells: respond to orientation
	- Complex cells: respond to a moving stimulus of a particular orientation
	- Hypercomplex cells: similar to complex cells; have a strong inhibitory area at one end of their receptive fields
- Ventral temporal pathway
	- Encodes the "what"
	- Also known as the perception pathway
	- Important for identifying objects
	- Damage causes deficits to identification
	- Retinal cells: midget ganglion cells
	- LGN communication: parvocellular neurons
- Dorsal parietal pathway
	- Encodes the "where"
	- Also known as the action pathway
	- Important for visually guided movements
	- Damage causes deficits to integration with vision
	- Retinal cells: parasol ganglion neurons (larger receptive field)
	- LGN communication: magnocellular neurons

*Fusiform facial area (FFA)*
- Fusiform gyrus is located in the ventral temporal lobe 
- Structure might be specialized for facial recognition
	- When subject is shown facial stimuli, this area lights up in an fMRI 
	- Is this its primary function, or its only function?

**6.3.2025**
*Homeostasis*
- Introduced by Claude Bernard
	- “All vital mechanisms have one goal - to maintain the uniformity of the conditions of life in the internal environment”
	- Proposed a framework for understanding homeostasis: ![[Screenshot 2025-03-06 at 2.12.36 PM.png]]
	- Homeostasis: various biological processes that keep body variables (temperature, water level, oxygen level, etc) within a fixed range 
		- The tendency of an organism to regulate its internal environment and maintain equilibrium (receptor → integrator → effector)
		- Receptor measures environment and sends information to integrator
		- Integrator compares information to set point and sends signals to effectors
		- Effectors: receive signals from integrator and restore set point
	- Set point: single level that the body works to maintain (ex. 98.6 is the healthy human body temperature)
	- Negative feedback: processes that reduce discrepancies from the set point
		- Aspects of motivated behavior can be described as negative feedback
	- Body temperature regulation is dependent on areas in the preoptic area and anterior hypothalamus (POA/AH) which require significant fuel
		- Different types of receptors (in skin and spinal cord) transmit information through somatosensory system to the POA/AH (integrator area) 
		- The POA/AH compares the sensory input to set point - if the body has strayed from the set point, a negative feedback loop begins
		- Signals are sent to the effectors
		- If body temperature is **above** set point:
			- Blood vessels dilate, blood flow increases
			- Sweat glands are stimulated, evaporation occurs
			- Respiratory centres stimulated
		- If body temperature is **below** set point:
			- Blood vessels constrict, blood flow lessens
			- Skeletal muscles cause shivering
			- Chemical signals cause an increase in cellular respiration
- Fevers
	- Purpose is to fight infection by killing pathogens and making the immune system more efficient
	- Sequence of activation:
		- Infection produces the activation of an immune response
		- Activated lymphocytes or macrophages release cytokines - more cytokines are released at the higher temperature
		- Cytokines cross blood-brain barrier and cause the release of prostaglandins and histamine in the OVLT
		- The set point for temperature is raised by the hypothalamus
- Flaw with homeostasis
	- High fevers are harmful to us as well as to the pathogens
	- People ignore their bodily cues

*Allostasis*
- Adaptive process in which the body prepares in a predictive way to an anticipated need or change
- The brain is plastic!
- Allostatic load: term coined by Brian McEwen to indicate the cost of the brain’s adaptations on the body
- Stress is a constraining force
	- Too little is as harmful as too much
	- Stress can be a factor in disease
	- Stress is a physical, chemical, and emotional factor
	- The positive aspect of stress is often overlooked
	- Chronic stress and acute stress have different effects
	- Reaching severe levels of stress will result in a breakdown

*Homeostasis is active adaptation and allostasis in anticipatory adaptation* 

*Thirst*
- Water constitutes 70% of our bodies (yikes)
- Sufficient fluid is needed for the circulatory system
- Without water we will die within about 3 days
- Osmoregulation:
	- Monitoring/regulation of the water level in the body
	- Hypothalamus, pituitary, medulla, kidneys, and adrenal gland all play a role
- Two types of thirst:
	- Osmotic thirst:
		- Intracellular
		- More abundant (67% of bodily fluids)
		- Involves intracellular fluid
		- Affected by tonicity and concentration gradient
			- Tonicity: ability of a solution to cause a cell to lose or gain water
				- Isotonic: solution with same solute concentration as the cell
				- Hypertonic: solution with higher solute concentration than the cell (cell shrinks)
				- Hypotonic: solution with lower solute concentration than the cell (cell swells)
	- Hypovolemic thirst:
		- Extracellular

				- Positive punishment:
					- Something is given that decreases probability of repetition of behavior
				- Negative punishment:
					- Something is taken away that decreases probability of repetition of behavior

				- CS: tone

*Hebbian synapses*
- Definition: activity (or lack thereof) between presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons affects the strength of the synapse
- Basics:
- **NMDA MOST IMPORTANT FOR INDUCTION OF POTENTIATION - IT MUST BE UNBLOCKED**

		- **Increase in AMPAR and synaptic size - amount of Ca determines this**
		- Lasting enhancement of synaptic strength
		- Cooperativity: threshold of axons with simultaneous stimulation needed to induce LTP
		- Specificity: only stimulated synapses are affected
		- Associativity: pairing of a weak input with a strong input enhances the response to the weak input (synaptic tagging: more receptors are made)
		- LTP is highly studied at glutamate synapses
	- Long term depression (LTD): lack of activity in the presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons results in weakening and shrinking of the synapse

	- When axon A excites neuron B repeatedly, growth and change occurs in one or both cells, increasing axon A’s efficiency is increased
	- Nicoll: synaptic modification occurs due to simultaneously activity between presynaptic and postsynaptic cells

*Substance use disorder*

	- Affinity: a drug’s ability to bind to receptors and transporters
	- Efficacy: effects of the drug once bound to a receptor
- Types of drugs:
	- Agonists:
		- High affinity, high efficacy
		- Mimic or increase the effects of neurotransmitters
		- Examples: benzodiazepines, cannabis 
	- Antagonists:
		- High affinity, low efficacy
		- Block or decrease the effects of neurotransmitters (by blocking the receptor)
		- Examples: anti-psychotics
- Reward circuitry:
	- Discovered by Olds and Milner in 1954
	- Circuit produces cravings
	- Key areas:
		- Mesolimbic dopaminergic system
			- Includes medial forebrain bundle
		- Ventral tegmental area
			- Large number of dopaminergic neurons
			- Project widely throughout the brain
			- Dopaminergic neural activity is affected by many psychoactive drugs
		- Nucleus accumbens
			- Central to reinforcing all kinds of experiences 
			- Primary location where addictive drugs release dopamine
- Criteria of substance use disorder:
	- Symptoms: compulsion to take a certain drug, loss of control in limiting intake, and negative affect when drug is not available 
	- Cyclical pattern: binge → withdrawal → anticipation → binge…
- Cravings:
	- Insistent search for a thing or activity
	- Distinctive feature of addictions
	- Cues can trigger a craving even after long periods of abstinence
	- For addicts, drugs release less dopamine, but cues associated with the drug release more
- Tolerance:
	- Decrease in effect as an addiction develops
	- Drug tolerance is learned
- Withdrawal
	- Body’s reaction to the absence of the drug
	- One hypothesis is that addictive behavior is an attempt to avoid withdrawal symptoms
- Theories:
	- Negative reinforcement theory:
		- Taking drugs to counteract withdrawal symptoms or a negative state
		- Problems with this theory:
			- Highly addictive drugs like nicotine and cocaine do not produce withdrawal
			- Addicts relapse long after withdrawal symptoms are gone
			- Some drugs like tricyclic antidepressants produce withdrawal but do not cause addiction
	- Positive reinforcement theory:
		- Taking drugs because they produce euphoric pleasure
		- Problems with this theory:
			- Euphoric feelings often fade with use
			- Some addictive drugs do not produce pleasurable feelings
			- The destruction that addiction causes far outweighs any pleasure from the drug
	- Incentive-sensitization theory of addiction:
		- **Generally accepted theory of addiction**
		- Mesolimbic dopaminergic system (MLDS) becomes sensitized to drugs and stimuli associated with the drug
		- When the MLDS is activated, it creates strong cravings for the drug
		- Classical conditioning of stimuli associated with drugs 
		- Instrumental conditioning of increased craving when the stimuli are present
		- Sensitization is long-lasting, far beyond withdrawal symptoms
		- Dorsal striatum functions during the learning process
- Stimulant drugs:
	- Cocaine:
		- Psychostimulant that causes increased arousal, euphoria, confidence, and hypersexuality
		- **Indirect agonist**: acts on dopamine, preventing reuptake
		- It is derived from dried coca leaves, creating cocaine hydrochloride
		- Methods of intake: snorting, smoking, injection
		- Crack cocaine:
			- Cocaine mixed with a base and something crystalized
			- It is more addictive because of its fast action
			- Methods of intake: smoking
	- Amphetamine:
		- **Indirect agonist**: acts on dopamine, diffusing into the presynaptic neuron and causing dopamine release from transporter - can also reduce amount of dopamine transporters
		- Highly addictive
		- Adderall is an amphetamine salt
		- Methods of intake: pill, snorting, smoking
	- Methamphetamine:
		- Crystalized form of amphetamine 
		- Symptoms of addiction: teeth grinding, heart problems, tremors, anxiety, bad skin, and overdose
		- Methods of intake: smoking
- Alcohol use disorder (AUD):
	- Habitual and uncontrollable use of alcohol 
	- 15 million adults in the US suffer from AUD
	- Most prolifically used, and most accessible, and by far the most harmful
	- Resulting health issues: liver damage, heart disease, digestive problems, cancer
	- Alcohol:
		- GABA agonist (agonist for glycine, antagonist for glutamate)
		- Works on GABA-A receptors
		- Acts similarly to alprazolam
		- GABA-ergic neurons inhibit neurons that inhibit neurons → alcohol consumption increases dopamine levels

		- Delta-FosB remains for a long time even after an addict stops taking drugs, and influences relapse

*Oxytocin*
- Neural hormone - “the love hormone”
- Known for facilitating lactation and childbirth in mammals
- Now shown to effect trust, empathy, and communication
- Causes decreased food intake 
- Found in NAc, where it increases plasticity
- Critical for pair bonding and social rewards
	- Pair bonding in voles:
		- Prairie voles exhibit strong pair bonding, while montane voles are solitary
		- Prairie voles have the highest concentration of oxytocin receptors in NAc
		- This density is absent in montane voles
		- Study showed that blocking oxytocin receptors in NAc prevents pair bonding in rodents
- Oxytocin interacts with serotonin system, stimulating excitatory synaptic currents
	- Blocking serotonin 1b receptors sharply decreases social behavior
	- Activation of serotonin terminals in NAc reverses social deficits

*MDMA*
- MDMA potently releases serotonin
- Enters through the serotonin transporter protein (SERT)  reversing its direction of SERT and causing massive efflux of serotonin
- MDMA increases sociability and reverses social deficits
- Problems occurred when administered during pregnancy
