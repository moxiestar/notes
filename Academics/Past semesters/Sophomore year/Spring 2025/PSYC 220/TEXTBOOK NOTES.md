**1.3**
*Resting Potential*
- Resting potential: difference in charge between inside and outside of neuron
	- While resting, the membrane maintains an electrical gradient/polarization
	- Slightly more negative inside the cell than out
	- Typically -70 millivolts
- Na & K channels
	- Membrane allows only certain chemicals to pass freely into the cell:
		- Oxygen
		- Carbon dioxide
		- Urea
		- Water
	- Membrane allows some chemicals to pass through gates that open and close:
		- Sodium
		- Potassium
		- Calcium
		- Chloride
	- Sodium-potassium pump transports 3 Na ions out of the cell while bringing 2 K ions in
		- Na is 10x more concentrated outside of the cell
		- K is more concentrated inside the cell
		- Na is pulled into the cell by the concentration gradient and the electrical gradient
		- When the channels open and Na floods in, the cell is prepared to respond
		- K gradually leaks out of the cell, increasing the electrical gradient across the membrane:

![[Pasted image 20250119110524.png]]

*Action Potential*
- The basic process:
	- At the resting potential, the cell is slightly negatively charged
	- When we dramatically increase the negative charge, the cell is briefly hyperpolarized
	- When we make the charge more positive, the cell is briefly depolarized
	- Enough depolarization causes the cell to cross the threshold, resulting in an action potential
	- The action potential then fades during the refractory period and returns to resting potential
- Na & K:
	- Before the action potential starts, Na ions are mostly outside, and K is mostly inside
	- Depolarization opens the Na and K channels
	- At the peak of the action potential, Na channels close
	- Opening of Na channels is crucial - many ions flood into the cell, steeply raising the voltage
	- K is then driven out of the cell, leading to the refractory period
	- Excessive stimulation leads to accumulation of Na - can be dangerous

![[Pasted image 20250119183909.png]]

*Propogation of the Action Potential*
- The action potential opens more and more sodium channels as it goes:

![[Pasted image 20250119185546.png]]

*Refractory Periods*
- Absolute refractory period: time wherein Na channels are fully closed and the cell is incapable of producing another action potential
- Relative refractory period: time wherein Na channels are partially closed and K is flowing out of the cell; the cell is capable of producing another action potential, but it will need intense stimulation
- Refractory period requires two things:
	- Na channels are closed
	- K is flowing out of the cell

*Myelination*
- Myelin (material composed of fats and proteins) increases speed of conduction
- Myelinated axons are only present in vertebrates
- Myelin sheaths are interrupted by breaks, called nodes of Ranvier
- Ion channels are concentrated in nodes of Ranvier
- Saltatory conduction: jumping of action potentials from sheath to sheath

*Local Neurons*
- Small, localized neurons that produce graded potentials - having a light inhibitory effect on neighbor cells

## Chapter Two: Electrochemical Transmission
**2.1**
*Synapse*
- Sherrington and Cajal discovered that neurons communicate in the gaps between dendrites and terminals: the synapse
- Sherrington's studies
	- Reflexes: muscular responses to stimuli
	- Leg flexion: sensory neuron -> sensory neuron -> motor neuron -> muscle
	- Reflex arc: circuit from sensory neuron to muscle 
	- Sherrington saw that reflexes originated in the spinal cord

![[Pasted image 20250122212641.png]]

- Sherrington's key observations
	- Reflexes are slower than action potential conduction 
	- Several weak stimuli presented at nearby places or times combine their effects
	- When one set of muscles is excited, another set is relaxed
- Speed of reflex
	- Roughly 15m/s through reflex arc
	- Action potentials can convey at up to 40m/s
	- Sherrington figured that the communication between neurons was slowing conduction down

![[Pasted image 20250122225140.png]]
- Temporal summation
	- Repeated stimuli over a brief period of time summate their effects
	- Presynaptic neuron: cell that transmits the signal
	- Postsynaptic neuron: cell that receives the signal
	- Excitatory postsynaptic potential: graded depolarization caused by influx of Na+ ions
	- Inhibitory postsynaptic potential: graded hyperpolarization caused by influx of Cl- ions
- Spatial summation
	- Multiple stimuli over small space will summate their effects
- Inhibitory synapses
	- Antagonistic muscles maintain balance throughout the body
	- If a flexor muscle is excited, the extensor muscle is inhibited
	- Spontaneous firing rate: period of producing action potentials without stimuli

**2.3**
*Chemical Signalling*
- Elliott proposed that sympathetic nerves stimulate muscles with adrenaline
- Loewi conducted his frog heart experiment, firing the vagus nerve and observing the decrease in heart rate - he experimented with the fluid around the heart and found that it contained effective chemicals. He concluded that neurons sent signals with chemicals
- Trajectory of chemical signalling:
	- Neurons synthesize chemicals that serve as neurotransmitters
	- Action potentials travel down axons
	- At the presynaptic terminal, depolarization allows Ca to enter the cell. Ca releases neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft
	- The neurotransmitters attach to their receptors, and cause alterations in activity
	- The neurotransmitters detach, possibly to be recycled

![[Pasted image 20250123085524.png]]

*Neurotransmitters*
- Categories
	- Amino acids: glutamate, GABA, glycine, aspartate, acetylcholine (modified AA)
	- Monoamines:
		- Indoleamine: serotonin (5-HT)
		- Catecholamines: dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine
	- Neuropeptides: endorphins, substance P, neuropeptide Y
	- Purines: ATP, adenosine
	- Gases: nitric oxide
- Nitric oxide
	- Oldest NT
	- Released by neurons when stimulated
	- Dilates nearby blood vessels, increases blood flow
- Neurotransmitters are synthesized from proteins in the diet:

![[Pasted image 20250123090747.png]]

- Amino acid tryptophan: precursor to 5-HT
	- 5-HT rises when we consume tryptophan and falls when we do not
- Clinical effects
	- L-dopa, precursor to dopamine, helps to treat Parkinsons
	- AMPT has no therapeutic use, but researchers use it to block dopamine
- Transmission of NTs
	- Synthesis of NTs takes place in presynaptic terminals
		- NTs are then released in vesicles
	- Release of NTs is caused by Ca
		- Depolarization opens voltage-gated Ca+ ion channels, leading to exocytosis
		- NT is released and diffuses across the synaptic cleft to fuse with a receptor (approx. 0.01ms)
	- Effects on postsynaptic cell
		- Ionotropic effect (faster - 0.1ms)
			- Ligand-gated ionotropic channels respond to NTs, opening enough to allow flow of one type of ion
			- Excitatory ionotropic synapses typically use glutamate
			- Inhibitory ionotropic synapses typically use GABA
		- Metabotropic effect (slower, longer lasting 100ms)
			- Neuromodulators bring about slower effects that can last up to 20mins
			- NMs can be released by any part of the cell - dendrites, soma, axon - and diffuse across a wider area
			- NMs include: dopamine, norep, 5-HT, neuropeptides
			- Metabotropic receptors are attached to G proteins (guanosine triphosphate)
			- Attachment of NMs bends the receptor, releasing the protein
			- The G protein causes increased concentration of a second messenger chemical within the cell

*Reuptake of Neurotransmitters*
- After the release of GABA or glutamate, transporter proteins move the NTs back into the presynaptic cell 
- This ensures the presynaptic cell has sufficient materials and prevents any lingering effect on the postsynaptic cell

*Negative Feedback from Postsynaptic Cell*
- Presynaptic terminals have autoreceptors for the same NTs they release
	- Autoreceptors detect the release of the NT and prevent further synthesis and release
	- Some cells release chemicals that travel back to the presynaptic terminal to inhibit further release of NTs
	- Reverse transmitterS: anandamide, 2-AG

*Gap junction synapses*
- Specialized electrical synapses for rapid, critical motor function (respiration etc)
- Membrane of one cell makes contact with another, and ions flow through gap junction channels 
- Leads to depolarization

## Chapter Three: Neuroanatomy; Clinical Neuroscience
**3.1**
*Neuroanatomy terms*
- Central nervous system: brain and spinal cord
- Peripheral nervous system: nerves that connect the CNS to the rest of the body
	- Somantic nervous system: nerves that connect the sensorimotor systems
	- Autonomic nervous system: nerves that control bodily organs
	- Enteric nervous system: nerves that control the digestive system
- Lamina: a layer of soma, separated from other soma by axons
- Column: cells perpendicular to the cortical surface
- Tract: a set of axons within the CNS
- Nerve: a set of axons in the PNS
- Nucleus: a cluster of soma in the CNS
- Ganglion: a cluster of soma typically in the PNS
- Gyrus: a bump on the surface of the brain
- Sulcus: a fold on the surface of the brain
- Fissure: a pronounced sulcus
- White matter: cortical tissue consisting of myelinated axons
- Gray matter: cortical tissue consisting of somas and dendrites

*Directional terms (back here again! joy to the world!)*
- Dorsal: top
- Ventral: bottom
- Anterior (rostral): frontal
- Posterior (caudal): rear
- Proximal: closer
- Distal: further away
- Ipsilateral: on the same side of the body
- Contralateral: on the opposite side of the body
- Lateral: peripheral 
- Medial: central
- Planes:
	- Axial plane: parallel to the ventral surface, under the eyes
	- Sagittal plane: through the middle of the head, between the eyes
	- Coronal plane: through the middle of the head, behind the eyes

*Overview of Neuroanatomy*
- Spinal cord (CNS)
	- Communicates with the whole sensorimotor system (other than the head)
	- Each spinal segment has a sensory nerve and a motor nerve on its left and right
		- Sensory soma are in the dorsal root ganglia, outside of the spinal cord
- Autonomic nervous system 
	- Neurons that communicate with the heart, intestines, and other organs
	- Sympathetic nervous system
		- "Fight or flight"
		- Prepares organs for a simultaneous burst of activity
		- Releases norep into organs
		- Ganglia: left and right of central thoracic-lumbar spine
	- Parasympathetic nervous system (craniosacral system)
		- "Rest and digest"
		- Facilitates vegetative non-emergent responses
		- Consists of cranial and sacral spinal nerves
		- Releases ACh into organs
		- Ganglia: preganglionic axons extend from spine to parasympathetic ganglia close to each organ -> postganglionic axons extend from parasympathetic ganglia to the organs themselves
- *Parasympathetic vs Sympathetic*
![[Screenshot 2025-01-30 at 2.41.00 PM.png]]
- Brain 
	- Hindbrain (rhombencephalon, metencephalon, myelencephalon)
		- Medulla oblongata
			- Extension of the spinal cord
			- Medulla connects to other parts of the body via 12 cranial nerves
				- I: Olfactory - smell
				- II: Optic - vision
				- III: Oculomotor - eye movements; pupil constriction
				- IV: Trochlear - eye movements
				- V: Trigeminal - facial sensation; mastication; swallowing
				- VI: Abducens - eye movements
				- VII: Facial - facial movements; taste from anterior 2/3 of tongue; crying; salivation; dilation of cranial blood vessels
				- VIII: Vestibulocochlear - hearing; balance
				- IX: Glossopharyngeal - taste from throat and posterior 1/3 of tongue; swallowing; salivation; throat movements during speech
				- X: Vagus - sensations from neck and thorax; control of throat, larynx, and esophagus; parasympathetic nerves to control stomach, intestines, heart
				- XI: Accessory - neck and shoulder movements
				- XII: Hypoglossal - control of muscles of the tongue
		- Pons
			- Contains nuclei for several cranial nerves
			- Site of axonal crossover for contralateral control 
		- Cerebellum
			- First to start developing, last to finish developing
			- Important for motor control, balance, and coordination
			- Other functions include: timing, learning, memory, attention, visual processing, language, emotional processing, social behavior
			- Primary function could be to detect sequences of events
	- Midbrain (mesencephalon)
		- Tectum: roof of midbrain
		- Superior/inferior colliculus: swellings on either side of the tectum
		- Tegmentum: intermediate level of midbrain
		- Substantia nigra: seat of dopamine-containing pathway - key for Parkinsons
	- Forebrain (prosencephalon, diencephalon, telencephalon)
		- Two hemispheres control contralateral movements
		- Outer shell: cerebral cortex
		- Inner shell: thalamus, hypothalamus, amygdala, insula, hippocampus, etc 
			- Thalamus: main source of input to cerebrum
				- Sensory input is sent to thalamus, processed, and relayed to cerebral cortex
				- Nuclei of the thalamus: dorsomedial nucleus, ventral lateral nucleus, ventral posterior nucleus, pulvinar nucleus, lateral geniculate body
			- Hypothalamus: essential for regulation of behaviors
				- Regulates feeding, drinking, temperature, sexual behaviors, aggression
				- Communicates with pituitary gland, altering the flow of hormones
			- Basal ganglia: subcortical structures lateral to thalamus
				- Structures: caudate nucleus, putamen (lateral), globus pallidus (medial)
				- Damage to basal ganglia impairs movement - Parkinsons, Huntingtons
				- Integrates emotional behavior to increase the vigor of movements
			- Basal forebrain (nucleus basalis)
				- Receives input from hypothalamus and basal ganglia
				- Axons release ACh to widespread areas in the cerebrum
				- Key for arousal, wakefulness, attention
				- Damaged in Parkinsons, Alzheimers
			- Amygdala: evaluation of fearful stimuli
			- Hippocampus: critical for formation of long-term memory
				- Semantic and episodic memories are formed in the hippocampus
- Ventricles 
	- CNS develops as a neural tube surrounding a fluid canal
		- Canal becomes the central canal of the spinal cord and the ventricles
	- Each hemisphere contains one of the two lateral ventricles
	- Third ventricle: positioned at midline, separates left and right thalamus
	- Fourth ventricle: centre of medulla
	- Choroid plexus: cells along the walls of the four ventricles that produce cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)
	- CSF flows froms lateral to third & fourth ventricles, then into central spinal cord and space between brain and meninges 
		- Cushions the brain during movement and contact
		- Provides hormonal and nutritional support for the CNS
		- Obstructed flow of CSF leads to swelling in ventricles and intracranial space
			- Hydrocephalus: spreading of skull bones following blockage of CSF in infants

**3.2**
*Cerebral cortex*
- Corpus callosum, anterior commissure: allow neurons in one hemisphere to communicate with neurons in the other hemisphere
- Grey matter is made up of laminae
	- Laminae are not present in all cortices but there are typically six layers
	- Laminae:
		- I: Molecular layer - dendrites, long axons
		- II: External granular layer - small pyramidal cells
		- III: Pyramidal cell layer - pyramidal cells
		- IV: Internal granular layer - small cells; main site for incoming sensory information
		- V: Inner pyramidal layer - large pyramidal cells; main source of motor output
		- VI: Multiform layers - spindle cells
	- Cells are organized into columns perpendicular to laminae
- Occipital lobe
	- Located in the posterior cerebrum
	- Function: visual processing
	- Structures: primary visual cortex (striate cortex)
		- Damage in the striate cortex causes cortical blindness
- Parietal lobe
	- Located between the occipital lobe and the central sulcus
	- Function: sensorimotor processing
	- Structures: postcentral gyrus (primary somatosensory cortex)
		- Sensory cortex receives information from touch receptors, stretch receptors, and joint receptors
- Temporal lobe
	- Located in the lateral side of each hemisphere
	- Function: auditory processing, emotional and motivational regulation, advanced facial processing and recognition
	- Structures: Broca's area, Wernicke's area
	- Klüver-Bucy syndrome: maladaptive social and sexual behaviors, tendency to put everything in mouth, and lack of fear due to temporal lobe damage
	- Temporoparietal junction: area where the temporal and parietal lobes meet 
		- Functions: sensory processing, attention, bodily awareness, cognition
- Frontal lobe
	- Located between the central sulcus and the anterior end of the brain
	- Function: cognition, emotional regulation, decision making, attention
	- Structures: ==precentral gyrus (primary motor cortex)==, prefrontal cortex, default network
		- Frontal portion of precentral gyrus is specialized for fine movements
		- Default network takes over when the brain is at rest and powers imagination
		- Prefrontal cortex has three major regions: posterior, middle, anterior
			- Posterior: movement control - overlaps with motor cortex
			- Middle: cognitive control, emotional reactions, associational memory 
			- Anterior: advanced decision making and planning
	- Connections in the frontal lobe were severed in the prefrontal lobotomy 
		- Lobotomy rose in popularity after practitioner Egaz Moniz won the Nobel Prize for her development of the procedure
		- Walter Freeman, a neurologist, created the ice pick lobotomy, performing tens of thousands of surgeries on patients
		- Symptoms post-lobotomy included apathy, lack of initiative, memory disorders, distractibility, loss of emotional expressions, lack of emotional regulation

*Cranial connections*
- Sensory processing connects various areas of the brain that normally would not interact
- Binding problem: how multiple areas of the brain connect to produce sensation
- Binding occurs if you perceive two sensations as happening at the same time in roughly the same place

**3.3**
*Clinical research methods*
- 1861: Paul Broca discovered the importance of Broca's area for speech production after observing a patient with damage to Broca's area
- Lesion studies have been helpful for learning the role and importance of various brain areas
	- Recreating lesion studies with animals - allows for more consistent research with controlled lesioning
		- Ablation: removal of a brain area
		- Lesion: damaged brain area
		- Stereotaxic instrument: device for the precise placement of electrodes in the brain
			- Precisely mapped electrodes allow researchers to create "mock" lesions, with short-lasting electrical damage
			- Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS): magnets on the scalp stimulate the neurons beneath
				- If the current is strong enough, the neurons are deactivated
				- A "virtual" lesion is produced
				- This enables researchers to study the area of the brain before and after damage occurs
- Effects of stimulation
	- Optogenetics: use of light to control a limited subset of neurons
		- Developed in 2009 by Karl Deisseroth
			- Discovery of a protein that responds to light - these proteins typically react by opening or closing ions channels
			- Development of viruses that insert these proteins into neurons
			- Development of thin optical fibers that shine light into predetermined areas of neurons
		- The technology allows researchers to control the stimulation (excitatory or inhibitory) of a single neuron on a millisecond scale
- Methods of imaging
	- Electroencephalography (EEG) - use of electrodes on the scalp to record brainwaves
		- Event-related potentials (ERP) are measured to track specific brain processes
		- EEG is useful for sleep and epilepsy studies
	- Magnetoencephalography (MEG - measurement of magnetic resonances produced by brain activity
		- Excellent temporal resolution
	- Positron-emission tomography (PET scans) - measurement of glucose to determine brain activity
		- Pt receives injection of glucose labeled with radioactive atoms
			- Glucose is more concentrated in more active areas of the brain, so this gives researchers a good map of brain activity
		- "When the radioactive atoms decay, they release positrons that collide with nearby electrons, emitting two gamma rays in opposite directions"
		- The computer measures the gamma rays and determines the point in between the two
		- Hazards: expensive, harmful
	- Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) - measurement of the energy released by hemoglobin after the removal of a magnetic field
		- Hemoglobin bound to oxygen reacts to magnetic fields differently than unbinded hemoglobin
		- The more active a brain area is, the more oxygen it uses, and the less binded hemoglobin there is
- Mapping brain imaging to behavior
	- Phrenology
		- Correlating specific areas to specific traits or behaviors
	- Best imaging methods for psychology
			- Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) - measurement of atomic axial rotation to gage brain anatomy
		- Computerized axial tomography (CAT) - measurement of blood flow with dye

## Chapter Four: Genetics; Development
**4.1**
*Mendelian Genetics*
- Mendel's experiments in the 1860s proved the existence of genes
	- Genes: heritable units that define traits across generations
		- Genes are composed of DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid)
			- DNA serves as a template for RNA (ribonucleic acid)
				- mRNA serves as a template for protein creation
			- DNA has four bases, ATCG (adenine, thymine, cytosine, guanine)
			- RNA has four bases, AUCG (adenine, uracil, cytosine, guanine)
- Protein synthesis:

![[Pasted image 20250210132328.png]]

- Chromosomes: paired strands of genes
	- Homozygous gene: same gene is present in both chromosomes
	- Heterozygous gene: different gene is present in each chromosome
- Genes can be dominant, recessive, or intermediate
	- Dominant genes: strong effect in both homozygous and heterozygous conditions
	- Recessive genes: strong effect in only the homozygous condition
- Genes can be sex-linked or autosomal
	- Autosomal genes: all genes other than X and Y chromosomes
	- Sex-linked genes: X and Y chromosomes
		- Females have XX chromosomes
		- Males have XY chromosomes
		- Most genes are X-linked, inherited from the X chromosome
	- Sex-limited genes: genes present in both sexes but active in only one
- Genetic changes
	- Mutations: changing the sequence of a DNA molecule
		- Bases can be changed, deleted, or substituted
		- This leads to the creation of a different mRNA strand and a different protein
	- Microduplications and microdeletions occur in chromosomes
- Epigenetics: study of gene expression
	- DNA is bound by histone molecules
		- It is wound around a nucleosome, the core of histone molecules
		- Adding different groups can facilitate or stop the expression of different genes
			- Acetyl groups facilitate gene expression
			- Methyl groups silence genes
	- Epigenetic changes (when one generation is conditioned to express a certain gene) can be inherited
- Heritability: traits that depend largely on gene expression
	- Heritability is studied through twin studies
		- Monozygotic (identical) twins vs dizygotic (fraternal) twins
	- Nature vs nurture - research has found that most traits depend on heritability

*Evolution of Behavior*
- Evolution: changes in the frequencies of genes over generations
	- Genes associated with higher reproductive success will be passed on 
- Artificial selection: purposeful breeding of more advantageous parents
- Lamarckian evolution: faulty idea that use of a certain limb/feature leads to genetic changes
- Evolutionary fitness: the number of copies of one's genes that are carried on to the next generation
- Evolutionary psychology: study of evolution of behaviors
	- Altruistic behavior - unique to humans? Why did this evolve?
		- Kin selection: selection of a gene that benefits an individual's relatives
		- Reciprocal altruism: helping someone else will cause them to help me too
		- Group selection: altruistic groups are more successful than uncooperative groups

	- Cells lining the ventricles divide
		- Some remain in the ventricles, becoming stem cells
		- Some migrate across the CNS, aided by immunoglobulins and chemokines
	- Neurons develop over the span of about 2 years
- Synaptogenesis begins before birth and continues throughout the lifespan
- Myelination begins in the spinal cord before progressing to the brain
- The human brain does not develop new neurons after birth, except for the stem cells in the olfactory bulb

*Neuronal survival*
- Muscles determine how many axons survive
- NGF (nerve growth factor) from the muscle promotes the axon's growth
	- An axon that does not receive NGF goes through apoptosis
	- NGF is a neurotrophin (a chemical that promotes the survival of neurons)
- Exposure to sickness or toxins during development, particularly gastrulation, is catastrophic
	- Fetal alcohol syndrome is caused by excessive maternal drinking
	- Alcohol causes apoptosis, halts neuron migration, impairs synaptic transmission
- Dendritic development
	- Animals raised in "enriched" environments developed more dendritic branches
	- Far transfer: studying one skill in the hopes of improving another skill
		- Far transfer has a weak effect - little evidence of its benefit
- Damage to one area of the cortex will result in that area's function being transferred elsewhere
	- For example: in a study where blind people were asked to differentiate Braille symbols, their occipital lobes showed activity
	- When the occipital lobe was temporarily lesioned, the subjects were unable to differentiate the symbols, showing that their occipital lobes (typically used for vision) were being used for the sense of touch
- Learning and practicing new skills changes brain anatomy
	- Focal hand dystonia: inability to move one finger at once

*Brain development over time*
- Adolescents are generally more impulsive than adults
	- The prefrontal cortex, responsible for inhibiting behaviors, is not fully developed until ~25
	- The reward pathway is also stronger in adolescents, and gets stronger over the teenage years
- Old age
	- Memory and reasoning fade
	- Cortical tissue shrinks
	- Synaptic transmission slows

## Chapter Five: Vision
**5.1**
*Vision*
- How far we see depends on how far light travels before it reaches our eyes
	- Light travels 93 million miles from the sun to reach us
	- We only see the rays that reflect off of objects and hit our retinas
- Sensation and sight are "felt" in the brain, not in the body/sensory organs

*Retinal Anatomy*
- Light enters the eye through the pupil (centre of iris)
- It is focused by the lens (adjustable) and the cornea (unadjustable)
- It is projected onto the retina (back surface of the eye)
- Images enter the eye in an upside-down mirror fashion

![[Pasted image 20250212115342.png]]
- Images travel from the retinal receptors to bipolar cells 
- Bipolar cells communicate with ganglion cells and amacrine cells
- Ganglion cells travel back to form the optic nerve
	- The point where it exits the eye is a blind spot
- Amacrine cells send signals to other bipolar cells, ganglion cells, and other amacrine cells

![[Pasted image 20250212123232.png]]

- Detailed vision is focused in the fovea, which lies in the centre of the retina
	- Midget ganglion cells: ganglions present in the fovea
		- Each ganglion cell responds to a single cone
		- Each cone in the fovea has a direct route to your brain! 
		- Midget ganglion cells provide 70% of input to the brain - vision is dominated by things seen in the fovea

*Retinal photoreceptors*
- Rods
	- More abundant in the periphery
	- 120 million rods in the retina
	- Optimized for low light
	- Bright light bleaches them
- Cones
	- More abundant near the fovea
	- 6 million cones in the retina
	- Optimized for bright light
	- Provide color vision
	- 90% of visual input
	- Colorblindness arises from errors in cone development
- Photopigments
	- Chemicals that release energy when struck by light
	- 11-cis-retinal is bound to opsins -> light -> all-trans-retinal bound to opsins

*Color vision*
- Visible light: 400-700nm (violet-red)
- Trichromatic theory (Young-Helmholtz): color is perceived by comparing responses across specified receptors
- Opponent process theory: color is perceived in terms of opposites (hence our perception of afterimages)
- Color constancy: the ability to recognize colors despite different environments/lighting
- Retinex theory: visual cortex compares information from various parts of the retina to determine the color and brightness of stimuli

## Chapter Six: Mechanical & Chemical Senses
**6.1**
*Sound & Audition* 
- Sound waves: periodic compressions of air varying in amplitude and frequency 
	- Amplitude: intensity/volume
	- Frequency: pitch (number of compressions per second, measured in hertz)
	- Timbre: tone quality/complexity
- Human hearing: 15 Hz to 20,000 Hz

*Anatomy of the Ear*
- Outer ear
	- Pinna (visible ear outside the head) helps to locate sound
	- Auditory canal (ear hole) allows sound to travel deeper into the ear
- Middle ear
	- Tympanic membrane (eardrum) connects to three bones that transmit sound to the oval window
		- Three bones: hammer, anvil, and stirrup
- Inner ear
	- Oval window - membrane in the inner ear that, when vibrated, moves fluid in the cochlea
	- Cochlea (snail) is the home of auditory receptors 
	- Hair cells (auditory receptors) are located on the basilar membrane and stimulate the auditory nerve
![[Pasted image 20250222165338.jpg]]

*Pitch Perception*
- Two mechanisms for detecting frequencies:
	- Frequency theory:
		- Basilar membrane vibrates in synchrony with sound waves
		- Auditory nerve axons fire as many action potentials as the number of Hz indicates 
			- 50 Hz = 50 action potentials
	- Place theory:
		- Basilar membrane is tighter at the base (where the stirrup meets the cochlea) and looser at the peak (the apex of the cochlea)
		- Sounds vibrate hair cells at different locations along the membrane according to their frequency
			- Lower frequency sounds vibrate hair cells near the peak, and higher frequency sounds vibrate hair cells near the base

*Auditory cortex*
- Most auditory inputs cross over to the contralateral hemisphere
- Primary auditory cortex: area in temporal lobe where auditory information is processed
	- Responds to imagined sounds
	- Damage does not produce deafness - area is critical for processing, not audition 
- Auditory system has a ”what“ and “where“ pathway 
- Planum temporale: area in the temporal cortex that processes the input of a sound moving from one location to another
	- Strongly connected to area MT which processes visual motion
- Tonotopic map of sounds: cells responsive to similar frequencies group together

![[Pasted image 20250222171024.jpg]]

*Sound Localization*
- The time that a sound reaches the ear allows us to locate the sound source
- When underwater, it is difficult to localize low- and medium-frequency sounds because sound travels faster in water
- Low frequencies are localized by phase differences
	- Phase difference: the way sound waves reach the ear depends on location - they may be out of sync in different locations 
- High frequencies are localized by loudness differences
	- Sound shadow: when a wavelength is shorter than the width of the head, the sound is louder for the closer ear
		- Less accurate for lower frequencies (lower than 2,000 Hz)

*Individual Differences*
- Amusia: tone deafness
	- Inability to detect a difference between tones smaller than the difference between C and C#, detect off-key singing, detect a wrong note, or read someone’s mood based on tone of voice
	- Results from lower connections between the auditory cortex and frontal cortex
- Absolute (perfect) pitch: ability to hear a note and identify it
	- Genetics play a rote
	- Receiving early musical training helps
- Deafness: hearing loss
	- Conductive (middle-ear) deafness
		- Bones of the middle ear are prevented from transmitting sound to the cochlea
		- Can sometimes by corrected by surgery or hearing aids
		- Cochlea and auditory nerve are normal
	- Nerve (inner-ear) deafness
		- Cochlea, hair cells, or auditory nerve are damaged
		- Can be inherited, or caused by disease or loud noises 
	- Tinnitus
		- Constant ringing in the ears
		- Common among patients who have lost their hearing during their life
		- After hearing loss in a particular range, patients will often experience tinnitus in that range, suggesting that those axons are being stimulated in another way

**6.2**
*Vestibular system*
- During movement, the vestibular organ (next to the cochlea) compensates by correctly moving your eyes - typically in the opposite direction from the movement
- Damage to the vestibular system causes loss of balance and coordination 
- Vestibular organ
	- Saccule, utricle, and three semicircular canals 
		- The three canals are all perpendicular, filled with fluid, and lined with hair cells
		- Movement causes the fluid to shift, pressing against the hair cells
		- Saccule and utricle record the position of the head at rest
		- Semicircular canals only record the rate of acceleration 
	- Hair cells serve as receptors
	- Otoliths (Ca carbonate particles) move with the head and push against hair cells accordingly 

*Somatosensory system*
- Somatosensation encompasses pain, touch, pressure, heat, etc
- Variety of different sensory organs
	- Pacinian corpuscles: receptors that respond to vibrations or displacements on the skin
	- Cold-sensitive neurons in the spinal cord are coded to detect decreases in temperature 
	- Heat-sensitive neurons in the spinal cord are coded to detect constant temperatures
	- Capsaicin: chemical that stimulates the receptors for painful heat
- Sensory information is received via spinal nerves, each of which innervates a dermatome (specific area of the body)
- Different areas in the sensory cortex respond to different types of sensations
	- Damage to sensory cortex impairs body perceptions
- Numbsense - phenomenon where, during damage to the sensory cortex, people can correctly identify the location of a touch but insist that they do not actually feel the touch
![[Pasted image 20250222184416.jpg]]
*Pain*
- Pain and depression are closely linked
- Women experience more pain than men (told you so)
	- Women and men react differently to pain (didn’t need a researcher to know that)
- Spinal pathways for pain and temperature
	- Pain is perceived via nerve endings
	- Axons for pain are thin and usually unmyelinated; they conduct slowly
		- Thicker axons convey sharp/first pain, while thinner axons convey dull/second pain
	- Mild pain triggers glutamate, while stronger pain triggers both glutamate and neuropeptides (substance P, CGRP)
	- Pain & temperature pathway decussates as soon as it enters the spinal cord from the receptors 
		- Cf. touch pathway - only decussates in medulla
	- Pain is processed in the thalamus, somatosensory cortex, amygdala, hypothalamus, and anterior cingulate cortex (last three code unpleasant emotional pain)
- Emotional pain
	- Emotional pain correlates with increased cingulate cortex activity
	- Pain-relieving drugs relieve emotional pain (????)
- Pain relief
	- CIPA prevents patients from feeling pain
	- Opioid mechanisms - systems that respond to opiates and relieve pain
		- Opiates bind to receptors in the spinal cord and periaqueductal grey
		- Endorphins: opiate-type chemicals found naturally within the brain
		- Inescapable pain is potent in stimulating endorphins and inhibiting further pain
		- Morphine is ineffective against sharp/first pain
- Gate theory of pain
	- Neurons that receive pain inputs also receive other inputs that can override the pain, such as pressure
	- Cannabinoids have been used to treat neuropathic pain 
		- After an injury, levels of histamine and nerve growth factor are up 
		- If sensory axons are destroyed, pain cells take over in their place
		- These two factors lead to neuropathic pain
	- Capsaicin has also been used for pain relief
- Placebo effect
	- When patients receive drugs/procedures with no pharmacological effects but are told that the drugs/procedures will help their condition, they show improvement 
	- Nocebo effect: negative effect shown if a patient expects to have an unpleasant reaction to a drug/procedure

*Taste*
- Taste is not seen across all species, and is only useful for determining whether it is safe to consume something
	- Flavor is the combination of taste and smell

		- Genetics have an effect, but the variations in taste depend on age and hormones too
		- Supertasters are more sensitive to strongly flavored foods

## Chapter Seven: Movement
**7.1**
*Muscles and movement*
- Each muscle fiber receives information from a single axon, but an axon may innervate more than one muscle fiber
- Three types of vertebrate muscles
	- Smooth muscles: digestive system, bodily organs
	- Skeletal/striated muscles: body/limb movement
	- Cardiac muscles: heartbeat

![[Pasted image 20250216153753.png]]

- Neuromuscular junction: synapse between a motor neuron axon and a muscle fiber
	- Axon releases ACh -> muscles contract
		- ACh deficit impairs movement
- Antagonistic muscles: muscles that control opposing movements (flexor vs extensor)
- Human muscle fiber types
	- Fast-twitch fibers
		- Aerobic 
			- Eventually the muscles will switch from using glucose and oxygen to using fatty acids
		- Quick contractions
		- Rapid fatigue
	- Slow-twitch fibers
		- Anaerobic
		- Slow, weaker contractions
		- No fatigue
- Proprioceptors
	- Receptors that detect the position/movement of a body part
	- Stretch reflex: contraction of a muscle in response to stretch
	- Types of proprioceptors
		- Muscle spindles
			- Parallel to the muscle
			- Respond to stretch
			- Cause contraction
			- Negative feedback loop
		- Golgi tendon organs
			- Perpendicular to the muscle - at the ends of the muscle
			- Respond to increases in muscle tension
			- Prevent excessive contraction
- Types of movements
	- Involuntary - controlled by reflexive/subconscious neural signals
		- Reflexes
		- Ballistic movement - executed as a whole
		- Other movements can be adjusted 
	- Voluntary - fully controlled by our thought processes
	- Central pattern generators: spinal cord mechanisms that generate rhythmic movements
	- Motor program: fixed sequence of movements

## Chapter Eight: Sleep
**8.1**
*Rhythms of Sleep*
- Endogenous rhythms
	- Circannual rhythms: yearly/seasonal internal calendar
	- Circadian rhythms: daily internal calendar
		- Our bodies do not respond well to changes in sleep and will always try to reset the sleeping/waking schedule to a 24-hour timeframe
		- Circadian rhythms dictate mood, energy, and sleep
		- Zeitgebers (external stimuli) reset our circadian rhythms
			- Light is the most common zeitgeber
			- Short-wave (blue) light resets circadian rhythms best
			- As a result, blindness disrupts circadian rhythms
		- Jet lag 
			- Disruption of circadian rhythms after crossing time zones
			- Going west: phase-delay (staying up and waking later)
			- Going east: phase-advance (going to sleep and waking earlier)
		- Night shift
			- Day sleeping must be done in a dark room
			- Night work must be done in an extremely bright room
			- Night shift workers are groggier and more accident-prone than day shift workers
		- Early birds vs night owls
			- People show different preferences and tolerances for sleep times
			- Across all cultures and several species, adolescents go to bed and wake up late
			- Night owls tend to perform worse on tests
- Generation of endogenous rhythms
	- Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)
		- Located in the hypothalamus
		- Automatically produces a circadian rhythm of action potentials
		- Exclusively controls and creates endogenous rhythms
		- Branch of the optic nerve (retinohypothalamic path) communicates with the SCN
			- Specialized retinal ganglion cells with their own photopigment (melanopsin) respond directly to light and help to reset circadian rhythms

- Genetic regulation
	- Genes known as *period* and *timeless* create circadian rhythms by producing proteins that promote sleep
		- Synthesis of protein increases throughout the day, peaking at night and declining in the morning
- Melatonin production
	- Pineal gland (posterior to thalamus) produces melatonin
	- Melatonin increases sleepiness 
	- Melatonin is found in all animals except sponges

**8.2**
*Types of unconsciousness*
- Sleep: intentionally produced state of decreased activity and responsiveness
- Coma: extended period of unconsciousness typically caused by brain injury
- PVS (persistent vegetative state) or unresponsive wakefulness syndrome: condition between sleep and moderate wakefulness, with minimal responsiveness and no purposeful behavior
- Minimally conscious state: condition between consciousness and unconsciousness, punctuated by brief periods of awareness
- Brain death: condition with no sign of brain activity or responsiveness

*Sleep stages*
- Associated brain waves
	- Alpha waves: steady waves that indicate a relaxed, wakeful state
	- K complex: large, sharp wave associated with a short inhibition of neural firing
	- Sleep spindle: burst of 12 - 14 Hz waves resulting from interactions between the thalamus and the cerebral cortex
		- Sleep spindles are associated with new learning and positively correlated with memory
	- During slow wave sleep, slow high-amplitude waves increase
	- Each cycle of sleep lasts ninety minutes and goes from 1 to 5, then back to 2, then 5; then repeat
- Stage 1
	- Associated waves: theta, alpha
	- Transition from drowsiness to sleep
- Stage 2
	- Associated waves: theta, sleep spindles, K complexes
	- Light sleep
- Stage 3
	- Associated waves: delta 
	- Deep, slow wave sleep
- Stage 4
	- Associated waves: delta 
	- Deep, slow wave sleep
- Stage 5 (REM or paradoxical sleep)
	- Associated waves: alpha, beta, PGO (pons-geniculate-occipital)
	- Dream-rich sleep with rapid eye movements and muscle atonia
	- REM sleep is associated with increased, irregular neural activity
	- Arousal and bodily fluctuations occur during REM sleep
	- REM sleep is brought about by dopamine released in the amygdala

![[Screenshot 2024-08-08 at 3.56.49 PM.png]]

*Higher processing*
- Reticular formation
	- Located in the midbrain
	- Regulates arousal and sleep
	- Pontomesencephalon - small structure that promotes sleep by releasing GABA
- Locus coeruleus
	- Located in the pons
	- Releases norep throughout the cerebral cortex
	- Fine-tunes attention to important stimuli
- Hypothalamus
	- Path that releases histamine promotes alertness
	- Another pathway, to basal forebrain, releases a peptide, orexin (hypocretin) and promotes alertness
- Basal forebrain
	- ACh pathway stimulates wakefulness
	- Adenosine increases sleepiness
		- Caffeine inhibits adenosine

*Brain activity during sleep*
- Though we are largely unresponsive during sleep, we still wake in response to certain stimuli (unfamiliar voices, babies crying)
- Pons and midbrain release GABA, inhibiting transmission of information across the cerebral cortex
- Some brain areas can go to sleep while others remain awake
- During REM, the pons and medulla inhibit the spinal cord neurons that control limb movement

*Sleep disorders*
- Insomnia
	- Lack of sleep
	- Symptoms: worsened memory, attention, cognition, and mood
	- Causes: noises, discomfort, stress, diet, medications, epilepsy, Parkinson’s, brain tumors, psychological disorders
- Sleep apnea
	- Breathless periods during sleep; patients wake gasping for breath
	- Symptoms: fatigue, impaired attention and learning, apoptosis
	- Causes: obesity, alcohol, tranquilizers
- Narcolepsy
	- Sudden fatigue during the day
	- Four main symptoms
		- Attacks of sleep during the day
		- Cataplexy (muscle weakness) brought on by strong emotions
		- Sleep paralysis
		- Hypnagogic hallucinations (dreamlike hallucinations that patients have difficulty distinguishing from reality)
	- Causes: genetic, loss of hypothalamic cells that release orexin
- Periodic limb movement disorder
	- Repeated involuntary movements of the legs or arms while asleep
- REM behavior disorder
	- Acting out of dreams during REM sleep
	- Patients often injure themselves or others
- Night terrors
	- Experience of intense anxiety during sleep, more severe than a nightmare
	- Dream content is minimal (a single image) or nonexistent
- Sleepwalking
	- Periods of seemingly capable movement while asleep
	- Sleepwalkers can walk, drive, climb structures, etc - often putting themselves in danger
	- Occurs during slow wave sleep
	- Causes: sleep deprivation, stress
- Sexsomnia
	- Engagement in sexual behavior while asleep

**8.3**
*Functions of sleep*
- Health benefits of sleep
	- Metabolism is decreased, synapses are strengthened and reorganized, fluid is drained from the brain
- Harms of sleep deprivation
	- Stress, poor mental health, accidents, worsened academic performance
	- Driving while asleep is as bad as driving while intoxicated
	- One night of sleeplessness activates the immune response
- Possible functions of sleep
	- Energy conservation 
		- Sleep saves energy at times when activity would be inefficient and possibly dangerous (for humans, at night, since we are not nocturnal)
		- During sleep, body temperature is decreased by several degrees, and muscle activity is decreased
	- Improved memory
		- Sleeping after learning something improves memory
		- REM and slow wave sleep both help memory
		- EEG data shows similar hippocampal activity during learning and sleep
		- Knowledge storage depends on sharp wave ripples from the hippocampus or thalamus to the parietal and frontal cortex

*Functions of REM sleep*
- REM declines with age
- REM could improve memory
- REM supplies corneas with oxygen by moving the surrounding fluid, protecting them from damage

*Dreaming*
- Activation-synthesis hypothesis
	- Dreaming is the brain’s attempt to make sense of distorted information 
	- Dreams begin with activity in the pons (PGO waves)
	- The brain connects this activity to other, unrelated cortical activity
- Neurocognitive model
	- Dreaming is thinking that takes place under unusual conditions
	- Dreams begin with spontaneous activity related to recent memories
	- During REM sleep, activity is increased in the pons, hypothalamus, amygdala, parietal cortex, temporal cortex, and other areas critical for emotions
	- Activity is decreased in the primary visual cortex, motor cortex, and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex
	- Lack of prefrontal cortex activity explains the lack of logic and planning in dreams
	- Damage to the parietal cortex prohibits dreams
	- Posterior parietal cortex is correlated with detail of dreams

## Chapter Nine: Internal Regulation
**9.1**
*Homeostasis*
- Introduced in 1929 by Cannon
- Biological processes that keep variables (temperature, WBC count, etc) within a fixed range
- Some values change over time (over a month, throughout the day) including weight and temperature
- Key terms:
	- Set point: single value that the body maintains through homeostasis
	- Negative feedback: processes that reduce discrepancies from the set point
	- Allostasis: dynamic changes to the set point that anticipate a need before it occurs

*Temperature regulation*
- Basal metabolism: the energy required to maintain constant body temperature
	- Metabolism occurs in brown adipose cells, which burn fuel like muscle cells and release it as heat
- Two categories of temperature regulation:
	- Ectothermy
		- “Cold blooded” - amphibians, reptiles, and fish
		- Using external resources to maintain body heat
		- Lower constancy in temperature
	- Endothermy
		- “Warm blooded” - mammals and birds
		- Using physiological mechanisms to maintain body heat
			- Heat is generated in proportion to body mass
			- Heat is radiated in proportion to surface area
		- Higher constancy in temperature
		- Endothermy allows mammals and birds to stay warm at all times
		- 37 C is an advantageous natural body temperature
			- Having a warm body temperature allows for rapid movement
			- Higher than 41 C, proteins denature and lose their function
- Methods of temperature regulation:
	- Evaporation - cooling
		- Only method that cools the body
		- Humans sweat to reduce temperature
		- Other animals pant or lick themselves (??)
		- Risks include dehydration, heat exhaustion, even organ damage
	- Muscle activity - warming
		- Muscle contractions (shivering etc) generate heat
		- Decreased blood flow to the skin keeps the internal organs warm
		- Many mammals fluff up their fur - humans inherited goosebumps
	- Behavioral methods
		- Putting on or taking off clothes
		- Changing level of activeness
		- Huddling with others
- Brain mechanisms
	- Hypothalamus regulations many temperature changes
		- Preoptic area/anterior hypothalamus (POA/AH area) sends output to the raphe nucleus (located in the hindbrain), controlling autonomic responses (shivering, sweating, heart rate, blood flow, metabolism, etc)
		- Temperature changes in both the skin and hypothalamus lead to behavioral responses to change temperature
- Fever's effect on temperature
	- During an infection the immune system releases prostaglandins and histamines 
	- This causes the POA/AH to raise the set point for temperature
	- Fever is something produced by the body to fight infection and make the immune system more efficient

*Learning & memory*
- Hebbian synapses
	- Hebbian synapse: synapse whose effectiveness is increased due to simultaneous activity in the pre- and post-synaptic neurons
	- Donald Hebb proposed a theory: when axon of neuron A repeatedly fires neuron B, growth takes place in both cells that improves the ability of axon A to excite cell B
	- Larry Katz: “neurons that fire together, wire together”
- Habituation
	- Decrease in response to a repeated stimulus
- Sensitization 
	- Greater response to mild stimuli, caused by exposure to more intense stimuli
- Long-term potentiation: axons connected to a dendrite bombard it with a rapid series of stimuli, leading to lasting potentiation 
	- Occurs most often in the hippocampus, at GABA synapses
	- It also occurs at NMDA and AMPA ionotropic receptors
		- When glutamate stimulates AMPA receptors, NMDA receptors are also activated
		- Ca enters the cell and changes gene expression, promoting synapses
		- After LTP, NMDA receptors revert to normal
	- Potentiation releases brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)
	- Properties of LTP
		- Specificity: exclusively activated synapses are strengthened - associated with protein aCaMKII
		- Cooperativity: concurrent stimulation by multiple axons produces stronger LTP than repeated stimulation by one axon
		- Associativity: pairing of a weak input with a stronger input enhances later response to the weak input
- Long-term depression: prolonged decrease in response at a synapse

	- Genetic influences
		- Several genes affect the likelihood of developing substance abuse
		- One gene affects the metabolism of alcohol (gene that produces less acetaldehyde dehydrogenase to metabolize acetaldehyde in ethyl alcohol)
	- Behavioral influences
		- Alcoholism is more common in impulsive, risk-taking, bored, thrill-seeking, outgoing people
		- Heavyweights are more likely to become alcoholics
- Treatments
	- Psychotherapy
	- Participation in groups like AA
	- Antabuse (medication that antagonizes acetaldehyde dehydrogenase, so that alcohol cannot be digested)
	- Methadone (drug similar to heroin that can allow addicts to ween off of opiates and onto something less intense)
	- Levomethadyl acetate (longer-lasting medication also used to treat opiate addictions)

**14.2**
*Mood Disorders*
- Major Depressive Disorder
	- Prolonged feelings of hopelessness, frustration, emptiness, and apathy
	- Depression is marked be negative bias: tendency to interpret everything in a negative way
	- Depression typically consists of depressive episodes, interspersed with periods of normal mood 
	- Cognitive symptoms include worsened attention, lower motivation, and impaired memory
		- These symptoms persist even if depression is successfully treated
	- Patients with depression also struggle with sleep issues, either insomnia or hypersomnia
		- Depressive patients enter REM sleep earlier than healthy individuals, and wake earlier: ![[Pasted image 20250402173259.png]]
	- Etiology: genetics, life events, pain (which decreases dopamine activity), stress
		- Twin studies show moderate heritability
		- 17 genes affect rates of depression
	- Treatment
		- Antidepressants
			- Types
				- Tricyclics
					- Block transporter proteins that reabsorb serotonin, dopamine, and norep into the presynaptic neuron after release
					- Block histamine receptors, ACh receptors, and some Na channels
					- Can cause dangerous cardiac side effects
				- Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors
					- Block serotonin from binding to serotonin transporter protein
					- Side effects are relatively mild
					- Serotonin norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors block both serotonin and norep
				- Monoamine oxidase inhibitors
					- Block monoamine oxidase, which breaks catecholamines and serotonin down
					- Negative interactions with tyramine
			- Mechanisms of antidepressants
				- Besides acting on specific neurotransmitters, antidepressant drugs also facilitate neurotrophins, especially BDNF (which is lower in depressive patients)
				- Attachment to BDNF receptors is slow, resulting in the slow course of antidepressants 
			- Efficacy
				- Antidepressants are generally more effective than placebos, but not by much
				- There is little evidence that different doses of SSRIs produce different effects
		- Psychedelics, such as MDMA or ketamine, could be a possible treatment
			- Ketamine both facilitates BDNF and blocks NMDA-type receptors in the lateral habenula
			- Strong negative side effects are associated with psychedelics
			- Higher doses of ketamine can induce episodes of schizophrenia (????)
		- Psychotherapy
			- Cognitive behavioral therapy is a common treatment route
			- Advantages: no side effects, long-lasting treatment
		- Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)
			- Epilepsy and schizophrenia are negatively correlated - inducing convulsions can reduce symptoms of schizophrenia
			- ECT does not work for schizophrenia, but it does alleviate treatment-resistant depression
			- ECT increases BDNF and neurogenesis
		- Deep brain stimulation (DBS)
			- Small stimulator is implanted in the brain to activate certain (sub)cortical areas 
		- Exercise can also help
		- Sleep deprivation has a short-lived but intensely positive affect on depression
- Bipolar Disorder
	- Patients alternate between depression and mania (restless activity, excitement, excessive self confidence, rambling speech, loss of inhibitions) 
	- Bipolar Type I: full-fledged manic episodes
	- Bipolar Type II: mild hypomanic episodes
	- Decrease in glucose during depression; increase during mania
	- Etiology: genetics, high creativity, childhood abuse
		- Twin studies have showed heritability for bipolar
	- Treatments:
		- Lithium salts (stabilize moods)
		- Better sleep (decreases the risk of episodes)

**14.4**
*Autism Spectrum Disorder*
- Symptoms: deficits in sociability, deficits in facial expression, repetitive movements or behaviors, resistance to change, abnormal responses to stimuli
- Comorbidities: sleep disorders, epilepsy, anxiety, poor coordination, lower intellect
- Autism can appear at as young as 9 months
- Etiology: genetics, prenatal exposure to toxins or infection, family history of autoimmune disorders
	- Autism can often be traced to a single gene
	- It can also be caused by de novo mutations
- Neural growth in autism
	- Brains grow more quickly than normal at a young age in autistic patients
	- In adolescence, this is combated by intense synaptic pruning
	- Genetic mutations cause increased sensitivity of the somatosensory cortex
- No current treatments are known
