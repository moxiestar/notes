**6.3.2025**
*Homeostasis*
- Introduced by Claude Bernard
	- “All vital mechanisms have one goal - to maintain the uniformity of the conditions of life in the internal environment”
	- Proposed a framework for understanding homeostasis: ![[Screenshot 2025-03-06 at 2.12.36 PM.png]]
	- Homeostasis: various biological processes that keep body variables (temperature, water level, oxygen level, etc) within a fixed range 
		- The tendency of an organism to regulate its internal environment and maintain equilibrium (receptor → integrator → effector)
		- Receptor measures environment and sends information to integrator
		- Integrator compares information to set point and sends signals to effectors
		- Effectors: receive signals from integrator and restore set point
	- Set point: single level that the body works to maintain (ex. 98.6 is the healthy human body temperature)
	- Negative feedback: processes that reduce discrepancies from the set point
		- Aspects of motivated behavior can be described as negative feedback
	- Body temperature regulation is dependent on areas in the preoptic area and anterior hypothalamus (POA/AH) which require significant fuel
		- Different types of receptors (in skin and spinal cord) transmit information through somatosensory system to the POA/AH (integrator area) 
		- The POA/AH compares the sensory input to set point - if the body has strayed from the set point, a negative feedback loop begins
		- Signals are sent to the effectors
		- If body temperature is **above** set point:
			- Blood vessels dilate, blood flow increases
			- Sweat glands are stimulated, evaporation occurs
			- Respiratory centers are stimulated
		- If body temperature is **below** set point:
			- Blood vessels constrict, blood flow lessens
			- Skeletal muscles cause shivering
			- Chemical signals cause an increase in cellular respiration
- Fevers
	- Purpose is to fight infection by killing pathogens and making the immune system more efficient
	- Sequence of activation:
		- Infection produces the activation of an immune response
		- Activated lymphocytes or macrophages release cytokines - more cytokines are released at the higher temperature
		- Cytokines cross blood-brain barrier and cause the release of prostaglandins and histamine in the OVLT
		- The set point for temperature is raised by the hypothalamus
- Flaw with homeostasis
	- High fevers are harmful to us as well as to the pathogens
	- People ignore their bodily cues

*Allostasis*
- Adaptive process in which the body prepares in a predictive way to an anticipated need or change
- The brain is plastic!
- Allostatic load: term coined by Brian McEwen to indicate the cost of the brain’s adaptations on the body
- Stress is a constraining force
	- Too little is as harmful as too much
	- Stress can be a factor in disease
	- Stress is a physical, chemical, and emotional factor
	- The positive aspect of stress is often overlooked
	- Chronic stress and acute stress have different effects
	- Reaching severe levels of stress will result in a breakdown

*Homeostasis is active adaptation and allostasis in anticipatory adaptation* 

*Thirst*
- Water constitutes 70% of our bodies (yikes)
- Sufficient fluid is needed for the circulatory system
- Without water we will die within about 3 days
- Osmoregulation:
	- Monitoring/regulation of the water level in the body
	- Hypothalamus, pituitary, medulla, kidneys, and adrenal gland all play a role
- Two types of thirst:
	- Osmotic thirst:
		- Intracellular
		- More abundant (67% of bodily fluids)
		- Involves intracellular fluid
		- Osmosensory neurons detect osmolality (concentration of dissolved particles) of extracellular fluid
		- Affected by tonicity and concentration gradient
			- Tonicity: ability of a solution to cause a cell to lose or gain water
				- Isotonic: solution with same solute concentration as the cell
				- Hypertonic: solution with higher solute concentration than the cell (cell shrinks)
				- Hypotonic: solution with lower solute concentration than the cell (cell swells)
	- Hypovolemic thirst:
		- Extracellular
		- Less abundant (33% of body fluids)
		- Involves intravascular fluid and intercellular fluid
		- Measured by baroreceptors in blood vessels, heart, and kidney
- Set point of hydration: 0.5 M
- Water intake must equal water output (~2.5 L per day)
	- Water input: food, liquids, cellular metabolism
	- Water output: evaporation, sweating, defecation, urination 
- Thirst receptors
	- Receptors in OVLT, subfornical organ, stomach, and elsewhere (helpful) relay information to the hypothalamus (supraoptic nucleus and paraventricular nucleus) and the lateral preoptic area, which controls drinking
		- Both nuclei control the rate at which the posterior pituitary releases vasopressin

*Learning and memory*
- Learning: 
	- Processes of acquiring information
	- Results in change in behavior
	- Types of learning:
		- Operant (instrumental) conditioning:
			- Pioneered by B.F. Skinner, who created the Skinner box
			- Participant’s response leads to a reinforcement or punishment
				- Reinforcement: increases probability of repetition of behavior
				- Punishment: decreases probability of repetition of behavior
				- Negative contingency: result that takes something away from the participant
				- Positive contingency: result that gives something to the participant
			- Four types of operant conditioning:
				- Positive reinforcement:
					- Something is given that increases probability of repetition of behavior
					- Reward or appetite event
				- Negative reinforcement:
					- Something is taken away that increases probability of repetition of behavior
					- Aversive event
					- Escape conditioning: the behavior terminates a current negative reinforcer
					- Avoidance conditioning: the behavior postpones or removes an upcoming negative reinforncer
				- Positive punishment:
					- Something is given that decreases probability of repetition of behavior
				- Negative punishment:
					- Something is taken away that decreases probability of repetition of behavior
			- Visualization:![[Screenshot 2025-03-20 at 2.44.00 PM.png]]
			  ![[Screenshot 2025-03-20 at 2.54.07 PM.png]]
			- Enforces the relationship between action and outcome
		- Classical conditioning:
			- Pioneered by Pavlov - Pavlov’s dog
			- Pairing of two stimuli can change the response to one of them
			- Enforces the relationship between two events
			- Unconditioned stimulus (UCS), which automatically causes response (UCR), is paired with conditioned stimulus (CS) to produce a new response (CR) to the CS
		- Fear conditioning:
			- Type of classical conditioning
			- Mammals have evolved to encode negative feedback and responses - this makes fear a good variable for reinforcing learning
			- Example:
				- CS: tone
				- UCS: electric shock 
				- CR: fear response to tone
				- UCR: fear response to shock
		- Observational (vicarious/witness) learning
			- Learning new behaviors indirectly by observing someone else’s behaviors
- Memory:
	- Processes by which information is encoded, stored, and later retrieved
	- Short-term memory
		- Requires rehearsal
		- Capacity: 7 ± 2 items
		- Duration: 15-30 seconds
		- Once an item is forgotten, it is lost
	- Working memory
	- Long-term memory
		- Does not require rehearsal
		- Capacity: unlimited
		- Duration: potentially forever
		- Hint can trigger “forgotten” items
		- Types:
			- Explicit (declarative) memory: memories that are easy to consciously recall
				- Episodic memory: memories of personal events
				- Semantic memory: memories of general knowledge
				- Areas: temporal lobe, hippocampus
			- Implicit memory: memories that cannot be consciously recalled
				- Procedural memory:
					- Knowledge of motor skills
					- Area: basal ganglia, cerebellum, motor cortex
				- Priming:
					- Experiences that influence behavior
					- Area: temporal lobe, hippocampus
				- Conditioning:
					- Developed responses to certain stimuli
					- Area: cerebellum, hippocampus, temporal lobe
	- Patient H.M. time!!
		- “Up until the time he finally died… I mean—”
		- Epileptic patient who underwent hippocampal removal in 1953
			- Unilateral removal of hippocampus was common for epilepsy at the time
			- Bilateral procedure on H.M. was experimental, but necessary due to the severity of his seizures
		- After the surgery, he suffered massive anterograde amnesia and some retrograde amnesia
		- His explicit memory was completely shattered
		- However, he retained better implicit memory, including fully intact procedural memory
	- Hippocampus has a huge role in episodic memory, but so does the prefrontal cortex

*Hebbian synapses*
- Definition: activity (or lack thereof) between presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons affects the strength of the synapse
- Basics:
- **NMDA MOST IMPORTANT FOR INDUCTION OF POTENTIATION - IT MUST BE UNBLOCKED**
	- Long term potentiation (LTP): simultaneous activity in the presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons results in strengthening and growth of the synapse 
		- **Increase in AMPAR and synaptic size - amount of Ca determines this**
		- Lasting enhancement of synaptic strength
		- Cooperativity: threshold of axons with simultaneous stimulation needed to induce LTP
		- Specificity: only stimulated synapses are affected
		- Associativity: pairing of a weak input with a strong input enhances the response to the weak input (synaptic tagging: more receptors are made)
		- LTP is highly studied at glutamate synapses
	- Long term depression (LTD): lack of activity in the presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons results in weakening and shrinking of the synapse 
		- **Decrease in AMPAR and synaptic size - amount of Ca determines this**
	- Overlap between formation of Hebbian synapses and classical conditioning
	- Engram: neural source of memory formation
- Eye blink task: 
	- Studies rabbits’ response to air puffs, involves the lateral interpositus nucleus (its role was determined through GABA inhibition) and cerebellum
	- Cellular plasticity in CA1/3 pathway: greater reaction after one day, but reactions return to baseline after one week
	- CA1/3 pathway is associated with new learning, not continued performed of learned behaviors
- Plasticity:
	- Hebb: repetition of an activity induces lasting cellular changes that increase its stability
	- When axon A excites neuron B repeatedly, growth and change occurs in one or both cells, increasing axon A’s efficiency is increased
	- Nicoll: synaptic modification occurs due to simultaneously activity between presynaptic and postsynaptic cells

*Emotions*
- Definition: conscious, involuntary mental reaction experienced subjectively as an intense feeling, accompanied by physiological and behavioral changes
- Four aspects: cognition, feeling, physiology, behavior
- Role of the autonomic nervous system:
	- Sympathetic nervous system:
		- Stimulates organs for “flight or fight” response
		- Inhibits vegetative function, including digestive processes
	- Parasympathetic nervous system:
		- Brings about “rest and digest” response
		- Increases digestion and other processes that save energy
- Theories of emotion:
	- Stimulus causes an emotional reaction, which then causes physiological changes
	- James-Lange: stimulus causes physiological causes, which then cause an emotional reaction
		- Emotions result from the body’s reactions
		- Pen study: subjects holding a pen between their teeth, forced to smile, are more likely to report amusement
		- HOWEVER patients with autonomic failure still experience (limited) emotions, and patients with Möbius syndrome (facial paralysis) cannot smile but still experience happiness
	- Cannon-Bard: stimulus causes physiological changes and emotion reaction simultaneously
	- Schachter-Singer: stimulus causes physiological changes, which are then labelled as emotions
- Muscles involved in emotion:
	- Superficial facial muscles (facial nerve VII): facial expression
	- Deep facial muscles (trigeminal nerve V): mastication, large facial movements 
- **Universal facial expressions: anger, disgust, fear, surprise, happiness, sadness, contempt**
	- Some evidence against this:
		- Experiments used exaggerated expressions
		- Posture can change expression
		- Culture has an effect on emotional expression
- Studying emotions in rodents is difficult: 
	- Tests used: elevated plus maze, open field, forced swim test, tail suspension test
	- Less aggressive tests used: 3-chamber sociability task, juvenile interaction
	- Where we run into problems:![[Screenshot 2025-03-27 at 2.36.15 PM.png]]
	- Now, with neural networks, it’s easier to measure rodent facial expressions
- Brain areas involved in emotions:
	- Emotional processing occurs all throughout the cortex, and locations vary from person to person
	- Cerebral cortex: thinking and interpreting
	- Limbic system: feeling
	- Brainstem: basic function and primitive emotions
	- Amygdala: fear and anxiety
		- Startle reflex (damage to amygdala prevents the animal from learning a new response to fear-based stimuli)
			- Startle response can only be modified if amygdala is intact
			- These changes take place in the pons and periaqueductal grey
		- Freezing behavior
		- Studied in the elevated plus maze in rodents
- Post-traumatic stress disorder
	- Heightened emotional reactivity in patients
	- Increased sensitivity to stimulation
	- Sensitization of nervous system, particularly skin conductance
	- Exaggerated startle reflex
	- Increased fear by conditioning
	- Affects on brain areas:
		- Anterior cingulate cortex 
			- Volume reduction and hypoactivation
			- Alterations in emotional regulation
		- Medial prefrontal cortex 
			- Volume reduction and hypoactivation
			- Decreased fear extinction
			- Reduced cortical capacity to inhibit fear
		- Hippocampus
			- Volume reduction
			- Deficits in contextual memory
		- Amygdala
			- Hyperactivity
			- Increased anxiety, fear, aggression

*Fear and anxiety
- Amygdala
	- Fear and anxiety can be easily quantified in rodents
		- Startle reflex (noise + shock in box)
		- Freezing behavior (fear)
		- Elevated plus maze (anxiety)
	- Communications:
		- **Startle reflex: basolateral amygdala to pons**
			- Basolateral amygdala receives input from central amygdala and hippocampus
			- Cochlear nucleus has a more direct line to the amygdala than to the cerebrum, leading to startle reflexes occurring before we’ve had time to think about it
		- Autonomic fear response: amygdala to hypothalamus
		- Avoidant behavior: amygdala to prefrontal cortex (PFC)
		- Fearful/phobic attention: amygdala to thalamus
		- Generalized emotional arousal: amygdala from bed nucleus of the stria terminalis (BNST)
		- Freezing behavior: amygdala to periaqueductal grey
- Stress responses
	- Fast stress response:
		- **1-3 seconds**
		- **Sympathomedullary pathway**
		- Activates the fight or flight response
			- Brainstem activity triggers the sympathetic nervous system, which releases norep 
			- Norep activates **adrenal medulla**, which releases **ep and norep** and affect the whole body
		- Symptoms: increased heart rate, blood pressure, breathing, blood glucose levels; dilated pupils, goosebumps
	- Slow stress response
		- **5-10 minutes**
		- **Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis**
		- Paraventricular nucleus in the hypothalamus releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (**CRH**)
		- **CRH** causes the release of adrenocorticotropic hormone (**ACTH**) from anterior pituitary
		- **ACTH** travels through the blood stream to the **adrenal cortex**, triggering the release of **glucocorticoids (cortisol)**
- Cortisol activity
	- Cortisol helps with long-term stress and prepares the body for future stressors
	- Increases blood glucose levels and blood pressure
	- Facilitates short-term memory
	- Diverts energy away from nonessential areas of the body controlled by the parasympathetic nervous system
	- Long-term high levels of cortisol can be damaging
		- Adult hippocampal neurogenesis (AGN)
			- Cells in dentate gyrus (also in olfactory cortex) produce new neurons! 
			- Can be improved with BDNF
			- Can be halted by high levels of cortisol
- Anxiety disorders YIPPEEEEE
	- Generalized anxiety disorder: chronic worry (BNST input)
	- Panic disorder
		- Frequent, unexplained periods of intense anxiety
		- Symptoms: rapid breathing, increased heart rate, sweating, trembling, nausea, numbness, dizziness
		- Pathway: amygdala input to hypothalamic nucleus
		- Etiology: genetics, hypothalamic abnormalities (YIPPEEE)
		- Chemical results: decreased GABA, increased orexin
	- Social anxiety disorder: fear of social interaction
	- Phobias: strong fear for things or situations
	- PTSD: recurring pathologies from traumatic experiences
	- OCD: intrusive and distressing compulsions and obsessions
- Anti-anxiety medications
	- Benzodiazepines: diazepam, alprazolam
	- Bind to the GABA-A receptor and facilitate the effects of GABA
	- Exert their influence in the amygdala, hypothalamus, midbrain, etc

*Substance use disorder*
- Pathological use of drugs and alcohol 
- Pharmacodynamics:
	- Affinity: a drug’s ability to bind to receptors and transporters
	- Efficacy: effects of the drug once bound to a receptor
- Types of drugs:
	- Agonists:
		- High affinity, high efficacy
		- Mimic or increase the effects of neurotransmitters
		- Examples: benzodiazepines, cannabis 
	- Antagonists:
		- High affinity, low efficacy
		- Block or decrease the effects of neurotransmitters (by blocking the receptor)
		- Examples: anti-psychotics
- Reward circuitry:
	- Discovered by Olds and Milner in 1954
	- Circuit produces cravings
	- Key areas:
		- Mesolimbic dopaminergic system
			- Includes medial forebrain bundle
		- Ventral tegmental area
			- Large number of dopaminergic neurons
			- Project widely throughout the brain
			- Dopaminergic neural activity is affected by many psychoactive drugs
		- Nucleus accumbens
			- Central to reinforcing all kinds of experiences 
			- Primary location where addictive drugs release dopamine
- Criteria of substance use disorder:
	- Symptoms: compulsion to take a certain drug, loss of control in limiting intake, and negative affect when drug is not available 
	- Cyclical pattern: binge → withdrawal → anticipation → binge…
- Cravings:
	- Insistent search for a thing or activity
	- Distinctive feature of addictions
	- Cues can trigger a craving even after long periods of abstinence
	- For addicts, drugs release less dopamine, but cues associated with the drug release more
- Tolerance:
	- Decrease in effect as an addiction develops
	- Drug tolerance is learned
- Withdrawal
	- Body’s reaction to the absence of the drug
	- One hypothesis is that addictive behavior is an attempt to avoid withdrawal symptoms
- Theories:
	- Negative reinforcement theory:
		- Taking drugs to counteract withdrawal symptoms or a negative state
		- Problems with this theory:
			- Highly addictive drugs like nicotine and cocaine do not produce withdrawal
			- Addicts relapse long after withdrawal symptoms are gone
			- Some drugs like tricyclic antidepressants produce withdrawal but do not cause addiction
	- Positive reinforcement theory:
		- Taking drugs because they produce euphoric pleasure
		- Problems with this theory:
			- Euphoric feelings often fade with use
			- Some addictive drugs do not produce pleasurable feelings
			- The destruction that addiction causes far outweighs any pleasure from the drug
	- Incentive-sensitization theory of addiction:
		- **Generally accepted theory of addiction**
		- Mesolimbic dopaminergic system (MLDS) becomes sensitized to drugs and stimuli associated with the drug
		- When the MLDS is activated, it creates strong cravings for the drug
		- Classical conditioning of stimuli associated with drugs 
		- Instrumental conditioning of increased craving when the stimuli are present
		- Sensitization is long-lasting, far beyond withdrawal symptoms
		- Dorsal striatum functions during the learning process
- Stimulant drugs:
	- Cocaine:
		- Psychostimulant that causes increased arousal, euphoria, confidence, and hypersexuality
		- **Indirect agonist**: acts on dopamine, preventing reuptake
		- It is derived from dried coca leaves, creating cocaine hydrochloride
		- Methods of intake: snorting, smoking, injection
		- Crack cocaine:
			- Cocaine mixed with a base and something crystalized
			- It is more addictive because of its fast action
			- Methods of intake: smoking
	- Amphetamine:
		- **Indirect agonist**: acts on dopamine, diffusing into the presynaptic neuron and causing dopamine release from transporter - can also reduce amount of dopamine transporters
		- Highly addictive
		- Adderall is an amphetamine salt
		- Methods of intake: pill, snorting, smoking
	- Methamphetamine:
		- Crystalized form of amphetamine 
		- Symptoms of addiction: teeth grinding, heart problems, tremors, anxiety, bad skin, and overdose
		- Methods of intake: smoking
- Alcohol use disorder (AUD):
	- Habitual and uncontrollable use of alcohol 
	- 15 million adults in the US suffer from AUD
	- Most prolifically used, and most accessible, and by far the most harmful
	- Resulting health issues: liver damage, heart disease, digestive problems, cancer
	- Alcohol:
		- GABA agonist (agonist for glycine, antagonist for glutamate)
		- Works on GABA-A receptors
		- Acts similarly to alprazolam
		- GABA-ergic neurons inhibit neurons that inhibit neurons → alcohol consumption increases dopamine levels
- Genetic changes in SUD and AUD:
	- CREB:
		- Increases in the nucleus accumbens with addiction
		- CREB activates genes that produce dynorphin, which reduces a drug’s euphoria, causing addicts to increase their dosage
		- CREB declines quickly when an addict stops taking drugs
	- Delta-FosB
		- Released with drug intake
		- Causes sensitization of cravings, including stimuli that induce cravings
		- Increases BDNF levels, which causes dendritic branching and spine formation, facilitating learning
		- Delta-FosB remains for a long time even after an addict stops taking drugs, and influences relapse

## Chapter Nine: Internal Regulation
**9.1**
*Homeostasis*
- Introduced in 1929 by Cannon
- Biological processes that keep variables (temperature, WBC count, etc) within a fixed range
- Some values change over time (over a month, throughout the day) including weight and temperature
- Key terms:
	- Set point: single value that the body maintains through homeostasis
	- Negative feedback: processes that reduce discrepancies from the set point
	- Allostasis: dynamic changes to the set point that anticipate a need before it occurs

*Temperature regulation*
- Basal metabolism: the energy required to maintain constant body temperature
	- Metabolism occurs in brown adipose cells, which burn fuel like muscle cells and release it as heat
- Two categories of temperature regulation:
	- Ectothermy
		- “Cold blooded” - amphibians, reptiles, and fish
		- Using external resources to maintain body heat
		- Lower constancy in temperature
	- Endothermy
		- “Warm blooded” - mammals and birds
		- Using physiological mechanisms to maintain body heat
			- Heat is generated in proportion to body mass
			- Heat is radiated in proportion to surface area
		- Higher constancy in temperature
		- Endothermy allows mamvmals and birds to stay warm at all times
		- 37 C is an advantageous natural body temperature
			- Having a warm body temperature allows for rapid movement
			- Higher than 41 C, proteins denature and lose their function
- Methods of temperature regulation:
	- Evaporation - cooling
		- Only method that cools the body
		- Humans sweat to reduce temperature
		- Other animals pant or lick themselves (??)
		- Risks include dehydration, heat exhaustion, even organ damage
	- Muscle activity - warming
		- Muscle contractions (shivering etc) generate heat
		- Decreased blood flow to the skin keeps the internal organs warm
		- Many mammals fluff up their fur - humans inherited goosebumps
	- Behavioral methods
		- Putting on or taking off clothes
		- Changing level of activeness
		- Huddling with others
- Brain mechanisms
	- Hypothalamus regulations many temperature changes
		- Preoptic area/anterior hypothalamus (POA/AH area) sends output to the raphe nucleus (located in the hindbrain), controlling autonomic responses (shivering, sweating, heart rate, blood flow, metabolism, etc)
		- Temperature changes in both the skin and hypothalamus lead to behavioral responses to change temperature
- Fever's effect on temperature
	- During an infection the immune system releases prostaglandins and histamines 
	- This causes the POA/AH to raise the set point for temperature
	- Fever is something produced by the body to fight infection and make the immune system more efficient

- Humans are made of 70% water
- In dire times, we conserve water by concentrating urine and decreasing sweat

		- When large amounts of bodily fluids are lost, the body becomes starved for water
		- Kidneys and blood vessels trigger the SFO and OVLT

		- Another hormone, angiotensin II, also constricts blood vessels and triggers thirst
		- Lost salts must also be replenished, so sodium-specific hunger is triggered
		- Aldosterone, released by adrenal glands, triggers retention of and craving for salt

## Chapter Twelve: Learning, Memory, and Intelligence

*Learning*
- Typical parameters of learning:
	- Classical conditioning: provoked by pairing two stimuli, one of which evokes an automatic response
		- Conditioned stimulus (CS): stimulus that initially does not provoke a response

		- Unconditioned response (UCR): response elicited by unconditioned stimulus
	- Operant (instrumental) conditioning: response results in a punishment or reinforcement
		- Reinforcer: result that increases the future probability of a response
		- Punishment: result that decreases the future probability of a response

		- Events that occurred recently

		- Cannot be retrieved if lost

*Learning & memory*
- Hebbian synapses
	- Hebbian synapse: synapse whose effectiveness is increased due to simultaneous activity in the pre- and post-synaptic neurons
	- Donald Hebb proposed a theory: when axon of neuron A repeatedly fires neuron B, growth takes place in both cells that improves the ability of axon A to excite cell B
	- Larry Katz: “neurons that fire together, wire together”
- Habituation
	- Decrease in response to a repeated stimulus
- Sensitization 
	- Greater response to mild stimuli, caused by exposure to more intense stimuli
- Long-term potentiation: axons connected to a dendrite bombard it with a rapid series of stimuli, leading to lasting potentiation 
	- Occurs most often in the hippocampus, at GABA synapses
	- It also occurs at NMDA and AMPA ionotropic receptors
		- When glutamate stimulates AMPA receptors, NMDA receptors are also activated
		- Ca enters the cell and changes gene expression, promoting synapses
		- After LTP, NMDA receptors revert to normal
	- Potentiation releases brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)
	- Properties of LTP
		- Specificity: exclusively activated synapses are strengthened - associated with protein aCaMKII
		- Cooperativity: concurrent stimulation by multiple axons produces stronger LTP than repeated stimulation by one axon
		- Associativity: pairing of a weak input with a stronger input enhances later response to the weak input
- Long-term depression: prolonged decrease in response at a synapse

## Chapter Fourteen: Psychopathology
**14.1**
*Substance Abuse*
- Abusing substances?? I would never do that, I love substances!
- Types of drugs
	- Agonists: drug that mimics or increases a neurotransmitter
	- Antagonist: drug that blocks a neurotransmitter
	- Affinity: drug’s ability to bind to a receptor
	- Efficacy: drug’s tendency to activate a receptor
- Dopamine
	- Reinforcement releases dopamine in the nucleus accumbens:![[Pasted image 20250402170234.png]]
	- Many drugs have an effect on dopamine
		- Cocaine and meth increase the release of dopamine in the NA
		- Nicotine stimulates the neurons that inhibit dopamine
		- Opiates inhibits the neurons that inhibit dopamine
		- Sex releases dopamine in the NA (rape, however, does not)
		- Music and sweet food also increase dopamine
		- Gambling and gaming activate the NA
	- Dopamine causes cravings, not pleasure
		- Craving: insistent search consistent with addiction
		- Cues associated with the drug of choice will incite cravings
		- Over time, drug use/recreation will cause the addictive behavior to produce less dopamine, as well as making the NA less response to other stimuli
- Tolerance
	- Phenomenon where effects of drugs decrease as addiction progresses
	- Allows users and addicts to take far higher doses than normal
	- Tolerance is learned
- Withdrawal
	- A reaction that occurs in addicts/users when drug use is halted
	- Symptoms in opiate withdrawal: anxiety, sweating, vomiting, diarrhea
	- Symptoms in alcohol withdrawal: irritability, fatigue, shaking, sweating, nausea
- Predispositions
	- Etiology of addiction: maternal drug use during pregnancy, childhood abuse, childhood neglect, family history, genetics, life difficulties 
	- Genetic influences
		- Several genes affect the likelihood of developing substance abuse
		- One gene affects the metabolism of alcohol (gene that produces less acetaldehyde dehydrogenase to metabolize acetaldehyde in ethyl alcohol)
	- Behavioral influences
		- Alcoholism is more common in impulsive, risk-taking, bored, thrill-seeking, outgoing people
		- Heavyweights are more likely to become alcoholics
- Treatments
	- Psychotherapy
	- Participation in groups like AA
	- Antabuse (medication that antagonizes acetaldehyde dehydrogenase, so that alcohol cannot be digested)
	- Methadone (drug similar to heroin that can allow addicts to ween off of opiates and onto something less intense)
	- Levomethadyl acetate (longer-lasting medication also used to treat opiate addictions)

**14.2**
*Mood Disorders*
- Major Depressive Disorder
	- Prolonged feelings of hopelessness, frustration, emptiness, and apathy
	- Depression is marked be negative bias: tendency to interpret everything in a negative way
	- Depression typically consists of depressive episodes, interspersed with periods of normal mood 
	- Cognitive symptoms include worsened attention, lower motivation, and impaired memory
		- These symptoms persist even if depression is successfully treated
	- Patients with depression also struggle with sleep issues, either insomnia or hypersomnia
		- Depressive patients enter REM sleep earlier than healthy individuals, and wake earlier: ![[Pasted image 20250402173259.png]]
	- Etiology: genetics, life events, pain (which decreases dopamine activity), stress
		- Twin studies show moderate heritability
		- 17 genes affect rates of depression
	- Treatment
		- Antidepressants
			- Types
				- Tricyclics
					- Block transporter proteins that reabsorb serotonin, dopamine, and norep into the presynaptic neuron after release
					- Block histamine receptors, ACh receptors, and some Na channels
					- Can cause dangerous cardiac side effects
				- Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors
					- Block serotonin from binding to serotonin transporter protein
					- Side effects are relatively mild
					- Serotonin norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors block both serotonin and norep
				- Monoamine oxidase inhibitors
					- Block monoamine oxidase, which breaks catecholamines and serotonin down
					- Negative interactions with tyramine
			- Mechanisms of antidepressants
				- Besides acting on specific neurotransmitters, antidepressant drugs also facilitate neurotrophins, especially BDNF (which is lower in depressive patients)
				- Attachment to BDNF receptors is slow, resulting in the slow course of antidepressants 
			- Efficacy
				- Antidepressants are generally more effective than placebos, but not by much
				- There is little evidence that different doses of SSRIs produce different effects
		- Psychedelics, such as MDMA or ketamine, could be a possible treatment
			- Ketamine both facilitates BDNF and blocks NMDA-type receptors in the lateral habenula
			- Strong negative side effects are associated with psychedelics
			- Higher doses of ketamine can induce episodes of schizophrenia (????)
		- Psychotherapy
			- Cognitive behavioral therapy is a common treatment route
			- Advantages: no side effects, long-lasting treatment
		- Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)
			- Epilepsy and schizophrenia are negatively correlated - inducing convulsions can reduce symptoms of schizophrenia
			- ECT does not work for schizophrenia, but it does alleviate treatment-resistant depression
			- ECT increases BDNF and neurogenesis
		- Deep brain stimulation (DBS)
			- Small stimulator is implanted in the brain to activate certain (sub)cortical areas 
		- Exercise can also help
		- Sleep deprivation has a short-lived but intensely positive affect on depression
- Bipolar Disorder
	- Patients alternate between depression and mania (restless activity, excitement, excessive self confidence, rambling speech, loss of inhibitions) 
	- Bipolar Type I: full-fledged manic episodes
	- Bipolar Type II: mild hypomanic episodes
	- Decrease in glucose during depression; increase during mania
	- Etiology: genetics, high creativity, childhood abuse
		- Twin studies have showed heritability for bipolar
	- Treatments:
		- Lithium salts (stabilize moods)
		- Better sleep (decreases the risk of episodes)

**14.4**
*Autism Spectrum Disorder*
- Symptoms: deficits in sociability, deficits in facial expression, repetitive movements or behaviors, resistance to change, abnormal responses to stimuli
- Comorbidities: sleep disorders, epilepsy, anxiety, poor coordination, lower intellect
- Autism can appear at as young as 9 months
- Etiology: genetics, prenatal exposure to toxins or infection, family history of autoimmune disorders
	- Autism can often be traced to a single gene
	- It can also be caused by de novo mutations
- Neural growth in autism
	- Brains grow more quickly than normal at a young age in autistic patients
	- In adolescence, this is combated by intense synaptic pruning
	- Genetic mutations cause increased sensitivity of the somatosensory cortex
- No current treatments are known
