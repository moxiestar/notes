***Genetics***
- DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) codes for proteins
- Broken down into four bases: adenine, thymine, cytosine, guanine
- Chromosomes result from DNA being wrapped around histones
	- Histones - four proteins with tails
	- Epigenetic markers are located on the tails
- Alleles
	- Two forms of the same gene
	- One found on each chromosome - inherited from each parent
	- Either dominant or recessive
		- Dominant genes act in both recessive and dominant conditions
		- Recessive genes only act in the heterozygous condition
- Homozygous: two alleles of the same type (both dominant, both recessive)
- Heterozygous: one dominant and one recessive allele
- Genotype: allele formation (EE, Ee, ee)
- Phenotype: physical traits or appearance caused by genes
	- Intermediate gene occurs in a phenotype when there is incomplete dominance in the heterozygous condition - results in a mixture of the two properties
- Mendelian genetics
	- Mendel demonstrated that inheritance occurs through discrete units of heredity (genes)
	- Law of segregation
		- Organisms contain two alleles for each gene, and these alleles separate in gametes
	- Law of independent assortment
		- Alleles for different genes are passed to the offspring independent of each other - basic probability 
- DNA -> RNA -> proteins
	- Transcription (DNA -> RNA): each base is copied over into one strand of RNA, with T being replaced by U
	- Translation (RNA -> proteins): The triplet bases in RNA determine codons and amino acids in proteins
- Nature vs Nurture
	- Nature
		- Physical and mental traits are rooted in genetics
		- Ultimate cause of behavior: genes, evolution, adaptation
	- Nurture
		- Physical and mental traits are rooted in experience
		- Proximal cause of behavior: learning, experiences, socialization
	- Influence of nurture - PKU (phenylketonuria)
		- Genetic disease caused by one mutation 
		- Lack of enzyme to convert phenylalanine into tyrosine (which can be converted into dopamine)
		- Phenylalanine builds up and leads to severe mental disabilities
- Genetic change
	- Mutation: heritable changes in DNA molecules
	- Microduplication/microdeletion: part of a chromosome is deleted or duplicated
		- Some researchers believe schizophrenia is a result of microduplications and microdeletions of neurological genes
- Epigenetics 
	- Study of gene expression (with modification of the DNA sequence)
	- Some genes are only active at a certain point or time in one's life
	- Changes in gene expression are critical for learning and memory
	- Epigenetic differences are likely behind the differences between identical twins
	- Modifications to gene expression
		- Methyl groups
			- Tag DNA transcription
			- Can cause the DNA to be wound too tightly on histones, preventing transcription
		- Acetyl groups
			- Can loosen DNA, allowing transcription
	- Epigenetics can be affected by 
		- Environmental chemicals
		- Development
		- Drugs
		- Aging
		- Diet
	- Mouse licking study
		- Attentive mouse mothers help affect methyl groups near the GR gene
		- The GR gene affects stress hormones
		- Mice with attentive mothers handle stress better as adult mice
	- Överkalix study
		- Kept excellent records of health, birth, and death
		- Cycles of famine were observed in the records
		- Descendants of children who had experienced famine had less heart disease and diabetes
- Behavioral genetics
	- Research on the genetic influences of behavior
	- Genome-wide association studies, twin studies
	- Fast-growing field in psych and bio
- Heritability
	- Statistic looking at the proportion of variation that is due to genetics or the environment
	- Can be difficult to assess
	- Twin studies, correlation between physical and behavioral measures
- Concordances
	- Research of probabilities of whether relatives will develop neuropsychiatric disorders

*Mendelian Genetics*
- Mendel's experiments in the 1860s proved the existence of genes
	- Genes: heritable units that define traits across generations
		- Genes are composed of DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid)
			- DNA serves as a template for RNA (ribonucleic acid)
				- mRNA serves as a template for protein creation
			- DNA has four bases, ATCG (adenine, thymine, cytosine, guanine)
			- RNA has four bases, AUCG (adenine, uracil, cytosine, guanine)
- Protein synthesis:

![[Pasted image 20250210132328.png]]

- Chromosomes: paired strands of genes
	- Homozygous gene: same gene is present in both chromosomes
	- Heterozygous gene: different gene is present in each chromosome
- Genes can be dominant, recessive, or intermediate
	- Dominant genes: strong effect in both homozygous and heterozygous conditions
	- Recessive genes: strong effect in only the homozygous condition
- Genes can be sex-linked or autosomal
	- Autosomal genes: all genes other than X and Y chromosomes
	- Sex-linked genes: X and Y chromosomes
		- Females have XX chromosomes
		- Males have XY chromosomes
		- Most genes are X-linked, inherited from the X chromosome
	- Sex-limited genes: genes present in both sexes but active in only one
- Genetic changes
	- Mutations: changing the sequence of a DNA molecule
		- Bases can be changed, deleted, or substituted
		- This leads to the creation of a different mRNA strand and a different protein
	- Microduplications and microdeletions occur in chromosomes
- Epigenetics: study of gene expression
	- DNA is bound by histone molecules
		- It is wound around a nucleosome, the core of histone molecules
		- Adding different groups can facilitate or stop the expression of different genes
			- Acetyl groups facilitate gene expression
			- Methyl groups silence genes
	- Epigenetic changes (when one generation is conditioned to express a certain gene) can be inherited
- Heritability: traits that depend largely on gene expression
	- Heritability is studied through twin studies
		- Monozygotic (identical) twins vs dizygotic (fraternal) twins
	- Nature vs nurture - research has found that most traits depend on heritability

*Evolution of Behavior*
- Evolution: changes in the frequencies of genes over generations
	- Genes associated with higher reproductive success will be passed on 
- Artificial selection: purposeful breeding of more advantageous parents
- Lamarckian evolution: faulty idea that use of a certain limb/feature leads to genetic changes
- Evolutionary fitness: the number of copies of one's genes that are carried on to the next generation
- Evolutionary psychology: study of evolution of behaviors
	- Altruistic behavior - unique to humans? Why did this evolve?
		- Kin selection: selection of a gene that benefits an individual's relatives
		- Reciprocal altruism: helping someone else will cause them to help me too
		- Group selection: altruistic groups are more successful than uncooperative groups

***Development***
- CNS begins to form on day 14 of embryonic development
- Dorsal surface of the neural plate thickens, forming the neural tube, filled with CSF
- Part differentiates into the brain, other part differentiates in the spinal cord
- Stages of development
	- Proliferation - production of new cells
	- Migration - movement of new cells to their locations
	- Differentiation - formation of axons and dendrites
	- Myelination - production of myelin sheath
	- Synaptogenesis - creation of synaptic connections
- Brain development: axonal pathfinding
	- Sperry's experiment - rewiring the retinas of lizards 
	- Retinas returned to their original locations 
	- Neurotrophins are responsible for pathfinding
		- Two key neurotrophins - NGF and BDNF

*Brain Development*
- Homeobox genes control the start of anatomical development
- Development of the CNS
	- 2 weeks: neural tube
	- 3-11 weeks: Neural tube enlarges into the hindbrain, midbrain, and forebrain
	- Central canal of the spinal cord, as well as the ventricles, develops
- Newborn brains weigh about 350g; 1 year old brains weigh about 1000g
	- Adult weight is around 1300g

![[Pasted image 20250210155557.png]]

*Neuronal development*
- Proliferation: production of new cells
	- Cells lining the ventricles divide
		- Some remain in the ventricles, becoming stem cells
		- Some migrate across the CNS, aided by immunoglobulins and chemokines
	- Neurons develop over the span of about 2 years
- Synaptogenesis begins before birth and continues throughout the lifespan
- Myelination begins in the spinal cord before progressing to the brain
- The human brain does not develop new neurons after birth, except for the stem cells in the olfactory bulb

*Neuronal survival*
- Muscles determine how many axons survive
- NGF (nerve growth factor) from the muscle promotes the axon's growth
	- An axon that does not receive NGF goes through apoptosis
	- NGF is a neurotrophin (a chemical that promotes the survival of neurons)
- Exposure to sickness or toxins during development, particularly gastrulation, is catastrophic
	- Fetal alcohol syndrome is caused by excessive maternal drinking
	- Alcohol causes apoptosis, halts neuron migration, impairs synaptic transmission
- Dendritic development
	- Animals raised in "enriched" environments developed more dendritic branches
	- Far transfer: studying one skill in the hopes of improving another skill
		- Far transfer has a weak effect - little evidence of its benefit
- Damage to one area of the cortex will result in that area's function being transferred elsewhere
	- For example: in a study where blind people were asked to differentiate Braille symbols, their occipital lobes showed activity
	- When the occipital lobe was temporarily lesioned, the subjects were unable to differentiate the symbols, showing that their occipital lobes (typically used for vision) were being used for the sense of touch
- Learning and practicing new skills changes brain anatomy
	- Focal hand dystonia: inability to move one finger at once

*Brain development over time*
- Adolescents are generally more impulsive than adults
	- The prefrontal cortex, responsible for inhibiting behaviors, is not fully developed until ~25
	- The reward pathway is also stronger in adolescents, and gets stronger over the teenage years
- Old age
	- Memory and reasoning fade
	- Cortical tissue shrinks
	- Synaptic transmission slows

***Vision***
- Light - form of electromagnetic energy
	- Visible light wavelengths for humans: 380-760nm
	- Amplitude is correlated with brightness (higher amplitude -> brighter light)
	- Wavelength is correlated with hue
	- Purity is correlated with saturation

*Anatomy of the eye*
- Path of light
	- Images enter the eye from the pupil 
	- It then hits the retina, where it is inverted
		- Within the retina, it goes from ganglion -> bipolar -> photoreceptors
		- It then travels back to the ganglion cells
		- The ganglion cells transmit to the optic nerve
		- The optic nerve transmits to the visual cortex
- Cranial nerves 
	- II. Optic (vision)
	- III. Oculomotor (eye movements - all eye muscles except for superior oblique and lateral rectus)
	- IV. Trochlear (eye movements - superior oblique)
	- VI. Abducens (eye movements - lateral rectus)
- Retinal anatomy
	- Blind spots occur due to concentration of axons in an area - optic disk
	- Five primary layers
		- *Receptors*
			- Rods - scotopic vision
				- Specialized for dim light, night vision
				- Slow recovery time
				- Multiple rods transmit with one bipolar cell
				- More dense (120 million)
				- Located in the periphery
				- Inhibitory cells - light decreases their activity
			- Cones - photopic vision
				- Specialized for daylight, color
				- Fast recovery time
				- One cone transmits to one bipolar cell
				- Less dense (6 million)
				- Located in the fovea
		- Horizontal cells
			- Responsible for **lateral inhibition**
				- Horizontal cells inhibit their neighbors, producing contrast in vision
				- Receptors excite both horizontal cells and bipolar cells
		- *Bipolar cells
			- Signal transmission
		- Amacrine cells
			- Refine signal transmission
		- *Ganglion cells* (give rise to the optic nerve)
			- **Only cells in the retina that can fire an action potential**
	- Fovea
		- Depression in the central portion of the retina
		- Allows for acute and detailed vision
		- Dense with receptors, especially cones
			- Each receptor in the fovea attaches to a single bipolar cell and a single ganglion cell (midget ganglion cell)
			- Small receptive field: one-to-one connection

*Optic nerve anatomy*
- Optic chiasm: where the left visual field and right visual field meet
- Optic pathways:
	- Left visual fields from both eyes go to the right side of the brain
	- Right visual fields from both eyes go to the left side of the brain
- Visual information is sent to the lateral geniculate nucleus in the thalamus
- From there it communicates to the primary visual cortex (striate cortex)

*Lateral geniculate nucleus*
- LGN ganglion cells
- Information from the contralateral eye goes to LGN layers 1, 4, 6
- Information from the ipsilateral eye goes to LGN layers 2, 3, 5
- Magnocellular pathway: layers 1, 2
	- Encodes the "where"
	- Information from parasol ganglion cells
	- Encodes color
	- Only communicates with LGN
	- Small receptive fields
	- Connects to the dorsal pathway
- Parvocellular pathway: layers 3, 4, 5, 6
	- Encodes the "what"
	- Information from midget retinal ganglion cells
	- Encodes movement and overall patterns
	- Communicates with LGN and other thalamic areas
	- Large receptive fields
	- Connects to the ventral pathway

*Visual cortex*
- Located in occipital lobe
- Communicates with frontal lobe in about 1/5 of a second
- Primary visual cortex (striate cortex, V1)
	- Input from LGN
	- Output to all other layers of the visual system
	- Each hemisphere represents the contralateral visual field
	- Layers 1, 2, 3, 5, and 6 - pyramidal neurons
	- Layer 4 - interneurons
		- Primarily receives LGN inputs
		- Ocular dominance columns
			- Eye inputs are segregated
			- Each eye has a dominant column
			- Alternating columns of ocular dominance throughout layer IV
- Extrastriate visual cortical areas (V2-5)
- Orientation columns
	- Cells have preferred orientation
	- Cells with the same orientation preferences are organized into columns
	- Cause of course they are
	- Preference for neighboring columns are not random
- Receptive fields
	- The point in space where light enters the retina; and from there strikes a rod/cone
	- Other visual cells derive their receptive fields from the connections they receive
- Types of cells
	- Simple cells: respond to orientation
	- Complex cells: respond to a moving stimulus of a particular orientation
	- Hypercomplex cells: similar to complex cells; have a strong inhibitory area at one end of their receptive fields
- Ventral temporal pathway
	- Encodes the "what"
	- Also known as the perception pathway
	- Important for identifying objects
	- Damage causes deficits to identification
	- Retinal cells: midget ganglion cells
	- LGN communication: parvocellular neurons
- Dorsal parietal pathway
	- Encodes the "where"
	- Also known as the action pathway
	- Important for visually guided movements
	- Damage causes deficits to integration with vision
	- Retinal cells: parasol ganglion neurons (larger receptive field)
	- LGN communication: magnocellular neurons

*Fusiform facial area (FFA)*
- Fusiform gyrus is located in the ventral temporal lobe 
- Structure might be specialized for facial recognition
	- When subject is shown facial stimuli, this area lights up in an fMRI 
	- Is this its primary function, or its only function?

*Vision*
- How far we see depends on how far light travels before it reaches our eyes
	- Light travels 93 million miles from the sun to reach us
	- We only see the rays that reflect off of objects and hit our retinas
- Sensation and sight are "felt" in the brain, not in the body/sensory organs

*Retinal Anatomy*
- Light enters the eye through the pupil (centre of iris)
- It is focused by the lens (adjustable) and the cornea (unadjustable)
- It is projected onto the retina (back surface of the eye)
- Images enter the eye in an upside-down mirror fashion

![[Pasted image 20250212115342.png]]
- Images travel from the retinal receptors to bipolar cells 
- Bipolar cells communicate with ganglion cells and amacrine cells
- Ganglion cells travel back to form the optic nerve
	- The point where it exits the eye is a blind spot
- Amacrine cells send signals to other bipolar cells, ganglion cells, and other amacrine cells

![[Pasted image 20250212123232.png]]

- Detailed vision is focused in the fovea, which lies in the centre of the retina
	- Midget ganglion cells: ganglions present in the fovea
		- Each ganglion cell responds to a single cone
		- Each cone in the fovea has a direct route to your brain! 
		- Midget ganglion cells provide 70% of input to the brain - vision is dominated by things seen in the fovea

*Retinal photoreceptors*
- Rods
	- More abundant in the periphery
	- 120 million rods in the retina
	- Optimized for low light
	- Bright light bleaches them
- Cones
	- More abundant near the fovea
	- 6 million cones in the retina
	- Optimized for bright light
	- Provide color vision
	- 90% of visual input
	- Colorblindness arises from errors in cone development
- Photopigments
	- Chemicals that release energy when struck by light
	- 11-cis-retinal is bound to opsins -> light -> all-trans-retinal bound to opsins

*Color vision*
- Visible light: 400-700nm (violet-red)
- Trichromatic theory (Young-Helmholtz): color is perceived by comparing responses across specified receptors
- Opponent process theory: color is perceived in terms of opposites (hence our perception of afterimages)
- Color constancy: the ability to recognize colors despite different environments/lighting
- Retinex theory: visual cortex compares information from various parts of the retina to determine the color and brightness of stimuli

***Movement***
Does movement require a nervous system?
- Sensory system facilitates movement and helps us navigate space
- Would we need senses if we could not move at all?
- Brain function: prediction-action

Three types of muscles
- Skeletal (striated) muscles - control the movement of the body in relation to the environment
- Smooth muscles - control the digestive system and internal organs
- Cardiac muscles - control the heart muscles

Muscle fibers
- Many individuals fibers
	- Each muscle fiber is innervated by one axon
	- One axon supplies more than one muscle fiber
- Neuromuscular junction - synapse between motor neuron axon and a muscle fiber
	- Release of ACh causes muscle contraction
- Muscles are primarily controlled by proprioceptors
	- Muscle spindles
		- Respond to stretch
		- Monitor muscle length
		- Cause contraction
		- Stretch reflex
			- Knee-jerk reflex
				- Stimulus: tendon tap stretches muscle
				- Receptor: muscle spindle stretches and fires
				- Afferent path: action potential travels through sensory neuron
				- Integrating centre: sensory neuron synapses in spinal cord
				- Efferent path 1: motor neuron → quadriceps muscle
				- Efferent path 2: interneuron inhibiting motor neuron → hamstring muscle
				- Response: quadriceps contracts, hamstring stays relaxed
	- Golgi tendon organs
		- Respond to muscle tension
		- Monitor muscle tension
			- Tendon reflex
				- Stimulus: increased tension 
				- Receptor: Golgi tendon fires
				- Afferent path: sensory neuron → inhibitory interneuron 
				- Integrating centre: inhibition in spinal cord
				- Efferent path: lower motor neuron inhibited
				- Response: muscle stays relaxed
- Flexor & extensor muscles
	- Antagonistic muscles that work in pairs
	- Flexor muscles raise limbs
	- Extensor muscles straighten limbs

Spinal transmission - **DAVE**
- **Dorsal - afferent, ventral - efferent**

Voluntary movements
- Upper & lower motor neuron involvment
- Guided by sensory feedback via thalamus
- Afferent pathway informs movement; efferent movement causes it
- Sensory cortex communicates with motor cortex

Pyramidal system
- Primary motor cortex
	- Active when a movement sequence is intended - “orders” an outcome
	- Primarily controls voluntary movements
	- Receives afferent information from sensorimotor cortices 
- Non-primary motor cortices
	- Posterior parietal cortex
		- Active when planning a movement
		- Damage causes trouble finding objects in space
	- Supplementary motor area
		- Initiates, plans, and organizes rapid sequence of complex movements
		- Performs inhibitory function when necessary 
		- Damage causes difficulty performing complex motor tasks
	- Premotor cortex
		- Plans motor sequences guided by external stimuli
		- Damage causes loss of motor skills (strength and ability are unaffected)
	- Prefrontal cortex
		- Active during a delay before movement and when planning movements
		- Damage causes disorganized movements (i.e., showering with clothes on)
			- Movements are disorganized practically/psychologically, not physically
- Spinal tracts
	- Lateral corticospinal tract
		- Axons from primary motor cortex
		- Contralateral tract (decussation in medullary pyramids)
		- Controls lateral parts of the body - extremities
	- Medial corticospinal tract
		- Axons from many parts of the cerebrum and midbrain
		- Bilateral tract (contra- and ipsilateral)
		- Controls medial parts of the body - torso, shoulders

Extra pyramidal system
- Basal ganglia
	- Functions

		- Golgi tendon organs

		- Ballistic movement - executed as a whole

	- Motor program: fixed sequence of movements

- Mirror neurons

	- Can be activated by simply thinking about the task

*Movement disorders*

	- Gradual death of cells in basal ganglia and cerebrum

***Sound & Audition*** 
- Sound waves: periodic compressions of air varying in amplitude and frequency 
	- Amplitude: intensity/volume
	- Frequency: pitch (number of compressions per second, measured in hertz)
	- Timbre: tone quality/complexity
- Human hearing: 15 Hz to 20,000 Hz

*Anatomy of the Ear*
- Outer ear
	- Pinna (visible ear outside the head) helps to locate sound
	- Auditory canal (ear hole) allows sound to travel deeper into the ear
- Middle ear
	- Tympanic membrane (eardrum) connects to three bones that transmit sound to the oval window
		- Three bones: hammer, anvil, and stirrup
- Inner ear
	- Oval window - membrane in the inner ear that, when vibrated, moves fluid in the cochlea
	- Cochlea (snail) is the home of auditory receptors 
	- Hair cells (auditory receptors) are located on the basilar membrane and stimulate the auditory nerve
![[Pasted image 20250222165338.jpg]]

*Pitch Perception*
- Two mechanisms for detecting frequencies:
	- Frequency theory:
		- Basilar membrane vibrates in synchrony with sound waves
		- Auditory nerve axons fire as many action potentials as the number of Hz indicates 
			- 50 Hz = 50 action potentials
	- Place theory:
		- Basilar membrane is tighter at the base (where the stirrup meets the cochlea) and looser at the peak (the apex of the cochlea)
		- Sounds vibrate hair cells at different locations along the membrane according to their frequency
			- Lower frequency sounds vibrate hair cells near the peak, and higher frequency sounds vibrate hair cells near the base

*Auditory cortex*
- Most auditory inputs cross over to the contralateral hemisphere
- Primary auditory cortex: area in temporal lobe where auditory information is processed
	- Responds to imagined sounds
	- Damage does not produce deafness - area is critical for processing, not audition 
- Auditory system has a ”what“ and “where“ pathway 
- Planum temporale: area in the temporal cortex that processes the input of a sound moving from one location to another
	- Strongly connected to area MT which processes visual motion
- Tonotopic map of sounds: cells responsive to similar frequencies group together

![[Pasted image 20250222171024.jpg]]

*Sound Localization*
- The time that a sound reaches the ear allows us to locate the sound source
- When underwater, it is difficult to localize low- and medium-frequency sounds because sound travels faster in water
- Low frequencies are localized by phase differences
	- Phase difference: the way sound waves reach the ear depends on location - they may be out of sync in different locations 
- High frequencies are localized by loudness differences
	- Sound shadow: when a wavelength is shorter than the width of the head, the sound is louder for the closer ear
		- Less accurate for lower frequencies (lower than 2,000 Hz)

*Individual Differences*
- Amusia: tone deafness
	- Inability to detect a difference between tones smaller than the difference between C and C#, detect off-key singing, detect a wrong note, or read someone’s mood based on tone of voice
	- Results from lower connections between the auditory cortex and frontal cortex
- Absolute (perfect) pitch: ability to hear a note and identify it
	- Genetics play a rote
	- Receiving early musical training helps
- Deafness: hearing loss
	- Conductive (middle-ear) deafness
		- Bones of the middle ear are prevented from transmitting sound to the cochlea
		- Can sometimes by corrected by surgery or hearing aids
		- Cochlea and auditory nerve are normal
	- Nerve (inner-ear) deafness
		- Cochlea, hair cells, or auditory nerve are damaged
		- Can be inherited, or caused by disease or loud noises 
	- Tinnitus
		- Constant ringing in the ears
		- Common among patients who have lost their hearing during their life
		- After hearing loss in a particular range, patients will often experience tinnitus in that range, suggesting that those axons are being stimulated in another way

***Vestibular system***
- During movement, the vestibular organ (next to the cochlea) compensates by correctly moving your eyes - typically in the opposite direction from the movement
- Damage to the vestibular system causes loss of balance and coordination 
- Vestibular organ
	- Saccule, utricle, and three semicircular canals 
		- The three canals are all perpendicular, filled with fluid, and lined with hair cells
		- Movement causes the fluid to shift, pressing against the hair cells
		- Saccule and utricle record the position of the head at rest
		- Semicircular canals only record the rate of acceleration 
	- Hair cells serve as receptors
	- Otoliths (Ca carbonate particles) move with the head and push against hair cells accordingly 

*Vestibular system*
- Structures
	- Semicircular canals
		- Functions: responding to rotational acceleration
		- Structures: anterior, lateral, posterior
		- Hair cells: ampulla - stereocilia are embedded in the cupula
	- Otolith organs (hair cells)
		- Functions: responding to linear acceleration
		- Structures: utricle, saccule
		- Ca carbonate particles that respond to head tilts and push against hair cells 
- Functions: balance, coordination, awareness of position and movement, adjustment of eye movements
- Pathologies: car sickness (disconnect between semicircular canals and otolith organs), vertigo
- Vestibular pathways
	- Vestibular receptors transmit to vestibular nuclei
	- Vestibular nuclei transmit to reticular formation, nuclei of the eye muscles, cerebellum, thalamus, vestibular cortex (located above lateral fissure)

***Somatosensory system***
- Somatosensation encompasses pain, touch, pressure, heat, etc
- Variety of different sensory organs
	- Pacinian corpuscles: receptors that respond to vibrations or displacements on the skin
	- Cold-sensitive neurons in the spinal cord are coded to detect decreases in temperature 
	- Heat-sensitive neurons in the spinal cord are coded to detect constant temperatures
	- Capsaicin: chemical that stimulates the receptors for painful heat
- Sensory information is received via spinal nerves, each of which innervates a dermatome (specific area of the body)
- Different areas in the sensory cortex respond to different types of sensations
	- Damage to sensory cortex impairs body perceptions
- Numb-sense - phenomenon where, during damage to the sensory cortex, people can correctly identify the location of a touch but insist that they do not actually feel the touch

*Somatosensory system*
- Stimuli
	- Discriminative touch
	- Pressure
	- Temperature
	- Tissue damage
	- Noxious stimuli
	- Body position
- Receptors (classified by structure, location, receptive fields, speed)
	- Cutaneous receptors:
		- Meissner’s corpuscle

		- Pacinian corpuscle

			- Smaller receptive fields
		- Nerve endings 
			- Coded for pain, temperature
	- Adaptation rates in receptors
		- Tonic receptors undergo little adaptation
		- Phasic receptors have a rapid decrease in response

***Pain***
- Pain and depression are closely linked
- Women experience more pain than men (told you so)
	- Women and men react differently to pain (didn’t need a researcher to know that)
- Spinal pathways for pain and temperature
	- Pain is perceived via nerve endings
	- Axons for pain are thin and usually unmyelinated; they conduct slowly
		- Thicker axons convey sharp/first pain, while thinner axons convey dull/second pain
	- Mild pain triggers glutamate, while stronger pain triggers both glutamate and neuropeptides (substance P, CGRP)
	- Pain & temperature pathway decussates as soon as it enters the spinal cord from the receptors 
		- Cf. touch pathway - only decussates in medulla
	- Pain is processed in the thalamus, somatosensory cortex, amygdala, hypothalamus, and anterior cingulate cortex (last three code unpleasant emotional pain)
- Emotional pain
	- Emotional pain correlates with increased cingulate cortex activity
	- Pain-relieving drugs relieve emotional pain (????)
- Pain relief
	- CIPA prevents patients from feeling pain
	- Opioid mechanisms - systems that respond to opiates and relieve pain
		- Opiates bind to receptors in the spinal cord and periaqueductal grey
		- Endorphins: opiate-type chemicals found naturally within the brain
		- Inescapable pain is potent in stimulating endorphins and inhibiting further pain
		- Morphine is ineffective against sharp/first pain
- Gate theory of pain
	- Neurons that receive pain inputs also receive other inputs that can override the pain, such as pressure
	- Cannabinoids have been used to treat neuropathic pain 
		- After an injury, levels of histamine and nerve growth factor are up 
		- If sensory axons are destroyed, pain cells take over in their place
		- These two factors lead to neuropathic pain
	- Capsaicin has also been used for pain relief
- Placebo effect
	- When patients receive drugs/procedures with no pharmacological effects but are told that the drugs/procedures will help their condition, they show improvement 
	- Nocebo effect: negative effect shown if a patient expects to have an unpleasant reaction to a drug/procedure

***Taste***
- Taste is not seen across all species, and is only useful for determining whether it is safe to consume something
	- Flavor is the combination of taste and smell 
	- Taste and smell axons converge onto many of the same cortical areas
- Taste receptors are located in taste buds on the tongue
	- Taste buds are modified skin cells with excitable membranes - they release NTs to neighboring neurons
	- They are replaced every 10-14 days, like skin cells
	- Taste buds are located in papillae (bumps) on the surface of the tongue, with 10 taste buds to each papilla and 50 receptors to each taste bud
	- Four types of receptors (sweet, sour, salty, bitter)
		- Some suggest a fifth (MSG or umami)
	- Mechanisms of taste receptors
		- Salt receptor allows Na ions to cross the membrane
		- Sweet, bitter, and umami receptors resemble metabotropic receptors (G protein is activated)
		- Sour and salty receptors resemble ionotropic receptors
		- 30 bitter receptors are found within the tongue
		- Bitterness is defined as something that’s somewhat toxic, but edible
- Taste processing
	- Taste is processed in the insula
	- Taste cells are not organized in groups
- Individual differences
	- Supertasters: those with higher numbers of fungiform papillae clustered close together on the anterior tongue
		- Tasters have an average number, while non-tasters have less
		- Genetics have an effect, but the variations in taste depend on age and hormones too
		- Supertasters are more sensitive to strongly flavored foods

***Olfaction***
- Sense of smell - reaction to chemicals in the membranes of the nose
- Olfaction is important for eating - smell affects flavor
- Olfactory receptors are located on the cilia of the olfactory cells in the olfactory epithelium 
- Orthonasal olfaction: smelling a scent in the air
- Retronasal olfaction: smelling a scent from the food in your mouth
- Humans have several hundred types of olfactory receptors 
- Olfactory receptors last for about a month and are replaced by stem cells
- Olfactory processing 
	- Receptors sensitive to particular chemicals are grouped together and connect to clusters (glomeruli) in the olfactory bulb 
	- In the piriform (olfactory) cortex, cells respond to a combination of chemicals and their responses continually change over time
- Individual differences
	- Olfaction declines with age
	- Genetics have an effect on olfaction
	- Women have better senses of smell than men

*Synesthesia*
- Condition where senses overlap, and a stimulus in one sense can evoke another sense (i.e., numbers can have colors)
- Correlated with absolute pitch
- Genetics have an effect
- Theory: axons from one cortex branch into another

Olfaction:
- Stimuli: odorants (volatile molecular compounds)
	- Infinite number found in the environment; humans can recognize 10,000
	- No higher cortex or clear physical properties to be mapped in the brain
- Olfaction is critical in mammals for finding food and mates, and avoiding danger
- Olfaction and gustation are intertwined 
	- Chemoreceptors: olfactory and gustatory receptors
	- Olfactory and gustatory axons converge onto many of the same cells in the endopiriform nucleus
- Nasal anatomy:
	- Olfactory mucosa: upper region of the nasal cavity, made up of nasal epithelium
	- Olfactory cilia: part of olfactory bipolar receptor cells that protrude through the olfactory mucosa
	- Receptors typically last for 1 month
	- There are about 6 million receptors and 300 types
- Olfactory pathway:
	- Receptors → olfactory bulb → olfactory tract → olfactory cortex, hypothalamus, etc
		- 1. Sensory neurons within nose are stimulated by odorants
		- 2. Axons leave the olfactory epithelium and penetrate the cribiform plate of the ethmoid
		- 3. First synapse occurs in the olfactory bulb
		- 4. The axons then travel along the olfactory tract to reach the olfactory cortex, the hypothalamus, and the limbic system (hence the emotional response to cells)
	- Within the olfactory epithelium, receptors are randomly intermingled
	- Same types of receptor neurons converge on the same glomeruli in the olfactory bulb
- Olfactory receptor mechanisms:
	- Odorant binds to olfactory receptors
	- G protein is activated
	- Adenylate cyclase is activated
	- cAMP level increases
	- Ca-activated Cl channels open
	- Membrane depolarizes
- Higher processing centers:
	- Ipsilateral olfactory bulb
	- Piriform cortex
	- Amygdala
	- Hypothalamus
	- Thalamus
	- Hippocampus
	- Orbitofrontal cortex

***Audition***
- Human hearing range: 20 Hz (17m) - 20 kHz (1.7cm)
- Frequency = pitch
- Amplitude = volume
- Purity = timbre (relates to sine)
- Ear anatomy
	- Outer ear
		- Structures: pinna (visible ear on head)
		- Collects sound waves
	- Middle ear
		- Structures: tympanic membrane (ear drum), ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes), oval window, round window
		- Converts and amplifies sound waves to mechanical vibrations
			- Ear drum converts, ossicles amplify
	- Inner ear
		- Structures: cochlea, semicircular canals
			- Cochlea
				- Basilar membrane
					- Wider at the peak than the base
					- Tonotopic organization - different parts of the cochlea detect different frequencies
					- Higher frequencies are transmitted at the base (narrower), lower frequencies are transmitted at the peak (wider)
				- Organ of Corti
					- Converts vibration into neural activity
					- Structures: hair cells (receptors), tectorial membrane, cochlear nerve
					- Sound travels up and back down the cochlea, encoding frequency and generating a traveling wave
					- Stereocilia (30-300 per hair cell) on hair cells detect vibrations through the fluid (endolymph)
				- Hair cells
					- Cochlear
						- Inner hair cells
						- Outer hair cells
							- Actively contract in response to changes in voltage
					- Vestibular (balance)
						- Ampulla of the semicircular ducts - rotational acceleration
						- Otolith organs (saccule and utricle) - linear acceleration
		- Transmits vibrations as neural impulses

- Theories of audition
	- Place theory: each area on the basilar membrane has receptors sensitive to one specific frequency
		- Issue: neurons cannot fire at 20,000 kHz
		- Phase-locking: the neurons fire together in a pattern to reproduce a higher frequency
	- Frequency theory: the basilar membrane vibrates together with the sound and causes axons to produce APs at the same frequency
- Auditory cortices
	- Primary auditory cortex (A1)
		- Located in superior temporal cortex (within the insula)
		- Organized by frequencies - different areas respond to different pitches
	- Secondary auditory cortex
		- Located in the superior temporal cortex (top of lateral fissure)
		- Provides higher level processing to identify sounds
- Auditory pathway
	- Cochlear nucleus → superior olive → inferior colliculus → medial geniculate nucleus → A1
	- Step-by-step:
		- Sensory axons from the cochlear nerve terminate in the cochlear nucleus
		- These axons project to the superior olivary nucleus, where most of them decussate
			- The olivary nucleus receives information from both ears and also transmits to other areas
		- The axons then project to the contralateral inferior colliculus, and then to the medial geniculate nucleus of the thalamus
		- The thalamic neurons relay the information to the primary auditory cortex

*Sleep*
- Endogenous rhythms
	- Circannual rhythms: yearly/seasonal internal calendar
	- Circadian rhythms: daily internal calendar
		- Our bodies do not respond well to changes in sleep and will always try to reset the sleeping/waking schedule to a 24-hour timeframe
		- Circadian rhythms dictate mood, energy, and sleep
		- Zeitgebers (external stimuli) reset our circadian rhythms
			- Light is the most common zeitgeber
			- Short-wave (blue) light resets circadian rhythms best
			- As a result, blindness disrupts circadian rhythms
		- Jet lag 
			- Disruption of circadian rhythms after crossing time zones
			- Going west: phase-delay (staying up and waking later)
			- Going east: phase-advance (going to sleep and waking earlier)
		- Night shift
			- Day sleeping must be done in a dark room
			- Night work must be done in an extremely bright room
			- Night shift workers are groggier and more accident-prone than day shift workers
		- Early birds vs night owls
			- People show different preferences and tolerances for sleep times
			- Across all cultures and several species, adolescents go to bed and wake up late
			- Night owls tend to perform worse on tests
- Generation of endogenous rhythms
	- Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)
		- Located in the hypothalamus
		- Automatically produces a circadian rhythm of action potentials
		- Exclusively controls and creates endogenous rhythms
		- Branch of the optic nerve (retinohypothalamic path) communicates with the SCN
			- Specialized retinal ganglion cells with their own photopigment (melanopsin) respond directly to light and help to reset circadian rhythms

- Genetic regulation
	- Genes known as *period* and *timeless* create circadian rhythms by producing proteins that promote sleep
		- Synthesis of protein increases throughout the day, peaking at night and declining in the morning
- Melatonin production
	- Pineal gland (posterior to thalamus) produces melatonin
	- Melatonin increases sleepiness 
	- Melatonin is found in all animals except sponges

*Types of unconsciousness*
- Sleep: intentionally produced state of decreased activity and responsiveness
- Coma: extended period of unconsciousness typically caused by brain injury
- PVS (persistent vegetative state) or unresponsive wakefulness syndrome: condition between sleep and moderate wakefulness, with minimal responsiveness and no purposeful behavior
- Minimally conscious state: condition between consciousness and unconsciousness, punctuated by brief periods of awareness
- Brain death: condition with no sign of brain activity or responsiveness

*Sleep stages*
- Associated brain waves
	- Alpha waves: steady waves that indicate a relaxed, wakeful state
	- K complex: large, sharp wave associated with a short inhibition of neural firing
	- Sleep spindle: burst of 12 - 14 Hz waves resulting from interactions between the thalamus and the cerebral cortex
		- Sleep spindles are associated with new learning and positively correlated with memory
	- During slow wave sleep, slow high-amplitude waves increase
	- Each cycle of sleep lasts ninety minutes and goes from 1 to 5, then back to 2, then 5; then repeat
- Stage 1
	- Associated waves: theta, alpha
	- Transition from drowsiness to sleep
- Stage 2
	- Associated waves: theta, sleep spindles, K complexes
	- Light sleep
- Stage 3
	- Associated waves: delta 
	- Deep, slow wave sleep
- Stage 4
	- Associated waves: delta 
	- Deep, slow wave sleep
- Stage 5 (REM or paradoxical sleep)
	- Associated waves: alpha, beta, PGO (pons-geniculate-occipital)
	- Dream-rich sleep with rapid eye movements and muscle atonia
	- REM sleep is associated with increased, irregular neural activity
	- Arousal and bodily fluctuations occur during REM sleep
	- REM sleep is brought about by dopamine released in the amygdala

*Higher processing*
- Reticular formation
	- Located in the midbrain
	- Regulates arousal and sleep
	- Pontomesencephalon - small structure that promotes sleep by releasing GABA
- Locus coeruleus
	- Located in the pons
	- Releases norep throughout the cerebral cortex
	- Fine-tunes attention to important stimuli
- Hypothalamus
	- Path that releases histamine promotes alertness
	- Another pathway, to basal forebrain, releases a peptide, orexin (hypocretin) and promotes alertness
- Basal forebrain
	- ACh pathway stimulates wakefulness
	- Adenosine increases sleepiness
		- Caffeine inhibits adenosine

*Brain activity during sleep*
- Though we are largely unresponsive during sleep, we still wake in response to certain stimuli (unfamiliar voices, babies crying)
- Pons and midbrain release GABA, inhibiting transmission of information across the cerebral cortex
- Some brain areas can go to sleep while others remain awake
- During REM, the pons and medulla inhibit the spinal cord neurons that control limb movement

*Sleep disorders*
- Insomnia
	- Lack of sleep
	- Symptoms: worsened memory, attention, cognition, and mood
	- Causes: noises, discomfort, stress, diet, medications, epilepsy, Parkinson’s, brain tumors, psychological disorders
	- Treatment: medication, lifestyle changes
- Sleep apnea
	- Breathless periods during sleep; patients wake gasping for breath
	- Symptoms: fatigue, impaired attention and learning, apoptosis
	- Causes: obesity, alcohol, tranquilizers
	- Treatment: CPAP machine, lifestyle changes
- Narcolepsy
	- Sudden fatigue during the day
	- Four main symptoms
		- Attacks of sleep during the day
		- Cataplexy (muscle weakness) brought on by strong emotions
		- Sleep paralysis
		- Hypnagogic hallucinations (dreamlike hallucinations that patients have difficulty distinguishing from reality)
	- Causes: genetic, loss of hypothalamic cells that release orexin
	- Treatment: medication
- Periodic limb movement disorder
	- Repeated involuntary movements of the legs or arms while asleep
- REM behavior disorder
	- Acting out of dreams during REM sleep
	- Patients often injure themselves or others
- Night terrors
	- Experience of intense anxiety during sleep, more severe than a nightmare
	- Dream content is minimal (a single image) or nonexistent
- Sleepwalking
	- Periods of seemingly capable movement while asleep
	- Sleepwalkers can walk, drive, climb structures, etc - often putting themselves in danger
	- Occurs during slow wave sleep
	- Causes: sleep deprivation, stress
- Sexsomnia
	- Engagement in sexual behavior while asleep

*Functions of sleep*
- Health benefits of sleep
	- Metabolism is decreased, synapses are strengthened and reorganized, fluid is drained from the brain
- Harms of sleep deprivation
	- Stress, poor mental health, accidents, worsened academic performance
	- Driving while asleep is as bad as driving while intoxicated
	- One night of sleeplessness activates the immune response
- Possible functions of sleep
	- Energy conservation 
		- Sleep saves energy at times when activity would be inefficient and possibly dangerous (for humans, at night, since we are not nocturnal)
		- During sleep, body temperature is decreased by several degrees, and muscle activity is decreased
	- Improved memory
		- Sleeping after learning something improves memory
		- REM and slow wave sleep both help memory
		- EEG data shows similar hippocampal activity during learning and sleep
		- Knowledge storage depends on sharp wave ripples from the hippocampus or thalamus to the parietal and frontal cortex

*Functions of REM sleep*
- REM declines with age
- REM could improve memory
- REM supplies corneas with oxygen by moving the surrounding fluid, protecting them from damage

*Dreaming*
- Activation-synthesis hypothesis
	- Dreaming is the brain’s attempt to make sense of distorted information 
	- Dreams begin with activity in the pons (PGO waves)
	- The brain connects this activity to other, unrelated cortical activity
- Neurocognitive model
	- Dreaming is thinking that takes place under unusual conditions
	- Dreams begin with spontaneous activity related to recent memories
	- During REM sleep, activity is increased in the pons, hypothalamus, amygdala, parietal cortex, temporal cortex, and other areas critical for emotions
	- Activity is decreased in the primary visual cortex, motor cortex, and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex
	- Lack of prefrontal cortex activity explains the lack of logic and planning in dreams
	- Damage to the parietal cortex prohibits dreams
	- Posterior parietal cortex is correlated with detail of dreams
