The function of the human nervous system depends on the conduction of electrochemical signals. Action potentials, or nerve impulses, are fast-paced electrical charges that allow neurons to communicate throughout the brain and nervous system. Every action potential follows a basic pattern, a progression that can be broken down into five key stages. First, for a neuron to conduct an electrical signal, it must be at its resting potential - the relative charge across the cell membrane - and it must receive a sufficient stimulus to cause depolarization. Depolarization - the reversal of the negative charge present in the neuron - then leads to a full action potential. After summation, the cell is repolarized, leading to the absolute refractory period, during which time another action potential cannot be produced. Finally, the neuron is hyperpolarized, and returns to its resting potential, beginning the cycle again. 

To gain a better understanding of the complete process of the action potential, it is necessary to conduct a more detailed review of each phase. As stated, the creation of an action potential requires the neuron to be at resting potential - the difference in electric charge between the inside and outside of the cell. For most cells in the CNS, this polarization is roughly -70 millivolts. A cell's resting potential is regulated through the selective permeability of the phospholipid bilayer membrane. In each of the eighty-six billion neurons in the human CNS, the membrane is lined with protein complexes that filter out different chemicals. One key complex is the sodium-potassium pump, which regulates the flow of sodium (Na+) and potassium (K+) ions across the cell membrane, using active transport to continually bring 2 K+ ions in and flush 3 Na+ ions out. Due to the abundance of Na+ ions surrounding the cell - ten times more concentrated outside than in - the continulation efflux of Na+ is essential to keep the resting potential stable so that the neuron can respond to stimuli. 

In addition to complexes such as the sodium-potassium pump, a number of protein channels line the lipid bilayer. These can be non-gated, meaning they typically remain open to substances, or gated, meaning they open and close in response to stimuli. Within the class of gated channels, there are three main types: voltage-gated, ligand-gated, and mechanically-gated. The action potential, since it is an electrical event, relies on voltage-gated ion channels, the Na+ and K+ channels in particular. The area where the action potential is generated - the axon hillock, which lies between the cell body and the axon - has a higher surface density of voltage-gated ion channels. Much like the head of a match, the concentration of ion channels in the axon hillock makes it easier for the action potential to "catch."

To illustrate the cruical nature of these voltage-gated ion channels, it may be helpful to consider an example where the channels are impeded. In the case that a drug partially blocks a cell membrane's K+ voltage-gated ion channels, the smooth firing of an action potential would be disrupted. The K+ ion channels are critical for a timely repolarization, so while repolarization, and eventual hyperpolarization, would still occur even if the K+ channels were partially obstructed, it would take place on a much slower time frame. Though this may seem like a small effect, this could quickly spiral and effect the entire nervous system, resulting in delayed neural and motor function. The cyclical flow of these ions is essential for the generation of action potentials and transmission of chemical signals.

Before an action potential is fired, both the Na+ and K+ voltage-gated ion channels are closed. The depolarization that fuels action potentials is typically caused by neurotransmitter stimuli. A sufficiently strong stimulus causes the Na+ channels to open, which will then trigger depolarization, as Na+ flood into the cell. For an action potential to fully summate, it must reach a certain intensity, known as the threshold. Once the depolarization has crossed the threshold, the action potential will fire in an "all-or-none" manner, meaning that the electrical signal will be conveyed bar none, and its magnitude will not decrease over distance or time. 

The action potential reaches its peak at around 40-60 millivolts. As it peaks, the Na+ ion channels suddenly close, while the K+ channels open. This leads to repolarization, with the voltage steeply decreasing towards the resting potential. During repolarization, the refractory periods begin. There are two refractory periods - absolute and relative - that occur during the firing of an action potential. Absolute, the first refractory period, refers to the time when the Na+ ion channels cannot be opened and no new action potentials can be fired. It is followed by the relative refractory period, when the Na+ channels may be opened, but the generation of an action potential will take a significantly stronger stimulus. Both refractory periods require two things: Na+ channels must be closed, and K+ ions must be flowing out of the cell.

As the relative refractory period comes to a close, the neuron experiences hyperpolarization. In an attempt to return to the resting potential, the neuron overshoots its target, resulting in a brief, more negative dip (~75mV) before returning to the baseline. At this point, the full cycle of the action potential has been completed; the signal has been conducted, and the cell is now prepared to conduct its next impulse.

Resting potential
- Membrane potential: electrical potential difference between the inside and outside of the cell
	- Membrane is composed of lipid bilayer and proten channels
	- Selectively permeable
		- Non-gated protein channels: generally open
		- Gated protein channels: open and closed
			- Voltage-gated ion channels
			- Ligand-gated channels
			- Mechanically-gated channels
	- Charge is generally more negative inside of neurons (typically -70 millivolts)
		- Charge is determined by Na & K and membrane permeability
- Resting potential: difference in charge between inside and outside of neuron
	- While resting, the membrane maintains an electrical gradient/polarization
	- Slightly more negative inside the cell than out
	- Typically -70 millivolts

Sodium potassium pump
- Sodium-potassium pump
	- Uses active transport - requires energy
		- Electro-chemical gradient across membrane is passive
	- Pumps 3 Na out and 2 K in
	- Concentration gradient acts to move Na inside but K outside
	- Electrical gradient acts to move both Na and K inside
- Sodium-potassium pump transports 3 Na ions out of the cell while bringing 2 K ions in
	- Na is 10x more concentrated outside of the cell
	- K is more concentrated inside the cell
	- Na is pulled into the cell by the concentration gradient and the electrical gradient
	- When the channels open and Na floods in, the cell is prepared to respond
	- K gradually leaks out of the cell, increasing the electrical gradient across the membrane:
- The basic process:
	- At the resting potential, the cell is slightly negatively charged
	- When we dramatically increase the negative charge, the cell is briefly hyperpolarized
	- When we make the charge more positive, the cell is briefly depolarized
	- Enough depolarization causes the cell to cross the threshold, resulting in an action potential
	- The action potential then fades during the refractory period and returns to resting potential
