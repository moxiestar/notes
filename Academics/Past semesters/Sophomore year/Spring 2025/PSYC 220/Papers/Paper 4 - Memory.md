Memory is one of the most powerful aspects of human cognition. It adds an extra dimension to human life, with people often describing influential events as being “seared” into their minds. Spatial memory, in particular, allows for navigation as simple as a walk across the kitchen and as complex as a taxi driver’s morning route. In a 2006 study, Maguire et al. examined the structure of hippocampal gray matter in London taxi drivers, expecting to see patterns reflective of their specialized spatial memory. The study’s results were conclusive: when compared to a control group, Maguire et al. found that taxi drivers had more grey matter in their mid-posterior hippocampi, and were more easily able to identify and locate themselves relative to London landmarks. 

In this study, participants (n = 33, all males) were recruited from a pool of certified drivers, all having completed a London-specific navigational training known as “The Knowledge.” 16 of the participants were taxi drivers, and served as experimental subjects, while 17 bus drivers comprised a control group. Maguire et al. chose to use bus drivers because they, like the taxi drivers, were intensely familiar with London streets and dealt with customers on a one-on-one basis. Additionally, the two groups had worked as drivers for an equivalent amount of time. ==When compared to an earlier study, which used non-drivers as a control group, bus drivers produced the same results; in that sense, when compared to taxi drivers, bus drivers were shown to be equivalent to non-drivers. However, it is worth noting that the bus drivers were required to follow particular, predetermined routes, unlike the taxi drivers. Just as the taxi drivers’ hippocampi had adapted to their spatially orientated profession, bus drivers’ brains will have adapted too, resulting in a specialization equally far outside of the norm. It would, perhaps, have been better to replicate the study with citizens who did not drive professionally.

To measure their cognitive capabilities, navigational skills, and stress levels, Maguire et al. used a variety of neuropsychological tests, and found a significant overall difference between the two groups (p = 0.001). To measure working memory capacity, the digit span and memory scale from the Weschler Adult Intelligence Scale was used; no significant main effect was found. To examine visual recall, the copy- and delayed-recall segments of the Rey-Osterrieth Complex Figure was used; taxi drivers performed more poorly (p = 0.02), particularly on the delayed-recall test. To measure facial memory, Maguire et al. used the Warrington Recognition Memory Test; no significant main effect was found. To examine stress levels, the Perceived Stress Scale and the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory were used; again, no significant main effect was found. To test the drivers’ knowledge of London landmarks, Maguire et al. designed a custom experiment where participants were shown images of different landmarks and asked to identify them and judge their proximity. Taxi drivers were better able to recognize the landmarks (p = 0.0001) and identify their proximity (p = 0.009). 

Data analysis on the participants was performed with functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), using a technique known as voxel-based morphometry (VBM). After collecting fMRI data on the participants, Maguire et al. saw that in taxi drivers, the mid-posterior hippocampi were populated with more grey matter. In the bus drivers, however,  more gray matter was seen in their anterior hippocampi. During the initial data collection, the two groups were shown to have equal experience. For the bus drivers, hippocampal size was not affected by the number of years they had been driving, whereas for the taxi drivers, gray matter in the mid-posterior hippocampus was positively correlated with years of experience.

Had Maguire et al. chosen to compare taxi drivers with Uber or Lyft drivers, they might have seen different results. For one, taxi drivers typically have more experience than Uber drivers, particularly navigational experience. Uber drivers often have multiple jobs, using driving as a way to make more money on the side, whereas taxi drivers will often rely on driving as their sole source of income, though this is becoming less common with time. They differ in terms of navigation, too: taxi drivers are familiar enough with their territory to navigate to numerous locations without aid, while Uber drivers will often depend on services such as Google Maps or Waze. Uber drivers may have shown little hippocampal specialization, especially when compared to taxi drivers. 

The study also touches on a popular topic in current psychology: neuroplasticity. In their discussion, Maguire et al. note that stress could have shrunk the anterior hippocampi in the taxi drivers, though this would have to be confirmed with a cortisol study, which could definitively monitor their physiological levels of stress. Stress can, of course, have hugely negative effects, but a healthy amount of stress can be useful to help learning and cognition. Among the drivers, increased stress could have had a positive effect on their learning. As stated above, Maguire et al. also found that experience affects hippocampus size, suggesting a strong capability for neuroplasticity. 

Though this study is compelling, particularly when it comes to the results, their methods could have been improved. As stated above, cortisol levels could have revealed a connection between stress levels in taxi drivers and anterior hippocampal shrinkage. Maguire et al. simply state that a cortisol-based study is ongoing; adding a physiological component into the current study would have significantly strengthened their results. Again as mentioned, this study found a strong positive correlation between years of experience and amount of gray matter in the mid-posterior hippocampus. This finding needs to be more closely analyzed and tested to prove its veracity. One notable strength is the study’s use of advancements in fMRI testing. With the help of new technology (VBM), Maguire et al. were able to highlight the connections between the amount of hippocampal gray matter and cognitive functions such as working memory or spatial recognition. The results of this study are promising, and more research into spatial memory and hippocampal development may show other, equally noteworthy connections.
