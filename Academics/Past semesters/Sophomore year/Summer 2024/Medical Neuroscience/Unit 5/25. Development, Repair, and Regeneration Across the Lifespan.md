**Synaptogenesis across Lifespan:**
- At birth there are few functional synapses, but after birth the number quickly rises during the first several years of life
- Growth of synapses slows and drops over time
- With autism, capacity for synaptogenesis is much larger than in the normal brain (unsurprising...)
- In ADHD, synaptogenesis is stilted when compared to normal brain

**Synaptogenesis across Lifespan:**
- 20% to 50% reduction in synaptogenesis across lifespan 
- Loss of grey matter as time goes by
- In those with ADHD, decrease is accelerated and more advanced
- White matter, however, increase over lifespan 
- Brain size increases up until 25, when it then plateaus and slowly begins to decrease
- Loss of synapses, but not neurons (loss of neurons is pathological)

**Plasticity in Maps in Brain:**
- Somatic sensory and motor maps can undergo intense organization across the lifespan
- Surgical changes in the human body will change the topographical map in the human body
- Pyramidal cells in upper layers of cortical circuits communicate to other cortical layers, as well as a broad swath of other areas, is responsible for synaptic changes that lead to LTP 
- Like connects to like within the cerebral cortex

**Peripheral Response to Injury:**
- A damaged peripheral nerve has a capacity to regenerate and reestablish connection 
- Macrophages consume debris in the wake of the injury, and Schwann cells proliferate 
- Growth-related genes are expressed, creating a growth cone 
- Re-established connections are never as perfect or strong as the original connection
- Increased levels of Ca can lead to programmed cell death of neurons 
- For neurons in the penumbra (periphery) of an injury that survive, will they have the capacity to regenerate and restore functional capacity? 
	- Yes and no - axons in white matter have a harder time, while grey matter axons have it easier
	- Around the margins of injured tissue, glial cells are proliferated 
	- Proliferation is so intense that the brain becomes congested - glial cells release a level of chemical signals that has a negative affect on growth potential of surrounding surviving neurons
	- After recovery, a "glial scar" forms in the affect area of surviving neurons 
	- Integral membrane proteins (OMgp, Nogo-A, MAG) suppress growth

**Response to Injury in Grey Matter:**
- Growth of horizontal networks within the cerebral cortex following an injury 
- This makes recovery easier - plasticity increases 

**Adult Neurogenesis:**
- Production of new adult neurons from neural stem cells is limited to a particular set of cells in the dentate gyrus of the hippocampus
- Population of neural stem cells that can differentiate into a variety of types
- As health improves, neurogenesis increases, and vice versa
