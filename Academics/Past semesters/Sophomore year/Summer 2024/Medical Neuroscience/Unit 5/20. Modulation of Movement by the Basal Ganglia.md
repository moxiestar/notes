**Basal Ganglia and Motor Control:**
- Interact with upper motor neurons to control proper initiation of movement - regulates (with inhibitory control) thalamic input which controls motor cortex
- Basal ganglia - collection of deep nuclei in forebrain, developed telencephalon 
	- Output travels to thalamus - modulating thalamo-cortical activity (initiation and suppression of movement)

**Parallel Pathways:**
- Many processing streams run through the basal ganglia
- Input from cerebral cortex (cortical input)
- Loops are primarly defined by input from cerebral cortex

**Basal Ganglia Dysfunction:**
- Hypokinetic movement disorders (ie, Parkinson's) - failure to initiate movement
- Hyperkinetic movement disorders (ie, Huntington's) - failure to suppress movement

**Basal Ganglia Loops:**
- Body movement loop - primarily concerned with limb movements
	- Input from motor & sensory (parietal) cortex 
	- Projects to putamen
	- Projects to globus pallidus (internal segment)
	- Projects to VL/VA nuclei (thalamus)
- Limbic loop - concerned with affect and motivation 
	- Input from telencephalic centers (amygdala, hippocampus, etc)
	- Projects to ventral striatum (nucleus accumbens)
	- Projects to ventral pallidum 
	- Projects to mediodorsal nucleus (thalamus)

**Basal Ganglia Anatomy:**
- Dopamine from substantia nigra is important for basal ganglia modulation - travels from substantia nigra to striatum, putamen, and caudate nucleus
	- Ventral tegmental area - provides dopamine projection to ventral striatum for limbic loop
- Subthalamic nuclei (diencephalon) provide input to globus palladis 
- Basal ganglia sits directly on top of brainstem 
	- Putamen sits on the lateral side of internal capsule
	- Caudate nucleus sits on medial side of internal capsule
	- Putaman and CN join together on the back of the brainstem

- Putamen is continuous with the head of the caudate 
- Caudate wraps around lateral ventricle
- Globus pallidus is hidden behind putamen 
- Thalamus is medial to basal ganglia

**Pathways in Basal Ganglia:**
- Direct pathway
	- Cerebral cortex -> striatum -> pallidum (internal) -> thalamus -> frontal cortex
- Indirect pathway
	- Cerebral cortex -> striatum -> pallidum (external) -> subthalamic nucleus -> pallidum (internal) -> thalamus -> frontal cortex
		- Importance of subthalamic nucleus - involves modulation of thalamus 
- Direct pathway serves to stimulate the frontal cortex (initiating movement), while indirect pathway inhibits the frontal cortex (suppressing movement)
	- Indirect pathway - striatal cells send inhibitory signals to pallidum, disinhibiting subthalamic nucleus, which then fires to the internal globus pallidus, inhibiting the thalamus
- Dopamine binds to metab receptors linking to second messengers system, affecting cAMP in post-synaptic neurons 
	- D1 receptors lead to rise in cAMP (expressed by neurons of striatum in direct pathway - activation of neurons rises)
	- D2 receptors lead to drop in cAMP (expressed by neurons of striatum in indirect pathway - activation of neurons drops) 
	- This is what facilitates movement, regulating the inhibition and disinhibition

**Disinhibition:**
- Inhibition of inhibitory circuits - series of inhibitory neurons connected to each other
- Diagram:
	- Neuron A is inhibiting Neuron B, and Neuron B is inhibiting Neuron C
	- Activity in striatum shuts down pallidum activity, thereby removing the inhibitory effect of the pallidum on the thalamus
	- Now the thalamus can send its excitatory signals to the motor cortex
- Basal ganglia circuitry serves to remove pallidum inhibition, disinhibiting the thalamus
![[Screenshot 2024-08-03 at 3.05.05 PM.png]]

- If A is at rest, then B fires, inhibiting thalamus
- When A is fired, then B is inhibited, allowing the thalamus to fire to the motor cortex

**Basal Ganglia Function Review:**
- Striatal neurons fire before and during onset of movement
- Basal ganglia has role in initiation and suppression of movement, but not during movement
- The initation and suppression is controlled by the balance of activity between the direct and indirect pathways 
- Has a kind of "center-surround" antagonism 
	- As you activate one movement, you suppress all other kinds of movement
	- This activation is extremely focused, and requires a shift to the direct pathway for the intended movement 

**Parkinson's - hypokinetic** 
- Symptoms:
	- Akinesia (lack of movement)
	- Rigid posture 
	- Resting tremor
	- Reduced facial expressions
	- Shuffling gait
	- Difficulty initiating and terminating movements 
	- Cognitive changes 
	- Dementia
- Caused by lack of dopamine (up to 70% for diagnosis)
	- Inability to initiate direct pathway, inability to suppress the indirect pathway 
	- Results in an increase in tonic inhibition 
- Deep brain stimulation can reverse the effects 

**Huntington's - hyperkinetic**
- Symptoms:
	- Involuntary flailing movements
	- Dementia
- Genetic origins - production from Huntington gene (40 repeats of CAG causes Huntington's)
- Loss of inhibitory project from caudate/putamen -> increased activity in thalamus
