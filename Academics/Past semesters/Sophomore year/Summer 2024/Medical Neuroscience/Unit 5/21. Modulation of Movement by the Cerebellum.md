**Cerebellar Anatomy:**
- Cerebellar cortex (green bits in first image)
	- Grey matter: outer cortex, inner deep nuclei
	- Connected to brainstem via three large stalks of white matter - peduncles
		- Middle is the largest, the superior is a way to get signals out of the cerebellum, and the inferior is bidirectional (but dominated by inputs)

![[Screenshot 2024-08-03 at 5.50.54 PM.png]]

**Cerebellar Function:**
- Error correction - motor learning 
	- Integrates execution commands on movement intention with sensory feedback to achieve moment-to-moment adjustment of behavior
	- Facilitate learning when errors are numerous
- Cerebellar outputs are directed towards upper motor neurons, NOT lower motor neurons 
	- Outputs often target thalamic nuclei
- Organ of agility - allows for quick thinking and graceful movement
	- Coordination of ongoing, multi-jointed movement
	- Coordination of ongoing, sequential cognitive processes (ie, problem solving)

**Cerebellar Anatomy:**
- Spinocerebellum - medial strip spanning two cerebellar hemispheres 
	- Midline is known as vermis
	- Mostly concerned with arm and leg movement
- Cerebrocerebellum 
	- Involved with circuitry in cerebral cortex; sends signals back to cerebral cortex
	- Skilled movements with distal extremities 
- Vestibulocerebellum 
	- Closely connected with vestibular nuclei
	- Controls and calibrates motor output from vestibular nuclei

![[Screenshot 2024-08-03 at 5.51.03 PM.png]]
- Cerebellum is folded into folia that extend over large swaths of the cerebellar cortex
- Deep nuclei reside in white matter in roof of fourth ventricle 
	- Dendate nuclei - largest deep cerebellar nucleus, related to cerebrocerebellum 
	- Interposted - lateral spinocerebellum 
	- Fastigial nucleus - vermis

**Inputs to Cerebellum:**
- Main input - pontine nuclei
	- Crosses midline to project into contralateral cerebellum via the middle cerebellar peduncle
	- Pontine nuclei are supplied by frontal and parietal cortex
	- Mainly terminates in lateral hemisphere
- Vestibular nuclei convey information about head movements
	- Mainly terminates in vestibulocerebellum
- Spinal cord conveys sensory information about movements of limbs
	- Mainly terminates in spinocerebellum
- Inferior olive crosses midline and sends information via inferior cerebellar peduncle - provides learning signal for cerebellum, conveying time for synaptic change
	- Inferior olive gets information from red nucleus 
	- Produces error correction skill that affects movements
- Inputs from pontine nuclei, vestibular nuclei, and spinal cord give rise to mossy fibers
- Inputs from inferior olive give rise to climbing fibers
	- Both climbing and mossy fibers terminate in cerebellar cortex and deep nuclei
- Cerebellum gets input from ipsilateral side of body

**Cerebellar Circuitry:**
- Main neurons of cerebellum are purkinje cells
	- Main integrator of cerebellar inputs
- Granule cells - small cells that grow axons towards outer layer of cortex and then splits into two parts. These split axons are called parallel fibers
	- Purkinje cells run perpendicular to parallel fibers
	- Granule cells receive input from mossy fibers - synapse is excitatory
	- Granule cells then give rise to parallel fibers which signal purkinje cells - synapse is also excitatory 
	- Purkinje axons then send their axons down and fire with deep cerebellar nuclei - synapse is inhibitory, because purkinje cells release GABA
- Climbing fiber is derived from olivary nucleus - makes contact with purkinje cells and wraps itself around the cell (sexy style?)
![[Screenshot 2024-08-03 at 7.47.41 PM.png]]
**Cerebellar Pathways:**
- Inputs to the cerebellum come from mossy fibers (also sending branches down into deep c nuclei)
- Granule cells then grow to form parallel fibers, which then synapse with purkinje cells
- Purkinje cells then synapse on deep c nuclei, but the synapse is inhibitory
- Climbing fibers give rise to synapse at deep c nuclei but also within the purkinje cell
- Diagram on right simplifies the pathway
- Motor learning depends on strength of inhibition - reducing synaptic inhibition strengthens the synaptic connection
	- Achieved via LTD in the cerebellum 
	- Climbing fiber - metabotropic receptor for glutamate leads to release of Ca

**Anatomy of Cerebellar Outputs:**
- Outputs leave cerebellum via superior cerebellar peduncle, crossing the midline and then travelling to a) brainstem and VL thalamus or b) superior colliculus 
![[Screenshot 2024-08-03 at 8.15.24 PM.png]]

**Descening Outputs from Cerebellum:**
- Deep c nuclei project to reticular formation, then to lower motor neurons in medial ventral horn 
- Purkinje cells project directly into vestibular nuclei, which then help to maintain balance within lower motor neurons

**Summary of Cerebellar Function:**
- Influences coordination of sequential movement, reason, and problem-solving 
- Those with cerebellar lesions lack cognitive sequential skill 
![[Screenshot 2024-08-03 at 8.27.23 PM.png]]

**Cerebellar Dysfunction:**
- Cerebellar lesions are always ipsilateral 
	- Symptoms include:
		- Ataxia (lack of coordination)
			- Intentional tremor: tremor during intended movement (ie, reaching out to pick something up)
			- Dysmetria: instability of a limb as it approaches something, typically undershooting or overshooting
			- Impairment of rapid, repetitive movements 
			- Decomposition of movement (ie, jerkiness of motion)
