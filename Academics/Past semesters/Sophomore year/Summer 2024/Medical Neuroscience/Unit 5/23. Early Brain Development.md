**Hox and homeotic genes:**
- Hox and homeotic genes - four clusters of hox/homeotic genes, responsible for establishing regional identity in developing CNS
- Position of CNs in brainstem reflects homeotic genes 

**Inductive Signaling:**
- Begins in neurulation - signals are expressed by notochord, influencing differentiation of regions in neural tube (floorplate, roofplate)
- Well-known inductive signals:
	- Retinoic acid - synthesized and released - as a lipophillic molecule it can travel anywhere
		- Binds to receptor in cell that can activate certain genes
		- Insufficient vitamin A suppresses the ability to produce retinoic acid
		- Excess of vitamin A can likewise be extremely harmful, inducing malformations
	- Bone morphogenetic protein (BMP)
		- Interacts with surface receptor, activates SMAD, which relocates to nucleus and acts as transcription regulator
		- Noggin (for real) and chordin can antagonize BMP can and prevent it from interacting with receptor, making the ectoderm differentiate into neuroectoderm (neural plate)
	- Sonic hedgehog (actually) 
		- Interacts with receptors (patched protein, which interacts smoothened protein)
		- Interactions then activate a series of transcription factors that modulate gene expression
		- Critical for proper closure of neural tube

**Transcriptional Code:**
- Coordinated expression of inductive signals (location, developmental time) - gives rise to regional identity in developing CNS, establishes dorsal and vental access to neural tube 
- Less is known about transcriptional code in forebrain

**Proliferation:**
- Making cells necessary to populate grey matter structures in developing CNS
- "Just mindboggling" - never a good sign!
- 250,000 cells are produced every minute in the developing embryo 
- Almost all neurons produced within second trimester 
	- If most of your neurons have been with you since birth, does that mean that your younger self is still inside of you?

**Mitosis in Neural Tube:**
- Precursor cells grow thing that makes contact with the outer and inner surface of the neural tube
	- Outer surface becomes the pial surface of the brain 
	- Inner surface remains the inner surface of the neural tube, which then becomes the lining of the ventricles in the brain
- Cell body then migrates back and forth between walls - DNA is replicated at the outer wall
- Once DNA is replicated, the cell travels back to the inner surface, where mitosis occurs
- Cells can split into progenitors and neuroblasts (cell that can further differentiate into glials and neurons)

**Migration:**
- Important for formation of cerebral cortex
- Ventricular area of neural tube - where mitosis takes place
- Separation between pial surface and inner surface grows as development progresses
- Cortical plate - first formation of cerebral cortex
	- The cortex is built in an inside-out fashion
	- Cells from 1st trimester populate the outermost layers of the cortex
	- Cells from 2nd trimester populate layer 6
	- As pregnancy progresses, cells populate the layers in descending order
	- The last layers to be populated are layers 2 and 3
- Neuroblasts must travel from ventricular area to cortical plate - they migrate via radial glial cells (progenitor cells that have retained their contact between the neural tube walls)
- Errors in migration results in mental retardation and disabilities

**Growth of Neural Circuits:**
- Begins in 3rd trimester
- Neuroblasts develop polarity - apical and basal surfaces. Axons emerge from apical surface and dendrites emerge from basal surface
- Growth cone develops at tip of axon
	- Characterized by flat apperance (lamellopodium, sheet foot)
	- Filopodium extend from the growth cone
	- Actin polymers assemble inside filopodium - their assembly leads to the growth of the filopodium, and their disassembly leads to the shrinking of the filopodium
	- Cell depends on Ca - what's new

**Molecular Signals:**
- Cell-associated molecules (in or between cells)
	- On the surface of the cell or embedded in the extracellular matrix
		- Molecular interactions between growth cones and parenchyma
		- Interactions trigger second messenger systems in growth cones that transduce extracellular signals into patterns of growth 
	- Stabilize linkages between axons and glia
- Diffusible molecules (diffuse through membranes)
	- Secreted by target structures and diffused into extracellular fluids
	- Can act over a much greater system within the developing CNS

**Molecular Interactions:**
- Image A: molecules interact with receptors (often the same kind of molecule)
- Image B: cell-adhesion molecules - interact with kinases that influence transduction cascades
- Image C: Ca-dependent cell-adhesion molecules (cadherins) - have an effect on assembly of actin filaments 
- Image D: ephrin proteins present on surface, interact with eph receptors - have effects on variety of second messenger systems and actin filaments
	- Development of topographical map - axons have to project from one place to another
	- Mapping is of a chemical nature so as to be fully tuned to the the behavior of the person/animal in question
	- Ephrins and eph receptors connect retina to tectum

**Diffusible molecules:**
- Secreted by target structures and diffused into extracellular fluids
- Affect growth and survival of neurons 
- Two categories: 
	- Tropic molecules - guide axons toward or away from source
		- Chemo-attractant molecules
		- Chemo-repellent molecules 
	- Trophic molecules - promote survival and growth of neurons
		- In the peripheral nervous system, they match axonal innervation with target tissue
		- Production is activity-dependent

**More Diffusible Molecules:**
- Chemoattractants begin to divert the growth of the axon towards the molecules
- Chemorepellents begin to divert the growth of the axon away from the molecules

**Trophic Support:**
- Hamburger experiment - (1) removed a limb on a baby chick, which drastically decreased the ipsilateral motor neurons
- Suggested that "target tissue" is essential for the survival of neurons
- (2) When a limb was transplated, there were twice as many ipsilateral motor neurons
- Two major conclusions of the experiment:
	- Decreased apoptosis of motor neurons with augmentation of target tissue (2)
	- Enhanced apoptosis of motor neurons when deprived of target tissue (1)

**Neurotrophin Hypothesis:**
- In development, the survival of neurons depends on the availability of a trophic factor
- Trophic factors are synthesized and released by target tissues
- Survival of neurons depends upon successful competition for the limited supply of the tropic factor
- In maturity, the regulated secretion of trophic factors may help shape neuronal connections in response to injury or adaptation to new patterns of neural activity

**Synapse Formation:**

- Ca-dependent adhesion molecules responsible for recognition

- Assembly of inductive network - construction of both sides of the proto-synapse
