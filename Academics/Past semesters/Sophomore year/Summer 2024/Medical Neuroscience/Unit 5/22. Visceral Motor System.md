**Autonomic Nervous System Functions:**
- Maintains the internal environment of the body and regulates activity of visceral organs, glands, and blood vessels
- Governs circulation, respiration, digestion, metabolism, bodily secretions, thermal regulation, and reproduction (god damn)
- Together with components of the somatic motor systems, the autonomic nervous system is involved in the experience and expression of emotional behavior, as well as other aspects of cognition 

**Divisions of the ANS:**
- Three parts of the ANS:
	- Sympathetic division (energy mobilization)
	- Parasympathetic division (energy conservation)
	- Enteric division (coordinating digestive reflexes)
- These three divisions work together to enforce homeostasis (constancy of the internal environment) and allostasis (change of the internal environment)
	- Ability to keep the conditions stable, and ability to change them quickly when needed

**CNS Systems for ANS Control:**
- Higher brain centers in limbic system (cingulate cyrus, orbito-medial prefrontal cortex, amygdala, hypothalamus, and brain stem (reticular formation, periaqueductal grey))
- Descending projections to lower motor neurons in brainstem and spinal cord 
- Interneurons and preganglionic neurons in the brainstem and spinal cord that communicate with indirectly with effector organs

**Sympathetic vs Parasympathetic:**
- Sympathetic: two-neuron chain from spinal cord to viscera - first neuron in thoracic spine (preganglionic) - then travels to ganglion 
	- Preganglionic cells are found in intermediolateral cell column in thoracic spine
	- Preganglionic releases acetylcholine, postganglionic releases norepinephrine
		- Both have metabotropic receptors 
- Sympathetic repares body for action of some kind, mobilizes resources of body

**Parasympathetic Function:**
- Two-neuron chain from CNS to viscera (preganglionic neurons are found in brainstem)
	- Ganglia only extend short distance and are often embedded in tissue itself 
	- Both pre- and postganglionic cells release acetylcholine 
	- Preganglionic cells are found in Edinger-Westphal nucleus, salivatory nuclei, dorsal motor nucleus of vagus, nucleus ambiguous
- Sylvius tour at 6:30

**Interactions between Parasympathetic and Sympathetic:**
- Functions are complementary and coordinated 
- Always tonic activity in both divisions 
- Increased activation is possible, but it is not "all or none"

**Higher Processing in CNS:**
- Nucleus of solitary tract integrates information, sending some of it to preganglionic neurons, both parasympathetic and sympathetic, then producing a reflex response 
- Visceral sensory input comes from two cranial nerves (CN IX and X) and collaterals of anterolateral pathway
	- These inputs can be chemosensory, mechanosensory, or nociceptive 
	- Path through medial dorsal spinal cord that conveys pain information, synapsing with the dorsal column nuclei and sending axons that enter the medial lemniscal pathway and project into insular cortex 
		- Pathway explains referred pain 
- Nucleus of solitary tract (caudal visceral division) receives input from CN IX and X and anterolateral system and integrates it (brain scan at 7:10)

**Integration of Visceral Signals:**
- Ascending information at the bottom, travelling to CN IX and X
- Information then projects to nucleus of solitary tract 
- From there, information can travel to reticular formation, parabrachial nucleus or hypothalamus, or it can return to the preganglionic neurons in brainstem and spinal cord as an output
	- From parabrachial nucleus, information travels to thalamus, hypothalamus, and amygdala 
	- From there, we reach the insular and medial prefrontal cortex
	- Medial cortex then controls homeostasis and allostasis by sending signals back down the loop

**Hypothalamic Anatomy:**
- Three main parts: anterior commissure, optic chiasm, mammillary bodies 
- Hypothalamus is full of many little things, including many nuclei
![[Screenshot 2024-08-04 at 3.28.57 PM.png]]

**Overview of Hypothalamic Function:**
- Five key functions:
	- Regulating blood pressure and electrolyte balance (drinking, salt appetite, blood osmolarity, vasomotor tone)
	- Regulating body temperature (thermogenesis, seeking warmer/cooler environment)
	- Coordinating energy metabolism (feeding, digestion, metabolic rate)
	- Governing reproduction (mating, pregnancy, lactation)
	- Controlling emergency responses - allostasis (distribution of blood flow, stress hormones, immunological response)
- Hypothalamus sits in key position in CNS - receives sensory inputs (sensory information, chemosensory, etc) and information from the cerebral cortex (amygdala, hippocampus, etc)
	- It then gives rise to outputs that control visceral motor, somatic motor, neuroendocrine, and behavioral responses

**Hypothalamic Nuclei:**
- Periventricular nuclei - related to production of hormones in anterior pituitary gland
- Supraoptic nuclei - sits above optic chiasm, hence the creative name
- Paraventricular nuclei - grow axons through infindibulum and release hormones in posterior pituitary gland
- Lateral/medial preoptic nuclei - involved in coordinating reproduction and sexual behavior, especially sexual orientation 
- Suprachiasmatic nucleus - also sits above optic chiasm, master clock of human body, generates circadian rhythm 
- Ventro/dorso-medial nuclei - involved in reproduction and parenting behavior, as well as controlling hunger
- Lateral hypothalamus - modulates vigilance

**Horner's Syndrome:**
- Can be bilateral, but usually unilateral 
- Symptoms: ptosis (drooping of eyelid), miosis (ipsilateral pupillary constriction), enophthalmos (apparent sinking of eyeball)
- Results from interruption in pathway that links hypothalamus with ganglionic neurons, impairing the sympathetic nervous system 

[Video 8](https://www.coursera.org/learn/medical-neuroscience/lecture/l9Dsd/micturition) ==**Micturition**==
*Learning Objectives:*
1. *Discuss the interplay among the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions of the autonomic nervous system and volitional somatic motor system in the control of micturition* 

**Process of Micturition:**
- Sensory afferents grow out from dorsal root ganglion cells that innervate the bladder - mechano-sensory receptors are sensitive to stretch of the bladder. Also chemosensory elements with free nerve endings 
- Sensory signals are conveyed via anterolateral pathway 
- Sympathetic innervation comes from integrative centers in brainstem and hypothalamus, travels to thoracic spine 
- Parasympathetic comes from sacral spine 
- Sympathetic promotes filling of bladder, parasympathetic promotes emptying of bladder
	- Filling: increase in sympathetic activity
		- Tone of detrusor muscle drops
		- Tone of internal sphincter rises
	- Emptying: increase in parasympathetic activity
		- Tone of detrusor muscle rises
		- Tone of internal sphincter drops
		- Inhibitory interneuron governs sphincter muscle

**Control of Micturition:**
- Pontine micturition center (catchy title) - must be coordinated to empty bladder
- Midbrain periaqueductal gray - governs PMC
- Periaqueductal gray receives input from hypothalamus and amygdala 
- These receive input from orbital-medical prefrontal cortex
- Social relation to micturition
