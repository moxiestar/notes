**Disorders:**
Visual deficits:
- a) Injury to the right optic nerve causes total right eye blindness 
- b) Lesion in the middle of the optic chiasm causes peripheral blindness (bi-temporal heteronymous hemianopsia)
- c) Lesion in right optic tract causes blindness in left side in each eye's field of vision (left homonymous hemianopsia)
- d) Lesion in right side of Meyer's loop causes blindness in contralateral superior quadrant in left side in each eye's field of vision (left superior homonymous quadrantanopsia)
- e) Damage to right occipital cortex causes blindness in left side in each eye's field of vision (left homonymous hemianopsia), but there's a small semicircular region of vision that's retained (macular sparing)

Movement:
Upper motor neuron syndrome:
- Early signs present like lower motor neuron injury - spinal shock (lack of output to skeletal muscle, resulting in profound weakness)
- As time goes on, muscle will become spastic, muscle tone will increase steeply, and reflexes will become grotesque 
- Clonus - oscillation in joint when joint is outstretched 
- Babinski's sign - fanning instead of curling of toes when the bottom of the foot is tickled
![[Screenshot 2024-08-02 at 5.40.39 PM.png]]
Basal ganglia dysfunction:
- Parkinson's (hypokinetic):
	- Symptoms: akinesia (lack of movement), rigid posture, resting tremor, reduced facial expressions, shuffling gait, difficulty initiating and terminating movements, cognitive changes, dementia
	- Caused by lack of dopamine (up to 70% for diagnosis)
		- Inability to initiate direct pathway, inability to suppress the indirect pathway 
		- Results in an increase in tonic inhibition 
	- Deep brain stimulation can reverse the effects 
- Huntington's (hyperkinetic):
	- Symptoms: involuntary flailing movements, dementia
	- Genetic origins - production from Huntington gene (40 repeats of CAG causes Huntington's)
	- Loss of inhibitory project from caudate/putamen -> increased activity in thalamus 

Dissociated Sensory Loss:
- Unilateral spinal cord damage - lesion in lower thoracic area 
	- Damages the ipsilateral mechanoreceptors and also the contralateral axons of the anterolateral systems 
	- Lower half of same side of body: reduced sensation, vibration, and proprioception 
	- Lower half of opposite side of body: reduced sensation for pain and temperature
	- Complete sensory loss around area of lesion

Cerebellar Dysfunction:
- Cerebellar lesions are always ipsilateral 
	- Symptoms include:
		- Ataxia (lack of coordination)
			- Intentional tremor: tremor during intended movement (ie, reaching out to pick something up)
			- Dysmetria: instability of a limb as it approaches something, typically undershooting or overshooting
			- Impairment of rapid, repetitive movements 
			- Decomposition of movement (ie, jerkiness of motion)

Sleep Disorders:
- Insomnia:
	- Inability to fall asleep, stay awake, or feel well-rested after sleep 
	- Can be caused by caffeine and mitigated by benzos
- Circadian Rhythm Disorder:
	- Disruption of circadian rhythm
	- Typically caused by lifestyle choices
- Sleep Apnea:
	- Cessation of breathing during sleep
	- Often affects those who are obese
	- Can be treated with a positive pressure mask to prevent pharynx from collapsing on airway
- Narcolepsy:
	- Allows for fragments of REM sleep to enter into waking state
	- Patients aren't actually sleep - they're sort of half awake
	- Cataplexy (partial or complete paralysis) can occur
	- Attacks are often brought on by intense positive emotions, including laughter
	- Mutation affecting orexin system
- Restless Leg Syndrome:
	- Movement in extremities, typically in lower legs, caused by a feeling of unease
	- Associated with iron deficency in dopamine neurons
