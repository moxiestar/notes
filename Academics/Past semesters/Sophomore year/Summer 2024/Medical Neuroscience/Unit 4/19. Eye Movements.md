**Eye Movements:**
- Eye movements adjust fixation, when either the visual target, your head is moving, or when you acquire a new visual target 

**Eye Muscles:**
- Superior rectus lifts the eye, inferior rectus lowers the eye
- Lateral rectus moves the eye away from the nose, medial rectus moves the eye in towards the nose
- Superior oblique rotates the eye outward and downward, inferior oblique rotates the eye inward and upward

**Eye Nerves:**
- Abducens nucleus abducts the eye via ipsilateral lateral rectus
- Trochlear nucleus (grows towards dorsal margain of brainstem - unusual - then axon crosses the midline) raises and rotates the eye outward via the contralateral superior oblique 
- Oculomotor nucleus controls all other ipsilateral eye muscles
	- CN III axons include Edinger-Westphal (iris constrictions) and the muscles that open the eye

**Types of Eye Movements:**
- Conjugate: eyes move in the same direction
	- Saccades: quick & ballistic 
	- Smooth pursuit: slow & visually guided (following something in visual field)
	- Optokinetic: smooth pursuit & saccades, visually guided (response to large scale movements in visual field)
	- Vestibuo-ocular: slow & vesitbularly guided (vestibulo-ocular reflex)
- Disconjugate: eyes move in opposite directions
	- Vergence (convergence & divergence): visually guided, near & far fixations (your eyes roll in and out to accomodate objects at different distances in the visual field)
		- Convergence - inward movement, for near objects
		- Divergence - outward movement, for distant objects

**Saccades:**
- Quick ballistic eye movement that allows us to change fixation from one target to another
- Typically 200ms is necessary for the eye to fixate on the new object 
- During the period of time (typically 150ms) when our eyes are moving, we are functionally blind - we do not process the motion that takes place!!
- New saccades cannot be generated until the end of the previous saccade
- We change fixation roughly 2-3 times a second!

**Smooth Pursuits:**
- A visual target is required 
- Visual target moves across visual field at some velocity
- A saccade is made to the target, then our vision is fixed and smoothly tracks the target

**Optokinetic Movements:**
- Optokinetic nystagmus 
	- Large visual target travels through visual field, and we make a saccade to see it, and as it moves, we track it with smooth pursuit
	- As it continues on, we have to make another saccade to "reset" our focus, then track the object with smooth pursuit again, and so on
	- Works in a complementary way to vestibulo-ocular reflex - when the movements fast enough, the vestibulo-ocular system kicks in

**Vergence Eye Movements:**
- When you move from a middle-distance point to a nearer point, your eyes rotate inward to focus on the new point 
- When you move from a middle-distance point to a more distant point, your eyes rotate outward to focus on the new point

**Eye Movement Physiology:**
- Amplitude of movement in the eyes is encoded in the rate and duration of activity in corresponding alpha motor neurons
	- To fire fully, the alpha motor neuron will need to be stimulated again and again, though the period of activity of the muscle will get longer and longer
	- If we move back in the opposite direction, the motor neuron goes silent - an interneuron suppresses its activity 
- Direction of movement is specified by which muscles are activated 
	- PPRF (paramedian pontine reticular formation) controls horizontal movements of the eyes
	- Coordinates firing of neurons that govern eye muscles

**Higher Neutral Control of Saccades:**
- Two main areas that control saccades: frontal eye fields (part of premotor cortex), superior colliculus
- These areas generate the signals that govern the type of saccade we make and how we make it
- Each area has an eye map, both sensory and motor - map of the movement that's necessary to achieve the functional goal
	- White regions indicate receptive fields 
	- Registration between sensory map and map for saccadic eye movements? Who knows! How do they test this stuff?
	- Stimulation of superior colliculus causes a small shift in focus
	- Superior colliculus receives signals from auditory, somatic, and visual system - it wants to direct our gaze to new stimuli
