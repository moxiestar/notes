**The Human CNS:**
- Sensory-motor integration gives rise to behavior 
- Somatic motor system controls most human movements - sends signals to striated muscles

**Motor System Organization:**
- Lower motor neurons innervate skeletal (striated) muscles and cause them to contract (found in spinal cord and brainstem)
- These neurons receive signals from sensory inputs
- Intersegmental circuits - circuits that span the spinal cord and brainstem 
- Upper motor neurons (found in motor cortex and brainstem) control outflow from lower circuits by providing descending input, but do not innervate neurons
- Motor cortex integrates signals, then plans and initiates movements
- Brainstem centers are involving in "setting the stage" for voluntary movements - controls posture and muscle tone for skilled movements
- Upper motor circuits also control the expression of emotional movement (areas of brain present in ventral and medial forebrain - orbital frontal lobe, medial midline of frontal lobe)
	- These structures are integrated with temporal lobe structures (amygdala and hippocampus) so that emotion and memory impact our movement
- Basal ganglia (initiation of movement) and cerebellum (coordination of movement) - not connected to muscle, so not strictly motor circuits. However they interact with networks of upper motor neurons

**Motor Units:**
- Alpha motor neurons send their axons through the ventral root into a spinal nerve, and as it the nerve supplies a muscle, the axon divides into several branches
- The branches then go on to innervate set of fibers within a muscle
- A single motor unit is composed of an alpha motor neuron and the muscle it innervates
- There are three types of motor units:
	- Slow - generates little force, and does so slowly. The smallest motor units governing the fewest fibers. Governs standing
	- Fast, fatigue-resistant - generates slightly more force and does so more quickly. Governs walking and running
	- Fast, fatiguable - generates most force and moves the fastest. The largest motor units governing the most fibers. Governs sprinting
- The slow unit can maintain force the longest, but the two faster units expire much more quickly, especially the fatiguable unit

**Muscle Spindle:**
- Myotatic reflex - stretch reflex that monitors and maintains the length of a muscle via the muscle spindle - collection of muscle fibers found in capsule (intrafusal muscle fibers)
- Axons connect horizontally to muscle fibers
	- Ia axons supply central portion of muscle fibers, where the nuclei aggregate 
	- II axons supply contractile portion of muscle fibers, away from the center
		- Connect to muscle fibers with "flower spray" ending
		- Contractile portion also has a motor supply, from gamma motor neurons
- Nuclear bag fibers - bulbous collection of nuclei in central region 
	- The nuclear bag fibers fire many potentials during the change in length of the muscle - concerned with dynamic movement
- Nuclear chair fibers - nuclei arranged in central row in muscle fibers
	- The nuclear chair fibers are concerned with modulation of movement
- Information travels to cerebellum via spinocerebellar tract
![[Screenshot 2024-08-01 at 4.21.20 PM.png]]
- A muscle reflex occurs in the following manner
	- Stretch is applied to muscle, activating group Ia axons
	- The sensory information runs into the spinal cord
	- Motor output from muscle unit of the stretched muscle 
	- The afferent fiber makes a monosynaptic excitatory connection (release of glutamate) on the alpha motor unit
	- Flexor muscle must also be released, so an inhibitory muscle neuron prevents the motor output traveling to flexor muscle
- Diagram illustrates the response for the knee jerk reflex
	- Monosynaptic excitation of the synergistic muscles
	- Di-synaptic inhibition to the antagonistic muscles

**Muscle gain:**
- Amount of extrafusal muscle contraction that can be applied by a given stretch/load
- If gain is low, the stretch will illicit only a modest output, leading to only a small increase in resistance 
- If gain is high, the stretch will illicit a much larger output and higher increase in resistance 
- Gain is adjusted by the output of gamma motor neurons to the contractile elements of intrafusal muscle fibers 
	- Gamma motor neurons reset the sensitivity of the muscle spindle 
- During active contraction, if only the alpha motor neuron is stimulated, the muscle spindle grows silent since the muscle shortens. This would lead to the intrafusal muscle fiber becoming lax, reducing the sensory outflow from Ia or II 
- If both the gamma and alpha motor neurons are stimulated, the muscle spindle can remain sensitive to the dynamic aspects of length change even though the muscle length is getting shorter
	- Descending inputs mainly control gamma motor neurons in this scenario
	- Reticular formation in the brainstem release biogenic amine NTs into spinal cord 
	- Biogenic amine NTs are modulatory - they can either increase or decrease the excitability of the post-synaptic targets
	- By adjusting the sensitivity of the spindle, its impact on the extrafusal muscle fiber can be controlled

**Muscle Tone:**
- Resting level of tension in a skeletal muscle, which depends on the firing rate of the alpha motor neurons 
- Activity of alpha motor neurons is regulated by feedback from muscle spindle
- Gamma motor neuron is critical for adjusting sensitivity, and eventually tone, of muscle spindle 
- Hypotonia - lesion to the spinal cord causing loss in muscle tone

**Golgi Tendon Organs:**
- Sensory element that provides a system to monitor the force generated by a contracting muscle 
- GTO is activated by tension in muscle contraction 
- Typically found in tendon that attaches muscle to bone 
- Nerve ending provided Ib afferent neuron interwoven with collagen fibrils
- The contraction of the muscle tugs on the interwoven structure, opening ion channels that bring about action potentials 
- Overall a negative feedback system - when the GTO is activated, it diminishes the output of alpha motor neurons supplying the contracting muscle and reduces the tension 
	- Ib inhibitory sensory neuron in between GTO and alpha motor neuron 
- When the flexor muscle tension is decreased, the extensor muscle tension is increased 
- Pathway is helpful for combating fatigue or protecting the muscle against excessive forces
- System is modulating by descending pathways

[Video 6](https://www.coursera.org/learn/medical-neuroscience/lecture/NGz0o/spindles-versus-gtos-and-withdrawal-reflexes) ==**Spindles vs. GTOs; Withdrawal Reflexes**==
*See above*

**Spindles vs. GTOs:**
- When a muscle is lengthened, there is an increase in activity in muscle spindles 
- The lengthening will not affect the GTO, however
- When a muscle is contracted, there is a decrease in activity in muscle spindles 
- The contraction will cause an increase in activity in the GTO

**Flexion Withdrawal Reflex:**
- Reflex engaged when we accidentally interact with a dangerous stimuli 
- Immediate response is to withdraw the limb as quickly as possible
- Incoming sensory signal from Ad fiber 
	- The limb must be withdrawn, but the other limb but be engaged to provide support 
	- Therefore interneurons must be present between incoming Ad fiber and outgoing motor signal, some excitatory and some inhibitory 
		- We must excite the ipsilateral flexor muscles and excite the contralateral extensor muscles
		- Di-synaptic excitation on ipsilateral side and di-synaptic inhibition on contralateral side

**Locomotion:**
- Requires coordination of flexor and extensor muscles - activities of local circuits can span the midline, and activity of the opposite ventral horn can be synergistic with the behavioral goal 
- Activity of alpha motor neuron outputs are coordinated through inhibitory and excitatory interneurons (CPGs)
- CPGs have some key elements: pacemaker neurons, modulation of descending inputs (controlled by brainstem), generation of different kinds of movement patterns

**LMN Syndrome:**
- Damage to the spinal nerves or circuits that govern lower motor neuron outputs
- Symptoms include: muscle atrophy, fibrillations and fasciculations (unusual contractions of muscles), paralysis or paresis (muscle weakness), areflexia (loss of myotatic reflexes), reduction in muscle tone

==**Key Takeaways from Module 10:**==
- Alpha motor neurons (lower motor neurons) innervate skeletal (striated) muscle and control muscle contractions. They are found in muscle spindles, which are innervated by Ia and II afferent axons
- Gamma motor neurons regulate the stretch reflex by controlling the tension in muscle spindles. 
- Golgi tendon organs, innervated by Ib afferent axons, provide a negative feedback system - when the GTO is activated, it diminishes the output of alpha motor neurons supplying the contracting muscle and reduces the tension/ There is a Ib inhibitory interneuron between GTO and alpha motor neuron 
- A motor unit consists of an alpha motor neuron and the muscle it innervates 
- Muscle gain is the amount of extrafusal muscle contraction that can be applied by a given stretch
- Muscle tone is the resting level of tension in a skeletal muscle, which depends on the firing rate of the alpha motor neurons 
- Lateral areas of the ventral horn control the extremities, while the medial areas control the proximities 
- There are three types of motor units, which are engaged in the following order:
	- Slow - generates little force, and does so slowly. The smallest motor units governing the fewest fibers. Governs standing
	- Fast, fatigue-resistant - generates slightly more force and does so more quickly. Governs walking and running
	- Fast, fatiguable - generates most force and moves the fastest. The largest motor units governing the most fibers. Governs sprinting
