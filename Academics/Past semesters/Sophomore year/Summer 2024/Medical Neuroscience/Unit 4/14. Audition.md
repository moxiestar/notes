**Overview of Auditory Function:**
- The auditory system transduces sound waves into distinct patterns of neural activity, integrated with other senses to guide behavior 
- Sound waves are collected and amplified by the structures that compromise the middle ear, then transfer energy to structures in inner ear
- There, sound waves are broken down into sinusoidal components, so that their component frequencies, phases, and amplitudes can be encoded in the firing of the receptor cells 
- In the inner ear and throughout processing stations, tonotopy is preserved 
- In the brainstem auditory information is first processed and divided into several parallel pathways in brainstem 
	- Some of these pathways compare the signals from the two ears, allowing for the localization of sound 
- Brainstem centers relay information to midbrain (inferior colliculus) which projects to thalamus (medical geniculate complex)
- Thalamus then projects to the auditory cortex, which processes more complex aspects of sound (such as speech)

**Nature of Sound:**
- Derived from spherical pressure waves vibrating air molecules
- Amplitude - intensity of the sound 
- Frequency - pitch of the sound

- Human speech and complex tones in music are both good examples of the challenging inputs processed by the auditory system 

**Anatomy of the Ear:**
[![Anatomy of the Ear - Hearing Solutions](https://www.hearingsolutions.ca/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/Anatomy-of-the-Ear.jpg)
- External ear is designed to gather sounds native to our environments, allowing sounds to be concentrated in the auditory canal (external auditory meatus)
- Middle ear: tympanic membrane, diaphragm at the junction between external and inner ear 
	- Behind the tympanic membrane are the ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes). They operate to achieve leverage over the sound energy transmitted from the tympanic membrane
	- There is a 200-fold amplification of energy from the impact of the sound energy on the tympanic membrane to the stapes pressing on the inner ear
	- Stapes vibrates in and out on the oval window 
- Inner ear: series of canals, including the auditory division (snail-shaped cochlea), and the vestibular structures which are sensitive to the movement of the head
- The cochlea has a biomechanical function (decomposes complex sounds into component frequences) and a neural function (transducing these frequencies into neural activity)
	- Cochlea: set of membranous channels, two larger channels (scala vestibuli, scala tympani) bisected by a smaller channel (cochlear partition - base of the partition is called the basilar membrane, top of partition is called the tectorial membrane)
	- In the middle of the cochlear partition are sensory cells called inner hair cells - cilia make contact with tectorial membrane 
		- Outer hair cells tune vibrations of the partition
	- Cochlear partition is innervated by the spiral ganglion - location of cell bodies that grow the auditory component of CN VIII
	- Scala media runs through partition - contains a different kind of fluid, critical for process of sensory transduction

**Inner Ear Function:**
- Oval window - where stapes makes contact with inner ear 
- When stapes hits the oval window, pressure waves run into fluids in scala vestibuli and scala tympani. Fluids are conducted along the length of the cochlea
- Waves of pressure cause vibration to occur within cochlear partition, causing deflection of cilia - key for transduction 
- Tuning properties of basilar membrane - membrane vibrates as waves are conducted throughout the cochlea
	- Near the base of the cochlea, the basilar membrane is taller and squatter, but thinner and wider at the apex of the cochlea 
	- Higher frequencies are represented at the base and lower frequencies are represented at the apex
- Outer hair cells are innervated by afferent axons - motoric in nature, changing their stiffness 
- Inner hair cells are innervated by sensory fibers that have grown out from spiral ganglion. Sensory endings receive electric signals generated in inner hair cells 
- As waves run from scala vestibuli through scala tympani, the cochlear partition begins to vibrate 
	- The pivot points of its two membranes are offset, so a shearing force develops 
	- Shearing force presses on the outer hair cells, causing them to wave back and forth
	- Movement of cilia opens and closes ion channels, allowing ions to enter tips of cilia and generate graded potentials in inner hair cells 
	- Cilia are set up like several dominoes of different heights, going from shortest to tallest. As they go back and forth, and the dominoes fall over with the shortest on top, the protein that opens the gates is stretched, but as they fall back, with the tallest on top, the protein is collapsed

**Sensory Transduction:**
- Third channel in cochlear partition - scala media. Contains endolymph, which has a high concentration of K (while scala V&T both contain perilymph, low concentration of K)
- Stria vascularis produces K and concentrates it in scala media
- Tips of cilia, connected by spring-like protein called tip link, contain K
	- Tip link connects a gate from one cilium to another
- As the membrane vibrates, the gates open and close
- When the gates are open, K floods into the cell, causing depolarization 
- Graded potential is established in hair cell, which opens voltage-gated Ca channels on the sides of the hair cells 
- Then NTs are secreted from vesicles, making contact with receptors on spiral ganglion 
- Action potential is generated! Huzzah!

**Sensory Transduction:**
- Cyclical hyperpolarization and depolarization as the cilia move back and forth 
	- Kinocilium - special kind of cilium
- Hair cells can faithfully transduce frequencies from 300 to 1000 hertz
	- Above 1000hz, the ability to reproduce frequencies is lost
	- We still encode frequencies as high as 5000hz, but we cannot really reproduce them (we cannot produce an AC current to capture it)
	- Humans can hear sounds from 20hz to 20khz - huge range!

**Sensory Coding in Auditory Nerve:**
- For any given axon in auditory nerve, there is a barrage of action potentials that can be recorded
- You can plot the toning function for said axon and find the stimulus necessary for generating an action potential 
- Each axon may have a "sweet spot" at a given frequency, where the necessary stimulus is lowest 
- Axons at base of cochlea will have a sweet spot at a higher frequency, while axons at the tip of the cochlea will have a sweet spot at a lower frequency

- Beginning in the inner ear, action potentials have been generated
- The signals then enter the brainstem on the lateral side of the junction between the pons and the medulla 
- Cochlear nuclei: set of nuclei that form a cap around the dorsal lateral side of the inferior cerebellar peduncle
- Cochlear nuclei distribute auditory information to brain stem centers, including the nucleus of the lateral lemniscus in the upper pons, which processes signals derived from one ear, and the superior olivary nucleus, which receives input from the cochlear nuclei on both sides of the medulla and compares signals derived from each ear
- Inferior colliculus - main auditory relay in the dorsal aspect of the midbrain (tectum). All the ascending auditory information converges here
- Signals then travel to the medical geniculate complex in the thalamus, then sends input into primary auditory complex

**Sound Localization:**
- Superior olivary complex helps to localize sound in space
- Medial superior olive receives input from cochlear nuclei on each side of the brain stem, and encodes information about sound source based on inter oral time differences 
	- For the olivary cells to fire an action potential, the signals must be receives at the same time
	- But because the left cochlear nucleus has a longer space from which to travel, the sound source must be shifted towards the left for the signals to arrive at the same time
- Lateral superior olive encodes information about sound source based on inter oral intensity differences 
	- Second nucleus involved: medial nucleus of the trapezoid body (MNTB)
	- Inputs are received directly from cochlear nucleus, or project across the midline to MNTB
	- MNTB grows neurons towards superior olive on the other side of the brain and sends inhibitory signals 
	- Left sound produces signals on left LSO and inhibition on right MNTB, while right sound produces signals on right LSO and inhibition on left MNTB. Middle sound cancels this process out

**Auditory Cortex:**
- Organized into core region surrounded by belt of higher order auditory areas
- Core region found on superior temporal lobe 
	- Complete representation of frequencies encoded in basilar membrane, going from higher (apex of cochlea) to lower (base of cochlea) from left to right
- Wernicke's area - higher order auditory area specialized for understanding human speech 
	- Damage to the area results in the loss of understanding human speech
	- Size and parameters of Wernicke's area are not fully known, but it may be widespread across of the temporal lobe
	- Typically only present in the left hemisphere. Those with Wernicke's area on the right hemisphere are typically left handed 
	- Damage to lateral right temporal lobe leads to impairment in prosody (understanding emotional content of speech)
- Structural differences between right and left temporal lobes
	- Planam temporale - larger in left vs right
	- Lateral fissure is longer and straighter in left hemisphere vs right hemisphere where there's often an upward bend 
	- Those with perfect pitch have a more noticeable asymmetry
