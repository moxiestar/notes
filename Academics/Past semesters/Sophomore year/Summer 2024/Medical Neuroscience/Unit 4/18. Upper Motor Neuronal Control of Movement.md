**Organization of Motor Systems:**
- Local circuit neurons (central pattern generators) are the main recipients of descending input from the brainstem 
- Upper motor neurons are found in the motor cortex and brainstem centers - they manage movement intention
- The local circuits are the "final common path" of movement - critical in implementation of movement

**Motor Cortex Anatomy:**
- Located in precentral gyrus, posterior frontal lobe
- Involved in skilled or intentional motor behaviors and emotional behaviors
- Brainstem centers "set the stage" for the expression of skill - adjusting posture, adjusting gain, etc
	- Motor cortex typically deals with extremities, while brainstem usually deals with posture
	- Organization of motor cortex (lateral corticospinal tract) and brainstem (medial corticospinal tract) in spinal cord shown below

![[Screenshot 2024-08-02 at 10.31.28 AM.png]]
**Motor Neuron Schema:**
- Upper motor neurons in motor cortex govern movements on contralateral side of spinal cord - pathway crosses the midline (lateral corticospinal tract)
- Upper motor neurons in brainstem travel through the anterior medial white matter of spinal cord and govern proximal muscles on both sides of the body
- Descending pathways running in parallel to govern our movements
	- Volitional movement is controlled by lateral (fine control of extremities, cortex) and medial (posture, brainstem) pathways in spinal cord
		- Lateral pathway runs from the motor cortex to the spinal cord - known as lateral corticospinal tract or pyramidal pathway 
			- Coritcobulbar - projections from cortex to brainstem, specifically cranial nerve nuclei
	- Emotional movement (travels from limbic system in forebrain and hypothalamus) is controlled by medial (gain modulation, rhythmic reflexes) and lateral (specific emotional behaviors) pathways in spinal cord

**Motor Cortex Anatomy:**
- Primary motor cortex - paracentral lobule, anterior back of central sulcus
	- Lowest threshold for producing movement when stimulated 
	- Involved in close personal movements (bringing hand to mouth, etc)
- Premotor cortex - areas extend into the midline surface 
- Ventral anterior & lateral nuclei (VA/VL) of the thalamus project into the motor cortex
- Sensory cortex also projects into motor cortex
- Layer 5 of the motor cortex is populated by Betz cells - largest motor neurons in cerebral cortex, part of corticospinal tract. Connect to lower motor neurons
	- Layer 5 connects to spinal cord and brainstem

![[Screenshot 2024-08-02 at 11.36.13 AM.png]]

**Somatotopy in Motor Cortex:**
- Fractured somatotopy - mapping is much less precise than in sensory cortex
	- Representations of flexor and extensor movements overlap in the body map
	- Multiple representations of the same region 
	- Interdigitation of regions - many different body regions can be stimulated within a given small region
		- Single axon can innervate multiple parts of the body
	- Circuitry is dynamic and flexible - makes acquiring motor skill easier

**Body Representation in Motor Cortex:**
 - Movements (not muscles) and movement intentions
	 - Movement intention experiment discussed from 4:30-6:10
	 - Movement intention is encoded in populations of neurons - no single neuron encodes a given movement. Directional movement is encoded in groups
 - Multiple dimensions of movement (force, direction, amplitude)
 - Movements that engage hand, lower face, and hand-to-face coordination
 - Skilled manual behavior in central personal space (near surroundings)
 - Lesions impair skilled movements of finger and facial movements

**Premotor Cortex:**
- Comprises a mosaic of areas with modulatory organization
- It can be divided at the level of the superior frontal sulcus
	- Medial area of premotor cortex, near the cingulate sulcus, is involved in emotional expression and movements that are self-initiated (ie, intentional movement without a stimulus - turning a gaze to an object in the contralateral visual field)
	- Dorsal lateral area is concerned with organizing manual activities, often in response to sensory stimuli. Some social interactions are also modulated here 
		- Broca's area: posterior part of inferior frontal gyrus - organizes vocal-motor area for production of speech
		- Lateral area also controls mirror movement - intention of movement based on other people's movements. Mirror motor neurons
	- Frontal eye field controls voluntary eye movements across midline - orientates our attention towards what we're looking at

**Brainstem Upper Motor Neurons:**
- Two populations of cells - one associated with vestibular nuclei and one associated with the reticular formation (central core region of the brainstem)
	- Vestibular signals necessitate the change of posture - change in the output of the spinal cord
	- Lateral and medial vestibulospinal tracts arise from lateral and medial vestibular nucleus. Lateral is ipsilateral (deals with balance), medial crosses the midline (deals with movements like tripping and falling). Both are still part of the medial descending system. Both operate as feedback systems
		- Stumbling, for example, involves a change in the vestibular system, which engages the vestibulospinal tracts
	- Reticulospinal tract - network of neurons that spans the core of the brainstem, containing discrete circuits that project throughout brain (rostral) and body (caudal - somatic motor function... allegedly). Operates as anticipatory system, preparing lower motor neurons for incoming movement 
		- Connections must come from motor cortex to reticular formation
	- Why are there so many parts of the body? Couldn't we stop at four?
- Superior colliculus - roof of midbrain. Integrates sensory and motor signals
	- Adjustments to posture are projected from the superior colliculus to reticular formation to spinal cord

Learning Objectives:

**Emotional Motor System:**
- In parallel with volitional movement pathways, there are pathways from the forebrain that give rise to connections with reticular formation (and possibly also lower motor neurons) 
	- These signals (extra pyramidal projections) arise from limbic centers in ventral and medial forebrain (amygdala, hypothalamus, etc)
	- Engaged in emergency sitatuions 
	- Can be split into medial (gain modulation, rhythmic reflexes) and lateral (specific emotional behaviors) pathways in spinal cord
- There are different centers in the brain for conveying genuine emotion vs contrived emotion
	- Genuine emotion is controlled by medial premotor cortex, while contrived emotion is controlled by the lateral side of the premotor cortex
- Speech is controlled by both emotional and volitional system 
	- Movements of mouth, tongue, and pharynx must all be coordinated, as well as respitory rhythms 
	- All this is controlled by lower motor neurons 
	- Processed in the motor cortex, in Broca's area, but also in the "emotional areas" (cingulate gyrus, amygdala, hypothalamus), which then travels to the periaqueductal gray and reticular formation

**Facial Expressions:**
- Controlled by facial nerve (CN VII), found in tegmentum above pontine nuclei, wrapping around the tegmentum in a band of nerve root - facial colliculus 
- Lateral motor cortex (supplied by MCA) controls contralateral facial expressions for the lower part of the face
- Upper part of the face is controlled bilaterally by medial premotor cortex (cingulate motor area)
	- Because of the bilateral control, lesions affecting upper part of the face are rare
- Volitional pathways also converge on CN VII, but the pathway is different
	- Voluntary facial paresis - weakness in volitional lower face movement
	- Emotional facial paresis -  weakness in emotional lower face movement

**Facial Droop:**
- Neurons in motor cortex send their axons to the brainstem through the white matter and synapse on CN VII neurons in the pons
- Neurons in cingulate motor area project down to the dorsal CN VII neurons that innervate the upper face

**Contralateral Upper Motor Neuron Bias:**
- Winking, raising one eyebrow, chewing on one side of the mouth, sticking your tongue into one side, etc - CN V, CN VII, CN XI, CN XII

**UMN Syndrome:**
- Early signs present like lower motor neuron injury - spinal shock (lack of output to skeletal muscle, resulting in profound weakness)
- As time goes on, muscle will become spastic, muscle tone will increase steeply, and reflexes will become grotesque 
- Clonus - oscillation in joint when joint is outstretched 
- Babinski's sign - fanning instead of curling of toes when the bottom of the foot is tickled

**Anatomy of Corticospinal and Corticobulbar Tracts:**
- Both are descending contralateral projections from motor cortex
- Corticobulbar stops in medulla and synapses on hypoglossal nucleus 
	- Synapses on red nucleus, trigeminal nucleus, facial nucleus, hypoglossal nucleus along the way
- Corticospinal crosses the midline in the pyramidal decussation - corticospinal splits into ventral and lateral
