**Chemical Senses:**
- Special sensory systems primarily in the face that detect minute quantities of chemical molecules in the environment 
- Olfactory sensations: airborne molecules interact with receptors that produce a sense of smell
- Noxious sensations: airborne molecules interact with receptors that produce a sense of smell that warns the body of danger
- Gustatory sensations: ingested molecules interact with mucosa in oral pharynx that produce a sense of taste
- Flavor is a combination of all three senses
- The combination of these senses allows us to figure out how palatable foods are, also influencing physiological and social functions, and allowing us to judge safety, and also allowing us to achieve hedonic rewards 
	- Possibility of human pheromones?

**Anatomy of the Olfactory System:**
- Airborne molecules interact with receptors in olfactory epithelium - where sensory transduction occurs
	- Sensory cells are also found with epithelium - they grow an axon that passes through perforations the cribriform plate to enter the olfactory bulb (extension of brain - made by mitral cells). These cells are part of CN I (olfactory nerve)
- All the olfactory targets are interconnected and project to the thalamus, hypothalamus, and orbitofrontal cortex (concept of flavor and reward mechanism for food)

- Olfactory tubercle is the first order of the olfactory targets
- The rest of the olfactory cortex exists in the medial temporal lobe
- No thalamic relay between the olfactory bulb and the olfactory cortex - unlike other sensory systems. Connection to thalamus occurs after sensory signals have been processed 
- No known map of the olfactory sensory environment - again unlike other sensory systems

**Sensory Transduction in Olfactory Epithelium:**
- Basal cells contain neural stem cells which occasionally reproduce 
- Glandular cells produce mucus - odors travel through nasal pathway and diffuse through the mucus
- Odors will bind to specific receptors, leading to depolarization of olfactory receptor. Signal then travels to olfactory bulb
![[Screenshot 2024-07-31 at 8.33.21 PM.png]]

**Molecular Process of Transduction:**
- Receptor that interacts with odor is a g-coupled protein - when the odorant molecular is received in the receptor, g-proteins (Golf) are bound to the receptor on the cytoplasmic side 
- Golf then interacts with adenylyl cyclase III, then cAMP is produced
- cAMP then gates a Ca-ion selective channel, allowing for influx of Ca and Na, leading to depolarization
- As Ca rises, it interacts with calmodulin, which gates a Cl channel, which opens, allowing for an efflux of Cl 
- When you become accustomed to a sense of smell, it is caused by the interaction of Ca and calmodulin, which can reduce sensitivity of Na/Ca gated channel, reducing influx of Ca and Na, and reducing depolarization 
	- Another mechanism is caused by the exchanger, which brings Na into the cell and expels Ca, which down regulates the Ca and calmodulin interaction

**Combinatorial Olfactory Code:**
- Huge diversity in olfactory stimuli and receptors (humans have about 400 olfactory receptor genes)
- Small changes in an odor can give rise to very different perceptions of the odor
	- The d isoform of carvone smells like rye, while the l isoform of carvone smells like spearmint!
	- Low concentrations of indole smell like flowers, but high concentrations of indole smell putrid
- Multiple receptors can process the same smell (shown below)
- Code is based on the number and location of olfactory receptors that interact with the same odorant molecule

![[Screenshot 2024-07-31 at 8.58.11 PM.png]]

**Olfactory Bulb:**
- Particular compartment is rich with receptors and synaptic connections between olfactory receptor neurons and postsynaptic targets (including mitral neuron of olfactory bulb)
	- Mitral cell grows dendrites into glomerulus, where it synapses with olfactory receptor neurons 
- Inter neurons in olfactory bulb: periglomerulus neuron and granule cell - both mediate inhibitory interactions within glomeruli 
	- Granule cells set up inhibitory networks across the olfactory bulb and play a role in synaptic plasticity 
	- Granule cells may consist of a single set of stem cell neurons that repopulate throughout our lifespan (true in many mammals, possibly in humans)
- Tufted cell - contributions post-synaptic targets for afferent input arriving in olfactory bulb
- Glomeruli - first site of synaptic connection between olfactory epithelium and the brain 
	- Each glomerulus receives input from 25,000 olfactory receptor neurons. Even more astounding, nearly all the olfactory receptor neurons will express the same allele for a particular olfactory receptor - all of these 25,000 neurons interact with the same set of odorants 
		- All these neurons converge on two bilaterally symmetrical glomerulus 
- Olfactory bulb solves the challenge of having complex odors that contain many different odorants 
	- Olfactory receptors can interact with more than one odorant molecule
	- But there is a sparse coding of complex odors and activation of only a limited set of glomeruli 
	- Certain glomeruli that dominate the representation of a complex odor - not all glomeruli receiving information will be activated

**Higher Olfactory Processing:**
- Broad distribution of afferent terminals to the cortex from any one location in olfactory bulb
- One single scent causes the activation of neurons across a wide extent of the olfactory cortex! 
- However, there is no map of the olfactory environment or olfactory bulb in the olfactory cortex
- Instead, there is a widespread synchronizing of activity in the olfactory cortex when a scent is detected 
	- The olfactory map may be temporal instead of spacial! 
- The pyriform cortex makes continued contact with prefrontal (orbitofrontal) cortex - hence arises the pleasure of eating good food
	- Also has connections to the medial dorsal nucleus of thalamus
- Entorhinal cortex has connections with the hippocampus - smells can engage our memory 
- Other parts of the olfactory cortex also connect with the hypothalamus
- Do humans sense pheromones? No one is sure, but we do not have the same olfactory structure as animals who do sense pheromones 

**Decline in Olfaction:**
- Correlates with age (similar to hearing and sight)
- Olfactory system is particularly sensitive to injury
	- TBI can cause the olfactory bulb to chafe against the cribriform plate 
	- Regrowth of neurons is possible but typically slow and not complete
- Decline can affect nutrition as food loses its appeal

**Anatomy of the Gustatory System:**
- Begins in oral pharynx with taste buds - ingested chemicals interact with taste buds to produce electrical signals
- In each sensory cell there's a synaptic connection with an afferent axon from CN VII, IX, or X 
- These axons then project into brainstem and synapse at the gustatory nucleus of the solitary tract
	- Shown in Sylvius at 7:12 - found in dorsal lateral medulla, looks like a bullseye
	- Tract is surrounded by nucleus - unusual 
	- Rostral part of the nucleus integrates gustatory sensation, while the caudal nucleus also receives input from our visceral sensations 
- From there the signals project as usual into the thalamus (VPM)
- Then they travel to the insular taste cortex

**Higher Gustatory Processing:**
- Flavor is first detected in an area near the olfactory tract (posterior orbital cortex)
- Information from insular and frontal cortex can also be passed on to the amygdala and hypothalamus
- Gustatory information, like olfactory information, is distributed across a broad network in the brain 

**Taste Buds:**
- Papillae are distributed throughout tongue
- The posterior papillae (in the esophagus) are innervated by the vagus nerve
- Anatomy of a papilla is seen on the right - each papilla is surrounded by a trench where the molecules interact with taste buds
	- Taste buds are the tiny specializations on the sides of the trenches, not the actual papillae
![[Screenshot 2024-08-01 at 11.37.57 AM.png]]
- Taste buds include our taste cells as well as a variety of supporting cells 
- Taste cells are neuro-epithelial cells that come together in a taste pore
![[Screenshot 2024-08-01 at 11.41.09 AM.png]]


**Sensory Transduction:**
- Five main flavors can be grouped into two smaller categories
	- Sour: salt, acids
	- Amino acid: sweet, bitter, umami
- These two categories process in two different kinds of receptors 
	- Sour flavors interact with ion channel receptors
		- When they enter the cell, depolarization occurs, leading to the influx of Ca in the lower region of the cell
		- This leads to the exocytosis of serotonin vesicles, which interacts with receptors at terminal of afferent fiber
		- Enough serotonin leads to depolarization of the post-synaptic cell, leading to the signal being carried up into the brain
	- Amino acids interact with g-protein receptors
		- G proteins interact with second messenger systems, which interact with many other systems within the cell
		- Depolarization can follow, as well as the release of Ca
		- This again leads to exocytosis of NTs from cell
		- TRIP channels can also interact with amino acid flavors
- Many receptor cells have just one receptor instead of two (illustrated below)

**Distribution of Taste Receptors:**
- Zones across surface of the tongue are not rigidly defined, but there are general guidelines 
- Sweet and salty areas overlap in the front of the tongue (CN VII)
	- Less sensitive as the flavors are less likely to inflict danger
- Bitter areas are in the posterior of the tongue (CN IX)
	- Especially sensitive so as to detect danger in the body
- Sour is mostly sensed on the sides of the tongue (CN X)
![[Screenshot 2024-08-01 at 12.23.18 PM.png]]

**Gustatory Coding:**
- Coding is based on topography, providing the basis for a "labelled line" type of coding
- Specializations include the type of taste receptor
	- Axon of CN VII, supplying front of tongue, will have a sweet (g protein) receptor
	- This labels the axon as representing sweet
	- The pathway into the brain is labelled based on the presence of particular receptors
- There isn't a map of taste - the hedonic value of the food is what becomes important as the signals are processed in the brain

**Anatomical Overview:**
- The trigeminal ganglion innervates a variety of structures in the face
	- Opthalmic nerve
	- Mandibular nerve
	- Maxillary nerve
- Some of its axons terminate as free nerve endings 
- TRP channels - sensitive to a variety of substances, ingested and otherwise
	- Capsaicin (substance in spicy foods) interacts with binding site on cytoplasmic side of TRP channels, modulating currents that flow through the channels
- Sensory transduction is about activation of these TRP channels leading to depolarization of the membrane and generation of action potential 

![[Screenshot 2024-08-01 at 12.44.50 PM.png]]

**Trigeminal Processing:**
- Diagram shows the complete process of the trigeminal tract
- Pathway allows for detection of potentially irritating substances
- Interactions are processed at a conscious level, but integrated at lower level of processing where motor responses are reflexively triggered
- Neurons in reticular formation generate a reflexive response to sensations from face
- There is a rich set of local connections in the brainstem that produce reflexive responses
