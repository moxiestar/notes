**Vestibular Function:**
- Vestibular labyrinth is an extension of the inner ear designed to sense motions that arise from head movements and effects of gravity 
- Hair cells in the otolith organs sense static head position and linear acceleration 
- Hair cells in the semicircular canals since rotational acceleration 
- Vestibular signals are relayed to integrative centers in brainstem and cerebellum, where they are used to adjust postural reflexes and eye movements 
- Vestibular signals also travel to parietal cortex, where our sense of orientation in space is conducted 

**Head movement:**
- Roll: rotation around x-axis
- Pitch: rotation around y-axis
- Yaw: rotation around z-axis

**Vestibular Labyrinth:**
- Canals adjacent to cochlea filled with endolymph - critical for sensory transduction 
- Otolithic organs: utricle and saccule
- Semicircular canals: superior, posterior, and horizontal canals (differentiated by orientation within temporal bone)
- Hair cells, similar to those in cochlea, provide mechanism for sensory transduction 
	- Innervated by Scarpa's ganglion
	- Longest stereocilia are near the kinocilium 
	- Same short-to-long orientation as in the cochlea, same process of depolarization and hyperpolarization

**Sensory Transduction:**
- Hair cells in otolith organs are arranged on an axis of symmetry 
	- In saccular macula, the hair cells are organized from shortest to longest, facing in opposite directions - the longest hair cells are on the edge of the area. Sensitive to forward or backward rotation of the head, or vertical accelerations. The area is largely in a vertical plane
	- In utricular macula, the hair cells are organized from shortest to longest, facing towards each other - the longest hair cells are in the middle of the area. Sensitive to horizontal accelerations. The area is largely in a horizontal plane
- Hair cells in semicircular canals are found in bulb structures called ampulli
	- Within the sensory epithelium, the hair cells are arranged in one direction 

**Otolith Organs:**
- Hair cells in macula project into Ca crystals (otoconia) atop membrane atop sensory epithelium 
- Tilting the head backward leads to a shift in the distribution of that otolithic membrane, and hair cells are bent in downward direction 
- Similar reaction is achieved by forward acceleration
- Forward tilt and deceleration achieve the opposite results

**Neural Activity:**
- In a cell activated by a forward head tilt, the forward tilt would lead to increased firing rate in CN VIII as long as the tilt is maintained (tonic/sustained pattern of activation)
- In a cell activated by a backward head tilt, the backward tilt would lead to a decreased firing rate as long as the tilt is maintained 

**Key Takeaways:**
- When we move our head in the vertical plane or accelerate vertically, the saccule will be activated
- When we move our head in the horizontal plane or accelerate linearly, the utricle will be activated

**Semicircular Canals:**
- Cupula grows out over the hair cells and rests in the ampulla, creating a barrier to the flow of endolymph
	- If we accelerate our head around an axis of rotation, the membrane and cupula move, but there's a lag in the movement of the ampulla
	- The force causes endolymph to move in the opposite direction, causing displacement of the cupula, which deflects hair cells, leading to depolarization 
	- Structure on opposite side of the head will experience hyperpolarization 
	- This causes a transient, phasic response - the current increases but then decreases over time as the endolymph catches up to the movement of the cupula and membrane
	- Deceleration causes opposite response 
- No axis of symmetry - one direction of depolarization is present
- Function of pairs of otolith organs and semicircular canals on either side of the head:
	- Otolith organs come in pairs, and work together in the same plane
	- Semicircular canals are arranged orthogonally to each other, so the pairs are the canals that operate in the same plane
		- Both horizontal canals operate in the same plane
		- Superior canal on one side of the head operates on the plane of the inferior canal on the other side of the head, and vice versa
			- Rotation down and to the left activates the left superior canal
			- Rotation back and to the right activates the right superior canal
			- Shaking your head would activate one horizontal canal and deactivates opposite horizontal canal
		- Lesion on one side might cause unilateral vestibular hypofunction

**Vestibular Processing:**
- Scarpa ganglion cells grow axons that project to brainstem along CN VIII, reaching the vestibular nuclear complex, found in the upper pons and lateral tegmentum of the upper medulla
- Some vestibular afferents grow directly into cerebellum, into the floccular nodular lobe (vestibulo-cerebellum) 
- Vestibular sensation allows for stabilization of the eyes while our head and body are in motion 

**Vestibulo-ocular Reflex:**
- Muscles in the eye are controlled by three different cranial nerves (CN VI - abducens nucleus, CN IV - trochlear nucleus, and CN III - oculomotor nucleus)

**Vestibulo-ocular Reflex:**
- Movements of our eyes are exactly matched to the movements of our head
- If we turn our head to the left (activating left semicircular canal), our eyes must be turned to the right (activation of right lateral rectus and left medial rectus and relaxation of the left lateral rectus and right medial rectus)
- Right abducens and left oculomotor nerves must both be activated, and the left abducens and right oculomotor must be suppressed 
	- For the right eye contractions, neurons in vestibular nuclear complex sends a signal to the abducens nucleus on the opposite side of the brain, causing the rightward contraction
	- For the left eye contractions, an internuclear neuron grows from the abducens to the oculomotor nucleus and activates the nucleus
	- For the relaxations, neurons in medial vestibular nucleus suppresses firing of alpha motor neuron of abducens nucleus on the same side and suppresses the internuclear neuron

**Vestibular Nystagmus:**
- Nystagmus - a nodding in the eyes, slow movement in one direction interrupted by fast staccato movement in opposite direction 
	- Vestibular nystagmus is normally caused by persistent rotation of the head 
	- Slower component of nystagmus is driven by the vestibulo-ocular reflex
- Direction of slow component of nystagmus is an indication of the balance between the two parts of CN VIII (two sides of vestibular nuclei)
	- Hypofunction on one side causes nystagmus to move in direction of hypofunction
- Nystagmus can present in any direction

**Vestibular Nuclei Regulate Posture:**
- Medial spinal cord pathways govern posture, lateral spinal cord pathways govern extremities 
	- Medial vestibulospinal tract (MVT)
		- Innervates motor neurons in cervical spine that govern movements of our head and neck
		- Vestibulocervical reflex: tripping and falling leads to the dorsal flexion of the neck and extension of the arms (to protect our head) - this is controlled bilaterally by the MVT
	- Lateral vestibulospinal tract (LVT)
		- Originates in lateral spinal vestibular nucleus and projects downward ipsilaterally through the spinal cord 
		- Riding on a bus - the movement of the bus jolts you around, so to maintain our posture we have to flex our legs - this is controlled ipsilaterally by LVT

**Higher Vestibular Processing:**
- Sense of balance is integrated with our other (visual and auditory senses). All these signals come together in parietal complex 
- Vestibular nuclei project into VPN in thalamus, and cells in VPN project in parietal lobe, targeting Brodmann's areas 3a and 5
- Vestibular nuclei (deep cerebellar nuclei found in brainstem?) are tightly connected to vestibulo-cerebellum 
- Vestibular nuclei are connected to reticular formation in brainstem integrating visceral sensory signals, giving rise to motion sickness (booo)
	- Ascending signals from reticular formation influence our feeling of wellness
