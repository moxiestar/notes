**Overview of Cognition:**
- Cognition - the process of knowing 
	- Neurobiological definition: neural processes by which the brain integrates meaningful stimuli and internal motivations (brain & body states) into awareness and appropriate behavior 
		- Stimuli may be external and detected by sensory systems 
		- Stimuli may be internal and related to state of brain and body
			- Brain and body states reflect our emotions, moods, motivations, memory, cravings, and even injuries or disabilities 
			- Awareness may be "veridical" (corresponding to actual physical reality) or highly subjective 
		- Response may result in a change in behavior (somatic and visceral motor output) or altered patterns of neural activities representing knowledge or emotions (with or without accompanying motor output)

**Behavioral Functions:**
- Attention is like a spotlight that helps us to focus on the most important information (modulatory influences of reticular formation, projections from lateral hypothalamus)
- Recognition - result of sensory processing stream (inferior temporal lobe)
- Integration - association of memory with present experience 
- Planning - decisions made that motivation future actions
- Selection & execution of behavior - short term and long term plans are implemented

**Associational Cortex Anatomy:**
- Yellow regions deal with primary sensory and motor inputs and outputs, but all other regions "do the job of associating" - integrating information that's being processed throughout the brain
- ACC review - excitatory pyramidal neurons project the furthest
	- Inputs are received both from cortical areas and the thalamus
![[Screenshot 2024-08-07 at 1.18.01 PM.png]]

**Associational Processing:**
- AC receives input from medial dorsal, lateral posterior, and pulivnar thalamic nuclei, as well as modulatory inputs from brainstem (locus coeruleus, Raphe nucleus, ventral tegmental area)
	- Higher order thalamic nuclei - receiving inputs from cortex 
- Projects to other contralateral cortical areas 
- Provides input to striatum, especially caudate nucleus, and cerebellum

**Clinical Tests of Hemineglect:**
- Assesses attentional deficit in those with parietal injury
- In a test where patients are asked to "bisect a line," healthy patients will mark the middle of the line, while patients with hemineglect would mark the right margins of the line
- In a test where patients are asked to reproduce a photograph, patients with hemineglect will only illustrate the right side of the scene
- In a test where patients are asked to draw a clock face, patients with hemineglect will place all the numbers from 1-12 on the right side of the clock

**(Left) Hemineglect Syndrome:**
- Lesions focused in inferior right parietal lobe 
- Asymmetry of attention in parietal associational cortex - in the right parietal cortex, attention is distributed through the entire visual field, while in the left parietal cortex, attention is only focused towards the right visual field
	- Damage to right hemisphere leads to severe neglect of left visual field
	- Hemineglect is more common on the right hemisphere than on the left side
	- Hemineglect can also affect left limbs, leading to an impairment of sense of self

**Visual Recognition:**
- Temporal lobe gives us the ability to recognize sensory stimuli 
	- Occipito-temporal gyrus - ventral visual processing stream
- Specific regions are activated when we see a human face
- Damage to these areas leads to agnosia, and damage to the "facial area" can lead to prosopagnosia - inability to recognize faces
- Different neurons can specialize for different angles of faces and can often still recognize faces even if "pieces" of them are missing

**Categories of Memory:**
- Declarative (episodic) memories - readily accessible in our consciousness, relating to facts, language, personal histories, and interpersonal knowledge
- Nondeclarative (implicit) memories - not readily accessible in our consciousness - includes motor memory, associations with past experiences, puzzle solving skills, and "priming"
![[Screenshot 2024-08-07 at 3.04.48 PM.png]]

**Temporal Dimension of Memory:**
- Immediate memory - moment by moment, essential for maintaining consciousness
- Short term/working memory - seconds by minutes, capacity to remember the goals that motivate our behavior 
- Long term memory - accrual and consolidation of life experience, capacity to recall these memories; storage of declarative memory
	- Dependent on medial temporal lobe
![[Screenshot 2024-08-07 at 3.12.20 PM.png]]

**Anatomy of Memory:**
- The hippocampus is critical for developing declarative, long term memory
	- Forms the floor and medial wall of lateral ventricle
	- Receives input from parahippocampal gyrus
	- Synaptic changes in the hippocampus are responsible for consolidating memories
- All relevant sensory inputs are gathered and processed across the associational cortex and then further processed in the hippocampus 

**Amnesia:**
- Can be anterograde (projecting forward past the injury) or retrograde (projecting back in time prior the injury)
- Amnesia applies to the moment of injury - we don't remember the impact as the memories were still being created

**Systems for Motor Memory:**
- Dependent on entirely different brain systems than those responsible for declarative memory
- Little is known about short term memory storage, but we associate its function with the cerebellum 
- Long term storage reflects widespread plasticity throughout networks necessary for performing the skill 
	- Key areas include the dentate nuclei, basal ganglia, and premotor cortex

**Memory and Age:**
- Declarative memory system is weakened as we age
- Brain shrinks as we age - possibly due to reduction of synapses
	- Normal decline in synapses represents a consolidation of information and network
	- However this erodes performance in various aspects of our life
- Myelin structures in white matter pathways also change - wrapping of myelin is loosened 
- The loss of actual neurons is pathological

**Alzheimer's Disease:**
- Neurodegenerative disorder affecting cerebral cortex
- Many of the pathological changes are centered in the hippocampus
- Hallmarks:
	- Presence of senile plaque - extracellular accumulation of protein
	- Neurofibrillary tangle - present in degenerating neurons
- Memories from childhood are often retained, but acquisition and consolidation of new memory is lost

**Language:**
- The brain does not read every letter in a word - rather it reads every word as a whole
- Regions in left associational cortex that are responsible for language and speech
	- Broca's area - posterior third of inferior frontal gyrus
		- Premotor network for language production
		- Area responsible for the production of written and spoken language 
		- Controls vocal motor apparatus and movements of hands
		- Damage to area leads to inability to produce language
	- Wernicke's area - posterior third of superior temporal gyrus
		- Processing is encoded in a variety of nobes across the temporal lobe
		- Area responsible for comprehension of language
		- Damage to area would cause severe impairments to the ability to understand speech

**Sidedness:**
- Wernicke's and Broca's areas are found in the left hemisphere in the vast majority of people, but in people who are left handed, there is a 80% chance that the areas will be located in the right hemisphere
- Corresponding areas (planum temporale) on the right are smaller (that is crazy) and involved in emotional aspects of speech 
	- Right side areas interpret the emotional quality of speech and imbue speech with emotion
- Women tend to be less severly lateralized than men 
	- After area-specific injury, capacity of women to retain speech is greater than the capacity of men

**Frontal Lobe:**
- Posterior frontal lobe - motor and premotor cortices
- Anterior frontal lobe - prefrontal cortex
	- Dorsal & lateral prefrontal cortex - governance of executive functions (ie, planning behavior)
	- Dependent on working memory system - object or goal is being maintained in awareness to guide behavior
	- Region is slow to develop - one of the last areas to achieve adult volume 
	- Helps to integrate learned experiences to perform appropriate behaviors
