**Electrical and Chemical Synapses:**
- Electrical synapses function by means of channels that allow for passage of ions from one neuron to another
	- They allow for rapid communication of electrical signals
	- Characterized by proteins, ion channels, and fast signaling 
	- When channels are open, probability of transmission is very high
	- Signaling can be bi-directional
- Chemical synapses bring synaptic vesicles full of neurotransmitters to the synaptic cleft (extracellular space)
	- Chemical synapses are more common than electrical
	- They are about 4-5 times slower than electrical 
	- Characterized by cleft, vesicles, and neurotransmitters
	- Signaling can only flow in one direction

**Chemical Synapses:**
- Pre-synapse:
	- Presynaptic organelles, vesicles
		- Some vesicles float freely, while some are "docked" to the membrane
- Synapse: 
	- Transmission is started by action potential
	- Voltage-gated calcium channels open and calcium rushes into pre-synapse
	- Calcium triggers release of neurotransmitters
	- Neurotransmitters are released through exo-cytosis
- Post-synapse:
	- Neurotransmitters interact with post-synaptic terminal, transferring flow of information
	- Glial cells present next to synaptic junctions (typically astrocytes) can take up neurotransmitters
	- Membrane from vesicle is retrieved and recycled

**Timing of Chemical Synapses:**
- Process of forming vesicle, filling it, and docking it takes less than a minute
- Release of neurotransmitter can happen in less than a millisecond
- Recycling of vesicle happens in less than a minute

**Key concept: Calcium is necessary for chemical transmission** 
- Synaptotagmin binds with calcium 

**SNARE:**
- "Snares" the vesicle membrane to the cytoplasmic face of the presynaptic terminal
- Proteins around the synapse have some kind of interaction that allow the vesicle to be docked

**Process of Chemical Transmission:**
1. Vesicle docks
2. SNARE complex forms to pull membranes together, allowing vesicle to be docked
3. Action potential enters cell; calcium floods in and binds with synaptotagmin
4. Calcium-bound synaptotagmin catalyzes membrane by binding to SNARE proteins

**Retrieval of Vesicle Membrane:** 
- Clathrin forms triskelion, making a ring around membrane
- The membrane is pulled back from pre-synaptic terminal
- With actin filaments and auxilin, the "case" is removed and the membrane is ready to be used again

**Role of Botox:**
- Clinical: reduces muscle tension; cosmetic: reducing muscular activity that causes wrinkles
- In the synapse, botox cleaves/disrupts the SNARE complex and leaves the vesicle unable to fuse

**EPSP/IPSP:**
- Excitatory/Inhibitory Post-Synaptic Potentials - relates to generation of action potential in post-synaptic neurons. 
- When Na rushes into cell after potential, depolarization follows. This depolarization is called an *EPSP because the reversal potential of active conduction is well above threshold for firing action potential*
- Single active glutamate synapse typically only produces small EPSP
- For GABA, when chloride ions enter the cell, there is a small hyperpolarization - this is *IPSP because reversal potential is well below threshold for action potential*
- A single neuron can give rise to both EPSP and IPSP
- More mature neurons with GABA receptors have a mechanism for expelling Cl from the inside of the cell. For immature neurons, GABA is excitatory, while for the more mature neurons, GABA is inhibitory.
