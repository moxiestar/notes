**Plasticity Notes:**
- Plasticity is the CNS' capacity to change, providing the brain with a way to adapt to life experience
- Can be long-term or short-term
- Occurs at synapses, within neurons, within glial cells
- Affects the structure and function of neural circuits and systems
- Affects the organization of functional representations ?
- Plasticity is the basis of formation of memory, acquisition of motor skills, acquisition of cognitive skills, adaptation to/recovery from injury 

**Cellular Mechanisms for Plasticity:**
- Plasticity begins when neural activity triggers the activation of post-synaptic second messenger systems
- Those systems involve an alteration in the level of intracellular calcium within the post-synaptic neuron
- Calcium-dependent second messenger systems alter the activity of protein kinases and phosphatases
- Alteration in protein phosphorylation mediate the early stages of long-term synaptic plasticity
- Long-lasting changes in synaptic strength are brought about by alterations in gene transcription! 

**Model System for Studying Long-Term Potentiation in Rodents:**
- Slice from rodent hippocampus 
- Three-neuron pathway through hippocampus
	- Information arrives in dentate gyrus
	- Connects to granule cell
	- Granule cell sends information to pyramidal cell in the CA3 region
	- Schaffer collateral axons sends information on to cells in CA1
- Tetanus - rapid-fire stimulation of neurons
	- Tetanic stimulation induces LTP 
	- When a neuron is tetanized, EPSP becomes LTP

**Key concept: LTP is the mechanism by which information is stored. In other words LTP is the way we learn**

**LTP Notes:**
- Specificity: in order for a pathway to be potentiated, it must be active
- Associativity: if a weak pathway is activated at the same time as a strong pathway, then the weak pathway can be strengthened 
- Potentiation is possible in many types of grey matter

**Glutamate Receptors & LTP:**
- When a cell with AMPA & NMDA receptors has not been activated, there is only a small depolarization. Glutamate is still released, still binds to AMPA & NMDA, but Mg in NMDA blocks the transmission 
- When a cell with AMPA & NMDA receptors is activated, glutamate is released, causing a more significant depolarization, enough to expel the Mg from NMDA. Huge rush in Ca
- LTP wakes silent synapses (lacking AMPA receptors) and AMPA receptors are inserted into the cell 

**LTP & Gene Expression:**
- Early phases of LTP: Ca-dependent increase and growth of new synaptic spines
	- New genes are activated

**LTD Notes:**
- LTD: Extensive period of low-frequency stimulation causes a reduction in EPSP
- Stimulation causes release of glutamate, which binds to receptors. Mg is expelled, but not as much Ca is released. Not as much energy is generated and the post-synaptic process is weakened

**Key concept: Both LTD and LTP require activity and activation of glutamate receptors. The key difference is the level of post-synaptic Ca**

**Plasticity in Cerebellar Cortex:**
- LTD is more prevalent 
- Purkinje cell (very beautiful allegedly) has complex geometry that allows it to receive as many as 1000 inputs 
- Inferior olivary nucleus: Olive in medulla is a product of this nucleus. The climbing fibers of the nucleus wrap around dendrites of Purkinje cells. This creates the strongest synapse in the brain
- Climbing fibers (CF) and parallel fibers (PF) cause LTD in cerebellum. EPSP is depressed by 50% 
- Synapses in Purkinje cells are glutamatergic, but only have AMPA and mGLuR (metabotropic receptors for glutamate) - still causes flood of Ca. Climbing fiber also causes depolarization, intensifying the overall depolarization and flood of Ca. AMPA receptors are internalized into vesicular compartments 
- In the cerebellar cortex, high levels of Ca lead to LTD, whereas in the cerebral cortex, the high levels of Ca lead to LTP

**STDP Notes:**
- Provides a framework for understanding plasticity one post-synaptic spike at a time
- Real synapses rarely experience intense bursts of high-frequency stimulation, as was used in the rodent experiments
- Pre-synaptic activity before post-synaptic activity - leads to LTP
- Post-synaptic activity before pre-synaptic activity - leads to LTD
- "20 milliseconds... it's kind of a magical interval"

**Donald Hebb's Postulate:**
- *Coordinated* activity of a pre-synaptic terminal and a post-synaptic cell would lead to the strengthening of the synaptic connection between them 
- *Uncoordinated* activity would weaken the synaptic connection
- "Neurons that fire together, wire together" - Larry Katz :'(
- STDP explains how synaptic change can occur one spike at a time under psychological conditions in neural networks
- Post before pre: incoordination (LTD); pre before post: coordination (LTP)

**Other Notes:**
- Change at synaptic level can produce change in entire neural circuits
- Net effect of firing or misfiring can change the structure and function of network properties
