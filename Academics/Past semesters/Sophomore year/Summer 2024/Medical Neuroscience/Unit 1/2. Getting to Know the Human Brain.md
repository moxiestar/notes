**Types of cells in CNS**
- Neurons: Primary processors of neural signals
	- Fundamental unit of function in CNS
	- Same machinery as in other somatic cells (nucleus, ER, mitochondria, etc)
	- But distinct from other somatic cells:
		- Rich diversity in morphology
		- Bioelectrical properties (somewhat similar to muscle cells)
		- Specializations for intercellular communication (secretion of neurotransmitters)
- Neuroglia: Supporters of electrical and chemical functions of neurons
- Vascular endothelium: Provides the supply of blood to the brain

![[neuron-diagram.jpg]]
- INPUT ZONE (R): 
	- Cell body possesses more cellular organs than other somatic cells
	- Dendrites extend surface area that allows the cell to receive input from other neurons
	- "Spines" (small modules) protrude from some dendrites; others are smooth (these cells have an inhibitory effect)
		- Pyramidal neurons: apical dendrites grow from the apex of the cell - distinguished properties
		- Multipolar neurons: basil dendrites emanate in all directions
- CONDUCTING ZONE (M):
	- Axons can be anywhere from several millimeters to multiple feet long
	- Axon can generate electrical signal: action potential
- OUTPUT ZONE (L):
	- Synapses allow neurons to transfer signals, electrically or chemically
		- Synaptic nodules or "boutons" grow out from axons

**Neuron placement:** 
- In the brain, neurons are often arranged in parallel, as if lying side by side
- Grey matter: The cell bodies and synapses of neurons in parallel 
- White matter: The axons and vascular endotheliums of neurons in parallel

**Notes on types of neurons:**
- Excitatory vs inhibitory
- Projection cells: Neurons that grow axons going away from cell body
	- Cortical pyramidal cells: longer dendrites and axons
- Cortical stellate cell: grows shorter axon and sends signals only to nearby neurons
- Sensory neurons convey sensory information 
	- Afferent neurons send information towards the CNS
	- Efferent neurons send information away from the CNS

**Neuroglia:**
- Support the metabolic and signaling functions of neurons
- Participate in the formation of neural circuits and synaptic plasticity 
- Make axonal insulation, or myelin! 
- Contribute to formation of blood-brain barrier
- Participate in inflammatory response in injured neural tissue, including phagocytosis of cellular debris
- Contribute to formation of scar tissue in damaged brain or spinal areas

 **Types of Neuroglia:**
 - Astrocytes
	 - Primarily found in grey matter (closely associated with cell bodies and dendrites)
	 - Help to maintain the balance of brain fluids
	 - Process neurotransmitters from synaptic clefts
	 - Assist in the formation of new synapses and circuits
	 - Contribute to blood-brain barrier and brain-ependymal barrier
	 - Contribute to formation of neural scars following injury
 - Oligodendrocytes
	 - Primarily found in white matter
	 - Produce/form myelin (cf Schwann cells found in somatic nerves)
		 - Oligodendrocytes come together at nodes of Ranvier, gaps between myelin segments - concentration of ion channels (regenerates electrical signal)
		 - Signal jumps from one node to another - saltatory conduction
		 - Insulating axons by generating layers of membrane (myelin) that wrap around axon segments
		 - Decreases ionic "leakage" of membrane
		 - Myelin aids in the propagation of neural signals along axons
	 - Produce antigens that influence growth of axons in growing and recovering brain
	 - Produce antigens subject to immunological attack in CNS diseases (ie, MS - myelin breaks down)
 - Microglia
	 - Mononuclear phagocyte residing in CNS
	 - Derived from hematopoietic precursor cells ? that migrate to brain 
	 - Exists in amoeboid and ramified forms 
		 - Ramified cells are dormant; in event of injury they convert to amoeboid cells to engage in phagocytic activity 
	 - Secrete signaling molecules (cytokines) that modulate inflammatory responses
 - Glial stem cells
	 - Can mature into a variety of cells
	 - Has major features of stem cells
		 - Proliferation
		 - Self-renewal
		 - Potency to make all their cells of a given tissue
	 - Some resemble astrocytes
		 - Subset of astrocytes located near ventricles, near blood vessels
	 - Others resemble oligodendrocytes
	 - Scattered throughout white matter

**Blood-Brain Barrier**
- Specialized permeability barrier between the capillary endothelium and the extracellular space in neural tissue
- Barrier prevents water-soluble molecules, pathological microbes, and toxicological compounds from diffusing into the CNS
- Certain drugs must be engineered to circumvent the barrier
- Where there are special populations of neurons secreting proteins that have an hormonal effect, the barrier becomes more porous to allow the proteins to travel into the brain
	- This happens in the hypothalamus and the pineal gland
- Wherever there is a hormone secretary function, there is loosening of the barrier
- Some diseases affect the integrity of the barrier
