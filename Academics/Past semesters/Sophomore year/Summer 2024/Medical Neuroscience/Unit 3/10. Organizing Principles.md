**Embryological Origins of the Thalamus:**
- Diencephalon forms from central nerve ? in embryo 
- Thalamus is in the posterior and dorsal aspect of diencephalon
- Hypothalamus and thalamus form walls of third ventricle

**Location of Thalamus:**
- Near centre of forebrain
- Egg-shaped structure near midline 

**Organization of Thalamus:**
- Many discrete collections of cells
- 20 or 30 individual nuclei - distinct pathways providing info to thalamus 
- Divided by white matter layer, running through middle (Y shape)
	- Anterior nuclear group (connections with pre-frontal cortex in cingulate gyrus)
	- Midline thalamic nuclei & medial nuclear group (contains mediodorsal nucleus)
	- Lateral nuclear group (connect to parietal cortex, visual regions of occipital, auditory structures in temporal, motor structures in frontal lobe)
	- Intralaminar nuclei (modulatory in function)

**Functions of Nuclei in Thalamus:**
- Receiving incoming sensory signals - relays information to respective divisions of cerebral cortex
- Nuclei receive inputs from somatic sensory pathways rising from spinal cord & brain stem > send it to somatic sensory divisions of cortex (postcentral gyrus)
- First-order thalamic nuclei receive primary inputs from spinal cord, relay them through white matter
- Higher-order thalamic nuclei receive primary inputs from descending pathways in cerebral cortex (thalamo-cortical loops)
- Non-specific thalamic nuclei project to upper layers of cerebral cortex - modulatory pathways

**Embryological Origins of Cerebral Cortex:**
- Third trimester: cerebral hemisphere appears, with central sulcus, lateral fissure, etc
- Cerebral cortex (cortex - "tree bark" in Latin) - outermost layer of grey matter
- "Grand achievement in the evolution of the mammalian nervous system"
- More neurons present in the infantile brain than the adult brain - "no one quite understands why"
- Cells exit complex cell cycle and become neuroblasts: these develop into neurons and glia and migrate over vast territory - how?
- Neuroblasts must travel from interior (ventricular zone) to exterior (cortical plane) of cortex, and migrate along scaffold provided by radial glial cells 

**Histology:**
- Cortical circuit 1: thin layer of myelinated white matter at the edge of cerebral cortex
- Cortical circuit 2: distinct but sparse population of neurons
- Cortical circuit 3: contains many larger pyramidal neurons
- Cortical circuit 4: thalamic recipient zone, target of first order principle thalamic nuclei that send signals into cortical network (contains stellate cells!!!!!)
- Cortical circuit 5: largest pyramidal neurons in the cerebral cortex, rich synaptic connectivity between neurons
- Cortical circuit 6: concentration of dense small cells 
- Below layer 6 - white matter underneath cerebral cortex

**Histology & Function of Thalamus:**
- From circuit 4, cells send signals up to upper circuits, 2 and 3. From there information can travel broadly
- Cells in circuit 6 relay signals back to thalamus

**Cortical Columns:**
- Canonical Microcircuit of the cerebral cortex
- ACC: amplification (thalamus provides major modulation for CNS, with circuit 4 amplifying signals), computation (upper circuits 1-3 send signals), communication (those signals travel throughout the brain)

**Cellular Architecture (Cortical Cytoarchitecture):**
- Betz cells (largest neurons in cerebral cortex)
- Paleocortex (pyriform cortex) & archicortex (hippocampus) - reflect evolutionary history of cerebral mantle in mammalian brain

**Brodmann's Areas:**
- Each area has a different function
- His development ushered in the modern era of neuroscience
- Cerebral cortex is more of a large network than a collection of separate areas
- Yet his designation is still helpful to consider

**Neuronal pathways:**
- Peripheral axon: axon of a neuron that grows out to reach the skin 
- First order neuron (with peripheral axon) fires to second order, which fires to third order, which fires to larger network
- This pathway allows neural signals to respond to an external stimulation and travel to the cerebral cortex
- Pathway is integrated to network that provides output (input is connected to output)
- Ascending pathways: sensory function. Descending: motor function
	- Contralateral representation (limbs are represented in opposite side of brain)
	- Understanding where sensory and motor pathways cross midline (decussation) is critical for knowing what level of CNS is impaired

**Sensory Transduction:**
- Process that allows for energy present in environment to be converted into electrical signals within nerve cells
- Sensory: Physical contact -> electrical signals
- Pacinian corpuscle: contains nerve ending with Na ion-selective channels sensitive to stretching forces 
	- When physical force is applied to tissue, ion channels open (how cool is that!!!)
	- Receptor potential - change in membrane potential, in a receptor ending of a primary sensory axon. Weak stimulus leads to small depolarization, stronger stimulus leads to full depolarization

**Information Coding: Labelled Lines:**
- Response of axon in sensory nerve is shaped by type of receptor that's present at end of axon. Receptor is specialized to encode a certain kind of information
	- Pacinian corpuscle: sensitive to vibration
	- Meissner corpuscle: sensitive to light touch
- Parallel processing: axons transmitting information in parallel between each other and to the CNS
- Neurons can adapt to different levels of stimuli, but they adapt at varying speeds

**Thresholds:**
- Mechonsensors (sensitive to sensory stimulation) have a low threshold for activation
- Pain fibers (free nerve endings in tissues) have higher threshold for activation

**Receptive Fields:**
- Area for which, when stimulus is present, the activity can be modulated in a specific nerve cell
- Center-surround organization
	- Center of receptive field gives rise to robust modulation of activity when stimulated. Surrounded by annular region (surround) 
	- If there is an increase in firing in the center, there is a decrease in firing in the annular

**Dermis Receptors:**
- Spacial disparity between different receptors (ie, Pacinian has a wider field, Meissner has a smaller field)
- Principles of convergence - degree of convergence determines size of receptive field for neurons in cerebral cortex
	- Higher degree of convergence (many smaller neurons converging onto cortical neuron) -> larger field of receptiveness
	- Lower degree of convergence (fewer smaller neurons converging) -> smaller field of receptiveness 

**Receptiveness on Skin:**
- In order to feel two separate sensory signals, you must activate two separate receptive fields
