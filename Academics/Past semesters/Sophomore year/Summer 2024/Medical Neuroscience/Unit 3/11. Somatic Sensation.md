**Organization of Somatic Sensory Systems:**
- Two parallel subsystems: mechanosensation and pain & temperature
- Basic functions of mechanosensory
	- Identifying shape and texture of objects
	- Monitors forces acting on body
	- Build up an image of our body
- Pain is adaptive, informing us about potential threat. Pain pathways are very important for modulating our behavior
- Two pairs of pathways that are spatially divided in CNS that serve sensory information: one for body, and one for the face

**Peripheral Mechanosensory Elements:**
- First order somatic cells: dorsal root ganglion cells (these split between body and spinal cord), enters dorsal root column when returning to CNS
- Pain and temperature cell in dorsal root enters dorsal horn
- Mechanosensory fiber grows along course of spinal cord; pain & temperature fiber makes synaptic connection upon entrance into the spinal cord (dorsal horn)
- Mechanosensory fibers are supplied by large & well-myelinated axons (Ia, II)
	- Pacinian and Meissner cells (Ab) are also well-myelinated but less fast
- Pain & temperature fibers (free nerve endings) are formed at ends of small, often unmyelinated axons (Ad, C)

**Sensory Transduction:**
- Physical deformation of receptors leads to ion channels opening and influx of Na+ ions -> depolarization
	- Once receptor potential hits threshold, action potential is fired
	- Ion channels are not activated by voltage, but by physical forces
	- Size of influx correlates to size of action potential 

**Variations in Mechanosensory Fibers:**
- Many specializations even within surface of skin
	- Light touch: Meissner corpuscle & Merkel cell neurite complex
		- Meissner corpuscle is specialization of Schwann cells -> layered structure that constitutes a corpuscle 
		- Merkel cell NC: specialization of nerve endings of cells that sit between dermal things. When action potential is generated, neurotransmitters are released from MCNC, activating nerve terminal of first order axons
	- Ruffini corpuscle - physiology not well understood - possibly sensitive to stretch but who's to say
	- Pacinian corpuscle: nerve ending encapsulated under many layers of Schwann cell lamellae. Depression leads to quick activation of the sensory ending

**Receptors in Body:**
- Muscle spindle: formed by specialized muscle fibers and certain types of receptor endings
	- Intrafusal muscle fibers found within spindle 
	- Collection of nuclei bundled near center 
	- Innervated by Ia and II afferent axons (II terminates in flower spray ending)
	- Spindle sends signals pertaining to stretch
- Golgi tendon organs: capsular region between tendon and muscle fiber made out of woven collagen fibers
	- Innervated by Ib afferent axon
	- Matrix puts pressure on axon terminals, opening ion channels and leaning to generation of receptor potentials 
	- Sensitive to muscle force
- Also receptors associated with joints

**Centers of Mechanosensory Processing in Forebrain:**
- Somatic sensory division of thalamus: 
	- Ventral posterior complex of thalamus connects with postcentral gyrus (primary sensory somatic cortex)
		- Two nuclei, lateral (processes signals from body) and medial (processes signals from face)
		- Lateral supplies upper half of sensory cortex, medial provides lower half

**Somtatotopy:**

- Sensory cortex divided into areas 1 (complex stimuli activating multiple areas), 2 (shapes of objects), 3a (proprioception), and 3b (cutaneous receptors) - all have different functions

**Sensory Pathways:**
- Body
	- Dorsal-column medial lemniscal system: mechanical stimuli 
	- Anterolateral system: pain and temperature
- Face
	- Principal sensory nucleus of the trigeminal complex: mechanical stimuli
	- Spinal nucleus of the trigeminal complex: pain and temperature

**DCMLS:** 
- Things to focus on: cell bodies, axons, and synapses 
- First order neurons are dorsal root ganglion cells found in the lumbar spinal cord, and enter through dorsal column. They ascend until they reach the dorsal column nuclei in the medulla
	- Two tracts travel up the cuneate and gracile tracts, terminating in the cuneate and gracile nuclei respectively 
- The axons then cross the midline, establishing location of decussation, then ascends the rest of the way through the medulla, finally reaching the thalamus (VPL)
	- When crossing the midline, the axons are internal arcuate fibers. After crossing, they form the medial lemniscus 
- From there the signal travels to the sensory cortex
	- The fiber that projects into paracentral lobule is a fiber that was activated by gracile tract, communicating sensory signals from lower limbs
	- The fiber in cuneate tract, however, does something else...?

**Pathway for the Face:**
- Trigeminal lemniscus (principal trigeminal nucleus) provides somatic sensation for the face 
	- First order neuron is ganglion cell in trigeminal nerve, enters brain stem through trigeminal nerve
	- Second order neuron (trigeminal lemniscus) in pons, in the chief sensory nucleus. It grows a neuron that crosses midline and enters into thalamus (VPM)
	- Third order neuron travels from VPM 

**Cerebellar Function:**
- Helps to facilitate agile movements of body and mind
- Receives input from cortex about what we are trying to do, then receives feedback from sensory systems about what we are actually doing. If there's a problem, the cerebellum generates an error signal that is sent back into the motor system 
- Receives information from spinal cord 

**Spinocerebellar Pathways:**
- Receptor is a proprioceptor - stimulated by muscular movements
- For lower body, first order neuron is dorsal root ganglion cell, entering dorsal column and ascending to thoracic/lumbar spinal cord, connects to Clarke's nucleus 
- Second order neuron runs through dorsal lateral white matter of spinal cord (dorsal spinocerebellar tract) which then runs to the cerebellum via inferior cerebellar peduncle
- For upper body, the neuron enters the dorsal column at the cervical level, then connects to the external cuneate nucleus, which then travels up to the cerebellum via inferior cerebellar peduncle
	- Clarke's nucleus: lower body
	- External cuneate nucleus: upper body
- We do have some awareness of these signals
	- When the nuclei travel up to the cerebellum, they give off collaterals, which synapse with neurons in dorsal column nuclei, which grow axons that contribute to the medial lemniscal pathway. These then enter our pathways for self-awareness

**Key concept: nociceptive processing of pain leads to emotional aspect of pain, which gives rise to the concept of suffering**

**Complex Phenomenology of Pain:**
- Multidimensional neurophysiology:
	- Somatic sensory (nociceptive) perception
	- Affective (emotional)
	- Secondary affect (impact of chronic pain on mental health)
	- Psychosocial context 
- Flux in clinical strategies for pain mitigation 
	- Change in assessments of pain 
	- (Positive) change in strategies for pain management 
	- Change in role for healthcare professionals in pain management

**Neurophysiology of Pain Perception:**
- First order neuron: dorsal root ganglion cell grows an axon that ends in a free nerve ending (on other end, the neuron enters dorsal column, grows up to caudal medulla, then makes synaptic connection along mechanosensory pathway)
- Receptor ending: free nerve endings, distributed broadly throughout tissues 
- Pain and temperature fibers are among the smallest fibers out of CNS
	- Ad fibers: lightly myelinated axons that end in fibers sensitive to mechanical stimulation and dangerous thermal stimulation 
	- C fibers: often unmyelinated axons that are polymodal. They respond to various sensations that signal some kind of danger for the body

**Sensory Transduction in Nociceptors:**
- Free nerve endings have special ion channels from the transient receptor potential family (TRIP channels)
	- Sensitive to heat, cold, protons indicating acidity, spice
	- Capsaicin (compound in chili peppers) modulates activity of TRIP channel, causing burning sensation 
	- Channels allow for passage of Na, K, and Ca, generating an action potential if threshold is reached
- Substantia gelatinosa - area in laminae II of the dorsal horn, where there is a rich assortment of neurons receiving input from first order pain neurons
	- C fibers terminate earlier, often in substantia gelatinosa 
	- Ad fibers terminate later, in laminae 5 & 6

**Categories of Pain Sensation:**
- First pain: sharp shooting pain that typically subsides in a matter of seconds, signals sent by Ad fibers
	- These signals travel directly to the thalamus, and from there to the sensory cortex
- Second pain: throbbing pain that lasts longer, signals sent by C fibers, more affective component 
	- These signals travel through dorsal horn, synapsing in superficial areas; from there projection neurons grow axons through spinal cord into brain stem, terminating among variety of structures in brain stem. Some signals may reach the thalamus but will fire in the medial area, connecting to areas related to cognition and emotion

**Notes on Anterolateral System:**
- First pain pathway (Ad) goes via the spinothalamic tract: tract that travels from dorsal horn to thalamus
- Second pain pathway (C) goes almost everywhere in brainstem, forebrain, and thalamus
	- Reticular formation: broadly distributed set of cells in the core of the brainstem. Multiple small nuclei are present, which control function of arousal and attention. Controls cognitive response to pain 
	- Periaqueductal gray: structure in midbrain that surrounds cerebral aqueduct, critical for feedback modulation of pain transmission in dorsal horn
	- Midline thalamic nuclei: informs emotional understanding of pain 
		- The more we feel pain, the greater the activity in the cingulate gyrus

**Nociception Processing:**
- Hyperalgesia: enhanced sensitivity to mechanosensory stimulation following onset of injury
- Variety of mediators increase the currents that flow through the TRIP channels, increasing sensitivity of free nerve endings 
	- Increase in sensitivity can stimulate other polymodal neurons, which can lead to allodynia, where a normally innocuous stimulus is perceived as painful
	- Glutamate synapses become more effective when affected by wind-up phenomenon 
		- Wind-up: when strengthened, transcription independent synapses leads to LTP and repeated activation of nociceptors, causing sustained depolarization of second-order neurons
- Cultural and emotional differences in reaction to pain

**Descending Pathways that Modulate Pain:**
- Periaqueductal gray - allows for feedback modulation of pain, receiving input from amygdala and hypothalamus 
	- Travels to medullary reticular formation, and from there the inputs terminate in the spinal cord
	- Locus coeruleus releases norepinephrine into dorsal horn
	- Raphe nuclei release serotonin 
	- Inter-neurons release enkephalin, inhibiting the activity of the nociceptive afferent
	- When these neurons "turn off" the output from the dorsal horn, they activate an analgesic effect

**Gate Theory of Pain:**
- Somewhere in CNS there is competition between signals driven from mechanoreceptor afferents and those driven from nociceptive afferents
- This competition allows from mechanoreceptors to "close the gate" on transmission of pain signals
	- Ab collateral from mechanoreceptors travels to substantia gelatinosa and synapses on dorsal horn projection neuron 
	- Firing of this Ab collateral can suppress the synapsing of the C fiber nociceptor, thereby "closing the gate" on pain 
	- For example, squeezing your thumb after you've hit it is a way of activating the mechanoreceptors in the spinal cord and suppressing the C fiber firing
	- Surgical intervention to suppress some forms of pain - stimulation of dorsal columns with micro-electrode 

**Chronic Pain:**
- Nociceptive chronic pain: results from ongoing simulation of nociceptors 
	- Involving both first and second pain 
- Chronic pain syndrome: results from no known nociceptive etiology (lower back pain like me!)
	- Largely a mystery for neurologists
	- Etiology is poorly understood, but it is not psychogenic 
	- Recent proposals include dysregulation of descending modulatory systems

**Neuropathic Pain:**
- Pain that arises from abnormal activity in nociceptive pathway, but unrelated to nociceptive stimuli 
	- Peripheral cause: nerve compression - physical opening of ion channels in area of compression, development of ectopic focus, action potentials are fired and CNS interprets these signals as pain
		- Often accompanied by abnormal somatic sensation 
	- Central cause: sustained sensitization of neurons in nociceptive pathway caused by wind-up
	- Central cause: abnormal activity in sympathetic outflow to viscera
	- Central cause: plasticity in higher centers (maladaptive for human behavior, ie, phantom limbs)

**First and Second Pain Pathways:**
- First pain (Ad): neurons grow from dorsal horn to thalamus (VPL) - spinothalamic tract. Third order neuron relays information to sensory cortex
- Second pain (C): neurons travel to the thalamus (VPM), but also much more broadly throughout brain stem (amygdala, hypothalamus, periaqueductal grey, superior colliculus, reticular formation)
	- From thalamus, signals travel to insular cortex, contributing to emotional response to pain
	- Second pain has a huge impact on mental health

**Spinothalamic Tract:**
- First order neuron: dorsal root ganglion cell, terminates in dorsal horn (may also bifurcate and travel up and down the spinal cord)
- Second order neuron: crosses midline, enters anterior and lateral white matter of spinal cord, then run up through the brain stem and synapse in thalamus (VPL)
- Third order neuron: sends information up into the sensory cortex, where pain is somatotopically localized

**Trigeminothalamic Tract:**
- Pain and temperature information from face is received by the spinal trigeminal tract of afferent axons, which then travels to the spinal trigeminal nucleus 
- Third order neurons then travel to thalamus (VPM) and information is then relayed to sensory cortex

**Dissociated Sensory Loss:**
- Unilateral spinal cord damage - lesion in lower thoracic area 
	- Damages the ipsilateral mechanoreceptors and also the contralateral axons of the anterolateral systems 
	- Lower half of same side of body: reduced sensation, vibration, and proprioception 
	- Lower half of opposite side of body: reduced sensation for pain and temperature
	- Complete sensory loss around area of lesion

**Dorsal Column Visceral Pain Pathway:**
- Visceral pain is derived from Ad and C fibers
- First order neurons: dorsal root ganglion cells, which synapse with second order neurons in between dorsal and ventral horns
- Second order neurons: ascend to medulla, where they connect with dorsal nuclei, then terminate in thalamus, which sends information to insular cortex
- This pain can be alleviated neurosurgically via a lesion in the dorsal column, severing the second order nociceptive fibers

**Referred Pain:**
- Communication between pain fibers in dorsal column or thalamus
- Pain from one area can be felt in multiple parts of the body (ie, heart attack pain)
