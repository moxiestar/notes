**Optic Anatomy:**
![[Screenshot 2024-07-30 at 10.44.29 AM.png]]
- Ganglion cell pathways:
	- Nasal retina cells send their axons through the optic nerve and cross the midline, then project to the diencephalon and midbrain on the opposite side of the brain
	- Retinal ganglion cells from the temporal retina send their axons through the optic nerve, but they remain on the same side of the brain 
	- When we receive visual signals, we get information about the same and the opposite sides of the visual field
- Lateral geniculate nucleus:
	- Main target of retinal ganglion cells with respect to vision and image formation 
	- Gives rise to optic radiation which supply the upper and lower banks of the calcarine sulcus (primary visual cortex)
- Calcarine sulcus (visual cortex):
	- Stria of Gennari (Brodmann's Area 17, striate cortex) - great example of cortical microcircuits 
	- David Hubel and Torsten Wiesel (Nobel 1981) - studied development and function of striate cortex
	- Retinal cells respond well to small circles of light, while visual cortical cells respond best to larger bars of light 
	- Orientational and directional selectivity - response to bars of light in specific orientations or directions

**Hubel and Wiesel Experiments:**
- Anesthetized cat was presented with different visual stimuli in various orientations
- Electrode is vertical in the circuit: series of neurons at different depths through cortical circuits - same orientation preference and receptive fields
- Electrode is more diagonal: orientation preferences are spaced out and two different receptive fields are encountered 

**Optical Imaging:**
- Shows coupling between blood supply and level of activity 
- Exposing surface of brain to light, then record the changes in absorption of light. Levels of oxygen and blood flow also change
- We can observe the levels of cortical activity change 
- Changing the orientation and direction shows the interdigitation of columns in the cortical circuits (basically, different neurons in the circuits respond to different orientations and directions)
- Activity is arranged around "pinwheel centers" in the circuits
- Animation at 8:50 shows this - absolutely amazing 
- Distribution of columns shown below with color and greyscaling 
- With calcium of some kind, you can detect the preferences of neighboring neurons
- Direction selectivity: a domain might represent a specific kind of direction, but it will be subdivided by motion

**Ocular Dominance:**
- Lateral geniculate nucleus is layered, receiving input from each retina, but the structure is monocular at a cellular level
- The cells project to the visual cortex, also monocularly, in columns called ocular dominance columns
- In the upper layers (2 and 3) of the visual cortex, the information is finally mixed, making stereopsis possible

**Stereopsis:** 
- Vision compiled by two eyes seeing slightly different views of each object 
- Diagram below - eyes are fixed on point b 
- Point a is seen by non-corresponding regions of the retinas - the regions don't project to the same point in the thalamus 
- Disparity between point a and point b gives us the ability to appreciate the depth of the two point. Same goes for point c 
- Objects nearer than the focal point give rise to near disparities, while objects farther than the focal point give rise to far disparities

**Parallel Pathways in Retina:**
- P, M, and K ganglion cells - project to different areas of the lateral geniculate nucleus
	- P cells are smaller - project to layers 3-6 (parvo-cellular layers)
		- Synapse to 4Cb in visual cortex
		- Specializes in form and color
	- M cells are the largest - project to layers 1 & 2 (magno-cellular layers)
		- Synapse to 4Ca in visual cortex
		- Specializes in broad patterns of motion
	- K cells are in between the two - project to zones in between the layers (konio-cellular layers)
		- Synapse to 3 in visual cortex
		- Specializes in what? No one knows! :-)
- Beyond the visual cortex, there are rings of higher-order visual areas
	- In occipital lobe - MT (middle temporal visual area) has enriched motion cells (notion of motion)
		- From there it projects up into the parietal lobe (dorsal parietal stream) and helps us to map the world around us
	- Ventral processing stream - helps us understanding what we are looking at
	- Where is dorsal, what is ventral
	- Injury in these areas leads to a worsened visual understanding of the world 
		- Lesion in fusiform gyrus leads to a deficit in the ability to recognize faces (prosopagnosia)
		- People with prosopagnosia can still see, but they lack the ability to correct recognize and process visual data relating to others' faces

**Retinal Anatomy:**
- The retina is diencephalic, evolving from the optic vesicle
- The adult retina is connected to the brain via the old stalk of the optic vesicle 
- Lateral geniculate nucleus relays visual signals to visual cortex, responsible for most aspects of vision 
- Suprachiasmatic nucleus in hypothalamus receives direct projections from the retina - also the generator of circadian rhythms, which arose from visual inputs
- Pretectum (reflex control of pupil and lens) and superior colliculus (orientates our eyes towards visual stimuli) - inputs from retinal cells to midbrain

**Visuotopy:**
- Visual world projects on retina as a mirror, upside down image
- Vertical and horizontal meridians divide the visual field 
- Diagram illustrates mechanism
- Far periphery of vision is seen only by the respective eye 
- The right and left visual fields overlap in the middle
- Fovea

**Visuotopy:**
- Different areas of the retina need to send their information together to produce a coherent image 
	- Left side of vision: left nasal retina, right temporal retina
	- Right side of vision: right nasal retina, left temporal retina
- The optic chiasm allows for the crossing of retinal projections
	- Right side of the optic chiasm represents the left field of vision, and vice versa

**Conveying Images to Visual Cortex:**
- Lateral geniculate nucleus has to grow axons (optic radiation) around the lateral ventricle to the visual cortex. The axons first run laterally, then posterior in white matter towards the occipital lobe 
- Lots of axons go everywhere to reach different areas of the visual cortex. Confusing (5 minute mark if this is on the quiz)
- Meyer's loop: temporal portion of optic radiation 
- Calcarine sulcus is formed by the cuneus gyrus and lingual gyrus 
	- Cuneus gyrus interprets lower part of visual field (supplied by upper part of retina)
	- Lingual gyrus interprets upper part of visual field (supplied by lower part of retina)
	- Rule of thumb: the field of the fovea is only the size of your thumb at arm's length

**Injury in Visual Field - Terms:**
- Anopsia: large area of blindness in an eye or visual field
- Hemianopsia: blindness in one visual hemifield 
- Quadrantanopsia: blindness in one quadrant of a visual hemifield 
- Homonymous: same visual hemifield seen by each eye
- Heteronymous: opposite visual hemifield seen by each eye
- Scotoma: small area of blindness in a visual field

**Possible Visual Field Deficits:**
- a) Injury to the right optic nerve causes total right eye blindness 
- b) Lesion in the middle of the optic chiasm causes peripheral blindness (bi-temporal heteronymous hemianopsia)
- c) Lesion in right optic tract causes blindness in left side in each eye's field of vision (left homonymous hemianopsia)
- d) Lesion in right side of Meyer's loop causes blindness in contralateral superior quadrant in left side in each eye's field of vision (left superior homonymous quadrantanopsia)
- e) Damage to right occipital cortex causes blindness in left side in each eye's field of vision (left homonymous hemianopsia), but there's a small semicircular region of vision that's retained (macular sparing)

**Pupillary Light Reflex:**
- Retinal ganglion cells connect to superior colliculus 
- Pretectum receives information from both retinas
	- Nuclei in pretectum control the constriction of the pupils 
	- Sensory signals going into one side of the circuitry are derived from both eyes
	- Pretectal nucleus gives rise to axons that target the Edinger-Westphal nucleus on both sides of the midline 
		- From EWN, axons grow out from CN III which synapses on ciliary ganglion, which travels down the optic nerve to supply ganglionic parasympathetic innervation of the constrictor muscle of the iris
		- From EWN the signals remain ipsilateral 
	- Pupillary constriction is controlled by the sympathetic system
