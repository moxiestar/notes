**Anatomy of the Eye:**
- Cornea is responsible for most of the refraction of light that happens as light passes into the eye
- Optical lens changes shape through accommodation 
- Anterior chamber: filled with aqueous humor - travels as vitreous humor from the back of the eye and becomes aqueous
	- If fluid is not properly drained, the pressure will damage the peripheral retina, resulting in loss of peripheral vision 
- Ciliary muscle supports shape of cornea
 ![[Screenshot 2024-07-22 at 4.20.13 PM.png]]

**Accommodation: Focusing an Image:**
- Requires adjustment of shape of lens in anterior eye
- A flatter lens is more appropriate for farther vision, while a fatter lens is better for near vision 
- Ciliary muscle surrounds lens, attached by zonule fibers - works like a camera lens 
- Contraction of ciliary muscle leads to rounding of lens and increase of refractive power
- Elasticity of lens gives it capacity to establish rounder shape
- Pupil is the space created by the iris - pupil is simply a window into the darker interior of the eye
- Iris controls light input to retina 

**Retina:**
- Embryological origins in diencephalon - the retina is part of the brain 
	- Optic pouch is formed in diencephalon, then grows out to meet other tissues and form the eye
- Pigmented epithelium - dark tissue at the back of the eye, small layer at the back of the retina
- The rest of the retina is composed of the neural retina
- In order for light to activate the cells that perform phototransduction, the light has to pass through the whole neural retina 
- Phototransduction happens adjacent to epithelium 

**Pigmented Epithelium:**
- Outer tips of photoreceptors "make intimate contact" with epithelium
- Epithelium keeps photoreceptors working properly and able to transduce photon energy into neural signals. Epithelium recycles discs of membranes that contain photopigments - discs are completely engulfed
- With trauma, the photoreceptors can become detached from epithelium - recycling is no longer possible

**Retinal Cells:**
- Outer segment - where discs that contain photo pigment are found
- Outer nuclear layer - where cell bodies of photoreceptors are found
- Outer plexiform layer - layer of synapses where photoreceptors interact with horizontal and bipolar cells
	- Bipolar cells make contact with both photoreceptors and lower cells (amacrine and ganglion)
	- Horizontal cells receives and gives input from and to photoreceptors
- Retinal ganglion cell - constitutes layer several cells thick across inner surface of retina
- Amacrine cell - gives rise to horizontal connections that stretch across inner plexiform layer
- Structure: alternating nuclear and plexiform layers
- Entire thickness of human retina is not more than a millimeter - no need to generate action potentials 
- Photoreceptors and bipolar cells generated graded potentials, while ganglion cells generate action potentials 
- Mueller cell (not pictured) - aids metabolism and signaling activity

**Photoreceptors:**
- Rods - named because of rectangular shape
	- When in the dark, the rod is depolarized, supported by "dark current"
	- Ion channel in plasma membrane in outer segment of rod, permeable to Ca and Na, gated by cGMP
	- When large amount of cGMP is present, Ca and Na diffuse into the cell
	- Other ion channels allow for efflux of K
- Cones - named because of triangular shape
- Process of phototransduction requires interaction of a photon of light with a photopigment 
	- Photopigment: complex molecule consisting of opsin protein and 11-cis retinal 
	- Opsin is member of 7 transmembrane protein family (allegedly "familiar to me")
	- Opsin fine-tunes the response of the 11CR while the 11CR interacts with photons
- Four kinds of pigments - rods have rod opsin, while cones can have short, medium, or long opsin
![[Screenshot 2024-07-22 at 7.22.43 PM.png]]

**Response to Light:**
- Short flash of light produces graded response, which produces hyperpolarization
- Series of molecular events that lead to reduction in cGMP
- When cGMP is reduced, the ion channels close, preventing Na and Ca from entering cell
- K channels remain open, allowing efflux of K
- The cell hyperpolarizes to -60mV

**Molecular Processes of Light Hyperpolarization:**
- When light strikes 11CR, carbon atoms 11 and 12 rotate, converting the 11CR into a trans configuration ?
- "You may remember a bit of organic chemistry there"
- Transformation leads to activation of the metabotropic element of opsin
- Transformation also leads to activation of g proteins including transducin 
- Something fucks up the cGMP and the channel closes
- All this leads to a lag in the visual system, making it one of our lowest sensory systems

**Different Features of Rods and Cones:**
- Rods:
	- Extremely sensitive to light
	- Low spatial resolution
	- Specialized for low light levels
	- Scotopic vision
- Cones:
	- Relatively insensitive to light
	- High spatial resolution
	- Specialized for high light levels
	- Photopic & color vision
- Specialization of rods and cones:
	- Graded and sustained response in rods
	- Graded but very quick response in cones
	- Many rods (15-30) connect to a bipolar cell
	- One cone connects to a bipolar cell
- Specialization for light:
	- Scotopic (absolute threshold of vision, extremely dark, no color): controlled by rods
	- Mesopic (transitional range of vision, some color): mix of both
	- Photopic (bright, color): controlled by cones
- Anatomical specialization:
	- Center of retina: fovea, higher density of cones and no rods at all 
	- On the edges of the retina there are far fewer cones and they tend to be larger
	- Rods are the opposite - more on the edges and fewer in the center

**Receptive Fields of Ganglion cells:**
- Ganglion cells: cells that generate action potentials that send signals to brain
- Receptive field: region of the body, that when stimulated can modulate the activity of nerve cells
- Same is true for retina, but only the photoreceptors are stimulated
- Retinal receptive field can be defined in terms of the location of light in the environment 
- Same center-surround structure, as in CNS
	- On center ganglion fires best when light strikes the center of its field
	- Off center ganglion fires best when light strikes the outside of its field
- Diagram illustrates how two types of ganglions respond to light
	- Center plus surround causes small but more even response

**More Notes:**
- Why the dichotomy of ganglion cells?
- NT released by bipolar cell is glutamate, receptor is metabotropic 
- Hyperpolarization of bipolar cell occurs 
- AMPA receptors in next cell - causes depolarization 
- On center ganglion cell is turned off in the dark
- Different receptors for the same transmitters provides the basis for our on and off responses in the center of our receptive fields

**Light at Center:**
- On center cell: hyperpolarization occurs - lower release of glutamate. Less glutamate causes the on center bipolar cell to depolarize. NT is released, and the on center cell is activated
- Off center cell: hyperpolarization occurs - lower release of glutamate. Less glutamate causes the on center bipolar cell to hyperpolarize. NT is released, and the off center cell is deactivated

**Dark at Center:**
- On center cell: depolarization occurs - release of glutamate. Bipolar cell hyperpolarizes. NT is released, and the on center cell is deactivated
- Off center cell: depolarization occurs - release of glutamate. Bipolar cell depolarizes. NT is released, and the off center cell is activated
