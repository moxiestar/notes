**Cell division overview:**
- Cell division/mitosis: reproduction of a cell resulting in two identical "daughter" cells 
- Asexual reproduction: creation of offspring by a single parent without sperm or egg
	- Offspring created by asexual reproduction are known as clones
	- Often used by single-celled organisms
- Sexual reproduction: creation of offspring by two parents with sperm and egg
- Cell division is critical for human embryonic development, but excess cell division is the cause of cancer
- Through cell division, the variety of different cell types required for human development can be created from a single original cell
- Binary fission: prokaryotic cell division
- Prokaryotes only have one chromosome, which must be split and passed on to the two daughter cells
- Eukaryotes have more genes than prokaryotes, and their genes are more complex
- Humans have roughly 20,000 genes
- Each human cell has 46 chromosomes, each consisting of a strand of DNA and protein molecules
	- Single chromosome = chromatin
- As chromosomes are separating, they are briefly known as sister chromatids

**Chromosomes:**
 - Homologous chromosomes: pairs of chromosomes bonded in body
	- Total chromosomes: 46
	- Pairs of chromosomes: 23
	- They have the same genes in the same locations (loci), but they may have different versions of the same genes
- XY chromosomes: sex chromosomes
- Autosomes: non sex chromosomes 

**Cell terms:**
- Life cycle: sequence of generation-to-generation stages in life of an organism
- Diploid: cell containing homologous chromosomes
- Haploid: cell containing a single chromosome set (one of each homologous pair)
- Gamete: sex cells with 22 autosomes plus an X or Y sex chromosome
- Fertilization: haploid sperm and haploid egg cell fuse to begin development
- Zygote: fertilized diploid egg - one set of homologous chromosomes from each parent cell

**Cell growth:**
- Most cells show anchorage dependence: they must be in contact with a solid surface to divide
- Density dependent inhibition: cells will stop dividing if the environment is very dense
- Cancer cells do not have DDI, so they will continue to divide even if the environment is dense
- Growth factors: cells will only divide in the presence of these proteins
- Tumor: mass of abnormally growing cells within normal tissue
- Benign tumor: tumor that remains in its original location
- Malignant tumor: tumor cells that spread into other areas of the body
- Cancer = malignant tumors, not benign tumors
- Immortal cells continue to divide as long as they have nutrients (normal cells only divide around 20-50 times before they stop)
- Typical eukaryotes spend 90% of their time in interphase
- Proto-oncogenes (tumor suppressors) stimulate cell division - mutations here can lead to cancer

Phases of mitosis:
- There are five stages of mitosis: prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase
	- Prophase: mitotic spindle forms, chromosomes are duplicated
	- Prometaphase: nuclear envelope dissolves, spindle begins to facilitate mitosis
	- Metaphase: spindle is fully formed, chromosomes align on the metaphase plate
	- Anaphase: chromatids are separated and pulled to opposite ends of the cell, cell is elongated
	- Telophase: cell is lengthened further and begins to divide

**Genetics:**
- Heredity: transformation of traits from one generation to the next
- Genetics: study of heredity
	- Began in the 1860s with monk Mendel - studied garden peas
	- Mendel wrote that parents pass heritable factors (genes) on to their offspring
	- Genes may be mixed but they retain their individuality from generation to generation
- Character: heritable feature that varies among individuals (ie color)
- Trait: variants for characters 

**Key genetics terms:**
- True-breeding varieties: varieties that produce the same variety over many generations
- Hybrids: offspring of two different varieties
- Cross/hybridization: fertilizing two different varieties
- P generation: true-breeding parents
- F1 generation: hybrid offspring of P
- F2 generation: hybrid offspring of F1
- Alleles: alternate forms of a gene that arise through mutation
- Homozygous: organism that has two identical alleles for a gene
- Heterozygous: organism that has two different alleles for a gene
- Dominant allele: allele that has an observable effect on the organism's appearance
- Recessive allele: allele that does not have an effect on the organism's appearance
- Law of segregation: gametes only carry one allele for each inheritable character because allele pairs separate during meiosis
- Punnett square: maps possible combinations of alleles
- Phenotype: observable traits
- Genotype: genetic makeup
- Monohybrid cross: cross between two individuals that are heterozygous for one character
- Dihybrid cross: cross between two individuals that are heterozygous for two characters
- Law of independent assortment: inheritance of one character has no effect on the inheritance of another
- Testcross: the mating between an individual with an unknown genotype and an individual that is homozygous recessive for that same character
- Rule of multiplication: probability of a compound event is the product of the separate probabilities of independent events
- Rule of addition: probability that an event can occur in two or more alternate ways is the sum of the separate probabilities of the different ways
- Complete dominance: inheritance in which the phenotypes of the heterozygote and dominant homozygote are indistinguishable, leading to identical traits (to the parent) in an individual
- Incomplete dominance: inheritance in which the phenotype of a heterozygote (Aa) is intermediate between the phenotypes of the two types of homozygotes (AA and aa), leading to a mix of traits in an individual 
- Co-dominance: inheritance in which two alleles of the same gene are expressed separately, leading to different traits in an individual 
- Pleiotropy: ability of a gene to influence many different characteristics
- Polygenic inheritance: additive effects of several genes on a single phenotypic character
- Chromosome theory of inheritance: genes are located in set places on chromosomes and the behavior of chromosomes during meiosis accounts for inheritance patterns

**Mendel's experiments:**
- Pea plant experiment
	- 1. Mendel prevented self-fertilization by cutting off the immature stamens (pollen, contains sperm) of a plant before they produced pollen
	- 2. To cross-fertilize the stamenless flower, he dusted the carpel (contains eggs) with pollen from another plant
	- 3. The carpel developed into a pod containing seeds (peas) 
	- 4. Mendel planted those seeds
	- 5. The seeds grew into offspring plants
