**Immunity:**
- Pathogen: a foreign mechanism that causes disease
- Antigen: a foreign molecule that elicits an adaptive response
- Antibody: a blood-borne protein that attaches to a given antigen and combats its effects
- Innate immunity: immunity present in organisms before exposure to outside pathogens
	- Innate external barriers: skin, exoskeleton, acidic environment, secretions, mucous membranes, cilia
	- Innate internal defenses: phagocytic cells, natural killer cells, defensive proteins, inflammatory response
	- Invertebrates only have innate immunity
- Innate immune cells (white blood cells):
	- Phagocytes:
		- Neutrophils: most abundant type of WBC, circulate in bloodstream, enter infection sites
		- Macrophages: phagocytes in interstitial fluid that break down pathogens
	- Natural killer cells:
		- Recognize cancer & pathogenic cells and release chemicals to kill them
- Interferons:
	- Innate defensive proteins that combat viral infections
	- Secreted by infected body cells to help healthy cells fight infection
- Complement system:
	- Group of 30 proteins that act together to defend system 
	- Circulate bloodstream, activated by substances on microbe surfaces
	- Process leads to lysis of foreign cells

**Types of immunity:**
- Innate immunity: immunity present in organisms before exposure to outside pathogens
	- Innate immunity is quick and recognizes a large range of pathogens
	- Innate immunity does not have any memory for previously encountered pathogens
- Adaptive immunity: immune response activated by exposure to an antigen and monitored by lymphocytes
	- Adaptive immunity is slower and recognizes specific pathogens
	- Adaptive has “memory” for pathogens that can last for decades
	- Antigen (antibody-generating): foreign molecule that triggers adaptive response
	- Antibody: immune protein created in response to antigen
		- Each antibody is specific to its antigen and cannot combat other antigens
- Active immunity: when the body creates its own antibodies in response to a pathogen
	- Vaccines deliver a small amount of a harmless variant of a pathogen, prompting the body to create its own antibodies
	- When the immune system is re-exposed to the pathogen, it will be able to quickly mount an adaptive response
- Passive immunity: when the body receives antibodies for a pathogen

**Inflammation:**
- Inflammatory response: an immune response in vertebrates triggered by the release of histamines, leading to inflammation in the affected area
	- Caused by any kind of damage to tissue 
	- Response disinfects tissue
	- Basic process of inflammatory response:
		- Tissue is damaged
		- Invading bacteria activate macrophages, which signal to increase blood flow
		- At the site, mast cells (WBC in connective tissue) release histamine
		- Capillaries dilate, becoming leaky - this allows more chemicals and cells to flow through blood vessels
		- Fluid passes from capillaries into affected tissue
		- Clotting proteins seal off affected area
		- Phagocytes migrate to affected area - neutrophils kill bacteria and infected cells
- Chronic inflammation can be harmful, but the inflammatory response is natural
- Inflammatory response can spread throughout the body - fever and high WBC count are symptoms
	- Overwhelming systemic inflammatory response leads to septic shock

**Lymphatic system:**
- Network of vessels and nodes that support immune responses
- Lymph nodes: small organs filled with macrophages and lymphocytes
- Lymphocyte: type of white blood cell that is largely responsible for the immune response
	- Lymphocytes originate in bone marrow
	- B cells: specialized lymphocytes that attack pathogens in bodily fluids
	- T cells: specialized lymphocytes that target infected cells
	- Both B and T cells have antigen receptors
	- Humoral immune response: activation of B cells, which secrete antibodies into blood and lymph to neutralize antigens within the body
	- Cell-mediated immune response: activation of defensive T cells, which destroy infected cells within the body

**Clonal selection:**
- Clonal selection: the process of an antigen activating its specific receptors on B cells, leading the lymphocytes to differentiate into memory cells and effector cells
	- Memory cells: B cells that are stored in the lymph nodes to be reactivated upon second exposure to the antigen
	- Effector cells: B cells that secrete antibodies into blood and lymph, tagging antigens for destruction by macrophages

- Primary immune response: initial adaptive immune response after exposure to an antigen
	- Few lymphocytes are activated
	- Reaction time is slow
	- Produces both memory and effector cells
- Secondary immune response: adaptive immune response after repeated exposure to the same antigen
	- Thousands of effector cells are activated
	- Reaction time is very fast
	- Produces both memory and effector cells

**Reproduction**
- Asexual reproduction:
	- One organism clones itself through mitosis
	- Advantages: fast, requires only one parent organism
	- Disadvantages: less genetic variety
- Sexual reproduction:
	- Two organisms (a male and a female) produce offspring through fertilization
	- Advantages: great genetic variety
	- Disadvantages: slow, requires two parent organisms
- Some organisms can reproduce both sexually and asexually
- Process of sexual reproduction:
	- Gametes: sex cells with a haploid set of chromosomes
	- Zygote: fertilized diploid cell formed by egg and sperm
- Internal fertilization occurs when the male’s sperm are deposited within the female’s reproductive system
- External fertilization occurs when the gametes are fertilized in an outside environment
- Gonads: organs that produce gametes


**Female anatomy:**
- Female gonads: ovaries
- Follicles: cluster of ovarian cells that form and release eggs
	- Follicles also produce estrogen
	- Ovulation: every 28 days, a follicle matures to create an egg, which is then released
	- After ovulation, when the egg is released, the remains of the follicle form the corpus luteum, which then degenerates if the woman is not pregnant
		- Corpus luteum produces progesterone and estrogen during pregnancy
- Oviduct (fallopian tube): 
	- Egg is fertilized in the upper oviduct, near the uterus
	- Zygote then travels to the uterus
- Embryo is implanted in the endometrium within the uterus 
- Ectopic pregnancy: 
	- Pregnancy that occurs outside the uterus (usually in fallopian tube)
	- Not compatible with life, must be resolved with surgery
- Cervix - opening of uterus:
	- Pap smear tests cervical cells for signs of cervical cancer

	- During menstruation FSH signals one of the follicles to develop, and the oocyte completes meiosis I and begins meiosis II, stopping at metaphase II

**Species:**
- Speciation: evolution of a new species
- Species classifications:
	- Biological species concept: based on the ability to reproduce and have fertile offspring
		- Reproductive isolation: barriers that stop two species from producing fertile offspring
		- Hybrids: offspring of two distinct species
	- Morphological species concept: based on anatomical criteria
	- Ecological species concept: based on ecological environment
	- Phylogenetic species concept: based on common ancestors
- Phenology:
	- Timing of life cycle events:
		- Development
		- Migration
		- Mating
		- Reproduction
		- Hibernation
- Species change
	- Crabs in Alaskan ocean - warmer temperatures caused their metabolism to rise, causing them to starve
	- Mosquitoes traveled to Kaua'i and bit a population of birds to death


**Populations:**
- Population: organisms from the same species that live in the same area and can breed
- Population ecology: the study of how populations interact with their environment
	- Focus on population density and growth
- Population density: the number of individual members of a species per unit of area
- Population dispersion: the way individual members of a population are dispersed across their area
	- Clumped dispersion pattern: individuals are gathered in patches
	- Uniform dispersion pattern: individuals are evenly distributed
	- Random dispersion pattern: individuals are randomly dispersed
- Population growth:
	- Exponential growth model: plots the unlimited growth of a population over a given time frame
	- Exponential growth equation: $G = rN$
		- G: growth rate of population (number of new individuals added)
		- *r*: per capita rate of increase (average contribution by each individual to the growth of the overall population)
		- N: population size
	- Limiting factor: environmental factor that limits population growth
		- Density-dependent factor: limiting factor based on population density
			- Intraspecific competition: competition between members of a population for a limited resource
		- Density-independent factor: limiting factor not based on population density
			- Seasonal changes, etc
	- Logistic growth model: plots the unlimited growth of a population while taking limiting factors into account
	- Carrying capacity: maximum population size that an environment can sustain
	- Logistic growth equation: $G = rN(K - N)/K$
	- When birth and death rates are equal, population growth rate is 0
	- Demographic transition: when both birth and death rates go from being high to being low
		- Less developed countries have an exponentially higher rate of growth than more developed countries

**Communities:**
- Community: set of populations living close enough to interact
- Interspecific interactions:
	- Competition: two populations fighting over the same resources
		- Effect on species 1: negative
		- Effect on species 2: negative
		- Example: squirrels & black bears
		- Intraspecific competition: individuals within the same species are competing for the same limited resource
		- Interspecific competition: two different species are competing for the same resource
	- Mutualism: two populations both benefitting from each other
		- Effect on species 1: positive
		- Effect on species 2: positive
		- Example: plants & myrcorrhizae
	- Predation: one species (predator) killing and eating another (prey)
		- Effect on species 1: positive
		- Effect on species 2: negative
		- Example: crocodiles & fish
	- Herbivory: an animal species consuming a plant species
		- Effect on species 1: positive
		- Effect on species 2: negative
		- Example: caterpillars & leaves
	- Parasitism: a parasite or pathogen benefits from its host species
		- Effect on species 1: positive
		- Effect on species 2: negative
		- Example: heartworms & dogs
- Ecological niche: role of a species in a community
	- Sum total of a species' use of biotic and abiotic resources in its environment
- Coevolution: an adaptation in one species that forces another species to adapt
