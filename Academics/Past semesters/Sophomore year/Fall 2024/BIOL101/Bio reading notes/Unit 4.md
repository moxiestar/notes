**Natural selection**
- Natural selection: individuals with certain more advantageous traits are more likely to survive to another generation
	- Key concepts:
		- Individuals do not evolve, the population evolves
		- Evolution is limited by historical constraints
		- Evolution is an interaction between chance, environment, and natural selection
		- Natural selection can only affect the intensity of genetics, not the actual genetic traits - it is an editing process
		- Evolution is not perfect or goal-orientated
		- Natural selection is dependent on time and place
		- Mutation (change in genetic code) gives rise to different traits 
	- Five types of natural selection:
		- Directional selection: individuals at one end of a phenotypic range reproduce more successfully than individuals at the other end of the spectrum
		- Stabilizing selection: intermediate variants of genes are favored over extreme phenotypes
		- Disruptive selection: extreme phenotypes are favored over intermediate variants
		- Balancing selection: all variants (extreme and intermediate) are equally favored
		- Sexual selection: individuals with certain traits are more likely to find mates

**Evolution**
- Artificial selection: purposefully breeding to encourage the most advantageous traits to be passed on
- Microevolution: a change in a population's gene pool over time
- Homology: similarity in characters resulting from shared ancestry
	- Homologous structures: traits in different species that are similar due to shared ancestry
- Vestigial structure: feature that once served a function in a species and is still retained in the species (ie, "tail" in human embryo)
	- Vestigial gene: genetic codings that can no longer be created due to mutations
- Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: frequencies of alleles and genotypes in a population remain constant over generations
	- Equation: $p^2 + 2pq + q^2$
		- $p^2$ – the probability of producing a homozygous dominant
		- $2pq$ – the probability of producing a heterozygous individual
		- $q^2$ – the probability of producing a homozygous recessive
	- Five conditions of genetic equilibrium:
		- a. The population must be large 
		- b. There must be no gene flow between populations
		- c. There must be no mutations
		- d. Mating must be random
		- e. There must be no natural selection
- Genetic drift: random change in gene pool of a population
	- Bottleneck effect: genetic drift caused by a drastic reduction in population size - leads to an overall genetically different population
	- Founder effect: genetic drift caused by the isolation of a few individuals from a larger population - leads to a new, genetically different population
- Gene flow (migration): the transfer of alleles or populations from one population/location to another
- Relative fitness: the contribution an organism makes to the gene pool of the following generation
- Sexual dimorphism: a marked difference between the appearance of males and females of a given species
	- Intersexual selection - competition between males and females
	- Intrasexual selection - competition between the same sexes
- Heterozygote advantage: greater reproductive success of heterozygous individuals over homozygous - preserves variation in gene pools


**Reproduction & speciation**
- Prezygotic barriers: prevent mating between speciation 
	- Isolation can be temporal, habitat, behavioral, or gametic
		- Habitat: species live in different areas
		- Temporal: species breed at different times of the year
		- Behavioral: species communicate in different ways
		- Mechanical: species physically cannot reproduce
- Postzygotic barriers: prevent hybrid zygotes from becoming viable offspring
	- Can be achieved through reduced hybrid viability, reduced hybrid fertility, and hybrid breakdown

**Cladistics**
- Cladistics: method of grouping organisms into clades based on common descent
- Clade: group of species including original ancestor and its descendants
	- Monophyletic: group of taxa including ancestor and descendants
	- Shared ancestral character: trait that is shared by members of a clade, but inherited from an ancestor that is not part of the clade
	- Shared derived character: trait that is unique to a clade
- Outgroup: related species in a lineage of interest - shares the least number of traits (frog in table below)
- Ingroup: lineage of interest
- Parsimony: using the simplest explanation for an observed phenomenon
- Molecular systematics: using DNA to refer relationships between species
