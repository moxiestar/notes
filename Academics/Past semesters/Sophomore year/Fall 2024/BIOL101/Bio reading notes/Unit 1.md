**Molecular biology - key terms**
- Organic compounds: carbon-based molecules (containg hydrogen and carbon)
	- Molecular diversity is based on carbon
- Isomers: compounds with the same formula but different structural arrangements
- Hydrophillic: compounds that are soluble in water
- Important molecules of all life on earth are either **carbohydrates**, **proteins**, lipids, or **nucleic** **acids** (bolded are macromolecules)
- Cells create macromolecules by joining molecules into polymers 
	- Individual segments of polymers are monomers
- Cells create polymers through dehydration reactions
	- Dehydration removes a molecule of water as two molecules bond
	- One monomer loses a hydroxyl group
	- One monomer loses a hydrogen atom 
	- As this happens, a new covalent bond forms, linking the two monomers
- Polymers are broken down through hydrolysis
	- Bond is broken by the addition of a molecule of water
	- Hydrogen atom bonds to one monomer and hydroxyl group bonds to the other
- Enzymes are specialized macromolecules
- In summary: 
	- Taking away a water molecule -> bonding of monomers
	- Adding a water molecule -> unbonding of monomers


**Functional groups**
- Functional groups:
	- Hydroxyl group: hydrogen atom bonded to an oxygen atom
	- Carbonyl group: carbon atom double bonded to an oxygen atom 
		- Can be located within or at the end of a carbon skeleton
	- Carboxyl group: carbon atom double bonded to an oxygen atom and a hydroxyl group 
	- Amino group: nitrogen atom bonded to two hydrogen atoms
	- Phosphate group: phosphorus atom bonded to two hydrogen atoms 
	- Methyl group: carbon atom bonded to three hydrogen atoms 


**Basic compounds**
- Carbohydrates: monosaccharides (monomers), polysaccharides (polymers)
	- Polysaccharides are longer chains of monosaccharide polymers 
	- Disaccharides are formed from monosaccharides through dehydration
	- Those who eat sugar for more than a quarter of their daily food have a 275% increase in the risk of death from cardiovascular disease
	- Almost all carbohydrates are hydrophillic due to the hydroxyl groups in the sugar monomers
- Fats: lipids, largely energy storage molecules
	- Lipids are hydrophobic and not macromolecules or polymers
	- Types of lipids: fats, phospholipids, steroids
		- Fat: large lipids consisting of glycerol (three carbons with a hydroxyl group) and fatty acids (carboxyl group and hydrocarbon chain)
			- Unsaturated fatty acids: fatty acids with hydrocarbon chains that contain one or more double bonds 
			- Saturated fatty acids: fatty acids with hydrocarbon chains with no double bonds
			- Hydrogenated fats: unsaturated fats converted to saturated fats via hydrogenation 
		- Phospholipids: major compotent of cell membranes
			- Contain two fatty acids attached to glycerol
			- Tails are hydrophobic, while heads are hydrophillic
		- Steroids: carbon skeleton contains four fused rings
			- Steroids vary in terms of the chemical groups attached to the rings
- Proteins: polymers of amino acids
	- Many different functions: enzymes, transport proteins, structural proteins, muscle proteins, storage proteins
	- A protein's function depends on its shape
		- Through denaturation, proteins lose their shape and their function
	- Proteins are made from amino acids linked by peptide bonds
	- Amino acids: amino group and carboxyl group 
		- General structure: central carbon bonded to a hydrogen + amino group + carboxyl group + R group
			- R group: variable chemical group
				- Either a hydrogen atom or (a) carbon atom(s) bonded to various groups
			- R groups can be hydrophillic or hydrophobic
		- Amino acids link together with peptide bonds 
		- A chain of amino acids is a polypeptide
			- Polypeptides are folded into proteins - R groups determine their structure and shape
![[Screenshot 2024-08-25 at 9.44.26 PM.png]]
- DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) and RNA (ribonucleic acid) are polymers known as nucleic acids
	- Monomers of nucleic acids are nucleotides
		- DNA nucleotides can be ATCG; RNA nucleotides can be AUCG (different nitrogenous bases)
		- DNA consists of two polynucleotides (double helix); RNA consists of one polynucleotide
	- DNA and RNA control the cell's activities by creating proteins
		- DNA is transcribed into RNA -> RNA interacts with protein-building machinery -> gene instructions are transcribed into amino acid sequence of polypeptide -> protein is created


**Protein structure**
- Primary structure: precise sequence of amino acids in polypeptide chain
	- Held together by peptide bonds
- Secondary structure: segments of the chain folded into local patterns
	- Held together by hydrogen bonds
	- Two types of secondary structure:
		- Alpha helix
		- Beta pleated sheet
- Tertiary structure: overall 3D structure of the protein
- Quaternary structure: only for proteins with more than one polypeptide chain
- Secondary and tertiary structure can be denaturated by heat and pH, but primary structure cannot be broken with heat and pH because the covalent bonds are stronger


**Cell biology - key terms**
- Cells are smaller because of the exchange of information across the plasma membrane 
- There is a careful volume to surface area ratio in cell size - if cells increase in volume, their surface area gets smaller
- Plasma membrane consists of hydrophobic phospholipid bilayer 
- Information enters and leaves the cell through vesicles and proteins on the membrane


**Cell anatomy**
- Nucleus stores the DNA of the cell in the form of chromosomes
	- Nuclear envelope (nuclear membrance, interspersed with pores that allow for things to travel into or out of the nucleus)
	- Chromatin (mass of DNA and proteins)
	- Nucleolus (location where rRNA is synthesized)
- Ribosomes make proteins for the cell to use 
	- Many proteins are made by free ribosomes floating in cytosol
	- A few proteins are made by bound ribosomes on the ER
- Endomembrane system: nuclear envelope, ER, Golgi body, lysosomes, plasma membrane, and various vesicles
- ER manufactures proteins
	- Rough ER has bound ribosomes on its surface
		- Produces proteins with ribosomes 
			- Polypeptide is synthesized by bound ribosome with mRNA, then transported into the ER, where it takes on its tertiary structure. It is packaged in a vesicle and brought to the Golgi body for final processing, and from there it is sent to the plasma membrane to the leave the cell
			- In the Golgi body, a protein will fuse into one side of a stack, travel through the stack, and then exit in a vesicle
			- Inside the stack, they may receive molecular identification tags
	- Smooth ER has no ribosomes on its surface
		- Functions: metabolic processes, synthesis of lipids and steroids, and enzymes that process drugs and alcohol, storage of Ca
- Lysosomes are digestive vesicles that break down various waste inside the cell
	- They can recycle waste (with damaged parts of the cell) or simply destroy it (with bacteria)
	- Tay-Sachs disease: lysosomal storage disease where brain cells are impaired by accumulation of lipids
- Mitochondria perform cellular respiration, converting chemical energy into ATP
	- Evolved from absorbed prokaryotes
	- Within the membrane, another membrane is folded into cristae, creating the mitochondrial matrix and increasing overall surface area
- Chloroplasts convert solar energy into chemical energy through photosynthesis
	- The inner area of the chloroplast is filled with stroma, a thick fluid that contains DNA and ribosomes
	- Stacks (thylakoids) of flat chips (granum) are suspended in stroma
	- Thylakoids are like power plants where chlorophyll harvests solar energy 
- Mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved through endosymbiosis 
	- Both mitochondria and chloroplasts contain a single DNA molecule, resembling prokaryotes
	- The endosymbiont theory states that mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved from prokaryotes swallowed by a eukaryote
- Cytoskeleton supports the structure of the cell, similar to the human skeleton
	- Cytoskeleton is made up of microtubles, microfilaments, and intermediate filaments
		- Microtubles are straight, hollow tubes composed of globular proteins called tubulins
			- Can be broken down and reassembled 
			- Used as "highways" within the cells for motor proteins to travel
		- Microfilaments or actin filaments are solid rods composed of actin
			- Involved in cell movements 
			- Support cell shape beneath the plasma membrane
		- Intermediate filaments (found in the cells of most animals) are made of various fibrous proteins that supercoil into cables
			- Permanent structures (cannot be reused)
			- Reinforce cell shape and support cell structures
- Cell membrane functions as a barrier
	- Fluid mosaic model: membrane consists of a variety of molecules suspended in the phospholipid bilayer
	- Membrane exhibits selective permeability - only lets certain molecules through
	- Function of proteins in membrane:
		- Enzymes - carry out sequential reactions
		- Junction proteins - attach to other cells
		- Transport proteins (channel/active) - allow molecules to enter/exit the cell
		- Receptor proteins - bind molecules and respond to their signals
		- Attachment proteins - help support the membrane
		- Glycoproteins - ID tags for the cell to be recognized
	- Cell membrane was an important step in cell evolution
	- Membrane: controlled environment, controlled in and outflow of molecules


**Prokaryotes & eukaryotes**
- Prokaryotes: small single celled (usually) bacterial organisms 
	- Unique features:
		- Nucleoid
		- Cell wall
		- Specific type of ribosomes
		- Specific type of DNA
		- Flagella
		- Fimbriae 
- Eukaryotes: main cell in all other types of life (Eukarya)
	- Unique features:
		- Nucleus
		- Golgi body
		- Smooth and rough ER
		- Mitochondria (evolved and absorbed prokaryotes)
		- Lysosomes
	- Eukaryotes are often partitioned into different compartments where cellular metabolism takes place
	- Plant cells - unique features:
		- Chloroplast
		- Cell wall
		- Central vacuole

**Chemical reactions - key terms**
- Diffusion: tendency for particles to spread out in space
- Concentration gradient: the overall concentration of the molecules across a space
- Equilibrium: equal concentration gradient
- Movement across a membrane in diffusion is passive transport
- Osmosis - movement of water across membrane
- Tonicity: ability of a solution to cause a cell to lose or gain water
- Tonicity is dependent on the concentration of the solution vs the cell
	- Isotonic: solution with same solute concentration as the cell
	- Hypertonic: solution with higher solute concentration than the cell (shrinks)
	- Hypotonic: solution with lower solute concentration than the cell (swells)
- Facilitated diffusion: transport across the membrane aided by transport proteins
	- Diffusion: nonpolar molecules
	- Facilitated: polar molecules, ions
- Active transport: protein powered by ATP moves molecules against their concentration gradient
	- Solute binds to transport protein
	- ATP provides energy for the protein to change shape
	- Molecules flow across membrane
	- Protein returns to original shape
	- Costs energy for the cell
- Exocytosis: transports large molecules (proteins, polysaccharides) across the membrane to the extracellular fluid via transport vesicles
- Endocytosis: transports large molecules inside the cell
	- Phagocytosis: pseudopod extends to envelop something in the extracellular fluid
	- Receptor-mediated endocytosis: molecule in extracellular fluid binds to receptors, creating a coated vesicle including the molecule
- Oxidation: loss of electron from a substance
- Reduction: addition of electron to a substance 
- Redox reaction: reduction and oxidation go together because the transfer of an electron always requires both gain and loss


**Enzymes in reactions**
- Activation energy: energy barrier that must be overcome before a chemical reaction can begin 
	- Creates barrier to cellular life
- Enzymes increase the rate of chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy
- Substrate: the specific reactant that an enzyme binds to
- Active site: the area on an enzyme where the substrate binds
- Once binded, substrates are then converted to products and released
- Every enzyme has an optimal condition for function
- Cofactors: metals that bind to the active site and facilitate chemical reactions
- Inhibitors prevent chemical reactions from happening by stopping substrate binding
	- Competitive inhibitors physically stop the substrate from binding
	- Noncompetitive inhibitors alter the shape of the active site


**Energy**
- Two basic forms of energy: kinetic (energy of motion - ex thermal) and potential (energy of location or structure, innate energy - ex chemical)
- Thermodynamics: study of energy transformation 
	- First law: energy cannot be created or destroyed 
	- Second law: every energy conversion increases entropy
	- Third law: I don't understand the third law
- Cellular respiration: transforming the chemical energy in organic molecules to produce ATP


**Basic chemical reactions**
- Two types of chemical reactions:
	- Exergonic reaction: reactants' covalent bonds contain more energy than those in the products, so energy is released and the products contain less energy than the reactants
	- Endergonic reaction: yield products that have high potential energy - energy is absorbed from the environment as the reaction happens, so the products contain more energy than the reactants
- Metabolism: total of an organism's chemical reactions
- Energy coupling: use of energy released from exergonic reactions to fuel endergonic reactions


**ATP processing**
- ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the basis of cellular energy
- ATP -> ADP + phosphate group + energy
- ATP is made through phosphorylation: an endergonic reaction, where a phosphate group from an existing ATP molecule is transferred to an ADP molecule
- ATP is used through hydrolysis; in an exergonic reaction, ATP is used to produce ADP, a phosphate group, and energy
- There are three types of cellular work, chemical, transport, and mechanical; all are powered by ATP


**Alternative energy production**
- Fermentation enables cells to produce ATP without oxygen (anaerobic respiration) via glycolysis
- NADH is recycled back to NAD+ (oxidized)
- Lactic acid - pyruvate is reduced to lactate
- Alcohol - pyruvate is reduced to ethanol

**Hormones**
- Endocrine system produces hormones through chemical and electrical signals
- Secreted by endocrine glands
- Hormones travel to target cells, which have hormone receptors and can affect various areas in the body
- Hormone classes:
	- Water soluble: cannot pass through the membrane and affect changes without entering the target cell. Bind to receptors in the membrane
	- Lipid soluble: can pass through the membrane and affect the DNA inside of the target cell. Bind to receptors inside the cell


**Diabetes**
- Pancreas secretes insulin and glucagon (antagonistic hormones), which maintain the level of glucose in the blood
- Type 1 diabetes: body cannot produce insulin so the blood sugar level stays elevated
- Type 2 diabetes: body produces insulin but the insulin receptors are defective
- Hyperglycemia: dangerous condition that can occur when too much insulin is in the body
- Hypoglycemia: condition that occurs when there is a lack of insulin in the body
- **Insulin lowers blood sugar**
- **Glucagon raises blood sugar**

**Cellular respiration vs photosynthesis**
- Photosynthesis uses sunlight to change CO2 and water into organic molecules and oxygen
- Cellular respiration involves consuming oxygen to break organic molecules into CO2 and water. Energy is released as ATP
- Photosynthesis: Sunlight + CO2 + H2O -> organic compounds + O2
	- $6CO_2 + 6H_2O → C_6H_12O_6 + 6O_2$
- Cellular respiration: O2 + organic compounds -> CO2 + H2O + ATP
	- $C_6H_12O_6 + 6O_2 > 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + ATP + HEAT$
- **Photosynthesis is endergonic**
- **Respiration is exergonic**


**Stages of cellular respiration**
- Stage 1: Glycolysis (glucose -> pyruvate), occurs in cytosol, ATP is released
- Stage 2: Pyruvate oxidization fuels the citric acid cycle, occurs in mitochondria, ATP and CO2 are released
- Stage 3: Oxidative phosphorylation, occurs in mitochondria, ATP, O2, and H2O are released

**Glycolysis**
- Occurs in: cytosol
- Requires: Glucose, 2 ADP, 2 P, 2 NAD+
- Produces: 2 ATP, 2 NADH, 2H+
- Glucose is oxidized using 2 ADP, 2 P, and 2 NAD+
- 2 Pyruvate molecules are produced, along with 2 ATP, 2 NADH, and 2 H+
- ATP is formed via substrate-level phosphorylation

**Krebs Cycle**
- Occurs in: mitochondria
- Requires: Acetyl CoA, 3 NAD+, ADP, P, FAD
- Produces: 2 CO2, 3 NADH, 3 H+, ATP, FADH2
- Pyruvate oxidation produces CoA
	- Using NAD+, coenzyme A, pyruvate loses CO2 and binds with CoA to become acetyl CoA
	- This produces NADH, H+, and CO2
- CoA begins the cycle, releasing 2 CO2
- A small amount of ATP is released
- FADH2 and NADH are generated
- The cycle continues

**Oxidative phosphorylation**
- Occurs in: mitochondria
- Requires: NADH, FADH2 
- Produces: H2O, roughly 32 ATP
- NAD and NADH electron carriers facilitate H travelling against its concentration gradient, from low to high
- H travels across the inner mitochondrial membrane, where it interacts with ATP synthase to form ATP
- H is picked up by O2, which forms H2O

**Other notes on photosynthesis**
- Trees are carbon sinks (absorb more carbon than they release via cellular respiration)
- Without trees to take up the CO2 from the atmosphere, our atmosphere would be overwhelmed with CO2 and temperatures would get hotter and hotter
- CO2 is released through consumption, decomposition, cellular respiration, and burning of wood
- Calvin cycle: inside stroma, converts CO2 to a sugar molecule, takes 6 tries to produce 1 molecule of glucose (input: NADP+, ADP, P - outputs: ATP, NADPH)
- Light reaction: inside thylakoids, converts light energy and water to O2 (inputs: ATP, NADPH - outputs: NADP+, ADP, P)
