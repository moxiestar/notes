**Chapters 4.1-4.4**
- Types of bonds:
	- Covalent bonds: pairs of shared valence electrons
		- Polar covalent bonds: unequally shared electrons between atoms
		- Nonpolar covalent bonds: equally shared electrons between atoms
	- ![[Screenshot 2024-09-19 at 2.09.30 PM.png]]
- Octet rule: all atoms except the smallest will move electrons until each atom has eight valence electrons
- Lewis dot symbols depict an atom's bonding capacity - the number of unpaired dots indicates the bonding capacity of that element
- Lewis structures: representation of a compound showing the connections between atoms ![[Screenshot 2024-09-19 at 2.18.31 PM.png]]
- A single bonding pair: single bond
	- Double bond, triple bond, etc
- Unshared electron pairs: lone pairs
- The number of unpaired Lewis dots indicates the bonding capacity for that element
- Electrons in the same group (going down the periodic table) have the same number of valence electrons
- Drawing Lewis structures:
	- 1. Determine the number of valence electrons (count all the electrons in the atoms, add or subtract for ions)
	- 2. Arrange element symbols to show how the atoms are bonded and then connect them with single bonds (element with greatest bonding capacity = central element)
	- 3. Complete the octets of the atoms bonded to the central atom (add lone pairs till each atom has an octet)
	- 4. Check the number of valence electrons with the number calculated in Step 1
	- 5. Complete the octet on the central atom

**Chapter 6 Topics:**
Molecular Orbital theory:
- Molecular orbitals: wave functions that represent discrete energy states in molecules
- Lowest energy orbitals fill up first
- Orbitals belong to all atoms in a molecule
- Bonding orbital: regions of increased electron density between nuclear centers that hold atoms together in molecules - electrons move between the two nuclei
- Antibonding orbital: orbital that weakens the bond between two atoms
- Sigma orbital: orbital with the highest electron density between the two atoms
- Pi orbital: orbital formed by mixing atomic orbitals above and below the internuclear axis
	- Internuclear axis: axis between the nuclei of two atoms
- Creating molecular orbitals:
	- The number of molecular orbitals equals the number of atomic orbitals used to create them
	- Atomic orbitals with similar energy and orientation mix more effectively than do those with different energies and orientations
	- Better mixing leads to a larger energy difference between bonding and antibonding orbitals and thus greater stabilization of the bonding MOs
	- A molecular orbital can accommodate a maximum of two electrons; two electrons in the same MO have opposite spins
	- Electrons in ground-state molecules occupy the lowest-energy molecular orbitals available, following the Aufbau principle and Hund’s rule
- LUMO: lowest unoccupied molecular orbital
- Bond order: bonding electrons - antibonding electrons
- Hydrogen bonding:
	- Donor: provides hydrogen atom, partially positive
	- Acceptor: accepts hydrogen atom, has a lone pair, electronegative 
	- Requirements: (N, O, F) + H

Intramolecular & intermolecular forces:
- Intermolecular - strongest to weakest: ion-dipole, hydrogen bonding, dipole-dipole, dipole-induced dipole, Van der Waals, London dispersion forces
- Intramolecular - strongest to weakest: ionic, polar covalent, nonpolar covalent
