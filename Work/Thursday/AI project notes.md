# Hearts, Minds, & Machines

## Issue 1 (2024)

**Study on consumer perceptions of AI**

- Quantitative survey (n = 895)
- Qualitative interviews (n = 20)
- AI survey (n = 50)

**Findings**

- **Most Americans have mixed feelings about AI** 


- **Majority (32%) feel neither wholly negative or positive**
  - 26% are optimists, while 41% are pessimists 
  - Optimists tend to be more experienced with AI, while pessimists tend to be less experienced 
    - Interestingly, Gen Z is largely pessimistic about AI, despite being experienced
  - Optimists embrace the new technology, while pessimists leave it behind
- **Balance between promises and perils**
  - **Promises (short-term, functional, personal)**
    - Increased efficiency 
    - Increased quality
    - New abilities
  - **Perils (long-term, emotional, collective)**
    - Loss of jobs
    - Loss of critical thinking
    - Loss of reality/humanity
- **Every AI promise is inherently linked to a peril** 
- **As use cases get more personal, AI feels more like a replacement for humanity than a tool**
  - Using AI in school or work is one thing, but using it creatively or intimately is another
  - Even within a given use case, as things get more personal, it feels more wrong to use AI
    - **Example:** using AI as a research tool increases productivity, but using AI to write your entire final paper feels like cheating 
- **Key takeaways**
  - Beware of the trigger point (avoid activating the inner pessimist)
  - Understand the reasons for resistance
  - Engage with AI in the right context
- **Questions**
  - This study is over two years old - how have views changed since then? More pessimistic? More optimistic? 
  - AI tools have come a long way in those two years, too. Claude can create a whole video game now, and it used to struggle to write a full research report. How does that change our understanding and application of AI? 
    - Were the AI tools shown at the end custom agents/tools? 
  - How do we apply these findings?

## Issue 2 (2025)

**Study on consumer perceptions of AI**

- Quantitative survey (n = 1009)
- Qualitative interviews (n = 22)

**Findings**

- **37% of Americans are optimistic about AI, but 35% have mixed feelings**
  - Optimism is increasing over time
- **But 43.9% of Americans believe an AI apocalypse could occur**
  - “Yikes!”
  - Most Americans picture a Wall-E scenario, where we would become lazy and lose control
- **Overall, Americans are becoming more polarized in their views on AI**
  - It’s what we do best! 
  - Views are most extreme around central areas of life, both personal and corporate 
- **AI pessimists still make up a third of the US population** 
  - They are only getting more pessimistic as time goes on 
- **Demographic split between pessimists and optimists**
  - Pessimists tend to have less experience with AI, while optimists tend to have more 
  - Pessimists tend to have equally negative opinions about social media, while optimists view it more positively 
  - Pessimists tend to have less education, while optimists tend to have more 
  - Women are more likely to be pessimistic, and Black people are more likely to be optimistic
- **Trends in AI exposure**
  - Huge split in AI attitudes on social media - particularly relevant for Gen Z
  - Both pessimists and optimists typically try AI out of curiosity, but both also say AI is not that intuitive
  - People typically turn to AI to outsource familiar tasks
- **Key question: how do we turn consumer curiosity into confidence?**
