## Motivation

**1. Why are you interested in participating in the Fellows program?**
As an artist, my interest in AI has been fueled by a drive for creativity. My path into AI safety, starting in my sophomore year at UNC, has been unusual and organic, uniting cognitive neuroscience, empirical research, and hands-on work with large language models. Most recently, my work as a research assistant in the Hopfinger Lab at UNC sparked a passion for ethical AI. As part of my research, I spent a year creating a complex Python-based behavioral experiment to study attention and working memory, a project that directly shaped my understanding of how neural networks, both biological and artificial, represent and prioritize information. It forced me to think carefully about how model behavior interacts with experimental design in ways I hadn't anticipated, and it made the questions at the heart of AI safety feel urgent and personal rather than abstract.
The Fellows program appeals to me because it's structured around exactly the kind of empirical, output-driven research I want to be doing. During my time at UNC, I’ve been privileged enough to work in a number of different environments, gaining experience in research, entrepreneurship, and tech. The Fellows program would allow me to combine and build on my knowledge across three domains, with mentorship from developers who are defining the field. I'm particularly drawn to the AI Welfare and Mechanistic Interpretability tracks, where I believe my background in cognitive psychology and neuroscience provides a perspective that's less common among applicants coming from pure CS. Being a young professional during a time of unprecedented change puts me in a unique position to produce research that matters, and this program seems like the clearest path to achieving this goal.

**2. Technical AI safety area you're currently excited about and why?**
Currently, I'm most excited about AI Welfare. My fascination with the sentience and morality of AI has grown since childhood, when humanoid robots were the stuff of blockbusters. What draws me to this area is that it sits at the intersection of two fields that are usually separate: philosophy of mind and empirical ML research. My background in cognitive psychology and neuroscience has taught me that questions of consciousness and experience are never simple or straightforward; adding AI to the equation only complicates matters further. As time goes on, models will become exponentially more capable in exponentially less time, heightening the need for thorough safety scaffolding. By building the right tools, from behavioral evaluations to theoretical frameworks, we’ll be able to reason more carefully under uncertainty. This is the area where I think my background in psychology could be most valuable, offering a humanistic perspective that pure ML researchers may lack.

## **Extra stuff**

Unlike many young developers, my path to AI was almost accidental. I first heard about generative AI during my senior year of high school - DALL-E and its competitors had just been released, and garbled renditions of beloved cartoons and celebrities were popping up all over the internet. As an artist and writer, I was dismayed by the idea of AI taking my job, or worse, taking over the world. Why would I talk to Chat-GPT when I could talk to my friends, or generate an image of Will Smith when I could find a perfectly good picture online? I told my parents that AI was a fad.

Even now, as a Student Ambassador for Anthropic and the founder of an AI-powered startup, the idea of using Chat-GPT to do my homework dismays me. For me, the goal isn’t getting AI to do my work, it’s learning how best to do my work with AI. It was my work as research assistant in the Hopfinger Lab at UNC, where I spent a year designing a complex, Python-based behavioral experiment, that ignited my interest in ethical AI.
