# Mosaic — Parent Meeting Framework
## One-on-one intro calls · 20–30 min

---

## Before the call
- Pull up their row in the tracker — note # of students, how they found Mosaic
- Have mosaiclearn.dev open to demo if needed
- Have their promo code(s) ready to send immediately after

---

## Structure

### 1. Open (2 min)
Warm, not scripted. Something like:

> "Thanks so much for making time — I really appreciate it. I'm Stella, I'm a student at UNC and I built Mosaic. Before I tell you anything about it, I'd love to hear a little about [child's name] and what made you reach out."

**Let them talk first.** You'll learn what actually matters to them.

---

### 2. Listen & qualify (5 min)
Let the conversation flow, but listen for:

- **Their kid's background** — total beginner, or some coding experience?
- **Their motivation** — school credit, career prep, genuine curiosity, keeping up with peers?
- **Their concern** — cost, time commitment, whether their kid will stick with it?
- **Their familiarity with AI** — do they know what Claude is? Have they heard of ChatGPT?

You don't need to ask all of these directly. Most will come out naturally if you ask one open question and listen.

---

### 3. The pitch — tailored (5–8 min)
Lead with what they told you, not a canned pitch. A rough shape:

> "So based on what you said — [mirror their words back] — I think Mosaic is a really good fit, and here's why..."

**Core points to hit (pick the ones that resonate for them):**

- **The gap** — schools teach coding by hand, nobody teaches students how to work *with* AI. That's the skill employers actually want right now.
- **How it works** — real challenges, not tutorials. Every level ends with something they built and keep.
- **Arc I is free** — no card, starts in the browser, no setup. They can try it before committing to anything.
- **NC CS credit** — if they're in NC, worth mentioning.
- **The certificate** — tangible credential they can put on a college application or resume.

**If they're a total beginner:** emphasize the on-ramp is gentle, Arc I requires zero coding experience, starts with Claude.ai in the browser.

**If their kid has some experience:** emphasize Claude Code in Arc II, the real project outputs, the capstone.

---

### 4. Demo (optional, 3–5 min)
If they seem hesitant or curious, offer a quick look:

> "Do you want me to just pull it up so you can see what the interface looks like?"

Walk them through: the desktop shell, the Brief window, the chat. Show a challenge briefly. Don't overthink it — you're just making it feel real and accessible.

---

### 5. Handle concerns (5 min)
Common ones and how to address them:

**"How much time does it take?"**
> Arc I is 3–5 hours total — they can do it across a few evenings. There's no deadline, progress is saved. Arc II and III are more involved but by then they're usually motivated.

**"Will my kid actually stick with it?"**
> Arc I is free — they can try it with zero commitment. If they're not into it after the first challenge, no harm done. Most kids who get through the first level keep going because they built something real.

**"Is $200 worth it?"**
> One-time payment, lifetime access, all future updates included. Compare it to a single coding bootcamp session or a semester of tutoring. And Arc I is completely free to try first.

**"My kid isn't very technical."**
> That's exactly who this is for. The whole premise is that you don't need to know how to code — you need to know how to work with AI. Arc I doesn't require any prior experience.

**"What's the difference between this and Khan Academy / other coding sites?"**
> Those teach syntax. Mosaic teaches students how to direct AI to build things. It's a different skill — and honestly a more useful one right now.

---

### 6. Close (2 min)
Don't drag it out. Once they're warm:

> "Awesome — I'll send you the access code right after we hang up. Arc I is completely free to start, so there's no pressure at all. Just have [child's name] give it a try and let me know how it goes."

If they want to think about it:
> "Totally — no rush at all. I'll send the code anyway and it'll be there whenever you're ready. Feel free to reply to my email if any questions come up."

---

### 7. After the call (immediate)
- Send the promo code via email — do it while the call is still fresh in their mind
- Update the tracker: mark "Date Sent" column
- Note anything useful in the Notes column (kid's name, experience level, any concerns)

---

## Asking for feedback

Ask for it in the call, but frame it specifically — not "do you have any feedback?" which gets you either nothing or a wishlist. At the end of the call:

> "One thing that would really help me — after [child's name] tries the first level, would you be willing to shoot me a quick note about what they thought? Even just one sentence. It helps me figure out what's working."

This works because it's low-effort (one sentence), time-bound (after the first level), and keeps the conversation going after the call.

Save deeper product feedback — "what would you add, what would you change" — for after they've actually used it. Feedback from people who haven't tried the product yet tends to be speculative. Once a kid has gone through Arc I, parents will have real observations worth hearing.

The exception: if a parent raises a specific concern during the call that you don't have a good answer for, note it. "That's really helpful to hear — I'm going to think about that" is honest and makes them feel like they're contributing to something. Just don't turn the whole call into a feedback session before they've even started.

---

## Things to remember
- **You're the expert here.** These parents reached out to *you* — they're already interested.
- **Short answers beat long ones.** If you catch yourself explaining for more than 30 seconds, stop and ask a question.
- **It's okay not to know something.** "I'll find out and follow up" is a perfectly good answer.
- **The goal isn't to convince — it's to match.** If Mosaic isn't right for their kid, it's better to know now.
