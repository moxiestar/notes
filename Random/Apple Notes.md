***Basic questions:***
**Tell me about yourself**
I’m a junior at UNC studying psychology and data science. On campus, I’m an undergraduate researcher in two labs - one that focuses on cognitive psychology and one that focuses on behavioral neuroscience. Off campus, I’m a graphic designer, writer, and artist. I hope to pursue a career in neural engineering, with the goal of developing accessible neural implants for patients with psychiatric conditions.

**Tell me about a time you helped someone understand something complicated**
Helping participants understanding the experiment

**How do you handle a difficult situation with a teammate or customer?**
This past spring, I took a class on psychology research, where we worked in groups to design, run, and analyze our own experiments. A project like this involves a lot of time and a lot of effort, and it’s easy to get overwhelmed by the scope. There were four of us in the group, and one of the other students stopped doing his part - he never showed up to class, he would stall on getting any writing done until the last minute, he was rarely available to meet, etc. It was a frustrating situation, and we weren’t sure exactly how best to handle it. We knew we needed to get the project done but we also knew that we couldn’t depend on him. We thought that kicking him out of the group was excessive, so we decided to take the initiative and get the project done ourselves. If we did hear back from him, or if he was available to meet or work with us, we were more than happy to have his help. But we knew that we would have to pull the weight. As we were reaching the end of the project, we discussed the situation with the professor, so that she would be aware of what was going on in case we needed to take further steps, and we did our best to end the project on good terms with him. 

**Describe a time when you had to learn something quickly**
When I first joined the cognitive psychology lab on campus, I started off doing pretty basic tasks — helping with data cleaning or monitoring experiments. My first real project was helping one of my supervisors design a psychology experiment, which involved using a software built on python. At that time, I’d used the software before, but I wasn’t super familiar with it, and I knew next to nothing about python. I don’t think I even knew how to write an if statement. When my supervisor first told me about the project, I didn’t expect it would be too complicated, but when I opened up the software and started looking at the research that had inspired the experiment, I quickly realized I was in over my head. I was overwhelmed, and while I wanted to try to build the experiment on my own, just to see if I could do it, I knew that I would need a lot of help. And thankfully I was able to get it — one of the other students in our lab is an incredibly skilled coder, and she helped me set up a basic framework. Once I had that basic structure in place for the experiment, I progressed a lot more quickly. I collaborated with both of my supervisors, as well as my PI, to fine-tune the design of the experiment, and I used several different AI agents to help refine my code. So, maybe this one block of code doesn’t work, I would have GPT rewrite it, and then I would have Claude rewrite it, and I’d sort of workshop their output until it worked. In the end, after several months of work, I was able to go from an extremely bare bones experiment to a fully-fledged psychological task. One of the biggest takeaways from that experience was what a collaborative effort it was, and I really enjoyed that aspect of it. 

**How would you explain AirPods to someone who doesn’t know what Bluetooth is?**
Bluetooth is a connection that we can’t see, which can make it more difficult to understand how wireless headphones work. I would say that Bluetooth is like a digital wire that links the headphones to the device, whether that’s an iPhone or a MacBook or an iPad. When you connect to Bluetooth, you’re plugging that digital wire in. AirPods are great because they’re so portable and accessible. I carry mine on a keychain that I hook onto my backpack, and they go pretty much everywhere with me. With my AirPods, I can take calls, listen to music, send voice messages to my friends, you name it. 

**Why do you want to work at Apple?**
I’ve been an Apple customer since I was probably nine years old, and in that time, I have never once had even a mediocre experience with Apple. The customer service at Apple is unfailing amazing. Apple employees aren’t just excited about Apple technology, they’re excited about you, about how you’re going to use that technology. And when you have an interaction like that, it makes you more excited about technology. For a lot of people, it also makes technology less scary and less complicated. As someone who’s passionate about making technology more accessible, I’m really eager to foster that excitement in potential customers and users. Technology doesn’t just exist in this academic or scientific vacuum - technology is about its users. And I’m excited about connecting potential users with technology that can enrich their lives. 
Something that I love about Apple is the way it merges design and technology. I think a lot of young engineers or techies underestimate the importance of design, but if a device is attractive, and pleasant and easy to use, people will enjoy using it more than a device that’s poorly designed. And I think Apple is a great example. I have a background in both art and science, and I know not just the importance of both, but the importance of the overlap between them. It’s something I’m really passionate about, and I’m eager to share that passion with others. 

**What is your favorite Apple product?**
My iPhone 15. I love how its functionality overlaps with its design. It’s beautiful, but it isn’t just beautiful. A phone is a tool for communicating with others, but my iPhone is so much more than that. It’s fun, it’s efficient, and every time I open it, I feel like I discover a new feature that I really enjoy.
