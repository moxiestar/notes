Those were long years, and easy ones, that stretched on, golden and grinning, into oblivion.

In childhood, their questions could always be answered. Everything that began came to a neat ending, no loose ties, nothing in the world bigger than their bodies, their apartment. Those days were easy, life was made simple by their wealth and connections.

The consequences would be felt later.

War had lost its glamour here, beyond the reach of red stars and propaganda. They were ashamed, not proud, of their old soldiers, their searing scars. They shrank from those dark faces, pale and purplish, from the blank moroseness in their eyes. The younger men watched their elders terrified, knowing they became more similar with each day that passed, grotesquely evolving into the veterans they had once mocked.

Newspapers were blazoned with the headline “WAR OVER AT LAST’ and subtitled with the less glamorous “Treaty of 1901 signed in Beijing — at least 200 losses for Russian forces.”

Had there ever been a reason? Probably there had, probably they had come up with one. Had I ever looked at him and felt anything other than what I felt for him now: a strange mix of love and terror?

He woke and lay shaking in bed, feeling that the world had ended. He saw the vaulted ceiling of the dormitory, and the moon, filmy through the window, a ghost in the blue of the night sky. And the men and boys asleep all around him, fuckers, he hated their smug smooth faces.

He didn’t know what he felt for him. He knew it was stupid, a waste of time. Maybe it was love. Sensibly he hoped it wasn’t. But then when they touched, there was that feeling. It was overpowering, delicious, and he wanted to go on feeling it for the rest of his life.

The easy smile that played upon his face captivated Sasha.

His silver cigarette case flashed in the lamplight, the initials cut from stars. The flame flickered ominously before the pilot’s face, and kissed for them.

What had he done with my hands, when he had dreamt of making love?

He did once care, he thought, cared about him as a gambler cares about his favorite horse, not love but anxiety, a feeling of fear always biting at the back of his mind. After years he had the sense of being strangled by his desire to be loved and understood.

They looked at each other for a long time but said nothing. Sasha saw the pity and the anger in his eyes, and both mortified him. Finally the man left.

At last, somewhere in the nether region between eleven-fifteen and eleven-thirty, we departed.

Outside the cathedral a congregation of guests had descended upon the street like an immense flock of tiny birds.

It fell like a dead bird into an emerald sea, flashed, and was gone forever.

Sitting on the end of my bed, he unpinned the medal from my fencing uniform and laid it on the blanket. He was struck by how greenish and dull the gold looked in the dark room. It was so goddamn ridiculous. He held it up, tried to see if it would catch any of the wan evening light that was trickling in through the windows, but it didn’t. It had all been for nothing.

King and country, glittering at the head of the parade, continued on past the crowds.

A gray dampness permeated the world, relentless, unceasing, yet gentle. Suddenly understanding why everything had happened the way it had, the pilot brought his face to his own and kissed his lips.

A beam of light flooded the compartment, and the camera flashed. The moment was over. The rays grew mottled, then faded altogether, and they went on, as the landscape danced in the windows and the track slowly split the country in two.
