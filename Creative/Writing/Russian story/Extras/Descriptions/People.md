He smiled: a proud curving of the lips, a little folding of the eyes. He always smiled in the same way, his eyebrows tilting, as if he knew something Sasha didn’t.

They were not good children, but they had good hearts and better smiles. They were denied nothing: someone always made sure they got their way.

It was because of that that she got engaged so young, hardly sixteen, because she saw love in everything. That was what her brother said.

They’d seen him do worse with less provocation.

He was short but carried himself like a sergeant major, and his voice had the roar of a sleep-deprived hound.

His father, who knew all about what it meant to be a man, said no son of his would dance.

Her eyes were gray and childish, but in them was the smile of a much older woman: the look of a caged bird, the flitting happiness and inevitable collapse.

Then, waving the match in her thin fingers, she unwound her shawl, and dropped it on the floor, where it lay, rusty red and half-unraveled, like a bloodstain.

Her hair — faded gold, like a piece of nice jewelry that had been left out in the rain — uncoiled in awkward swaths. The hairpins fell to the floor one by one; the tarnished locks were spread clumsily over her sharp shoulders.

Opposite Sasha, engrossed in his work, was the Major-General. A perfect military man, with prominent features and a jacket was decorated with a score of medals. His hair, once dark, was tending towards grey. But his eyes, small and kind, shone with too much happiness to be frightening.

Suspicion rested in his face, creased his brow, and made his eyes dull to the light sifting through the windows. He drummed his fingers on the desk and drew a long, contemplative breath.

The carriage passed an alleyway, and the beam of a streetlamp shot through the window. The light hit his face like a wave from an electric ocean. His cheeks were red, cold. When he exhaled, his breath curled velvety before him.

A butler answered when he knocked, a black-haired man who struck him as suspiciously young and good-looking.

He knew it was not her fault, that the sister was often irrational and strange. She had always been like that — it had cropped up at intervals throughout her life like a congenital disease. There was something in her commanding black stare that sometimes flinched.

Through her veil, all that was visible was the creeping flush on her cheeks, a crimson that could’ve easily been mistaken for the flush of happiness. Their father was not discerning, and did not know that when the sister smiled she meant to scream.

The Captain was an immediate disappointment, a slight and unassuming man. Even the sister, tall though she was, was forced to look down her nose at him.

There was a blandness about him that was almost nauseating.

She had once, they all knew, been famously beautiful. Now the twinkle in her gray eyes, the tinge of crimson on her cheeks, and the unerring line of her straight nose were all that remained of her beauty.

In her face, if he looked at the right moment, he could catch a resentment so deep it was electrifying.

His eyes shone; his skin, fair and radiant, beckoned the pilot like a prayer. He watched as Peter reloaded his gun: the steadiness of his hands, the calm gaze. The birds hung heavy, pathetic and matted with blood.

She was a good, innocent girl, but there was something about her innocence that struck him as dangerous.
