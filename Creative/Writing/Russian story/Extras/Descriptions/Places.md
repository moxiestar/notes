It was neither pale nor bright but had a constant glow of late-afternoon yellow sun. In all seasons the house looked the same, a manmade evergreen.

The beds were small, in rows as regular as graves.

He remembered watching the day fade into evening, the sun dying over the flight of green fields.

It was summer, and the world was flying close to the sun.

In barracks, small and inadequate, prints and diagrams hung on the white walls. Thin carpets interrupted the course of the scuffed wooden floor. Several windows, always latched, offered a view to the courtyard.

When the curtain had fallen he said he was going to Nevsky, where pleasure was cheaper.

The night was enveloping, the stars hopeful in the dark. An autumn fog had descended over the city. He could barely make out the street lights at the intersection. The rest was lost in murky gloom.

The long march across the courtyard was sacred in the dark, the trees weighed down by their leaves, bent in sleep. The sky, dusty with clouds, arching above the school. The crescent moon holding the city under its sway.

Later that day, caught in Major-General Radetsky’s office, Sasha gazed around the room, too comfortable in his stiff chair. Dark and overly cluttered, high walls inching in on the room as if to swallow it. And everywhere, books, more books than the room could hold, more books, Sasha thought, than one man could possibly read.

The embankment was cold and rough beneath their fingers. The breeze swept up at them from across the silver waves. On the other side, a copse of oak trees stood by a monument, more ancient and formidable than the city itself.

The sun glittered like so many gemstones on the water’s surface, and the afternoon loomed: sweet, yellowish, and spoiled.

The first snow of the year was always the best. The cheer in the streets, the bright layer of clouds, the blanket of white, the blinding quiet in the early mornings.

When he tried to imagine returning to the estate, he saw an abandoned house, no people, no noises. All the trees and gardens were dead. The cheerful place full of laughing guests and liveried servants existed only in his memory: it died with his mother, lay rotting in a barren grave.

Above the glowing fireplace, Golitsyn’s portrait hung like a monument: he stood in tweed and plus fours, flanked by two spaniels, holding a shotgun. The artist had captured the auburn hair, the broad chest, the hooded eyes, the arrogant smirk. Bonaparte crossing the Alps could not have produced a more entrancing effect.

The buildings stood like unearthly trees on a mattress of black. In the darkness all was one.

Rows of brightly-colored buildings, little pastries lined up in a cardboard box, ran briskly by.

They rode for thirty minutes, while the evening bled into night before them. The copse showed in the distance, nearer and nearer. Soon they dismounted and tied up their horses. Now the world had turned lilac. The clouds that gathered at the horizon glowed golden and rich, set on fire by the dying sun.

The river parted around them, glittering between green and blue.

The fireworks began, red and blue and white, biting into the gentle air, scattering themselves throughout the sky like seeds in the flesh of a dark fruit.
