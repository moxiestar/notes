II

All boarding schools are the same: miserable places with uncomfortable beds and heartless instructors. My time at the Corps des Pages taught me a great deal, and the things that seemed so terrible to me there no longer fazed me in the least: I got used to the discipline, the crude jokes, and the disappointing meals years ago. The vindictiveness of the staff and students was the only thing that really dismayed me. For all the years I had spent in my father’s house and at the Corps, I had never learned to be unbothered by cruelty — I had only learned to expect it.

St. Petersburg was an old city, and its leaders were cheap. The Emperor had no money to build state-of-the-art schoolhouses; at this rate he hardly had enough money to keep the country running. The schools, then, were housed in the old Rastrelli palaces left vacant by their banished owners.

Those palaces were like shells, the Guards School in particular. It looked, plainly put, like a prison. The only remnant of its old wealth was the ballroom, now an armory. It was easy to imagine fashionable couples sweeping across the inlaid floors under the frescoed ceiling, but instead of dancers there was an extensive collection of weapons and firearms. The building was a patchwork of the military and the aristocracy; nothing was either out of place or at home. The dining hall, the largest room the school, was a ghost of a room, even at mealtime. A black and white tile floor mirrored a vaulted ceiling; twenty-odd wooden tables were neatly arranged beneath a set of dull chandeliers. The classrooms were just as ghostly; they would’ve been almost pleasant if it weren’t for the piercing stare of the teacher presiding at the blackboard. The dormitory was designed solely for practicality: bland, blank, cavernous. The windows were small and dusty, as though embarrassed of the meager views they hold. The beds were small, in rows as regular as graves. The portraits of the Emperor and Empress that hung on the back wall were incidental. They did not inspire any noble, patriotic feelings; instead, they were merely amusing decorations.The second-years were luckier: their dormitory was at least brighter, with a whole row of windows overlooking the courtyard. But the beds and portraits were the same.

I longed for the view from my room back at home. I remembered watching the day fade into evening, the sun dying over the flight of green fields, and I grew homesick. Autumn was fast approaching, but for a few more weeks summer would burn out in its glorious twilight. I felt as if I was blind to it. The season would pass without affecting me.

Our classes were exacting; the work was incessant. The curriculum included almost everything under the sun: mathematics, science, literature, German, French, Russian, music, art, history, horsemanship, fencing… the list went on, and perhaps never ended. By the time we graduated we were supposed to have mastered every single subject. As of yet I’d learned nothing, and mastered even less.

Ilyusha, one of my friends at the Corps des Pages, was there too, in all his curly-haired splendor. He was quick to make friends, and introduced me to two of his: Misha and Vitya, both younger and more hot-headed than us.

‘Prince Alexis,’ Misha remarked, the first time we met. ‘I thought you were too rich to go to school.’

‘Alyosha thinks so too,’ Ilyusha put in. ‘Don’t encourage him.’

For Ilyusha and Vitya it was all just sport, but Misha really disliked me as much as he said. The Colonel — the secretary to the headmaster — hated me too; I had poked fun at him after one of his outbursts.

‘You’re all embarrassments!’ he was saying, his face growing redder and redder. ‘What would Major-General Radetsky say if he saw you in this state?’

‘I imagine he’d have something to say if he saw *you* in *that* state,’ I muttered.

He straightened himself up to his full five-foot-five, his grey mustache bristling, his pince-nez nearly slipping off his nose. Then, snatching a book from a nearby table, he struck me across the face. It hurt like hell, and I’d seen him do worse with less provocation.

I tried to look on the bright side. At the Corps des Pages I had to endure four years; here it was only two. But I would’ve done anything to go home.

Yet at the same time the thought of going home scared me: even though I knew my mother and father and sister were good people, I couldn’t help feeling that, now that I’d gotten away from them, I’d realize all their faults, and become reluctant to ever go back.

At the Corps des Pages my strength lay in planning. History bored me, dancing drove me mad, and I was useless with mathematics. But planning, fortification, maneuvers — these I could understand. To me they were an art, a puzzle to unfold, a kind of strange origami. War, when laid out on a charming tricolored map, was little more than a game. Father had centered all my childhood games on war; by the time I was thirteen, war was like chess to me. The Corps did nothing to dispel my illusions, and it was not until last year that I truly understood the terror of war.

A cavalry officer, an old friend of my mother’s, had come back from the insurrection in China with a searing scar across his cheek. His whole face was colored, pale and purplish, and there was a deep, blank moroseness in his eyes. When he walked across the room he moved slowly, as if it pained him. I watched him terrified, knowing that someday I would have the same sad stare, the same limp.

The rebellion was over; it ended last week. Every newspaper was blazoned with the headline ‘WAR OVER AT LAST’ and subtitled with the less glamorous ‘*Treaty of 1901 signed in Beijing — at least 200 losses for Russian forces.*’ They said there would be another war soon, with Japan: a war no one wanted for a country we had never cared about. Yesterday there was an immense protest on Nevsky Prospect: workers, students, socialists, and all the rest. The protests had become almost a weekly occurrence; down every side street was the rustle of revolutionary pamphlets. People talked of Lenin, of Marx, of someone called Kerensky. In Petersburg no one spoke of the Emperor except to disapprove. What a terrible time to be alive.

Of course life went on, whether we liked it or not. We had our first fencing lessons today. Sports like fencing must have been invented to make you feel like you can’t do anything right. Try as I might, I couldn’t make any sense of the technique: all I knew what that you had to do it right, and quickly.

Our teacher, meanwhile, seemed to expect us to be winning tournaments within the year. His name was Shuvalov; he was short but carried himself like a sergeant major, and had a voice like a sleep-deprived hound. As for the classroom, it was small and rather inadequate. Prints and diagrams hung on the white walls; thin pistes interrupted the course of the scuffed wooden floor. Several windows — always latched — offered a view to the courtyard.

There were fencing lessons at the Corps, but they were perfunctory and we never took them seriously. This was entirely different. Shuvalov was not to be laughed at. The lesson began with a demonstration: two of the second-years fought a match in the center of the room. Their movements were so quick that they blurred together: a streak of white striking another streak of white, and then drawing suddenly back, and then lunging forward again. I was entranced, but understood nothing. When the match was over, they took off their masks and looked around with an air of superiority. The boy on the right had won; he was glowing with victory. With a handshake, they went off to their next class.

‘Now,’ Shuvalov began. ‘You will all take your places…’

Épée in hand, I became more confident. As Shuvalov gave his commands, I listened closely, and planned each move, every parry. Being paired with Ilyusha meant that all this planning was completely unnecessary: the only thing Ilyusha worked hard at was flirting with courtesans.

When Shuvalov cried out for us to advance, Ilyusha laughed, a silly laugh. I knew I would win.

One night last week Ilyusha brought me to a performance of *Giselle*.

As a child I loved ballet. My sister had dancing lessons, as was the custom, and I watched enviously from the hall as she twirled in pink tulle. Katya, two years younger than me, was always better at everything: manners, lessons, charming smiles. When we were little we competed to see who would win, but I gave up before I was twelve. The old jealousy came back as I spied on my sister’s pirouettes, and I begged my parents to let me have lessons. But Father, who knew all about what it meant to be a man, said no son of his would dance.

The performance was bland. Ilyusha went for the girls; I went for the memories. When the curtain had fallen he said he was going to Nevsky Prospect, where pleasure was cheaper. It was always wild there: men in top hats with tortoiseshell canes, civil servants with their double-breasted black uniforms half unbuttoned, hussars too drunk to speak, street-sellers hawking their various wares.

What Ilyusha wanted, he found in a small, dark storefront with a rotting sign.

‘Coming with?’ he beckoned, lingering on the step.

‘No, I’d rather not,’ I said. I wanted to walk, to look at the stars, always more beautiful when you saw them alone.

‘Typical,’ he said. ‘Well, goodnight.’

Blushing, I followed him inside. I knew what he would tell the others if I left.

The rooms were impenetrably dark, the walls papered with flaking stripes and the floor carpeted with a bleak, Turkish pattern. It was the sort of place mothers warned their naïve young daughters about. All around the parlor, men sat on great, dirty chairs with garish girls in their laps. The laughter that filled the room was sickening.

By the time I looked around, Ilyusha had already disappeared. From a corner, a light-haired girl in red came toward me; as she grew closer, the lamplight cast a greenish, unnatural glow over her face. Her eyes were grey and childish, but in them was the smile of a much older woman.

‘You want to be with me?’ she asked, in a sing-song voice.

‘I don’t know,’ I stammered. ‘Yes, I suppose so.’

She laughed. ‘Come with me.’

I followed her down a hall and into a small, dark room, the grimmest place I’d ever seen: nothing but a bed, a table, and an unlit candle. Silent, she bent over the table, took a box of matches from some hidden drawer, and lit the wick. Then, waving the match in her thin fingers, she unwound her shawl, and dropped it on the floor. It lay there, rusty red and half-unraveled, like a bloodstain. I reached to pick it up, but she brushed me aside, laughing.

‘Don’t bother.’ Smiling now, she settled herself on the rumpled duvet. ‘I’ve never seen you here before.’

‘No.’

‘You don’t talk much, do you?’

I shrugged.

‘Well?’

‘I don’t know what to say.’

‘That’s better than nothing. Come here.’

As I approached, she reached up to unpin her coiffure. Her hair — faded gold, like a piece of nice jewelry that had been left out in the rain — uncoiled in awkward swaths. The hairpins fell to the floor one by one; the tarnished locks were spread clumsily over her sharp shoulders. It ruined her air of beauty.

‘How old are you?’ I asked, standing before her.

‘Does it matter?’ She stood up, giggling, her dainty hands fumbling with the hooks on my jacket-collar. ‘You’re here now. No one will ever know what happens between us.’

‘Tell me how old you are.’

‘I’m eighteen.’

‘No, you aren’t,’ I said, taking her face in my hands. ‘Tell me the truth.’

She looked down. ‘I turned fifteen last month.’

‘How long have you been here?’ I asked.

‘What’s with you? It doesn’t matter.’ Still looking at me, she sat back down on the bed and began to unbutton her skirt. Against the cheap red silk, her skin looked chalky and pallid. As she tossed her blouse to the floor, I was horrified by the smallness of her hands — the hands of a young child.

‘What is it now?’

I shook my head.

‘You might as well leave if you don’t want to be with me.’

In her face I saw a desperate anger.

‘No,’ I said. ‘I’m tired, that’s all.’

She stood up and came close to me. I made myself touch her waist, caress her skin, press her to me. I closed my eyes, and felt the electric softness of her skin as she put her arms around me, kissing my face. Her lips, which had looked so strangely bright in the parlor, were gentle and cold: the touch of a corpse.

The room seemed to close in. I ought to enjoy this, I thought, I ought to be delighted that she was undressed in my arms. I brought my hand to stroke her hair, and felt the dimples in her cheeks and the soft curves of her breasts. Through the door I heard someone laughing. Far off, in another room, one couple was loudly making love.

I drew back. This time she smiled.

‘You’re funny,’ she said. ‘Maybe next time you’ll bring me a rosary. Aren’t you going to do anything?’

‘What’s your name?’

‘Klara.’

‘Who brought you here?’

She rolled her eyes and sank onto the bed. ‘Oh, now it’s time for a philosophy lecture. Spare me your advice, I’ve heard it all. I know, someday I’ll have to atone for my sins. I’ll have plenty of time to atone later. Come here.’

I sat by her side.

‘You’re awfully handsome for a brooding type,’ she said, touching my cheek.

‘I wish I could do something to help you — send you to school or something like that.’

‘Don’t try to help me. When has school ever helped anyone? If you sent me to school, I’d kill myself.’

‘I know the feeling.’

‘You’re at a military school, aren’t you?’

I nodded.

‘Well, I’ll look for you in the obituaries.’

‘Thank you,’ I said, laughing.

She laughed too. Putting her arms around my neck, she leaned in to kiss me.

I shied away. ‘Klara, I have to go.’

‘Why?’

‘I can’t,’ I stammered. ‘Here.’ Hands shaking, I held out a hundred-ruble note.

She gaped, then took the note and gazed at it as if it was a relic.

‘You’re mad,’ she said. ‘You aren’t going to leave, are you?’

‘I can’t stay, I’m sorry. Goodnight.’

Guilt and horror consumed me — I felt like a murderer. I left, trying to hide myself as I went down the stairs and out the door into the dark of night.

The night was enveloping, the stars hopeful in the dark. An autumn fog had descended over the city; ten meters ahead was as far as I could see. The rest was lost in the murky gloom of the wasted evening. Nevsky was spread out before me, all overcoats and troikas. It was beautiful: a world completely foreign, one to which I would never belong. I remembered Klara’s naked body and dimpled smile, and felt ill.

In ten minutes’ time I was back at school. The long march across the courtyard seemed sacred in the dark, the trees weighed down by their leaves, bent in sleep, the sky, dusty with clouds, arching above the school. The crescent moon held the city under its sway.

The gardens always seemed magical to me, particularly so in the moonlight, where everything was cloaked in deep blue. It was enchanting there, an expanse of rich ultramarine, the radiant moon surrounded by a swath of white clouds, and a copse of oak trees on either side, circling it like a frame. The minutes passed, and my guilt with them. Guilt is heavier by night, but somehow less discernible. Any names I might put to my guilt would fade away with the coming of morning, ridiculous and dreamlike, no more tangible than a vision from a nightmare.

An hour had passed without my realizing it. I was still sitting by the walls of the palace when Ilyusha returned.

‘What on earth are you standing out here for?’ he asked, laughing. He had missed a button on his overcoat, and I stared at it, then suddenly remembered my jacket-collar, and hurriedly did the hooks back up.

‘It’s a nice night,’ I tried.

His laugh pierced the air. ‘I knew you were going after another girl. Ran off, did she?’ With the experienced lover’s convivial grin, he shook his head. ‘Alyosha, what I can’t understand is how, for all your good looks and your haughty pretenses, you’re so unlucky in love.’

‘I can’t understand it either,’ I said.

We went in.

On Mondays I had longs breaks between my classes; after lunch today I spent almost an hour in the library, browsing the shelves. When I was younger I read all the time, but at the Corps the library was full of textbooks, and reading became a chore. Sometimes my grandmother would give me books, and I’d sneak them in under my coat, but the older boys usually found them and kept them for themselves. Now, with a real library at my disposal, I had begun to read again.

I had just swiped a copy of *The Eternal Husband*, and as I walked toward the door I started to read the first page. Engrossed, I reached confidently for the handle, and ran straight into one of the second-years.

We both drew back. He was tall and fair-haired, with an intensely serious face and piercing blue eyes. But for all his staring, he said nothing.

I dropped the book and saluted. ‘Excuse me. I’m terribly sorry.’

He waved my hand down.

‘Don’t tell the Colonel,’ I pleaded.

The boy laughed. ‘Tell the Colonel? Why would I?’ He spoke with a strong, lilting accent: German, I thought. ‘It’s not illegal to read, is it?’

‘No, but — well, you know how it is.’

‘Yes, I know. Here.’ He knelt down and picked up my book.

‘Thanks. Awfully kind of you.’

‘Not at all. I come here to read too, sometimes, but mostly I’m too busy.’

‘So am I. Thanks for not ratting me out.’

He frowned. ‘For what?’

‘You know — not telling on me. Thank you.’ I waited a moment, then held out my hand. ‘I’m Alexis.’

‘I know who you are.’

‘Right,’ I stammered. ‘Well, I’ve got to go to class. See you around.’

Later that day, caught in Major-General Radetsky’s office, I fidgeted in my chair and gazed around the room. Dark and overly cluttered, it seemed as if the walls were closing in on me. There were more books than there was space in the room: textbooks, novels, collections of poetry — more books, it seemed, than one man could possibly read.

Opposite me, engrossed in his work, was the Major-General. He was tall and dark-haired, with prominent features and a greying beard. His jacket was decorated with a score of medals. But his eyes, small and kind, robbed him of all his consequence.

‘Well,’ he began, sighing incidentally, ‘what do you have to say for yourself?’

‘It wasn’t my fault,’ I said. ‘I didn’t do anything. It was a misunderstanding.’

‘A misunderstanding?’

‘Yes, Your Excellency. I swear, I didn’t do anything.’

‘I’m delighted to hear it. You had an excuse, then, for being out past ten o’clock?’

‘I was at a concert, Your Excellency.’

‘A concert?’ he repeated, almost smiling. ‘And it lasted till midnight?’

‘No, Your Excellency. It ended at nine-thirty, and I would’ve come back straight away, but the woman sitting next to me fainted, and I thought it would be ungentlemanly to leave her when she was unwell. So I called a carriage and I brought her to the doctor. But when I got to the doctor —’

The Major-General held up his hand. ‘All right, spare me. So, you helped this sickly woman, and by the time you were finished… *helping* her, the clock had struck twelve.’ He took off his glasses and set them gingerly on the desk. ‘Remind me, when is lights off?’

‘Ten o’clock, Your Excellency.’

‘Right,’ he said. ‘And you came back two hours after that, which would, if I’m not mistaken, make you late for bed.’

‘Yes, Your Excellency.’

‘I’m glad we got that sorted out. A hundred verses and you’ll never hear about it again.’

‘Yes, Your Excellency.’

His expression softened. Twitching, he broke into a laugh. ‘You can’t have it both ways, Prince Alexis. If you behave poorly, you have to face the consequences.’

‘I didn’t behave poorly, I was —’

‘Helping a woman who fainted, I know. Pardon me, Prince, but if you want to convince me you’re innocent, you could at least put some effort into it.’

‘But it’s true,’ I cried.

He made a face. ‘In any case, your behavior this semester has been shockingly bad.’

‘Yes, Your Excellency,’ I said, gritting my teeth.

‘Don’t you enjoy your classes?’

I hesitated. ‘It’s not that. I mean…’

‘You mean?’ he prompted, clearly enjoying himself. ‘You mean what? Perhaps you think you’re above hard work and discipline — is that it?’

‘No, of course not, Your Excellency.’

‘The kind of flippancy that may have gotten you far at the Corps des Pages will do nothing for you here. The Guards School is built on camaraderie. Camaraderie doesn’t seem to be your speciality. Take a bit of advice from me, if you will, Prince Alexis: if you think yourself above hard work and dedication, you’ll never get anywhere, in the military or in life.’

‘Yes, Your Excellency.’

‘Well?’

‘Excuse me, I’m not sure what to say.’

‘Wonderful. You can follow my advice in silence.’

‘I wanted a commission,’ I admitted. ‘I don’t care for higher education.’

The Major-General chuckled. ‘Yes, I’m beginning to see that.’

‘Pardon me, Your Excellency.’

‘That’s all right. You’re dismissed. Try not to get into any more trouble for the rest of the week.’

‘I’ll do my best, Your Excellency,’ I quipped.

‘Don’t lie. Dismissed.’

His criticisms didn’t bother me: it was the fact that I’d gotten caught. At the Corps I had made an art out of lying, but in the past month I’d lost my talent. I made idiotic blunders while sneaking out or told ludicrous stories to cover myself up. As a youth I went four years without anyone catching me: now I felt sure someone would catch me before the end of the term.

‘It’s funny, you know,’ I mused. ‘I feel like I know you very well, but I don’t even know your name.’

Glancing up from the sparkling depths of the river below, the second-year boy frowned. ‘You don’t know me?’

‘No. Should I?’

He laughed. ‘That wasn’t what I meant.’

‘No, I understand. Believe me, I’m used to it. It’s horrible, then the whole world knows your face and your family history. I’m sure you’ve heard all kinds of awful things about me.’

He hesitated. ‘I have heard that your father is very rich, that your mother is a famous beauty, that you have estates all over Europe, and that — I think — you have a sister, don’t you?’

‘Yes, that’s right.’

‘And she is engaged, yes?’

‘Yes, to Prince Albert Ernst of Battenberg,’ I said, rolling out the name. ‘I can’t tell whether she’s happy about it or not.’

‘Why not? Is there something wrong with him?’

‘There’s nothing wrong with him exactly, but he’s one of the dullest people I’ve ever met. Are you engaged?’

‘No, but my older brother. Alexandrine of Mecklenburg — a German, like your sister’s fiancé. The rest of my siblings are not yet engaged.’

‘The rest?’ I prompted.

‘Three brothers, and four sisters.’ He pronounced the word three as if it was spelled without an *h*. His accent was beautiful: a dipping here and there, a softness at the beginning of a word, an emphasis on a strange part of a phrase. ‘There’s eight of us, all together.’

‘You have seven siblings?’

‘Seven too many.’

Hesitating, I turned back to the river. The embankment was cold and rough beneath my fingers, and the breeze swept up at us from across the silver waves. On the other side, a copse of oak trees stood by a monument, more ancient and formidable than the city itself.

‘Who are you, anyway?’ I said. ‘You’re German, aren’t you?’

‘No, Danish. My father’s the Crown Prince of Denmark.’

I whistled. ‘You’re King Christian’s grandson?’

‘Yes.’

‘Then what are you doing here?’

‘It’s a long story.’

‘At least tell me your name.’

He smiled. ‘It’s Peter. And you? Why are you here?’

‘Because he went here, when he was young, and he apparently wants me to suffer through the same experience,’ I drawled.

‘Don’t you want to serve in the army someday?’

‘I used to. I don’t know. I’m not very good at taking orders.’

‘So I’ve heard.’

‘What do you mean, so you’ve heard?’

Flashing a smile, he shrugged. ‘One of my classmates said that you were in trouble with Major-General Radetsky. They say you like to go out late at night.’

‘For the last time, I was at a concert. Plenty of the older boys sneak out — why can’t I?’

‘Oh, you’d be a terrible soldier,’ he laughed.

I laughed too, in spite of myself. ‘Shut up.’

‘Don’t get on Radetsky’s bad side. He’s the sort of man you would want on your side, not against you, if you got into trouble. They say he is one of the best generals in the whole Russian army.’

‘Well, there’s not much competition — our army’s in shambles.’

‘You’re not very patriotic.’

His face, when serious, was piercing and regal. Embarrassed, I looked away.

‘I don’t really like it here,’ I admitted.

‘That’s all right. It’s a strange place. I have been here for longer than a year now, but I still do not understand this country at all. My aunt Dagmar told me that Russia was just like any other place in Europe, only grander and stranger. All the magazines at home say that Russia is savage and wild, that there are polar bears running loose everywhere, that there are little peasant girls in red dancing on the street corners, that every man is as devout as a monk.’

‘I don’t believe in God,’ I broke in.

‘You don’t?’

‘No.’

‘What do you believe in, then?’ he asked. ‘You must believe in something.’

‘I don’t know. I don’t think anyone has to believe in anything.’

‘Don’t you?’ From inside his jacket, he brought out a cigarette case, engraved with the Danish coat of arms, and lit one. ‘You do smoke, don’t you? Even if you are a nihilist.’

‘Yes, of course,’ I said, and took the cigarette that he offered me. ‘And I’m not a nihilist. I don’t believe in God, that’s all.’

‘But you must believe in something,’ he insisted.

‘But I don’t. What does it matter? Not everyone here is Christian. Orthodoxy isn’t ubiquitous, you know. I don’t know what your aunt told you, but we aren’t all stereotypes from magazines.’

He faced me with that serious look. There was something intensely kind in his expression; his eyes smiled even when he didn’t.

‘You can teach me, then,’ he finally said.

I laughed. ‘Teach you what?’

‘Teach me what Russia is really like.’

‘I hardly know. I’m not sure anyone knows.’

He took a drag from his cigarette and exhaled a frigid cloud of smoke. ‘My aunt knows.’

‘Your aunt’s the Empress Dowager, isn’t she?’

‘Yes.’

‘I’ve met her.’

‘Have you? She’s lovely, isn’t she?’ Again there was that gentle joy in his eyes. ‘She and I have always always gotten along. She was the one who arranged for me to study here in Russia. Before this I was at a military school in Denmark. You were at a military school before this too, weren’t you?’

My eyes drifted back to the river. The sun glittered like so many gemstones on the water’s surface, and the afternoon loomed, sweet, yellowish, and spoiled.

‘Yes, I was at the Corps des Pages,’ I said. ‘But I just graduated this spring — I’m only seventeen.’

‘I thought you were older. I just turned nineteen.’

‘Many happy returns.’

He grinned. ‘I thought you were older because — well, it’s silly. You seem to me like the sort of person who would have aspirations of being a great general. Or a great something.’

‘Do I? I’m not pompous, am I?’

‘No, but you — I don’t know. It’s something about your face. I have the feeling that you are very brave.’

‘Not at all,’ I scoffed. ‘I’m the worst coward of them all.’

‘See, I told you it was silly. Oh, the bell — I have to go. I’ll see you tomorrow.’

‘Yes, see you.’

I watched him go, picturing his face. When he looked back, grinning, I smiled too.Shuvalov, standing like a gargoyle at the front of the class, sighed deeply. ‘Normally, most first years have had their first matches by now, but you’re all so hopeless that you might as well go back home to your nursery rhymes.’ It was not his first condescending lecture about our ineptitude. ‘Probably,’ he went on, with his usual frankness, ‘you’ll all make utter fools of yourselves.’

I clenched my fist around the cold handle of my épée. I’d never met my opponent before, but I knew his face; he was one of the oldest and clumsiest in our year, and his form was terrible. My victory was perfunctory.

It was the next match that was the real test: my opponent was quicker than me, and I had to struggle just to get a single point.

‘Footwork!’ Shuvalov yelled, frowning at us. ‘Footwork, Voronetsky! You look like an old woman trying to walk up a flight of stairs.’

Offended, I did my best to lunge a little more deftly.

‘Yes, that’s better,’ he said, sounding unimpressed. ‘Faster, Kadinsky! That was clumsy.’

My opponent’s smug smirk faded. While he was distracted, I made an attack; he was too slow in parrying, and I got a point. After that it was easy to win. He had lost his confidence, and became shaky and almost pathetic. Shuvalov seemed pleased, though he gave me a lecture about my footwork. Footwork is Shuvalov’s great creed in life. The older boys, who sometimes practice with us, say that Shuvalov criticizes them incessantly for their footwork. They have drills on footwork once a week, and they complain about those drills on all six of the remaining days.

In the stairwell after class, Ilyusha caught up to me, tugging on his jacket as he ran. I waited, trying to guess what his next outlandish remark would be.

‘Pondering the nature of love?’ he asked, as if on cue.

I hesitated. ‘Not really.’

‘Well, I am. I want your advice. I’m not sure Tonechka is so good for me.’

‘Who?’

‘You remember — Tonechka.’

I searched my memory, and found nothing of use. ‘I’m sorry, I…’

‘The girl I met after we went to see *Swan Lake*,’ he explained. ‘You’re not very sentimental, you know, Alyosha. You southerners are supposed to be the romantic ones. But you aren’t romantic at all. Unless you were born in Petersburg. Were you?’

‘No, I was born at my father’s estate in Moscow, in eighteen-eighty-four. What’s the matter with this girl of yours?’

‘Nothing’s the matter with her, exactly. I just don’t like her as much as I used to.’

‘Why not?’

He shrugged. ‘I’m not sure. Love’s so fickle, you know.’

‘Fickle?’ I repeated. ‘That’s the last thing I’d say about love.’

‘Perhaps it’s because you’ve never been in love.’

I managed a laugh.

On my left was a window; I turned over my shoulder and gazed down at the courtyard, where the second-years were practicing their drills in the wind, amidst torrents of falling leaves. Autumn had set in.

‘Well, I’m not as lucky as you,’ I said.

‘You’ll get there. Anyway, what should I do about Tonechka?’

‘I’m really not sure. Like you said, I’ve never been in love — how should I know?’

Ilyusha shrugged. ‘Didn’t mean to offend you.’

‘You didn’t. Are you in love with her?’

‘I suppose,’ he said.

‘Do you think about her at night, before you fall asleep?’

He grinned. ‘Well, when you say it like that…’

‘I mean, do you care about her?’

‘Care?’ he repeated. ‘She’s a prostitute, Alyosha.’

I smiled. ‘Then you don’t love her.’ Tipping my cap, I ran ahead down the hall.

Losing to Peter was truly humiliating. I knew, of course, that he was a better fencer than me, but I didn’t know that I was so totally useless.

‘Voronetsky,’ Shuvalov bellowed near the end of class. ‘Give us a good fight.’

Surprised, I looked up and fumbled for my épée. There were hardly ten minutes left till the break, and I’d assumed I wouldn’t have to fight a match.

‘Prince Peter,’ he hollered. ‘Yes, you. Come here.’

My heart sank. I glanced over at Ilyusha, sitting on the sidelines with a book, and made a face. Grimacing, he shrugged.

The calm smile on Peter’s face terrified me — thin, flickering lips curved in a pink crescent. He was laughing at me.

I walked up to the piste, heart pounding. We saluted each other, put on our masks, and waited.

‘*En garde!’* Shuvalov called out. ‘*Prêts? Allez!*’

It was as if he had won before the fight even began. Flustered, I did my best to parry his attacks, but he warded me off, and, striking my épée with his, seized his new opening and nicked my shoulder.

‘When are you going to go after him, Voronetsky?’ Shuvalov leered. ‘Now or next year?’

Looking away, Peter suppressed a laugh. I lunged forward and tried to get his arm, but he stepped aside and I nearly lost my balance. As I stumbled back to my feet, I felt his épée graze my shirt. By then I was angry. I forgot my training and swatted savagely at him, but Peter, calm as ever, dodged each of my attacks.

‘Why don’t we just call it off now?’ Shuvalov taunted.

No one likes to be patronized, but I knew he was right. With every one of my attacks, Peter’s épée would come down triumphant and I would be forced to retreat, parrying desperately. Furious, I advanced, but Peter parried, skirted my épée, and got me on the arm.

Around that time I gave up. For the first half I put up a fight, but as I watched him score his third point, all my determination left me. I knew I would lose, and there was nothing I could do about it except seethe and stagger idiotically around the piste.

While I was fuming, he got his fourth point. Then, with that same calm smile, he lunged forward and struck my chest. It was over before I could even think of parrying.

‘You bastard,’ I cried.

‘Shut your mouth, Voronetsky,’ Shuvalov snapped.

I dropped my épée and stared down at the floor.

‘What’s the matter, you aren’t used to losing?’

I rolled my eyes.

‘Well, get used to it,’ he hissed. ‘With that attitude you’ll be lucky if you ever win another match. What are you standing there for? Sit down. And pick up your sword.’

My cheeks burned. Humiliated, I snatched my épée up and threw myself on the bench. The shame I felt was almost suffocating. I hid my face in my hands and tried to shut it all out.

After class I stalked the halls, glaring bitterly at everyone I passed. Finally, exhausted, I leant on a window ledge and put my hand up on the cold glass.

A minute passed. Peter came up to me, walking slowly. I knew it was him, even without looking up, because he hesitated before he spoke, as if trying to find the right words.

‘I didn’t mean to offend you,’ he said.

‘Just leave me alone,’ I groaned.

When I looked up and saw the expression on his face, cold and drawn, I softened.

‘Sorry. Look, it’s been a long day. And I… I didn’t mean to yell at you back there, but it was infuriating, you beating me like it was nothing. I’m the best fencer in my year, you know.’

‘I never said you weren’t.’ He held out his hand. ‘Truce?’

‘All right.’

‘I’ll practice with you sometime, if you like.’

‘I don’t need your help.’

He sighed.

‘I know you mean well, but your everlasting natural talent gets a little old now and then.’

‘Everlasting natural talent?’ he repeated, mocking my tone.

‘Everything you do, you’re good at. Just like my sister. I’ve spent my entire life hearing my father boast about her. I’ve had to work to get here.’

‘You’ve had to work?’ he scoffed. ‘Your father’s the richest man in the country — I don’t think you’ve ever had to work a day in your life.’

I lunged for him, but he stopped me.

‘Enough of this, it’s stupid,’ he said. ‘You’re a very good fencer.’

‘Don’t pity me.’

‘I’m not pitying you.’

‘But —’

‘That’s enough. My word is final.’

I groaned. ‘I’m delighted to hear it, Prince Charming.’

Laughing, he took out his cigarette case, pure silver gleaming with the Danish coat of arms, and held one out to me.

‘Thanks.’

He said nothing. The smoke spread before his face and threw a grayish blush on the window pane.

‘So,’ I said. ‘Your father’s the Crown Prince.’

‘I’m not in line for the throne, if that’s what you want to ask.’

‘Do you wish you were?’

‘Not really. It’s exhausting, a life like that. Parades, interviews, courtesy trips. As it is, my life is simply dull. If I were in line for the throne, I would have to spend every day preparing, studying, training.’

‘I couldn’t do it,’ I said. ‘I’d be a terrible king.’

‘You would.’

‘Your father didn’t raise you to be polite, did he?’

Peter grinned. ‘He raised me to be honest. You can count on honesty, but you can’t count on kindness.’

‘If you hate me so much, why bother talking to me?’

‘I don’t hate you. I think you’re naïve.’

‘Ah.’ I rested my chin on my hands. ‘Maybe you’re right.’

‘You’re not half as brilliant and complicated as you think you are.’

‘I don’t think I’m brilliant and complicated.’

He laughed. The cold made his cheeks pink.

‘Whatever you say,’ I drawled. ‘Your wish is my command, Your Royal Highness.’

‘Is it really? I shall have to put that to the test sometime.’

We both smiled, looked at each other a little too long, then looked away, off down the lonely hall.

A small silver frame, engraved with an unexciting pattern, stood on the Major General’s desk; in it was a picture of a plain but attractive woman. As I sat opposite him, waiting for his next accusation, I peered at the photograph, trying to find in its depths some magical secret.

‘My wife,’ the Major-General said, with a pinched smile. He picked the picture up and held it out to me.

Feeling pinched, I gave him an awkward smile. ‘Perhaps I’ll be able to meet her someday.’

‘She’s dead.’

‘Oh, I’m so sorry, I —’

He held up his hand to silence me. ‘She passed away many years ago. As deaths go, hers was peaceful: she died suddenly after the birth of my son.’

I nodded. It struck me as odd that he hadn’t remarried. ‘How old is your son now?’ I asked.

‘Fourteen. He’s a student at the Corps des Pages.’

‘I was at the Corps des Pages,’ I said, helpfully. ‘Is he enjoying it there?’

The Major-General grimaced. ‘As much as one *could* enjoy the Corps des Pages.’

‘And he’ll come here, once he’s graduated from the Corps?’

‘If he can get a commission, then he’ll go straight into the service. It would be simpler that way. But if he can’t get a commission, then yes, I suppose he’d come here. Education is only useful for some people, as you well know. I believe for him it would be a waste.’

I hesitated, running my thumb along the edge of the picture frame. ‘For whom is it useful?’

‘For those who know how to use it.’

‘Naturally,’ I muttered.

‘I know you think that education has no value whatsoever —’

‘I didn’t say that, Your Excellency.’

‘No, but I can see that you think so. It’s true that education doesn’t compare to time spent in the service, but you’d be a fool not to see the value of these two years. When I was your age, I thought little of school. One of my teachers at the cadet corps where I studied gave me a bit of advice that I’ve found useful: your education is only what you put into it.’

My years at the Corps des Pages had killed any interest I’d had in education. His speech was all platitudes.

‘I think I’ve put a great deal into my education,’ I said, setting down the photograph.

‘You’ve never had a reason to think anything else.’

‘You weren’t at the Corps des Pages. You don’t know what it’s like.’

‘Prince Alexis, if you keep up like this, you’re going to be expelled,’ he said.

‘So much the better. Isn’t it a good thing that I’d rather serve in the army than spend more time at school? Everyone talks such talk about our duty to our country. What good is… well…’ I stopped, remembering my manners just in time.

‘What good is further education,’ he finished. ‘I understand your frustration. I really do. But you’re here, not in an army barracks. You might as well try to get something out of your education. The Corps des Pages may have prepared you for hell, that I won’t deny, but it won’t have prepared you for a time of war. This school was founded to do just that: prepare you for the actualities of war. Here, let’s test my theory. Tell me, when did the Battle of Waterloo take place?’

‘On the eighteenth of June, eighteen-fifteen,’ I answered.

‘Very good. Tell me, what exactly led to the Battle of Waterloo?’

‘After his brief exile on the island of Elba, Napoleon gathered his forces, sailed to France, marched to Paris with the support of his old marshal, and met the armies of the Coalition in Waterloo, where, due to poor planning on Napoleon’s part and good strategy on the Coalition’s part, the French were defeated,’ I rattled off.

‘Very good,’ said the Major-General, with an amused look. ‘Now tell me, if you had been Bonaparte, at the head of the Grande Armée, what would you have done to prevent your defeat at Waterloo?’

I faltered. ‘Your Excellency, I…’

‘Exactly,’ he said. ‘You know the history of war. You know the names, the dates, the faces. But you don’t know how to wage war. That’s why you’re here.’

‘Your Excellency, Bonaparte was a great general. Some say he was the greatest to ever live. If he couldn’t win at Waterloo, then how can you expect me to?’

‘You’re the son of a great general.’

‘My father’s not great. He just thinks he is.’

The Major-General raised his brows in warning.

‘You don’t know him,’ I said. ‘I know him.’

He shrugged. ‘It’s true, not anyone can win a war. But a good education will save you from making the amateurish mistakes that Russian officers are known for. You’re not stupid — you have the brains to understand history, so you ought to have the sense to see the value of education.’

‘I don’t understand history,’ I said. ‘I was merely taught to recite facts. That’s not understanding.’

‘You’re splitting hairs.’

‘But —’

‘Prince Alexis, will you be quiet? For God’s sake, it’s as if someone was paying you to talk. Now listen. You can take my advice or not, but if I were you I would try to learn as much as you can in these two years. Education is a great gift, if you know how to use it. Do you understand?’

‘Yes, Your Excellency,’ I said.

‘Good. That’s all.’

Saluting him, I left, and walked to class with a feeling of vague frustration.

‘This is you?’ I said, turning the cabinet card over in my hands. I had looked at the front long enough: a picture of a young Peter framed in sepia, dashing in suit and ascot, with his perennial regal smile. His cheeks were a little darker than the rest of his face; he must have been blushing when the picture was taken. I wondered whether he’d been shy before the camera. God knows he had no reason to be shy. The photographers of the world would fight over a face like that.

‘Give it back, Alexis.’

‘Eighteen-ninety-six…’ I read. ‘What does this say?’

Peter snatched at the photograph, but I held it out of his reach.

‘Let me see.’

I tossed it to him.

‘It says *Charlottenlund Slot*,’ he answered.

‘What the hell is that?’

‘It’s my father’s summer house. I was born there.’ He slipped the picture back into his pocket.

‘Lord, if I had your good looks…’ I began, and trailed off, sighing.

He grinned. ‘You’re one to talk. What do you have to complain about?’

‘I don’t know.’

‘You’re lucky. I always wanted dark hair when I was younger.’

I ran my hands through my hair. ‘You should see my sister’s. It’s black as pitch. Is everyone in your family as fair as you?’

He nodded. ‘Yes, except for my older brother. He has dark hair, almost as dark as yours. But I have my mother’s colors.’

‘Your mother must be beautiful,’ I said, half hoping he wouldn’t hear me.

‘Not especially.’

Surprised, I looked across at him.

‘She’s… very stubborn,’ he said.

‘I see. So is my father. He always gets his way.’

We were silent for a moment.

‘What’s that from?’ he asked.

‘What?’

‘That.’ He pointed.

I touched the side of my face, where a thin line ran from my temple to my cheek. ‘I had an accident as a child.’

‘Did it hurt?’

‘I don’t really remember.’

In math class that afternoon, I was distracted. Albrecht, our teacher, was distracted too. His cruelty was well-known: he had once slapped a second-year for mixing up his fractions. But today he was late to class. When he arrived he came in slowly, taking off his hat and gloves without the slightest hint of urgency. He seemed almost unaware that so many sets of eyes were trained on him.

‘He must have gotten awfully lucky with his French lady,’ Ilyusha whispered. There was a pet theory that Albrecht was in love with the daughter of a merchant from Giverny.

‘I suppose he must’ve.’

‘Do you think he’ll retire if he gets married?’

‘Don’t get your hopes up.’

Smiling serenely, Albrecht laid his coat over the back of his chair and flipped through the papers on his desk.

‘Cadets,’ he droned, ‘we shall return to our study of the cotangent theorem.’

Class was a breeze. Albrecht let us off with only a few exercises. When one of the younger boys made an error at the blackboard, Albrecht gently explained the theorem and sent the boy back to his chair with a generous smile.

‘If only he’d been in this kind of mood when you’d carried off that scheme with the books,’ Ilyusha mused.

‘It wouldn’t have been so fun then,’ I said. ‘He would’ve forgiven us. I wanted revenge, not forgiveness.’

Ilyusha laughed.

The first snow of the year was always the most perfect. The cheer in the streets, the bright layer of clouds, the blanket of white, the blinding quiet in the early mornings. We wore our overcoats outside for the first time that autumn, and as we rested between drills, I fingered Golitsyn’s card: it had been stuck in the seam of my pocket since the day he gave it to me.

The holidays are hardly a month away. The Major-General has agreed, on account of a mix-up in the schedule, to give us nearly three weeks of leave instead of just two: a great relief to us all. Yesterday I had a lengthy letter from home — Katya and Mother send their best; Father sends nothing.

Home seemed a strange place. When I thought of home I thought not so much of the place on the map but more the place in time. The look of the house and the layout of the estate were clear enough in my mind, but the people within were blurred. When I tried to imagine returning to the estate, I pictured an abandoned house, with no people, where all the trees and gardens were dead. The cheerful place full of laughing guests and liveried servants only existed in my memory; it died with my childhood.

Sitting in Peter’s dorm late last week, I watched the second-years riding back and forth across the courtyard. He sat close to me, his head on my shoulder, his fingertips grazing my arm.

‘You said you wanted to tell me something,’ he observed, as if complaining.

‘I forgot.’ I turned back. The whole room, and the whole world outside of it, briefly centered on him, as he slowly looked up at me. Electric, blue, unflinching, his eyes held my gaze.

‘Don’t,’ I said. ‘Someone will come in.’

He lowered his eyes, and settled in the crook of my neck.

‘Move.’

‘I don’t want to.’

I put my arm around him. ‘I have to go soon.’

‘You’ve hardly been here five minutes.’

‘I told you I couldn’t stay.’

Again he glanced at me, and we both laughed.

‘Well?’ he said.

‘Well what?’

Shaking his head, he shut his eyes. ‘You have a funny smile, you know.’

‘Do I?’ I asked.

With a laugh, he took his cigarette case from his beside table and lit one. ‘Yes. You have — I don’t know how to call it — you always look as though you’re staring into the sun when you smile.’

‘I feel like I’m staring into the sun when I look at you.’

He flushed. There was a silence, which likely lasted no longer than a minute, but seemed to stretch for all eternity. Finally he said, ‘Cigarette?’ and held out his silver case. I took one, and he lit it, looking intently up at me, his hands too close to mine, his eyes too provocative.

‘Thank you.’

‘It’s nothing.’

He rested against me again. Lifting my hand, I smoothed his hair.

‘Do you ever wish you weren’t royalty?’

‘I don’t mind it.’

‘I wish I wasn’t a prince. Of course I can’t complain about inheriting my father’s fortune. But I don’t like being my father’s son. I wish I could go away, and travel the world — go live in France, or somewhere exciting.’

‘Somewhere exciting,’ he parroted, laughing. ‘Yes, I think you’d do well in France. If I hadn’t known you were Russian, I would have thought you were French.’

‘Would you really?’

‘Yes, certainly. You sound French. And there’s something romantic about you.’

Blushing, I laid back on the bed and gazed up at the ceiling. ‘Of course, compared to Your Highness, I know I’m terribly unremarkable…’

‘I never said that.’

‘You might as well have. I thought you hated me.’

‘You see, you are remarkable, that’s the trouble,’ he said. ‘I think being ordinary would do you good. Someday you’ll end up like Icarus, flying too close to the sun.’

‘Icarus indeed. It doesn’t pay to be ordinary.’

‘You and I wouldn’t know either way.’

‘No, I suppose we wouldn’t. Don’t you wish you could get away from here?’ I asked. ‘And go back home — only not home. Somewhere else. Somewhere better.’

‘Yes, I do wish that sometimes.’

‘Do you like it here? In Russia, I mean?’

‘I don’t… mind it,’ he said, slowly. ‘I don’t enjoy it here at school, but it’s really not so bad — God knows it could be worse. And being here, I get to see my aunt, and she is always kind to me. She lets me stay at the Winter Palace during the holidays. Everything is so splendid there. And then over the summer I can go back home, and next year I shall graduate. So really it’s not so bad.’

‘Do you miss home?’

‘Every now and then.’

‘I’ve never been to Denmark.’

‘Really?’

‘Really. I’ve only ever been to France and Germany. My sister was sickly when she was little, and Father used to take all us abroad in the summer, in the hopes that the fresh air would do her some good. We have estates in Nice, and in Giverny, and in Baden-Baden. I miss those vacations. I was never really happy,’ I said quietly, ‘but it was beautiful there.’

‘Neither of us have ever seen the real world, I don’t think.’

‘No, rather not.’

‘But then again, this awful place is all too real.’

‘If this was all there was to the world, I would’ve shot myself already,’ I quipped.

‘Yes, so would I.’

‘Pardon me, Highness, but I thought you enjoyed military school. I thought you found the discipline invigorating.’

‘I don’t like it any more than you do,’ he said. ‘I just don’t complain, that’s all.’

‘I see. And I ought to learn from your example, I take it?’

‘Yes, you ought,’ he laughed. ‘No, Alexis, I think you know to look out for yourself. You seem determined to have your own way with things.’

I wanted to ask whether he still found me unattractive, but thought that would be putting rather too fine a point on the matter. For a little while we were silent, and I was conscious that he was looking at me, and expecting me to look back. Finally I did. I thought, we were utterly alone, and if I kissed him once, no one would know. His eyes seemed to get brighter and bluer the longer I looked. I knew by his face that he was thinking something he could not say, and I wondered what it was. When he smiled at me, it was as if I’d seen something that I hadn’t known was possible. His smile was pink and intensely beautiful. There was a great truth between us, but we both had to keep still, otherwise everything would be ruined forever. I wished he would say something irrevocable, but I knew he couldn’t, just as I couldn’t.

‘Are you going home for the holidays?’ he asked, startling me.

‘Yes. I’ll have to put up with my sister for two weeks. All she talks about in her letters is her engagement to Prince Battenberg. I don’t know why she won’t just be sensible about it. It’s a marriage, not a scene from a ballet.’

‘It’s hard to be sensible when you’re in love.’

‘It’s not love — it’s marriage.’

He laughed. ‘Don’t you think there’s love involved in marriage?’

‘Not in hers. It’s diplomatic.’

‘Ah, I see.’

‘And she shouldn’t bring love into a diplomatic proceeding,’ I went on. ‘They should be married as soon as possible, and then we can all forget about it.’

‘She won’t be able to forget about it,’ he said. ‘She’ll have to see him every day.’

I rolled my eyes. ‘Stop being such a sentimentalist. You don’t know my sister. She doesn’t have a heart. She’ll forget about it sooner than any of the rest of us.’

‘Does she love the Prince?’

‘I don’t know. What does it matter?’

‘Alexis, you’re being heartless.’

‘I’m not being heartless, I’m being practical.’

Now he actually scoffed. ‘Practical? You sound like my mother…’ and he rattled off something in Danish, in a high, feminine voice. ‘We must think of our duty to our country,’ he translated, smiling. ‘That’s what she always says. I feel like I’m going to shut my eyes and open them again and see my mother in front of me instead of you.’

‘Oh, fuck off,’ I said.

He laughed. ‘But really, don’t you feel bad for your sister?’

‘Why should I? She can still marry the man. She’ll still inherit a dowry. And when she’s married, if she wants to have lovers, then she can have as many as she pleases. No, pardon me, but I don’t see why I should feel bad for her. She’s rich; she’s pretty; she’s never had to work or study, and she never will. The only unpleasant thing in her life is that the man she loves doesn’t love her as much as she loves him.’

He smiled. ‘You don’t know what an awful plight that can be.’

‘Certainly I do. All I mean to say is that... well... it’s easier for her.’

‘How is that?’

‘You know how.’

With these words our argument, petty as it had been, was instantly forgotten.

He leaned forward; his hand rested on mine. ‘Stay for a moment, Alexis. The Major-General won’t want you.’

‘And you do?’ I said.

‘Pardon me?’

I couldn’t tell whether he hadn’t understood me or had simply been offended by my bluntness. The question could not be repeated — it was bad enough that I had said it once, and I wouldn’t say it again.

‘Nothing, excuse me,’ I said. ‘I have to go.’

‘Alexis, wait —’

I left him, feeling that I’d ruined everything. That night I was dismayed, rather than surprised, that I dreamt of him.

For the rest of the week I avoided him. He appeared nightly in my dreams, taunting me with his smiles. It wasn’t until Saturday that we met again — I found a note under my pillow that read simply, *Meet me in the armory. Midnight.*

The armory was deserted and dark, almost like the music room at home: a listless expanse of inlaid floors and arched windows, except in Father’s music room the walls were lined with violins and here they were lined with firearms. Peter, careful to shut the door quietly, advanced into the room as though he were its deity.

‘Come here,’ he whispered, looking half back at me.

‘You come here.’

I leant against the door, and watched as he slowly walked toward me until his face almost touched mine.

‘Are you all right?’ he asked again.

‘Don’t know,’ I said. I could hardly breathe. Something had risen up in me.

A long moment passed; finally he reached out and touched my hand with his. Another second was spent, gazing terrified at each other; then he clasped my hand.  With the same nervous poise, he leaned in and kissed my lips. We parted, hardly able to face each other, and then kissed again, as though by accident.

‘Sorry,’ he said, breaking away.

‘No, it’s my fault.’

‘It’s not your fault. I got carried away.’ He put his hand to his mouth, and, shaking his head, walked toward the window.

‘I don’t know how you stand it here,’ I said. ‘It’s awful. Unbearable.’

‘Unbearable?’ he repeated, looking through the window. ‘I don’t find it so. Awful, yes, perhaps. But it’s not unbearable.’

‘That’s because you’re royalty,’ I grumbled.

‘I don’t think so. And you’re as good as royalty. You’re richer than royalty, yes?’

I shrugged.

‘It’s easier for me because I’m well-behaved, that’s all.’

Wondering whether he would kiss me again, I gazed at him and smiled, and he smiled back. There was so much I wanted to ask him and so little I could think to say. Both our smiles gave way to laughs. He seemed to feel the same way I did, and he busied himself with the window, gingerly opening the latches.

‘Come look.’

I did. Last night’s clouds had passed away; the sky was clear and dark, glittering with stars.

‘See, it’s not so bad,’ he said.

‘I still don’t know how you stand it,’ I repeated, now in a whisper.

Smiling, I leant against him, and we both pretended to look through the window. In all the night there were only two things: the rustle of the trees outside and the press of my arm to his. Terrified by my longing, I shut my eyes and put my arms around him. Before that moment, it seemed, I had never really touched him, never mind the accidental kiss by the door. To touch him was wonderful, and I wanted to have him close, closer than he could ever physically be, so close that we would never let each other go again. Against my chest I felt the pounding of his heart. He shifted, and ran his hand over my hair, and his lips vaguely touched my face — an accident or a confession, I couldn’t tell which, but I returned his kiss, pressing my lips to his shoulder through the fabric of his shirt — the warmth of his skin was lovely.

‘Peter…’ I said.

‘I know. Don’t say anything stupid.’

I laughed. ‘I’m not in the habit of it.’

‘Really?’ he teased.

We parted. He was flushed.

‘Haven’t I ever said anything clever?’ I asked.

He shook his head, grinning.

‘Nothing, never?’ I asked, and brought him closer, my hands at his waist. ‘If I could say anything clever, I’d say it for you. You, O sweet prince, would obviously know a hundred cleverer things to say,’ I went on, in dramatic tones, ‘but I would try.’

He was laughing. I loved the sound of his laugh; and while I was listening and admiring him, he reached out and took my face in his hands, and the touch sent an ecstasy through us both.

‘Why did you pretend to hate me for so long?’ he asked.

‘I don’t know,’ I said. ‘But you hated me too.’

His eyes flitted over my face. With an awkward, discomposed sort of air, he drew himself up and said, ‘I never hated you. It was you who hated me.’

‘Well, I thought you did.’

‘You hated me without reason, then?’

‘No, there was a reason,’ I said, feeling that I was driving at some particular point but unsure what that point was. Had there ever been a reason? Had I ever looked at him and felt anything other than what I felt for him now: a strange mix of love and terror?

‘What was the reason?’ he asked.

‘I don’t know. Wouldn’t it be better if I still hated you?’

‘No.’

‘It’s better this way?’

‘Yes, a million times.’

‘You’re quite sure, Highness?’ I asked. ‘Is your word quite final this time?’

‘Quite final,’ he said. ‘You still haven’t told me: why did you hate me?’

‘I was afraid,’ I said, my words tripping over my breath. ‘That was the reason.’

‘And are you still afraid?’

I shook my head, whispered, ‘No, I love you,’ without truly meaning it, and kissed him a second time.

In my dreams I made love to him. I saw his strong figure undressed and his handsome face glowing, watch as he turned to meet my eyes. One night I dreamt we were in the second-year dormitory. As we embraced the softness of cotton suddenly enveloped me. Peter was laughing, idly, the most lovely sound, and I touched him. For a moment he fell silent, blushing a little, and then came closer and lay in my arms. I was delighted by his laughter, by the warmth of his hands, and kissed him hard, the way I’d wanted to in the armory. All at once it was over. I woke, feeling that the world had ended, but saw the vaulted ceiling of the dormitory, and the moon — filmy through the window, ghostly against the blue of the night sky — and the rest of my classmates, all fast asleep. I lay in the dark, horribly awake, and wished I was back at home.

First I avoided him; then I avoided the dreams. Finally the exhaustion caught up with me. As I walked the halls after lunch, which I hadn’t eaten, dizziness consumed me. My head began to spin uncontrollably, and my hands trembled. Staggering, I reached out to catch Ilyusha’s arm, but found, to my horror, that he had vanished. A sudden, clammy darkness came upon me; that too I tried to resist, but could not. The hallway disappeared, closing in on itself in a brilliant shower of sparks.

When I came to, I found myself on an unfamiliar couch in an unfamiliar room. Briefly, while the dizziness set in, I feared the worst. Perhaps I’d been kidnapped — perhaps I was in the lair of a foreign criminal who had brought me here to murder me. Then, glancing around the room, I saw an illustrated Bible verse in a small gilded frame on the wall: not exactly the mark of a murderer.

‘He’s awake,’ said a voice that I knew and liked, but could not put a name to.

‘What’s happened?’ I said. ‘Where am I?’ A sudden wave of dizziness sent me into the depths of the couch, groaning. ‘My head hurts…’

‘It’s all right, Prince Alexis,’ another voice offered. In my delirium I thought it was my father, and became still dizzier, still more confused. Opening my eyes again I saw a man was sitting behind a large desk — the Colonel, surely.

‘Oh, God,’ I said. ‘It’s not my fault, I swear.’

The man seemed unfazed by this. ‘Certainly not. It’s quite all right.’

Dizziness ate away at me. I thought I would faint again, and shut my eyes. A hand touched my face; I shrank from it. A minute or two passed. The two men spoke to each other, and the sound of footsteps rattled throughout the room. Someone turned me over and pressed a cold cloth on my forehead. I was in a state as of one who sleeps, but believes he dreams: turning over agitatedly in bed, thinking he is far away, not quite sure whether he is master of his own thoughts, for they seem to come from somewhere else: everything in a haze, grey and mauve and uncertain.

‘Alexis,’ said the first voice.

‘No,’ I groaned.

A peal of laughter rang out and startled me. My eyes flew open, and I saw Peter at my side, and the Major-General standing not far off.

‘What happened?’ I said.

‘You fainted,’ the Major-General said. ‘I was told that you had collapsed on your way to class, and had your classmates bring you here. There’s a bad case of flu going around, and it’s unpleasant in the infirmary — you’ll be more comfortable here.’

‘Thank you, Your Honor,’ I said, forgetting his title in my delirium.

There was a brief but tense silence.

‘Your Excellency,’ I corrected.

‘Perhaps you are sick enough to warrant a visit to the infirmary. If you keep up like that, I’ll be reporting your last words,’ he quipped, laughing.

‘I’m so sorry, Your Excellency. I’m not myself.’

‘If you left off my title, I would have you put in irons for treason,’ Peter said.

‘You couldn’t do that — you’re not even Russian,’ I groaned. ‘Am I ill?’

Peter laughed, and I felt his hand, cool and gentle, touch my cheek. Delirious, and longing more than ever to have him close, I opened my eyes, and saw that he was smiling. There was such a look of fondness in his eyes; something determined about his expression. Looking at him I understood that he cared for me immensely, and that there was nothing I could do to stop him caring.

‘You’ll be all right,’ he said, taking his hand away.

I tried to reply, but found I couldn’t. The Major-General got me water, and I sat up and drank clumsily, feeling more embarrassed than ever. None of this, I thought bitterly, would’ve happened if Father had only let me get a commission. The aching strain of tears crept upon me, and I shut my eyes, groaning once more. 

‘Hush, hush,’ he whispered. ‘It’s all right. Your Excellency, he has a fever.’

‘I don’t,’ I said, swatting at his hand. ‘I’m perfectly well. I’m fine, I swear. Leave me be. I’m all right, really I am.’

‘Perhaps you are, but you look like a ghost,’ Peter said, smiling. 

‘I feel like one. My head aches like you can’t imagine.’

‘Stay and rest. I’ve got to go to class. Major-General Radetsky, see that he rests.’

The Major-General nodded, an amused smile wrinkling his face. Saluting, Peter left, and then all was quiet.

When I knocked on the Major-General’s door the next day, he hollered an answer from within. He had summoned me during break, and I knew from the tone of his voice that he was displeased with me. Getting in trouble with the Major-General was an event; he was incredibly lenient as headmasters went. Only the worst students ended up in his office, shrinking in their chairs and clinging to their shabby alibis.

‘Are you feeling any better?’ he asked, gesturing for me to take a seat.

‘Yes, Your Excellency.’

‘No more fainting fits, I hope?’

‘No, Your Excellency.’

He looked up from his work, considered my expression as though to object to it, then returned to his work. For several minutes neither of us spoke. His silence was not simply silence, but a purposeful lack of speech that sent me spiraling into the deepest pits of anxiety.

Finally he gave up his work, sank back into his chair, and pinched the bridge of his nose. ‘Listen — I’m sure you know why I called you in here.’

‘No, Your Excellency.’

‘Enough,’ he groaned. ‘Listen, Prince Alexis, I don’t want to make this any harder than it needs to be. The Colonel would have you flogged, but I don’t think that’s necessary. He pounces on students with a kind of malicious delight that I find disgusting and immature.’ The Major-General sighed. ‘He was already part of the staff when I was hired, and he makes himself impossible to dismiss. But that’s besides the point. I’ve begun to suspect, for a variety of reasons, that you’re involved with the Prince of Denmark.’

I stammered out a protest.

‘One moment. The trouble is, Prince Alexis, you’re a good student — or at least you have the potential to become one — and Peter’s the best in his class. If I expel Peter, I’ll have the King of Denmark and the Emperor of Russia after me. I am not going to have this school mixed up in a royal scandal. And I don’t feel that I could expel you, either, not when your father is a patron of the school. You see what sort of position this puts me in.’

‘I see what position it puts you in, but I don’t see where it leaves me.’

‘Well, that depends.’

‘On what?’ I retorted.

‘I’m getting to that, Prince Alexis,’ he said. ‘Have some patience. Let me ask you plainly: is it true? I mean to say — do you have something to hide?’

‘We all have things to hide,’ I said slowly.

‘Prince Alexis, answer me seriously. I could have you expelled if I found reason to do so. You have no right to mock me.’

‘I’m sorry, Your Excellency.’

‘Tell me honestly,’ he said, ‘do you have something to hide?’

Didn’t I. If I’d thought about it hard enough, I could’ve made myself believe that I’d done all sorts of unspeakable things. Yes, it seemed I had more things to hide than not: I didn’t believe in God, I wasn’t particularly patriotic, and I had spent my life making the sort of mistakes that could send me to prison.

‘Prince Alexis,’ the Major-General began.

‘No, Your Excellency,’ I said. ‘I have nothing to hide.’

‘Were you in the armory with Prince Peter last week?’ the Major-General asked.

The armory appeared in my mind as one of a series of distasteful, shameful episodes. We hadn’t intended to do anything wrong; we had simply been longing for each other for several months, and the armory had been the culmination of it.

‘Your Excellency, I…’

‘Tell me the truth,’ the Major-General insisted. ‘Lying will get you nowhere.’

‘Yes, I was with him.’

‘And did you do anything that would make you ashamed before God?’

I was ashamed before myself and the Major-General, never mind about God. I remembered Peter’s hands at my waist, his gently mocking smile, his lips touching mine. He had stepped closer to me, so that our thighs brushed, and I had a vague memory of running my hand down that thigh, but I wasn’t sure whether I had just imagined it to satiate some awful desire.

‘No, Your Excellency,’ I said.

‘Are you sure?’

‘Yes.’

‘Have you ever had relations with another man?’ the Major-General asked.

‘No, Your Excellency,’ I said, wondering what counted as relations.

‘Truthfully?’

‘Yes, I swear.’

The Major-General sat back in his chair. For a moment he seemed to forget I was there. My heart pounded. I remembered, with horrible, plaintive vividness, how handsome Peter had looked in the moonlight, how golden his hair had been, tousled after the long day. More than anything, I remembered the feeling I’d had: that if I didn’t kiss him, I would die.

‘But is it true?’ the Major-General asked.

‘Is what true?’

He groaned. ‘Is it true that you’re involved with him?’

‘No.’

‘Voronetsky, don’t lie to me,’ he thundered.

For a moment I couldn’t answer. I touched my face with my hand, as if to cleanse myself of what I’d done. ‘I kissed him,’ I said. ‘Twice.’

‘I see,’ said the Major-General. ‘Prince Alexis, do you love him?’

‘I mean — he’s my friend. I care about him very much.’

‘You’re not answering my question. Do you love him?’

I felt ill. I thought of Peter, with his regal manner and his incessant teasing and his lovely blue eyes and the appealing scent that had lingered on his shirt that night in the armory and the angled lines of his hands, which I liked to admire during our fencing lessons. His hands had always seemed perfect to me, and his smile too. He had the most splendid smile. Sitting there, it occurred to me that I hardly knew what love was, but at the same time I was certain that, were I left alone with Peter, I would’ve liked little more than to kiss him till we were both short of breath. Some nights I couldn’t sleep for thinking of him. I didn’t know if it was love that I felt for him, but whatever it was, I felt it intensely, and I wanted to go on feeling it for the rest of my life⁠1.

‘Do you love him, Prince Alexis?’ the Major-General repeated, stern now.

‘No, Your Excellency,’ I said.

‘It was nothing more than a caprice, then?’

‘Yes… I don’t know what got into me.’

Again the Major-General sat back and sighed. Suspicion rested in his face, creased his brow, and made his eyes dull to the light sifting through the windows. He drummed his fingers on the desk and drew a long, contemplative breath.

‘Please, Your Excellency,’ I said. ‘I swear I’m innocent. I’d never be with another man like that. I swear I wouldn’t — on my honor. That sort of thing is beneath me.’

‘Beneath you?’ he repeated. Something like sarcasm ran under his tone.

‘Yes, Your Excellency. I wouldn’t bring disgrace on the school, or on myself.’

‘You’ve nearly brought disgrace on both the school and yourself.’

‘We didn’t do anything *really* wrong.’

‘Oh, and you know all about right and wrong?’

‘I’m not a whore,’ I snapped. ‘It was just a silly thing, a whim, a caprice — call it what you like, but it didn’t mean anything. We weren’t thinking.’

‘Our truest feelings often show when we aren’t thinking.’

I looked down. ‘Yes, Your Excellency.’

‘I didn’t mean to corner you like that, Prince Alexis, but you must understand it’s terribly difficult for me to believe you, when you have a record of bad behavior. It’s easier, in some ways, to believe that you’re lying than that you’re telling the truth. Plenty of other students have lied about this sort of thing. You wouldn’t be the first. I understand that sometimes there are things that we can’t admit.’ He held up his hand to stop my protests. ‘Now, now. I’ll listen openly to anything you have to say.’

‘What could I possibly say?’ I muttered. ‘I’d never stoop to such lowly and criminal behavior. I’ve been brought up to do what’s right. Please believe me.’

‘It’s precisely this kind of desperation that makes me doubt your innocence.’

‘How could I not be desperate? You’re threatening to expel me.’

‘I’m not threatening you with anything. I only want to know the truth.’

‘I’ve told you all the truth there is,’ I said.

‘Prince Alexis, I’ve been director of the Guards School for ten years. No one has ever told me all the truth there is. Something is always kept secret.’

‘I’m not keeping anything secret,’ I said, horrified at my trembling voice. ‘I’ve told you everything. And God knows Peter doesn’t have any secrets.’

‘Naturally,’ the Major-General said. ‘I have no doubt about the Prince’s innocence. He’s an exemplary student.’

I nodded. ‘I swear to you, I hardly know him. To tell the truth,’ I went on, forcing a smile, ‘I never thought he liked me much.’

‘I see,’ the Major-General mused. ‘So now you’ve told me all the truth there is. You kissed him — twice, as you say — but you did nothing else. You don’t know Peter that well, and you have no sentimental feelings about him and he has no sentimental feelings about you. Is that right?’

I hoped to God that Peter did have sentimental feelings about me, but there was no bringing that up now. ‘Yes, that’s right,’ I said.

‘You say you would never stoop to any kind of criminal behavior. But you must realize that what you did that night could very well be considered criminal.’

‘It would only be criminal if we’d made love, and we didn’t,’ I hissed.

Surprise flashed across his face.

‘Sorry,’ I said, looking at my hands.

‘You’re awfully touchy about this.’

‘Am I going to be expelled?’

Half a minute passed in silence. His eyes shifted slowly over his desk.

‘No,’ he said.

‘I’m not?’

‘No,’ he repeated. ‘You’re a spoiled child, and you’ve behaved badly, but I don’t think you have a cruel bone in your body. Getting you into trouble for this would be pointless. There would be no sense in it. So long as you swear not to get this school involved in a scandal.’

‘Of course. But I’m not going to be expelled?’

‘No, you’re not, once and for all. I believe you’re stuck here until your graduation day. There’s no need to fret.’

‘Thank you. Your Excellency, I —’

‘Don’t start with that nonsense again. It’s quite all right. It would be downright evil if I got you into trouble now. Don’t worry about this any more.’

‘It’s terribly kind of you.’

‘Yes, it is, but I think I’m right to do it.’

‘I don’t know how to thank you.’

‘You can thank me by making sure it never happens again.’

For a moment, guilt shot through me. I wanted to tell him the truth. He had shown me a mercy that I hadn’t deserved. I remembered every one of my conversations with Peter — his teasing remarks, his noble smiles, and the foolish ways I’d tried to impress him alike; remembered the ridiculous kisses we’d indulged in that night in the armory, and I wished suddenly that I had made love to him, for now I’d never have the chance again. If only we could get away, run away from this place, to some other country, some place not governed by a plethora of hypocritical laws, where we could live in peace, where I might, without hiding, hold his hand.

It was midnight, the darkness enveloping. Tomorrow morning I would travel to Moscow for the holidays. Today was our last day at school: I spent it at Mariinsky, enjoying a concert of Beethoven conducted by one of the students at the Petersburg Conservatory. Ilyusha and his friend, uninterested in music of the old school, went to the vaudeville; I walked with them as far as the bridge over the Fontanka.

‘Beethoven,’ Ilyusha lectured, fatherly, as we walked, ‘is just the sort of thing that makes you seem stuck-up and prudish.’

‘I like Beethoven,’ I said.

‘There you go.’

‘Ilyusha’s right,’ his friend said. ‘No one listens to Beethoven these days. Why not come to the theater with us? You won’t find any pretty girls at a symphony hall, that’s certain.’

Yes, I thought, that’s just why I’m going.

The music reassured me: a paradox of gentle melancholy and strident passion, unfolding chord by chord, the strings billowing up from the stage and the winds piercing the music with an occasional flicker like incarnate birdsong.

By the time the concert was over, the evening had gone by too; night had come, and fallen upon the city in bluish darkness. It brought me back to that night in September, after Ilyusha and I had seen *Swan Lake*, and I’d stumbled back from the brothel as though from an unsettling dream. The weight of the year hadn’t settled in then, and I hadn’t noticed the omens of autumn. September is a duplicitous month, but December is definitive. Tonight the blackness was complete; it seemed so thick that, had I held out my hand, I would’ve been able to touch the darkness itself. Only the moon escaped it, and shone through the mottled clouds as if through a blanket.

Behind me, the door swung open, and a crowd poured from within the theater. A set of footsteps approached me; without looking up, I knew it was him. Lying in bed last night, I had told myself what I would have to do: tell him, as kindly as I possibly could, that I couldn’t meet him again. It was terrible, but there was nothing we could do. But when I turned to face him, all my calculated plans fell to pieces. To be near him was to be undone.

‘Cigarette?’ he said, offering one, while his smile held my fate.

‘Thanks.’

We stood in the dark and smoked without speaking. A faint strain of music spun into the street like a thread from a spool.

‘It’s late,’ I said, flicking at the lid of my watch.

‘We’ll catch a carriage.’

A carriage, closed and furtive, presented all kinds of dangerous possibilities. ‘I’d rather walk.’

‘You’re cold.’

‘I’m not.’

Smiling, he raised a single brow.

We wandered through the streets. The city was deserted, save for a handful of civil servants who spilled half-conscious from the brothel at the corner. We were silent, watching the passersby the way tourists stare at foreigners. Now and then our eyes met, and we smiled. In the dark he looked like a knight from a painting, bright and reckless, drawn by someone unreasonably fond of him.

As we turned onto Nikolskaya Avenue, snow began to fall in twinkling torrents. Peter drew closer, looking over his shoulder as if the wind would think us guilty. In the air around him was a faint but intoxicating scent of heliotrope.

‘Can’t we get a carriage?’

I hesitated. The storm swept down upon us, a violent onslaught. It was brutally cold. ‘All right,’ I said. ‘There’s a cab on the corner there.’

The drive proved as miserable as the walk through the snow. All I could think of was him, and everything else — the carriage, the blizzard, the driver clicking his tongue to spur on the horses — faded to nothing. One glance in his direction proved he was already gazing at me, waiting for something; I knew what. I looked away, and sat running my thumb over the coat of arms engraved on my wallet, as if that might bring it to life.

‘It’s on nights like this that I love Russia,’ he said, speaking softly. ‘It almost seems like home. I wish I could stay here forever.’

Again the saccharine speeches. It seemed to me that he had mistaken the romance of a desolate night for a kind of nostalgic patriotism. Certainly Russia wasn’t all dramatic carriage rides through raging snowstorms. Mostly it was dull and cold and sometimes even ridiculous.

‘You’ll go back home when you graduate?’ I asked, wanting to change the subject.

‘Yes,’ he said. ‘Unless my aunt wants me to stay.’

‘And then what? You’ll get a commission in the Danish army?’

‘Yes. And I’ll have to get married.’

‘Supposing you became king,’ I suggested.

He shook his head. ‘I’d be a terrible king. I’d end up like Ludwig the Second.’

‘The mad king,’ I said, in the drollest tone I could manage.

The carriage passed an alleyway, and a streetlamp suddenly shone through the window; the light hit his face like a wave from an electric ocean. His cheeks were red, and when he exhaled, his breath curled in benevolent, velvet shapes before him.

‘You’re cold,’ I said.

He nodded. ‘A little.’ Reaching across, he interlaced his fingers with mine. The chill of his skin shocked me. By touching me he had disrupted some balance, a balance we hadn’t seen or known about until it was thrown from its place.

As I was agonizing, he lifted my hands and softly kissed them.

‘Don’t,’ I said, pulling my hands away.

There was a painful silence.

‘And you? Are you going to be married?’ he finally asked.

‘Yes,’ I said. ‘Probably to one of the Emperor’s daughters, when they come of age.’ Feeling that I was treading on dangerous ground, I slowly went on: ‘I wish I didn’t have to marry. I don’t like my name, and I don’t want to give it to anyone else.’

‘I’d give you mine, if you wanted it.’

I nodded, dizziness creeping upon me.

‘I’m in love with you,’ he whispered.

‘Oh God, don’t.’

‘I know you love me too.’

‘Peter, don’t.’

His fingers, trembling, curled against my palm.

‘We shouldn’t do anything rash,’ I said.

‘How is it rash if I have loved you for months?’ All that matters to me is that I love you.’

Finally I looked up at him, and as I looked, another streetlamp beamed into the carriage. His beauty stunned me. I didn’t know how would I go on living, knowing what we wanted with each other. There was something frightening and awful about it all, yet something wonderful too. After a moment, my resolve broke. I touched his cold cheek and kissed his lips.

‘Do you love me?’ he whispered.

‘Peter, I can’t.’

‘You don’t love me, or you can’t say it?’

I shook my head.

‘The Major-General found out — he could’ve had me expelled.’

Peter’s eyes widened. For a moment he was silent, thinking. ‘Kiss me again,’ he said. ‘And after that I won’t ask you anything else.’

I nodded. We inched closer together, till our faces brushed. Our first kisses were shy and gentle, but I remembered what he said and knew that this was the last time we would be together. Taking him in my arms, I kissed him passionately, too passionately, and felt his hands clutching at my back. It was not a happy embrace, but I wanted it to go on and on. I realized, not a little embarrassed, that it was the only time I had kissed someone sincerely.

We parted. I hid my face in my hands and groaned.

‘I’m sorry,’ he said. ‘I wanted you to know that I loved you.’

A strange sense of joy swept over me. ‘Yes, I know,’ I said. ‘But we can’t…’

Against all odds he began to smile, almost to laugh. ‘I wish we could run away somewhere,’ he said, stroking my cheek. ‘I know that sounds idiotic and ridiculous, but — well, I don’t know. Lord knows you have enough money for both of us to live off. I could work as a physician, and you could be a writer… I don’t know. Alexis, I love you — that’s all I know in the world.’

‘If you keep up like that, I’m going to lose my mind.’

He laughed. ‘I suppose it’s all my fault.’

‘It’s not. Don’t say that. If anything, it’s my fault. That’s why we have to stop. I could never live knowing that I’d ruined everything for you.’

‘Then you don’t love me?’

I couldn’t face him. In his eyes was an accusation, and a temptation that I couldn’t resist. Breathless, dazed, I stared at the floor of the carriage, the worn wooden boards with their chipping black paint.

‘I’m so sorry.’ It was inadequate, but I had nothing else to say.

He wouldn’t answer.

‘I shouldn’t have kissed you,’ I went on. ‘I do care about you. You have to believe me. But I don’t think we should get mixed up like this.’

He shook his head.

‘Peter, please.’

‘No,’ he cried. ‘Don’t say anything else.’

Suddenly the carriage stopped, and we both jumped. The driver’s voice — a low, droning Russian — sounded from outside. In my confusion, I could hardly piece together what he was saying. With violently shaking hands, I found my wallet, fumbled for a banknote, unsure of how much he’d asked for or how much I was giving him, and offered it through the window.

As I opened the door, a gust of cold air blew into my face, and shocked me out of my reverie. Stepping down, I found myself ankle-deep in snow; rifts of it had piled up in the courtyard. The world was a flurry of white.

Turning back, I held out my hand to help Peter down from the carriage.

‘It’s still snowing — you might slip,’ I said.

Reluctantly, he took my hand.

‘Look, we’ve made an awful mess of this,’ I said, as the carriage, its bells ringing, retreated into the night. ‘I do care for you. I don’t blame you at all.’

‘I don’t blame you at all either.’

I reached for his hand, but he shook his head.

‘Don’t make this any worse,’ he muttered. ‘Alexis, I — never mind.’ And he brushed past me and went in, ignoring my calls.

For a while I stayed there, shaking all over, yet somehow oblivious to the cold.

‘God,’ I whispered to myself, ‘forgive me everything...’
