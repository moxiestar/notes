Of course she is lonely in New York. No one comes to New York for company. She is almost angry with herself for not anticipating it, the ache welling up in her throat, the isolation, the angry restless whirring of Lower Manhattan, the city pulling at its seams. She sits high among the concrete monoliths, the sea of grays and blacks and blues, trapped in her window like a cell in a gaping spreadsheet. The cage rose up quickly, before she realized she was the one building it, driving up the walls, locking the doors. Sometimes humans are such self-sabotaging machines.

It has not been an easy transition. The move itself was easy, the flight was fast. Her mother leaves barely crying and she goes on, around the corner to a friend, a bar, an overpriced dinner. She scatters souvenirs around her room, keychains, museum tickets, a postcard from the Frick, where she stands every weekend in front of the Polish Rider thinking about Frank O’Hara and what love means in a city like New York. She puts the note he left her on her bedside table, reads it every night. Soon he visits and she has new souvenirs, new memories, new things to laugh about, new things to try to forget. It is not simple the way it was when they were children, when they watched each other across the pool without speaking. It is not fun the way it was back in college, in sappy senior year, skipping classes to kiss on the bench in the garden. She does not see the knots being tied until she is stuck, chest aching, crying on the subway. It catches up with her in one vicious week and suddenly she only eats dinner every other night, suddenly she stands a little closer to the tracks, suddenly she starts to wonder. It is always like this, the fatalism, the catastrophe, the *too much*. She always wants it to be different, always thinks she has changed, always thinks she will be enough. 

Now she waits. Now she is not sure how long it has been. Too long, she knows. She is underwater, treading at the mouth of the cave, thrashing and flailing. At the office it is torture. Sometimes she is homesick, sometimes she looks too long at the Polaroids pinned on the corkboard behind her desk, blurry faces she is desperate to remember. Sometimes she listens too loud to her father’s music, his unhappy songs, the albums that spun on the turntable while they danced hand in hand around a slumping leather couch. She remembers his big strong hands, his hands that are now swelling, streaked with gray hairs. She has avoided the consequences of her life so well until now, until New York, and now they hit her like a cross-town train.
