## ***McLaughlin saves Scofield after a suicide attempt.***

After the third knock, McLaughlin begins to get suspicious. He puts his face up against the glass slit in the big wooden door and shuts one eye, squinting with the other. The house is quiet and dark. Purple streaks of evening fall across the foyer, painting the spotless floor. He can just see the edge of the kitchen, suspended in a bluish haze. One corner of the thin woven carpet that runs along the hall is wrinkled, crimped, like someone tripped over it on their way to bed. 
McLaughlin steps away. “Scofield?” he calls, knocking again. “Doc, I don’t got all day here.”
The door swings open, almost into his face. He jumps back, a little stunned, and opens his mouth to say something. Then he stops, frowning. Scofield’s face is pale, his bloodshot eyes framed by mauve shadows. His hair, usually combed back from his face in slow waves, is strewn across his forehead, matted and parted by sweat. 
“Everything all right here?” 
Scofield nods, wiping the beads of sweat off his lip. “Yeah, fine.”
“I was just comin’ over to ask about my mailbox. Was hopin’ we could come to an agreement.” McLaughlin shifts his weight. “But seein’ as you… uh….” He gestures. “What’s goin’ on?”
“I’m fine,” Scofield says, breathless. “Thanks, Detective.”
As he makes to shut the door, McLaughlin holds it open. “Call me Leslie. What’s the matter with you?”
“Leslie, don’t act like you want to get mixed up in my life.” Scofield makes a face. “I know you mean well. But we don’t have to keep doing this act where we pretend we actually want to talk to each other. It’s been a long week. Just….” He holds up a hand and draws a long breath. “Just go back home.”
McLaughlin hesitates. “Fuck off. You ain’t right in the head.”
“I told you I’m fine. Go back across the street and leave me the hell alone.”
“Let me in.”
Scofield throws up his hands. “Jesus Christ,” he mutters, but steps aside for McLaughlin to come in. He watches the officer’s back as he walks past, his eyes darting from shoulder to shoulder, the way a deer looks across a highway. At the base of his throat, he tastes his heart pounding, faster with each second. He struggles for breath. And as McLaughlin passes through the rooms, he stands by the door, waiting. 
“Look,” McLaughlin says, voice echoing from the kitchen, “I’ll make this quick. What are you on?”
“Not what you think I’m on.”
“What, snow?”
“No.” Scofield almost laughs. “I wish.” 
McLaughlin opens a drawer, the cutlery clattering within. “I won’t get you in trouble.”
“I’m not worried about that.” 
“Why the hell are you staying in the entryway like a fuckin’ creep? Don’t wanna talk to me?” The drawer slams shut. McLaughlin moves on to the dining room. “Like I said, I ain’t gonna get you in trouble. I just…” Then he’s silent for a while. 
The light from the garden glows on the table. A plate, still full, lies before the head, a fork resting stained at its side. A small orange bottle sits empty where the wine should be. Next to it, a loaded gun: an old Sig Sauer with a chipped slide. 
Scofield listens, his hand on the wall, as McLaughlin drops the magazine and sighs. 
“What did you take?”
“Diazepam.”
McLaughlin emerges, a dangerous look on his face. “In English?”
“Anxiety medication,” Scofield says. He holds up his hand. “Don’t.”
“How many’d you take?”
“Don’t — shit, Leslie, I don’t know. Fifty? Probably fifty.” He clenches his jaw. “Don’t try to be a hero, Leslie. Go back home.”
“And let you die?”
Scofield nods. “Believe me, you’d be doing me a favor.”
“Shut the hell up. You have no idea —”
“Yes, I do. Every day I watch people die. Every day I fight against that. Against death. I see infants with their chests split on, living on ventilators. And it’s waiting for me, every single day, when I get home. I feel it.” He’s breathing hard. Strands of his greying hair are falling across his forehead. “I don’t want to fight. Everything in my life, I’ve ruined. I want to stop….” He scrunches up his face. “I want to stop pretending that someday I’m going to wake up and it’s all going to be fucking rainbows and sunshine. It is not getting better. It’s not going to get better.” 
McLaughlin watches him from under hooded eyes. 
“You think you’ve got me figured out, don’t know? Rich screw up doctor who can’t handle his own career? Bad neighbor who blares the car radio at three in the morning, right?”
McLaughlin shrugs.
“You don’t know anything about me.”
“Okay,” McLaughlin says, in his slow Southern drawl. “Why?”
Scofield shakes his head. He reaches up to touch his face, misses, and fumbles to rub his eyes. “Leslie, I’m going to…” He squints. “I’m going to black out within the next ten minutes. If you want to interrogate me, you’ll have to do it quickly.”
“Why?”
“Why what?”
McLaughlin groans. “Why try to kill yourself?”
“My life is over, Leslie. My life is over whether or not I pull that trigger. I’m already dead. I don’t feel anything.” He wipes his mouth. His words are beginning to slip into each other. “I’m exhausted. I used to be angry, but I’m too exhausted to even be angry. There’s just n….” He frowns. “Why can’t I say that? There’s n… there’s nothing.”
“You need to sit down. We should get you to a hospital.”
“I am not dying in a hospital.”
“Nick, let me help you, for fuck’s sake.”
Scofield’s gaze is getting abstract, glassy. “No,” He says, smiling. “No, let me. Let me.”
Their time is running out. McLaughlin glances around the hall, as if he’ll find a med kit hidden in a compartment in the wall. He darts into the kitchen, rummages through the drawers, comes back empty-handed. 
“Come on, you’ve gotta have something in your bathroom. You’re a doctor, for God’s sake. Look at me, Nick. Look at me.”
Scofield leans against the wall, his mouth slack on his face.
“Nick. Jesus fucking Christ.”
“Flumazenil,” Scofield says. That’s all he can say. “Flumazenil.”
It takes McLaughlin too long to find the master bathroom. By the time he does, Scofield is slumped on the bedroom floor, his limbs heavy and limp beside his body. McLaughlin is kneeling by the sink, the fluorescent bulbs illuminating a small bottle of clear liquid. The name on the label looks right, even a little familiar — he remembers it, barely, from a medical course he took in his early days at the station. Big words like contraindicated and antidote come to mind as he extracts a heavy dose of the medicine with a spare syringe. Only someone as neurotic as Scofield would keep the poison in the same cabinet as the cure. 
The veins in Scofield’s arms are prominent, somewhere between blue and green. When McLaughlin pinches the skin above them, their edges blur. His hand trembles a little, his thumb too heavy for the plunger. He keeps his teeth gritted, his eyes fixed on the angled tip of the needle as it pierces Scofield’s skin. Flinching, he begins to empty the syringe.
After the fourth dose, McLaughlin stops. He sits with Scofield for a long time, more than half an hour, while the doctor’s face changes, paling from peach to grey. His breathing slows, then, briefly, stops, and his chest sits flat and tepid on the carpet. The ribbed shell of his trachea lies still under his skin. Minutes pass. The house begins to get dark. Outside, traffic slows, but the signs of life continue: sirens and birdsong on a street he can’t see. Like Scofield, McLaughlin has watched death up close. He has, like Scofield, let people die. People he loved, people he liked, people he hated but whose bodies terrified him as rigor mortis took control. In days he does not want to remember, he was the one to pull the trigger. He was a good shot, a good soldier. He did everything right. 
When Scofield’s breathing starts again, a gentle rush over the sounds of evening, McLaughlin starts breathing too. He stops his prayer halfway through and waits for Scofield’s eyes to open. Finally, they do. The color floods back into his face, spreading from his jaw to the bridge of his nose. He blinks, opens his mouth and closes it a few times, like he’s not sure what he’s meant to do with it. McLaughlin takes his hand and squeezes it.
“Hey,” he says.
Scofield shuts his eyes. “Fuck you,” he whispers. His voice is hoarse. “Fuck you.”

They sit on the bathroom floor, their heads against the wall. Craning his line of sight to the corner of his eye, McLaughlin watches Scofield. Sweat beads on his forehead. A constellation of punctures lies in the crease between Scofield’s forearm and bicep. Beneath it, his veins are becoming brighter, still blue and faint, but an alive sort of blue, no longer the blue of a decaying body. Offshoots of a beating heart, like the roots of an evergreen. McLaughlin knows the worst is over. He sees it in Scofield’s face: he has something. It isn’t hope. It’s almost hope. It’s the kind of thing you have when you’ve learned over and over to be wary of joy, even wary of life itself. 
Reaching into his pocket, McLaughlin takes out a pack of Marlboros and an old silver lighter, a rotting heirloom. He puts one between his teeth and clicks the lighter into life. The flame dances in front of his face. Not looking over, Scofield holds out his hand. McLaughlin places a cigarette in his palm, then leans in to light it.
“Didn’t know you smoked,” he says.
“Bad habit.”
“Why’d you do it?” McLaughlin shifts his weight, folding one leg to sit further back. “What happened to you?”
“Sorry?”
“Somethin’ happened to you. I know your son died, read about it when I was lookin’ you up, right when I first moved in. But there ain’t much out there about him, your son. And there’s even less about you, just a bunch of medical shit, and Lord knows that’s all above my head.”
For almost a minute, Scofield is silent. He draws his lips into a thin line, his teeth catching on the slightly chapped skin. “My son didn’t die,” he says, “I killed him.”
McLaughlin scoffs. “Jesus Christ, you kiddin’?”
“Why would I lie about that, Leslie? I killed my son on the operating table.” He draws a deep breath. “I don’t want to tell you about that.”
“Well, I just saved your life with my bare hands, and I wanna know.”
“Don’t try to take the higher moral ground,” Scofield mutters. “I never asked you to save my life.”
“Why won’t you answer my fuckin’ question?”
“I killed my son. What else is there to say?” Scofield’s breathing is ragged again. “You want me to snap or something? Start crying, tell you how sorry I am about all this, how I’ve made all these mistakes and I’ll never be able to forgive myself? I’m not going to do that. This isn’t your problem, and you don’t want it to be your problem.”
“Don’t blame me because you were too much of a coward to figure out your issues. I mean, come on, doc, what the hell are you doin’? Jesus, sometimes I think you just like to talk. If you really wanted to die you’d have gotten that nice Sig of yours and pulled the trigger already. Seriously, what the hell is this pussy shit, tryin’ to overdose on anxiety medicine like you’re a teenage girl in the psych ward? It’s embarrassing.”
“Fuck you. Don’t you dare speak to me like that.”
“I mean, what is all of this shit about? You feel bad? Is that what it is?”
“Get out.” Scofield flashes a glare. “Get the hell out of my house.”
“What happened to you? You messed up, didn’t you? With those implants. And you tried to cover your tracks.”
Scofield leans forward to face McLaughlin. “I never tried to cover my tracks,” he says. “I took the implants out of commission right after my son’s operation.”
“When I looked you up, before I was movin’ in, I heard about the big articles you published. Who was Patient H.S.?”
“Patient H.S. was my son, Leslie.” Scofield flashes an angry stare. “Patient H.S. was my son. Henry Scofield. He was my only son, and my only child.” 
“Why’d you do it?”
“The surgery? It was a last resort. We’d tried everything else.”
McLaughlin thinks. “He was born sick?”
“Yes.” Scofield hesitates. “He was born in the spring, when I’d only been practicing for a couple years. He was a beautiful kid.” There are tears welling up in his eyes. “He had my wife’s hair, jet black. He was tall for his age. I guess he got that from me. He was funny, too. He’d sit with me when I got home from work, when I was so tired I couldn’t say anything. He’d just sit with his head on my shoulder, and we…” Scofield stops. His whole body shakes with the effort not to cry. “Henry was four when we first realized he was sick. He had multiple sclerosis. The symptoms developed over time: seizures, aphasia, sensory loss, atrophy.”
“That’s one of them… neurological things, ain’t it?”
“Yeah, it’s a neurodegenerative disorder. It was a bad case. I don’t know if it was from my side or hers. It doesn’t matter. None of the treatments worked and.…” He purses his lips, tasting salt. “Around the time he got sick I’d developed this implant. You drill a burr hole, place it inside the skull. I’d tried it on patients with Parkinson’s, and I thought maybe there’d be a chance it would….” He shrugs. “I told him he didn’t have to do it. He knew how bad his prognosis was. I asked him again and again on the day of the surgery if he was sure he wanted to go through with it. He always said the same thing.” Scofield wipes a tear from his cheek and looks at his fingertips as if they belong to someone else. “He’d say, It’s gonna give me more time, isn’t it?” Scofield has his eyes trained on the sink, not blinking, hardly moving. “We started the surgery,” he continues. His voice is getting tight. “It was a long surgery, we knew that going in. We knew he was weak, but we weren’t worried. We got the opening done and we started preparing the implant. Somehow, we caused him to hemorrhage.”
“Isn’t that what doctors call, uh, convocations?”
“Complications,” Scofield corrects, almost amused. “Yes, it was. While he was still on the table, he started seizing. Then he… then he went into a coma. We kept him in the hospital, on a ventilator. He stayed on the ventilator for two years. After Allison and I split up, I was the one who removed his life support.” He takes a long drag from his cigarette. “I was the best surgeon in the state, maybe in the country. It wasn’t enough. I know death is part of my job. They gave us that lecture in the first week of med school. But it was different when it was him. I loved him more than anything else in the world. He was it. He was everything. And it wasn’t enough.”
The bathroom gets quiet again. The evening has long since faded to night. The hands on Scofield’s watch are beginning to glow. From the bedroom, McLaughlin can hear birdsong, intermingling with the sounds of wind. A tiny sliver of Scofield’s unmade bed cuts the bathroom mirror in two. The shadows on the sink are deep and tangible, blue and green like the veins in the doctor’s arm. 
“I heard about another name when I was lookin’ you up,” McLaughlin says.
“Curtis,” Scofield supplies, resting his hands on his knees. “Christina Curtis. She was a resident at Duke back in the eighties. She worked with me on the implant project.”
“And then she left Duke.”
“Yes, in 1983.”
“Why?”
“Towards the end of the project, I caught her falsifying data, lying about patient outcomes. I threatened to tell the head of the hospital and have her kicked out. But it wasn’t a huge concern of mine. It should’ve been, but I was too focused on my son to worry about trying to diagnose Curtis.” He takes another drag. “It only became a concern of mine when she lied about the surgery.”
McLaughlin looks over. “Curtis was there when your son died?”
“No, no. She was on a different case that day. But she told the head of the project that the surgery had gone well.”
“Oh, Jesus.” 
“Yeah. When I found out, I was furious. I nearly tried to kill her. By the time the project head had gotten wind of it, the rumors had jeopardized the hospital’s reputation, my career, my marriage. I had no idea what to do with any of it. There was nothing I could do. All these things you hear about happening to other people, but you never think they’ll happen to you.”
There’s a pause. “And that was when she left?”
“The hospital didn’t want any more press — neither did the government — so they stripped her license and sent her to a lab out in Hillsborough. And I stayed at Duke, and kept doing my work.” Scofield lets his head fall back against the wall, rolling his cigarette between his forefinger and thumb. “And here we are.”
“And when did you fuck her?”
Scofield sighs. “Tactful as always, Leslie. That was before my son died. It was late, and we’d been drinking. I never thought of her like that. I’m not even sure how it happened.”
“You must have liked her a little bit, if you ended up in bed with her.”
“That’s what Allison thought too. I did like her, at first. I liked her mind.”
“You liked her mind?” McLaughlin sounds confused.
“Yes. How could I not? It was astounding, the way she thought about things. It was like seeing this entire world that I never could have imagined. If I ever loved her, it was her mind that I loved. It all came down to that. It wasn’t so much that she was smart. I mean, she was. But there was something else. She was ruthless. Everything mattered so much to her. I guess I saw myself in her. She was young, and she was desperate to prove herself, and she was willing to go further than most.”
Finally, Scofield turns away. His pager is still in his pocket, but he is off this weekend. He timed it nicely. Taylor wouldn’t have wondered. The nurses wouldn’t have asked questions. He would’ve sat at the head of his overpriced dining table and slowly slumped in his chair, his head rolling back, exposing his neck to the hungry air. And two days would have passed, and then three, and Eleanor would’ve noticed the dark windows and called the police, and they would’ve found his body, stiff and greying, in that same chair. But it is only Friday night, and he is still alive, still breathing. As he’s turning back, McLaughlin catches his arm, the sleeve of his button-up. He studies Scofield for a minute: his tired eyes, his strong nose, his sweaty hair. With his other hand, he touches Scofield’s cheek, grazes his thumb over a freckle on his chin. 
“Don’t start,” Scofield says, but it happens anyway, the coming together, the roughness. The acidic sting on Scofield’s teeth. It isn’t calculated. McLaughlin holds Scofield’s face in his big hands, as close as he can get. It isn’t close enough. Scofield doesn’t let go. Then the fall, the sharp chill of the floor, the hairs on McLaughlin’s arms standing up, and he keeps his eyes set on Scofield’s. 
“Am I hurting you?”
McLaughlin shakes his head. He’s short of breath, digging his nails into Scofield’s arm. His head is heavy on the floor. The tiles stick to his skin as he shifts, pulling on his back. Scofield, one hand by McLaughlin’s shoulder, feels it too, the glass like glue on his palm. He sees his shadow reflected in the tiles near McLaughlin’s body, blurring as he moves. He likes the sounds McLaughlin makes, those surrendering sounds, and likes the way his face is changing. Gasping, McLaughlin reaches for Scofield, but can’t get his hand in the right place, and Scofield lifts him up, gripping a fistful of hair and drawing his head back till the veins snake around his neck. 
“Good,” he says. “Good.”
Their faces are level. They don’t kiss. McLaughlin tries to speak, but can’t say anything.
“Okay.” Scofield touches his face. “Stay with me. Look at me.”
Then he feels it, as he brings McLaughlin in, and he holds his hand up. His fingers are covered in blood. It drips down his palm to his wrist. It coats McLaughlin’s neck like a collar. And on McLaughlin’s face, a blank look, his eyes glassy and wide. 
“Leslie,” Scofield says. “Leslie, what’s going on?”
He sees it, following the trail up the side of McLaughlin’s cheek. The incision, splitting McLaughlin’s scalp in two, his hair matted with blood and his brain pulsing beneath, bright and smooth like velvet. 
“Oh my God,” Scofield says. “Fuck. I don’t…”
McLaughlin slips back and falls on the tiles. His head rolls on his neck and settles at a sickening angle. His mouth hangs open, like he still has something else to say. And Scofield looks down at his hands and sees a scalpel. 
“Oh, God.”
When he sits up, he feels the sheets under his palms. 
The room is dark, and he is alone. 



“Wouldn’t he have known about the surgery? I mean, you gotta report those things to the hospital, don’t you?”
“Everyone in the hospital knew, sure. But we reported to the government.”
“No shit. Some DARPA shit?”
“Something like that.”
“Any chance you remember the name of the guy who ran the project?”
Scofield screws up his face. “Man. One of them had a really unusual name… Singleterry, that was it. Lionel Singleterry. And Daniel Erhart.”

In the kitchen, he sorts through the week’s mail. Bills, advertisements, invitations, subscriptions. He throws most of it out, junk or not. One catches his eye. Gage Construction Services. He hasn’t had work done on the house since the early nineties. 
Sipping his coffee, he tears the envelope open. 
“*Enclosed is a bill for the sum of two hundred dollars*…” He nearly spits out his coffee. “Two hundred dollars? Jesus Christ… *to be paid to Michael McLaughlin*.” A knowing smile comes across his face. He tosses the letter aside and makes his way to his study.
“Okay, Detective,” he says. “Two can play that game.”

## *Scofield and McLaughlin hash it out.

“You billed me for a new fuckin’ Ferrari?”
McLaughlin stands in the rain, water coursing down his face. He’s still got his badge and cuffs on his heavy belt. His holsters hang around his shoulders, tugging on the fabric of his white shirt. He wipes the water from his eyes and blinks. 
In the doorway, Scofield raises his eyebrows and smiles. “What did you expect? You charged me for your damages, so I charged you for mine. Eye for an eye, Detective.”
“I’m gon’ tell you where you can stick your fuckin’ damages, doc.”
“Yeah, I’m sure you are,” Scofield mutters.
“All right, I’ve had enough of your whinin’. You listen to me.”
Scofield spreads his hands. “I’m all ears.”
“Fuck you. You ran over my mailbox. That was your fault.”
“Absolutely. And I apologized more times than I can remember. But you didn’t want —”
“Oh, I didn’t want?” McLaughlin steps in, brandishing an accusatory finger. “When I get done with you, doc, your own family ain’t gon’ be able to recognize you. I’m gonna give you a new fuckin’ face. Forget your Ferrari, I —”
But Scofield is looking past him, over his shoulder. He squints. His brow wrinkles, his glasses shift on his nose. He brings his hand up to his face. “Do you ride?”
McLaughlin scoffs. “Excuse me?”
“The Harley in your garage.” Scofield points. “You forgot to close the garage door.”
“Shit. God damn it. Of course I fuckin’… Hold on.”
“Sure thing. Look.” He stops McLaughlin before he can leave. “I’ll pay for the stupid mailbox. Let’s forget about this. I hate fighting. Go take care of your garage. This isn’t worth it.”
McLaughlin nods. He takes off down the driveway at a run, and Scofield watches him as he dashes across the street. There’s a small sense of relief — at least one thing’s taken care of. 
Inside, he sinks into the couch in the living room and pulls out the new edition *Nature Neuroscience*. Buffeting against the windows, the sound of the rain is almost distracting. He likes it. The first article is about attentional capture — too scientific. He flips to the second article, a study of neural stem cells. One of the authors is an old friend, a colleague from forgotten days. A big graph of an fMRI punctuates the page. He digs in. 
As he reaches the end of the abstract, he hears a knock on the door. He bookmarks his place and traipses across the room. 
It’s McLaughlin.
“What?” Scofield says, a little annoyed.
“Happen to have a towel in this fine establishment of yours?” McLaughlin peaks past him and scans the hall. Bored, he leans a hand on the doorway.
“A towel? You don’t have your own?”
McLaughlin clicks his tongue. “Seem to have misplaced them.”
“And by misplaced them, you mean you left them in the laundry?”
“Just let me come in, for fuck’s sake. I’ve been wantin’ a tour ever since I moved down here. Don’t act like you don’t have the most ’spensive place within these hundred miles. Eleanor told me when she came over with her housewarmin’ cakes that you keep up a nice damn place. Big paintings and oriental rugs.”
Sighing, Scofield shrugs. He gestures to the entryway behind him. “Okay, Detective, be my guest.”
McLaughlin brushes past him, stopping just off the edge of the welcome mat, looking up at the Tiffany chandelier. He runs his hands through his hair and shakes some of the water out, smattering drops across the wall. Scofield pinches the bridge of his nose. 
Oblivious, McLaughlin continues on his odyssey. He picks through the kitchen drawers, selects an old cleaning cloth, and towels off his hair and his arms. With this cursory task accomplished, he flings the cloth over his shoulder and surveys the kitchen. A mug stands on the counter — McLaughlin spins it around to read the Duke Neurosurgery emblem imprinted on the opposite side. Scofield’s morning coffee. McLaughlin downs the last sip. 
“Big drinker?” McLaughlin teases.
“Of coffee, yes.”
“Figures.” He walks over, sits down on the midcentury loveseat, and begins to untie his boots. McLaughlin’s boots are big and heavy, the kind only a veteran or a forester would wear. And they are dirty. The carpet — spotless when Scofield first came home — is slowly but steadily beginning to turn brown. One by one, he discards the boots by his feet, then sits back, a satisfied smile gleaming on his face. Now that the raindrops have begun to dry on his skin, Scofield can see the scars snaking over his arms: at least ten, maybe twenty, running from his shoulders to his hands. One decorates his collarbone, the edge just visible above his shirt. Another lies on his chin, dark and feathery.
“Fuck you lookin’ at?” McLaughlin says. 
“Admiring the view,” Scofield quips, picking his article back up.
His guest grins. “Must be off work today, huh?”
“Yeah, I’m back on call next weekend.”
“Any plans tonight?”
Scofield shakes his head. “Not really. I was hoping to get through this.” He waves the journal in his hand. “But it’s been lying on my coffee table since July. I don’t think it’s in the cards for me.”
“Seein’ anyone?”
“Today? No. Just you, I guess.”
“And tomorrow?” The grin flashes across McLaughlin’s face again. 
“Oh, you meant… Right. No, I’m not seeing anyone. I think I’m past that. Even if I wasn’t, I’m far too busy.” Silence for a moment. “What about you? You’re not married, are you?”
McLaughlin throws back his head and laughs, a hearty, warm laugh. “Do I look fuckin’ married to you?”
“Kids?”
“No, sir.” He makes a slashing motion across his neck and clicks his tongue. “I ain’t the type for kids, don’t think. I’d be a real bad influence.”
“What makes you say that?”
“Do I seem like a good influence?”
“Well…” Scofield finds himself laughing. “Not… exactly, no. But you’re much nicer to be around when you aren’t angry, I’ll give you that.”
The detective shifts his weight, spreading his arms on the back of the loveseat. “Likewise, doc.”
“All right, my turn to play shrink. Why’d you move down here?”
McLaughlin sits forward again, rests his arms on his knees. “I ain’t got some long impressive story. I graduated high school, no plans to go to college. Small town Texas, all my buddies joined up. Figured I would too. Seemed like a smart thing to do. I spent ten years in the army, climbed the ranks. Tough stuff, but I liked it. Then I got had an accident.”
“An accident?” Boyish curiosity brightens Scofield’s face.
“Yeah, shoulder. Anyway, I was out of commission. They sent me home, and I got a job with law enforcement, detective stuff, you know. Then I moved into homicide.” He whistles. “Nearly put me six feet under. Worked in homicide for the past, uh, that’d be twenty years. Last fall, I’d got real sick of it. I’d been workin’ up in Maryland but wanted to move somewhere smaller. Wanted to go back to the south, back home, but not *home* home, you know? So I moved this spring. Got a job with the force here — same stuff, but fewer cases that end up on the national news. Keeps me occupied so I don’t go stir crazy, lose what’s left of my damn mind.”
“And how is it?”
“Pretty good, doc, pretty good. Can’t complain. I got a damn good life, been very fortunate.”
Scofield makes a face. He opens his mouth to say something, but can’t. “Good,” he finally replies.
“You ain’t been so lucky, huh?”
“Let’s not do that.”
“Doctor’s orders, huh? Here, I got one for you. What’s the difference between God and a surgeon?”
“Christ… pass. God makes mistakes?”
“God doesn’t think he’s a surgeon.”

“I’m real sorry.”
“I know.” He lets go.

“She was.”
McLaughlin taps his ashes on the table. “Did you ever think she might have…”
“I thought of it, yes. But I don’t think she had it in her to do something like that. Christ, who knows. Maybe she did. It doesn’t matter now.” He gets up, puts out his cigarette. “Look, I need to turn in.”
“Like hell you do.”
“I’m too tired for this. Take anything from the fridge you want, just don’t bother me.”

“Oh, come on. You said it yourself, I’m too much of a coward to ever pull the trigger, McLaughlin. You can’t fix me, if that’s what you’re hoping.”

Scofield wrinkles his nose. “Not from you.”

McLaughlin’s stubble scratches against his chin. It’s a kiss they’ll never forget.

Scofield squints. “I can’t see a thing.” He fumbles for McLaughlin’s lips. 
“That’s what your hands are for, doc. You cut people open with these hands, huh?”
“Want me to cut you open?” He traces a line through McLaughlin’s hair. “Nice little incision. I could see your mind. Your heart.” A quick kiss. “I could see inside of you. I want to see inside of you. I want to be inside of you.”
They come together again.

But it wasn’t a huge concern of mine. It should’ve been, but I was too focused on my son to worry about trying to diagnose Curtis.” He takes another drag. “It only became a concern of mine when she lied about the surgery.”
McLaughlin looks over. “Curtis was there when your son died?”
“No, no. She was on a different case that day. But she told the head of the project — we reported to the government — that the surgery had gone well.”
“Oh, Jesus.”
“You’re telling me. When I found out, I was furious. I nearly tried to kill her. By the time the project head had gotten wind of it, the rumors had jeopardized the hospital’s reputation, my career, my marriage. I had no idea what to do with any of it. There was nothing I could do. All these things you hear about happening to other people, but you never think they’ll happen to you.”
There’s a pause. “And that was when she left?”
“The hospital didn’t want any more press — neither did the government — so they stripped her license and sent her to a lab out in Hillsborough. And I stayed at Duke, and kept doing my work.” Scofield lets his head fall back against the wall, rolling his cigarette between his forefinger and thumb. “And here we are.”
“And when did you fuck her?”
Scofield chuckles. “Tactful as always. That was before my son died. It was late, and we’d been drinking. I never saw her like that. I’m not even sure how it happened, but it did.”
“You must have liked her a little bit, if you ended up in bed with her.”
“That’s what Allison thought too. I did like her, at first. I liked her mind.”
“You liked her mind?” The concept is foreign to McLaughlin. 
“Yes. How could I not? It was astounding, the way she thought about things. It was like seeing this entire world that I never could have imagined. If I ever loved her, it was her mind that I loved. It all came down to that. It wasn’t so much that she was smart. I mean, she was. But there was something else. She was ruthless. Everything mattered so much to her. I guess I saw myself in her. She was young, and she was desperate to prove herself, and she was willing to go further than most.”
“Further than you?”
“Further than any doctor I’d ever met. She was dangerous.” Finally, he turns away. His pager is still in his pocket, but he is off this weekend. He timed it nicely. Taylor wouldn’t have wondered. The nurses wouldn’t have asked questions. He would’ve sat at the head of his overpriced dining table and slowly slumped in his chair, his head rolling back, exposing his neck to the hungry air. And two days would have passed, and then three, and Eleanor would’ve noticed the dark windows and called the police, and they would’ve found his body, stiff and greying, in that same chair. But it is only Friday night, and he is still alive, still breathing. It will be a long, miserable weekend. 
“I wanted to help her,” he says. “Just like I wanted to help my son. I thought that was my job, helping people. But after everything that happened I didn’t know what my job was. I still don’t. Whatever it was, I’d failed, in every way I possibly could.” He turns to face McLaughlin. “I don’t want to keep living like this. Believe me, it’s as miserable as it looks. But if I move on, I lose him. And I don’t want to lose my son. He’s all I have. Those memories are all I have. If I don’t remember him, that’s it. And who else will?”
