## *Dr. Scofield meets the Texan, who is unfortunately real.*

Scofield turns up the volume on the speakers in his Ferrari F50. It’s five thirty in the morning, he’s got a fresh pair of scrubs on under his North Face fleece, and *Smooth Operator* is playing on the radio. His first case is scheduled for seven-fifteen: a basic resection. It shouldn’t go longer than three hours. These quick surgeries are his favorites, when the incision takes longer than the actual procedure. He loves solving simple problems.
He shifts gears, reverses out from under the awning. This time of day is Scofield’s favorite: empty, quiet, sun just slipping out above the trees. No one else is awake. He can enjoy it alone. 
*No need to ask, he’s a…*
In the passenger seat, his Nokia buzzes. It’s Taylor. *Bonnie brought cupcakes, daughter’s bday.* Chuckling, he reaches over to reply. The car shudders. Over his shoulder, he hears the sound of scraping, then something crumbling. He glances out the back window. His Ferrari has introduced itself to the neighbor’s mailbox. 
Slapping his palm on the steering wheel, he mutters something to himself. He takes the car out of reverse, puts his foot on the gas. He’ll deal with it later. 
“Hey!”
He stops the car. In the rear view mirror, he sees the neighbor approaching from across the lawn. 
The Texan, as Scofield refers to him, is a recent addition. His house, once a new build, stood unoccupied for eight lonely years. But with how dark the Texan keeps it, the garden a mess and the blinds always drawn, it might as well still be empty. Scofield saw the sign on the lawn, saw the moving truck, even saw the Texan sitting at his dining table with a cigarette and a book, but he was half-convinced the man was a figment of his imagination. To his dismay, the Texan is real. 
“Hey! What the hell?” The Texan comes up to the car and knocks on the window. “Why don’t you roll this fuckin’ thing down? Hear me?”
Scofield hears him. 
Leaning down, the Texan rests his hands on the top of the car door. “Fuck you think you’re doin’?”
“Look, I’m in a rush. I need to be in the operating room in thirty minutes. I’ll come—”
“I don’t give a damn where you need to be. I’m fixin’ to make myself breakfast and you come down here and plow through my fucking mailbox.”
“Sir, I—”
“It’s McLaughlin, Major McLaughlin.”
“Nick.” Scofield holds out his hand.
“I ain’t shakin’ your fuckin’ hand, doc.”
“Listen, Major, I’m very sorry about your mailbox. Believe me, I could do without the bills for the car. I’ll come by tomorrow morning when my cases are done.”
McLaughlin scoffs. “Tomorrow mornin’? You fuckin’ kiddin’ me?”
“I work the night shift at Duke.”
“Yeah, I heard all about you and your night shifts. Heard you come back at the ass crack of dawn blaring your fuckin’ Foreigner. Who the hell listens to Foreigner anymore?”
Scofield rolls his eyes. “Anything else?”
“Oh, wanna play that game? Go to hell.”
“I have a patient in pre-op who has a high-grade glioma pressing on her frontal lobe,” Scofield lies. He’s a good storyteller, if nothing else. “If I don’t get to that operating room by six, my team will not be able to perform this surgery. You can’t just postpone a craniotomy because of a neighborhood dispute, Major.”
“A neighborhood dispute? You ran over my mailbox at five-thirty in the fuckin’ mornin’ and you’re callin’ this a neighborhood dispute? I don’t give a shit about your top-grade… whatever the hell you said.”
“High-grade glioma,” Scofield says. “It’s an aggressive brain tumor.”
“Couldn’t care less.”
Scofield throws up his hands. “Take it up with my boss, then. I’m leaving. If you’re willing to have a civil conversation, I’ll come back tomorrow morning.”
“Like hell you will, doc. Are all surgeons assholes or is it just you?”
“Just me,” Scofield says, flashing an unamused smile. “Sorry about the mailbox, Major.”
