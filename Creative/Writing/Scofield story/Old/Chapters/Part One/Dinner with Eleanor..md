## ***Eleanor and Scofield get dinner.***

Scofield turns over in bed. It’s still early — not long past six thirty, he thinks. Had this been a weekday, his alarm would have gone off two hours ago. Instead, today is a Saturday. He won’t have to see the inside of the hospital until Monday morning. On his left, half hidden by big linen curtains, the sun is coming through the window. Autumn hasn’t set in quite yet — the days are still bright, still warm. Lying on his back, he holds his hand up to the light. His fingers glow red, almost transparent, fragile and alive. He’s held the hands of hundreds of infants in the hospital, felt their tiny fingers clenching his palm, seen their wide eyes and bubbly smiles. Whatever it is that they have at that age, they always lose it, and they never get it back. 
On his bedside table, his cellphone begins to buzz, sliding across the mahogany like it’s trying to escape. Scofield reaches over, fumbles for it, and clumsily picks it up. The contact displayed on the miniature screen reads TAYLOR ANESTHESIOLOGY. He’s not one for nicknames.
“Hello?” he says, putting the phone up to his ear. His voice is still hoarse.
There’s a pause. “Nick, it’s five-fifteen in the afternoon.”
Scofield stops. He sits up, the sheets falling from his body. “You’re kidding me.” He pulls the phone away to check the time. 5:14. “Shit,” he says, as insightful as he’s ever been. 
“Are you still in bed? What the hell are you doing?”
“I… I guess I was tired. What are *you* doing? Why are you calling?”
“Just looking through this week’s files. I sent you a note in Outlook Express.” The software has been a novelty ever since it arrived at the hospital, with its convenient drop-down menus and endearingly pixelated symbols. 
“You want me to check e-mail on my Saturday off? Come on, George. You’ve got to be kidding me.”
Taylor scoffs, the noise fragmented in the tiny Nokia speaker. “I don’t know if you’ve heard, but you’ve got this cool thing at Duke called a job,” he says. Scofield can picture his grin. “Some asshole pays you to run the neurosurgery department.”
“Okay, okay,” Scofield interjects. “Point taken. How’re the girls?” 
Between his wife and his two daughters, Taylor is the only man in his family. He isn’t sure whether Scofield refers to them as the girls because it’s easier, or because he doesn’t remember their names. Knowing Scofield, he thinks it’s the latter. 
“They’re good,” Taylor says. “Lila’s out with her boyfriend, that kid from Shanghai. I don’t know what it is about him, but I don’t like that kid. And before you say anything, it’s not because he’s Asian.”
Scofield laughs. “I wasn’t going to say anything.”
“And Meredith had a game earlier today, which went pretty well.” Taylor’s youngest daughter is the goalkeeper on her middle school’s soccer team. A picture of her, with a big grin and a bigger trophy, stands on the mantlepiece in Taylor’s suburban home. 
“And the wife?”
“Well, you know what they say. Happy wife, happy life, right?”
“Something like that,” agrees Scofield, pulling a white undershirt on over his slacks. He passes by the mirror and desperately tries to comb through his hair, the phone still pinched between his shoulder and ear. “What’s the deal with the case? Some kind of issue with the file? Don’t tell me we’re going to have to contact admin again.”
On the other end of the line, Taylor chuckles. “No, no need for that. Just an error in the file. Probably a typo. When you log into Outlook, you’ll see it. Get it back to me tonight, if you can.”
Dipping his fingers under the faucet, hoping the water has gotten warmer, Scofield splashes his face in the sink. “Jesus, I’ll do my best. I haven’t even eaten anything, George.”
“So it’s bad when I skip lunch for a surgery, but you can rot in bed all day?”
“I’m hanging up.”
“You need a woman in your life, Nick. You need —”
“I need another woman in my life like I need a hole in my head.” Scofield touches the small freckle on his chin. His fingers are soft, but his skin is tough. A little sunburnt around the cheeks, and wrinkles, too many wrinkles, around his brow and his eyes. 
“For fuck’s sake, Nick. Don’t —”
“I’m hanging up.”
“Don’t let this shit kill you,” Taylor says, but the words barely get out before Scofield ends the call. He puts the silver cell phone on his desk, above a book he hasn’t read, and checks the time. 5:20 now. The numbers on his watch are faint, they’re wavering. He can feel the engraving on the back, *I’ll always come back to you.* Why keep the watch? Why use it every day, wear it to work? He keeps asking himself the same question, but never has an answer. The mind of a surgeon: good with questions, bad with answers. Someday, he thinks, he will fix this. 

For now, someone is knocking on the door. He hopes it is not the Texan, who has been sending him increasingly outrageous bills for his mailbox: first an invoice from Home Depot, now a note for emotional damages. Scofield finds it hard to believe that an army veteran born and bred in the deep south has ever experienced emotional damages, but he supposes stranger things have happened. 
Already in the foyer, he sees his guest — the strong face, the red hair. She’s wearing blue jeans and a beige blouse with the sleeves rolled up almost to her elbows. 
“Eleanor,” he says, opening the door. 
“Hi,” she says. “Is this a good time?”

As far as he can remember, Scofield has never been inside his neighbor’s house. It is small, but comfortable. Even the entrance is inviting, built in the age when the highlight of the evening was sitting at the dining table with Doris Day on the radio. The light is warm — an orange glow is cast by glass lamps that must have belonged to Douglasses of years past. He can imagine the progenitors standing in the yellow kitchen, smiling, traversing the rooms to straighten picture frames and gaze through windows. A staircase, nestled in the corner of his eye, entices him. Eleanor catches him looking, and he laughs.
“Sorry,” he says. “Little out of practice here.”
“Oh, it’s all right. I hope you don’t mind my asking you over.”
He shakes his head. “Not at all.”
“I know this is out of the blue. I know we haven’t talked in years, really. The other day doesn’t count, I don’t think. This is my off weekend, and I saw your car in the driveway and I figured you were probably off too, and, I don’t know, I haven’t really seen anyone in so long. You know how it gets, these long days.”
“Yeah,” he says, nodding through a mouthful of pasta. “Yeah, I do. You, uh, have a lot of doctors over for dinner?”
“No, never.” She’s laughing. “I just didn’t know what to do with myself.”
“You’re telling me.”
Eleanor studies his face. The eyes, blue like a child’s, set below the dark brows, shining in his little silver glasses. The confidence in his mouth, the shadows on his jaw and chin. All over his face is this sorrow, this regret, this fatigue, so big it touches each of his features in a different way. You can’t look at him without seeing it. She likes that about him. Not the sadness so much, but how he carries it. 
“Where’s Tony?”
“Probably out with his little girlfriend. I think they’re working on their science project together. He keeps saying he’s going to bring her over so they can play computer games, but I think he always chickens out.”
“Ah, I know that feeling.”
They sit in silence for a minute. Their forks scrape over their plates, slipping on the ridges in the china. The food is good, unsophisticated. Scofield can cook, but not well, and has gotten too used to eating hospital lunches with his surgical mask still around his neck. 


*Eleanor knocks and invites Scofield to dinner.*
*Later, one of Eleanor’s colleagues tries to find Scofield on sixdegrees, and finds a woman in Scotland with the same name.*
