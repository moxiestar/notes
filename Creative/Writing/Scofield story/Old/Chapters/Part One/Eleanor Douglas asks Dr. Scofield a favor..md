## ***Eleanor Douglas asks if Scofield will meet with Tony.***

He sits bolt upright in bed, gasping, hand on his chest. He’s at home. None of it is real. He sweeps the room, expecting to see the IV bags and the monitors, to feel the blood rising at his hips. Instead he sees the hardwood floors, the Turkish carpet, last night’s pair of scrubs. The Matisse on the wall. The lines of early morning window light falling on the dresser. 
Sighing, he falls back against his pillows and shut his eyes. 
“Dr. Scofield?” 
The voice is coming from the foyer: probably his neighbor. Emma? Eloise? Something feminine to that effect. He’s never been good with names. Leaning over, he checks his pager, his watch. It’s Saturday. He’s off the trauma shift. 
As he stumbles through the kitchen, buttoning his pajamas, he tries to figure it out. Emmaline, or whatever it is, hasn’t shown her face in months. A year, even. They introduced themselves back when they moved in, used to have nice family dinners together. Now things are different. He sees her through the window, her reflection mirrored in his face, and waves sometimes. But they never speak. Why now? A noise complaint? He vaguely remembers pulling into the driveway at three in the morning, groaning as he discovered a scratch on his Alfa Romeo. He hopes she isn’t trying to rekindle a friendship, forcing a connection out of convenience. 
Rubbing his eyes, he opens the door. It is his neighbor, just as he feared — a short, tough woman with long reddish hair pulled back from her face. Her face has hardened in an unusually appealing way: a strong mouth and deep, patient eyes. Under a taupe Patagonia fleece, she’s wearing a pair of custom pink scrubs. 
“Dr. Scofield,” she says, smiling. She’s got that slow Eastern North Carolina accent. 
“Ah,” he says. “Emily?”
“Eleanor. Eleanor Douglas.”
“Right. Good morning.”
“It’s 2pm.”
“Is it?” He checks his watch again. “So it is. I thought… never mind.” He purses his lips. “What brings you… excuse me, but what brings you over?”
“Mind if I come in?” She peeks over his shoulder at the expansive living room beyond. 
He hesitates. “Sure.”
Smiling her easy smile, she slips past him into the foyer. There are paintings by artists she doesn’t know, tables that cost more than her monthly salary, lamps designed by European architects with names like Carl or Alfred. More than anything, there are artifacts. A human skull, an old scalpel, a textbook by John Fulton, a huge print-out of a cranial MRI. 
“You’re still at Duke, aren’t you?”
Scofield nods. “That’s right. Can I get you anything? Coffee?”
“Cigarette would be great, if you don’t mind.”
“Where are you working now? UNC? Sit anywhere, by the way. Make yourself at home.” 
“Yeah, I work nights in the ER.”
Eleanor chooses a chair, the one that looks the least like a sculpture and the most like a piece of furniture. She sits with her hands folded in her lap and watches as Scofield makes himself a cup of coffee. A heavy pour. She wonders if the rumors are true. On a glass table nearby, an issue of the American Journal of Psychology lies atop a neuroanatomy fold-out textbook. She cracks it open and flips through the colorful pages. 
“You sure have a lot of books,” she says, feeling vastly undereducated. 
He shrugs. “I haven’t read most of them. My colleague asked me to read that journal two years ago. Haven’t touched it.” With his coffee in one hand, he takes a pack of Camels off the end of the kitchen counter and tosses it to her. 
She lights one, continues through gritted teeth: “Too busy?”
“Something like that.”
“Have you always been a neurosurgeon?”
“You can’t exactly change your mind halfway through a career in medicine, can you? You’d know.” He takes a sip of his coffee, wincing. Too hot. “Yes, I went straight through from undergraduate to residency. If I’d known you’d be interviewing me, I would’ve at least put on my white coat.”
She laughs. “You’re not from around here, are you? At least, you don’t sound like it. You and… Allison, wasn’t it… moved from up north, didn’t you?”
“I grew up in Connecticut.”
“Then why Duke?”
Scofield takes another sip. The coffee’s cooled down. “I started off in epilepsy research. Duke has a great department, and… Allison and I had just gotten married, and we didn’t want to stay at Yale.”
“And does your wife still work at Duke too?”
He lowers his eyes, makes a face. “She did, back then. We separated.”
“Sorry, I didn’t—”
He waves her apologies away. “It happens. What about you, E…”
“Eleanor.”
“Eleanor, right. Married?”
“No, not really my style. Besides, I’ve already got my nephew runnin’ around with his computers and things. I don’t need another man cluttering up my house.”
Scofield chuckles. “Can’t blame you.”
“Look, Dr. Scofield, I came to ask you about my nephew, actually. He lives with me, you know, has for a while. I guess you might’ve met him before, back when we all used to have dinner together. He’s a good kid, but he’s got too many ideas. He says he wants to be an engineer. Ever since those new computers came out, he’s been taking every appliance in the house apart and putting them back together, only they’re always different after he’s done with them.”
“There’s never been a better time to be an aspiring engineer. Your nephew’s lucky.”
“I guess it is. That’s what everybody’s saying. It’s just too far out of my depth, that’s all. I’d help him myself if I could — I’ve tried to — but he outsmarted me when he was still in diapers. It took me two months to figure out how to operate the new computer at the front desk, the one the circulator uses.” She laughs, and she thinks she can see the corner of Scofield’s mouth twitching too. “Everything works better on paper, if you ask me,” she adds.
Scofield shrugs. 
“Sorry, I’m rambling.” She clasps her hands around her knees and takes a deep breath. “Enough technology talk. I’ve been wondering if you might be willing to help my nephew. Let him come over on the weekends and talk about science. I know you aren’t an engineer, not exactly, but you did all that work with implants. You know more than I do, in any case. It wouldn’t have to be long, just an hour or so. He’s a nice kid.”
“Well, I’m on call most weekends,” he says. “You know how it is, working in the hospital. Time off is just a fantasy.” He hesitates, looking past his guest. It’s been almost a decade since he’s seen the Douglas boy. In his mind, he has an image of bright eyes, an earnest smile. He feels a pang of guilt.
“It wouldn’t have to take up much of your time,” Eleanor says. “I don’t mean to put you on the spot, but he really looks up to you.”
“All right,” Scofield concedes, “I’ll see what I can do. What did you say his name was?”
