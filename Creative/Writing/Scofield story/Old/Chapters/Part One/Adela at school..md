## **His name is Tony.

“Hi, Tony.” Adela shoots Tony Douglas an awkward smile and hikes her backpack up by the straps. She’s got her big combat boots on, her faded Alice in Chains t-shirt. Her thick hair is pulled back, held in place by a star-shaped clip. In the old school hallway, with her bag weighing her down, she looks small and angry, like a hermit crab with an oversized shell.
Tony isn’t much taller than Adela. His hair is reddish like his aunt’s, primly combed back from his face. He’s got a pair of Buddy Holly glasses, scotch-taped in the middle, and an orange sweater over his button-up and his khakis. 
“Good weekend?” he says, pushing his glasses up on his nose. 
Adela purses her lips. “Yeah, not bad.”
He waits for her to ask him about his weekend. She doesn’t. “Me too,” he says, walking a little faster to try to keep up. “I was thinking about Mr. Tsao’s science project.” He’s been practicing his assertiveness since his aunt gave him a book on public speaking last year, but this is way above his pay grade. “I was thinking maybe we could be partners. We could maybe work on it together. I have a bunch of supplies and stuff at my house. Eleanor wouldn’t mind having you over.”
Adela smiles. “Thanks, but I’d rather work alone.”
“I could help you come up with an idea. I have a lot of practice with engineering. I mean, not that you need my help.” He laughs. “Uh, Mr. Tsao said we could get extra credit if we work in pairs.”
“Extra credit?” Adela’s heard that one before. It’s one of her mother’s favorite phrases.
“Up to five bonus points on our final.”
“Five?” She hesitates. “Okay, I’m in.”
Tony pumps his fist in the air. “It’s gonna be great. I’ve got this whole setup at home. You’re not gonna believe it. I could probably make a human cyborg with all that stuff.”
Adela stops. 
“What?”
“Nothing, just thinking.”
Over their heads, the bell starts to ring. Tony checks his Casio. “Meet me at lunch?”
Adela’s already walking away down the hall. “Yeah, sure.” She waves over her shoulder. 
Tony waves back. She doesn’t see him. 

## **Lila yells at Adela.

There are a lot of things that Adela doesn’t understand about teenage girls. She has learned through trial and error, mostly error, that there is a right and a wrong way to be a girl, and that her way is the wrong one. Sometimes she thinks that other girls were given a handbook when they came out of the womb, and only have to flip through the book to find the right answer to any of their questions. They know what to say, how to say it, when to say it. They know how big their smiles should be, what color eyeshadow they should wear. They know how to get their way. Adela, for whatever reason, must have lost her handbook somewhere in the move from Shanghai. 
If the girls at school have handbooks, Lila has the best one. Lila Taylor is a lot of things. She is tall, but not too tall; skinny, but not flat. She is smart — not book smart: she can’t spell suspicious — but people smart. She has a perfect, contagious smile, big and wide, and long, blonde, movie-star hair. Lila is the kind of girl your crush would have a crush on. 
It’s not that Adela wants to be Lila — she doesn’t. But she is nothing if not jealous, and like a horse can smell fear, Lila can smell envy. She’s standing in the hall today, flanked by a pair of less impressive clones. A pale pink sweater is draped across her collarbones, with two shapely shoulders sectioned by her bra straps. Around her neck is a gold locket, real gold: a present from her parents on her thirteenth birthday. Her dad is a doctor, the kind that puts people to sleep. More importantly, the kind that makes a lot of money. Underneath her sweater, a magenta miniskirt shapes her hips. Any other girl in that skirt would’ve gotten dress-coded before they even stepped in the door. But not Lila. 
Walking past, Adela keeps her eyes on the door. Through it, she sees Mr. Tsao, with his shaggy hair and his sweater vest, standing behind his desk, flipping through his materials. She’s been looking forward to this lecture for weeks.
“Addy,” Lila calls, just as Adela puts her foot through the doorway. 
Adela stops. “I’m not talking to you.”
“You just did.” Lila flashes a self-satisfied smile. “So, how’s your little boyfriend? You and Tony gonna put your train set in the science fair?”
“Fuck off.”
“I bet he’s gonna put a big red heart on each of the trains, just for you.” 
“You wish.” Rolling her eyes, Adela shoves her way past the girls. She can hear them laughing. 
“The only thing worse than being a nerd is being a dyke nerd,” Lila says under her breath, leaning towards one of her minions. “That girl is the biggest dyke in the school.”
Adela turns around. “I’m not a dyke.”
Lila raises her eyebrows. She scans Adela’s outfit.
“I’m not,” Adela says, shifting her weight. “Just ’cause I don’t want to spend all my time fucking random guys doesn’t mean I’m a dyke.”
A chorus of giggles rises from Lila and her friends. “Somebody’s mad,” Lila says.
Adela shrugs. “Say it to my face.”
Lila glances around, a pink haze rising on her cheeks.
“Don’t feel like it? Scared someone’s gonna hear you calling me a dyke? That wouldn’t be a good look for you. Although Thing 1 and Thing 2 here will probably have your back.” Adela gestures to the minions. The joke goes over their heads.
“Look, just drop it, okay? It was just a joke.” 
“I didn’t hear anyone laughing.” Adela turns to Lila’s second in command. “What’s your name? Stacy or something, right?”
“Hannah.”
“I’m Stacy,” the other clone adds.
Adela nods, narrowing her eyes. “Did you think it was funny, Hannah?”
Hannah offers a pathetic shrug.
“What about you, Stacy? You look like a total bitch, you probably love jokes like that.”
Stacy looks away.
“Adela, you’re totally overreacting,” Lila steps in. “If you don’t want people to think you’re a dyke, you shouldn’t dress like one. Everyone at school thinks you’re a lesbian. You look like a boy, Adela.”
“Too bad. At least I don’t look like a dumb blonde. You know what people think about you? They think you gave Mr. Skinner a handjob so you could pass U.S. history. The only thing worse than being a dumb blonde is being a dumb cunt.”
Lila’s mouth falls open. She scoffs. “You’re such a bitch.”
“Takes one to know one.”
“I am not a bitch. Last year, I was voted most likely to save a drowning kitten.”
“Who voted for you?” Adela cries. “Mr. Skinner? Stacy?”
“Oh my God,” Lila mutters. “Okay, you know what? Get out of my face. Your brother’s right, you are so annoying. I bet you’re just like your mom.”
Adela stops. She doesn’t really care if Lila calls her a dyke. She’s heard worse. And she knows that Lila’s right — her outfits do make her look like a boy. But her mother is off limits. Drawing in a long breath, she swings her arm back and punches Lila in the face. 

## **Mr. Tsao saves Adela from detention.

Leaning back in his chair, Principal Leibniz folds his hands. He isn’t a young man. He’s seen his fair share of stupid kids, bomb threats, cafeteria fights. He’s expelled ten people in the past seven years: their files are locked in one of his cream-colored cabinets, a three-course meal for the moths and the dust. But he’s never had a student quite like Adela. 
“Miss Zhang,” he begins, pronouncing her name like it rhymes with *pang*, “this is the fourth time this year you’ve picked a fight with Miss Taylor and her friends.”
Adela opens her mouth to speak, but he quickly continues. “Now, now. I’ll be the first to admit I have a limited understanding of the...” He waves his signet hand in the air, spreading his heavy fingers: “…politics of middle school girls. I’m sure you… well, I hope you have a good reason for doing what you did to Miss Taylor.”
“Yeah,” Adela breaks in, “I had a great reason.”
“Mr. Leibniz,” Leo says, leaning forward in his chair, “I’m so sorry about my sister’s behavior. I know it doesn’t look like it, but she’s going through a rough stretch right now. And she’s come a long way.”
“Oh, shut the fuck —”
Sitting in between the siblings, Mr. Tsao holds up in his hands. “Leonard, Adela. Leave it to me,” he says. “Mr. Leibniz, I know what Adela did seems unacceptable to you. But I was in the classroom the whole time. You heard what Adela said, but I heard what Lila said too. It’s Lila who should be getting detention, not Adela.”
Leibniz draws his mouth into a thin line. “Steve, I can’t just run on your hunch.”
Mr. Tsao sighs. “Lila told Adela she was a chink nerd.”
“That’s like if I called you a balding cuck,” Adela adds, flashing Leibniz a sarcastic smile. 
“Not helping,” Mr. Tsao says, sighing again. “Adela wasn’t picking a fight.”
“I find that hard to believe,” Leibniz mutters.
“Yes, I realize that. But you have to take my word. She was just trying to defend herself. These girls, they’re brutal.”
Flabbergasted, Leibniz throws up his hands. “Okay, no detention. But if this happens again —”
“You got it.” Mr. Tsao is already standing up. “Adela, let’s go. We’re late for class.”
Adela’s grinning. She picks her backpack up off the floor, punches her brother in the shoulder, and follows Mr. Tsao out the door. Leo, grimacing, slowly stands up, eyeing Leibniz like a scared dog. He shuts the door behind him, and Leibniz is left alone. He takes out a file and tosses it on the table: ZHANG, ADELA. 
“Kids,” he mutters, uncapping his ballpoint pen. “Fucking kids!”

## **Mr. Tsao’s cyborg lecture.

“Cyborgs,” says Mr. Tsao. He draws a long, steady line under the word on the blackboard. “Some of you may know that in 1960, Manfred Clynes and Nathan Kline published a book called *Cyborgs and Space.* Or, better bet, I’m guessing more than a few of you have heard *OK Computer*, right? *Paranoid Android*? Show of hands — who here can tell me what a cyborg is?”
Tony’s hand shoots up. At the back of the classroom, Adela rolls her eyes. 
“Anthony?”
“A cyborg,” Tony says, “is like an android, except it has the brain and nervous system of a human.”
Mr. Tsao nods, a small smile on his face. “That’s exactly right, Anthony.” On the board, he writes, *CYBORG: Human? Robot?* He pauses. Underneath, he writes, *Both?*
He turns back to the class. “Well? Let’s get a volunteer here. Thank you, Anthony, but we should give someone else a chance to answer. How about you, Lila?”
Lila Taylor slowly lifts her head. A white bandage blocks out the bridge of her nose. Under her desk, she snaps her pink flip phone shut. “Um,” she begins, starting off strong, “I don’t know. I think all this stuff is pretty freaky. What if they’re evil robots? They could destroy our world.”
Mr. Tsao shrugs. “It’s definitely possible. What do you think, would you want to live alongside cyborgs or robots? Thumbs up?”
A few brave students raise their hands. 
“Interesting. Thumbs down?” He nods, watching the hands fly up. “That’s what I thought. Well, thankfully for you, it’s not currently possible to create something like this. But what if it was? Let’s say a doctor and a scientist could come together, build the world’s first human cyborg. What do you think it would look like?” He waits. “A boy? A girl? Tall? Strong? Would it be a servant? A soldier?” He drums his fingers on the corner of his desk. “It would only make sense for it to be able to hear, see, talk. But could it think? Could it feel?”
Adela looks down. She tries to imagine what it would be like to not think, to not feel. She can’t.
“And that’s where the million dollar question comes in. Where do we draw the line between humans and robots?”
Someone tugs on Adela’s sleeve. She glances over. The kid who sits next to her, a skater with a tattoo of a skull on his hand, is holding out a note. She takes it. *Could we create a cyborg for the science fair??* Tony’s handwriting. From across the room, Tony shoots her a curious smile. 
Mr. Tsao clears his throat. “The bigger question, then, is, how human is human enough? Most science fiction writers agree that having a brain, or a mind, makes us human. But engineers probably think that computers have brains too. If the only human thing about a cyborg was its brain, would it still be human?”
Tony sticks his hand up. 
“Yes, Anthony?”
“Is this going to be on the exam?”
Mr. Tsao smiles. “No.”
