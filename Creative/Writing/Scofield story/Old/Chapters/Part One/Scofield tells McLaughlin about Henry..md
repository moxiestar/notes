“Somethin’ happened to you. I know your son died, read about it when I was lookin’ you up, right when I first moved in. But there ain’t much out there about him, your son. And there’s even less about you, just a bunch of medical shit, and Lord knows that’s all above my head.”
For almost a minute, Scofield is silent. He draws his lips into a thin line, his teeth catching on the slightly chapped skin. “My son didn’t die,” he says, “I killed him.”
McLaughlin scoffs. “Jesus Christ, you kiddin’?”
“Why would I lie about that, Leslie? I killed my son on the operating table.” He draws a deep breath. “I don’t want to tell you about that.”
“Well, I just saved your life with my bare hands, and I wanna know.”
“Don’t try to take the higher moral ground,” Scofield mutters. “I never asked you to save my life.”
“Why won’t you answer my fuckin’ question?”
“I killed my son. What else is there to say?” Scofield’s breathing is ragged again. “You want me to snap or something? Start crying, tell you how sorry I am about all this, how I’ve made all these mistakes and I’ll never be able to forgive myself? I’m not going to do that. This isn’t your problem, and you don’t want it to be your problem.”
“Don’t blame me because you were too much of a coward to figure out your issues. I mean, come on, doc, what the hell are you doin’? Jesus, sometimes I think you just like to talk. If you really wanted to die you’d have gotten that nice Sig of yours and pulled the trigger already. Seriously, what the hell is this pussy shit, tryin’ to overdose on anxiety medicine like you’re a teenage girl in the psych ward? It’s embarrassing.”
“Fuck you. Don’t you dare speak to me like that.”
“I mean, what is all of this shit about? You feel bad? Is that what it is?”
“Get out.” Scofield flashes a glare. “Get the hell out of my house.”
“What happened to you? You messed up, didn’t you? With those implants. And you tried to cover your tracks.”
Scofield leans forward to face McLaughlin. “I never tried to cover my tracks,” he says. “I took the implants out of commission right after my son’s operation.”
“When I looked you up, before I was movin’ in, I heard about the big articles you published. Who was Patient H.S.?”
“Patient H.S. was my son, Leslie.” Scofield flashes an angry stare. “Patient H.S. was my son. Henry Scofield. He was my only son, and my only child.” 
“Why’d you do it?”
“The surgery? It was a last resort. We’d tried everything else.”
McLaughlin thinks. “He was born sick?”
“Yes.” Scofield hesitates. “He was born in the spring, when I’d only been practicing for a couple years. He was a beautiful kid.” There are tears welling up in his eyes. “He had my wife’s hair, jet black. He was tall for his age. I guess he got that from me. He was funny, too. He’d sit with me when I got home from work, when I was so tired I couldn’t say anything. He’d just sit with his head on my shoulder, and we…” Scofield stops. His whole body shakes with the effort not to cry. “Henry was four when we first realized he was sick. He had multiple sclerosis. The symptoms developed over time: seizures, aphasia, sensory loss, atrophy.”
“That’s one of them… neurological things, ain’t it?”
“Yeah, it’s a neurodegenerative disorder. It was a bad case. I don’t know if it was from my side or hers. It doesn’t matter. None of the treatments worked and.…” He purses his lips, tasting salt. “Around the time he got sick I’d developed this implant. You drill a burr hole, place it inside the skull. I’d tried it on patients with Parkinson’s, and I thought maybe there’d be a chance it would….” He shrugs. “I told him he didn’t have to do it. He knew how bad his prognosis was. I asked him again and again on the day of the surgery if he was sure he wanted to go through with it. He always said the same thing.” Scofield wipes a tear from his cheek and looks at his fingertips as if they belong to someone else. “He’d say, It’s gonna give me more time, isn’t it?” Scofield has his eyes trained on the sink, not blinking, hardly moving. “We started the surgery,” he continues. His voice is getting tight. “It was a long surgery, we knew that going in. We knew he was weak, but we weren’t worried. We got the opening done and we started preparing the implant. Somehow, we caused him to hemorrhage.”
“Isn’t that what doctors call, uh, convocations?”
“Complications,” Scofield corrects, almost amused. “Yes, it was. While he was still on the table, he started seizing. Then he… then he went into a coma. We kept him in the hospital, on a ventilator. He stayed on the ventilator for two years. After Allison and I split up, I was the one who removed his life support.” He takes a long drag from his cigarette. “I was the best surgeon in the state, maybe in the country. It wasn’t enough. I know death is part of my job. They gave us that lecture in the first week of med school. But it was different when it was him. I loved him more than anything else in the world. He was it. He was everything. And it wasn’t enough.”
The bathroom gets quiet again. The evening has long since faded to night. The hands on Scofield’s watch are beginning to glow. From the bedroom, McLaughlin can hear birdsong, intermingling with the sounds of wind. A tiny sliver of Scofield’s unmade bed cuts the bathroom mirror in two. The shadows on the sink are deep and tangible, blue and green like the veins in the doctor’s arm. 
“I heard about another name when I was lookin’ you up,” McLaughlin says.
“Curtis,” Scofield supplies, resting his hands on his knees. “Christina Curtis. She was a resident at Duke back in the eighties. She worked with me on the implant project.”
“And then she left Duke.”
“Yes, in 1983.”
“Why?”
“Towards the end of the project, I caught her falsifying data, lying about patient outcomes. I threatened to tell the head of the hospital and have her kicked out. But it wasn’t a huge concern of mine. It should’ve been, but I was too focused on my son to worry about trying to diagnose Curtis.” He takes another drag. “It only became a concern of mine when she lied about the surgery.”
McLaughlin looks over. “Curtis was there when your son died?”
“No, no. She was on a different case that day. But she told the head of the project that the surgery had gone well.”
“Oh, Jesus.” 
“Yeah. When I found out, I was furious. I nearly tried to kill her. By the time the project head had gotten wind of it, the rumors had jeopardized the hospital’s reputation, my career, my marriage. I had no idea what to do with any of it. There was nothing I could do. All these things you hear about happening to other people, but you never think they’ll happen to you.”
There’s a pause. “And that was when she left?”
“The hospital didn’t want any more press — neither did the government — so they stripped her license and sent her to a lab out in Hillsborough. And I stayed at Duke, and kept doing my work.” Scofield lets his head fall back against the wall, rolling his cigarette between his forefinger and thumb. “And here we are.”
“And when did you fuck her?”
Scofield sighs. “Tactful as always, Leslie. That was before my son died. It was late, and we’d been drinking. I never thought of her like that. I’m not even sure how it happened.”
“You must have liked her a little bit, if you ended up in bed with her.”
“That’s what Allison thought too. I did like her, at first. I liked her mind.”
“You liked her mind?” McLaughlin sounds confused.
“Yes. How could I not? It was astounding, the way she thought about things. It was like seeing this entire world that I never could have imagined. If I ever loved her, it was her mind that I loved. It all came down to that. It wasn’t so much that she was smart. I mean, she was. But there was something else. She was ruthless. Everything mattered so much to her. I guess I saw myself in her. She was young, and she was desperate to prove herself, and she was willing to go further than most.”
