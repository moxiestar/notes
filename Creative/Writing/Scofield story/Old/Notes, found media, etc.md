## **Interview with Scofield:**

INTERVIEWER: …That’s what I think, anyway. All right, is it recording? Okay, let’s go. I’m going to be asking you a series of questions about your work at the hospital, your project, and then we’ll discuss the operation. Just for the record, Dr. Scofield, would you explain the kind of work that you do here at Duke University Hospital?
SCOFIELD: Yes, I’m a functional neurosurgeon. I perform surgeries here at Duke, and I run a clinical lab, where we focus on neural engineering. 
INTERVIEWER: And what exactly is neural engineering?
SCOFIELD: It’s exactly what it sounds like: engineering for the brain. I develop neural implants, tiny devices that are placed into a patient’s brain during surgery. These devices can alleviate the symptoms of neurological disorders, mostly movement disorders, like Parkinson’s. 
INTERVIEWER: Thank you, Dr. Scofield. It is my understanding that you collaborated with Dr. Christina Curtis to create a prototype of a neural implant?
SCOFIELD: Yes.
INTERVIEWER: When did you begin working with Dr. Curtis?
SCOFIELD: Uh, about a year ago, after she first came to Duke. She was a resident in the neurosurgical department, but she had an interest in psychology. She wanted to find a way to use the implants for psychiatric disorders, like, I don’t know, anxiety, schizophrenia. 
INTERVIEWER: Did Dr. Curtis have any psychiatric training when she began working in your lab?
SCOFIELD: Not as far as I know. 
INTERVIEWER: Did Dr. Curtis have any surgical training when she came to Duke?
SCOFIELD: Not beyond the training she’d had at medical school, which was probably nil. 
INTERVIEWER: Okay. Before Dr. Curtis joined your lab, she was part of a team of residents, right?
SCOFIELD: Yeah, there were, I don’t know, five residents on the floor at that time? She was in her first year, so she could only assist in basic procedures. I allowed her to scrub in on one of the more complex implant cases, and later that week, she came to my office to ask about my research. 
INTERVIEWER: Did she complete a formal application to join the lab?
SCOFIELD: Yes, all my researchers have to apply before they can begin work. 
INTERVIEWER: And once you had accepted her application, she had to complete training too, didn’t she?
SCOFIELD: Yes, a short course on working with animals. 
INTERVIEWER: Animals?
SCOFIELD: We test the implants on laboratory rats. 
INTERVIEWER: Ah. And do you believe she honestly completed that training? …Dr. Scofield?
SCOFIELD: Earlier I would’ve said yes, but now I’m not sure. 
INTERVIEWER: While working with Dr. Curtis, did you ever notice strange behaviors or even an altered mental state?
SCOFIELD: I don’t think so. 
INTERVIEWER: Were you aware at the time that Dr. Curtis had been formally diagnosed with borderline personality disorder and antisocial personality disorder?
SCOFIELD: No, I had no idea. 
INTERVIEWER: And you never thought there was anything off… or unusual?
SCOFIELD: No. 
INTERVIEWER: Okay. What was Dr. Curtis’ role in your lab?
SCOFIELD: Dr. Curtis cared for the animals, performed behavioral and physiological tests on the animals, collected and analyzed data… I’m sure she made us coffee, too, if that’s what you’re getting. 
INTERVIEWER: Is it true that Dr. Curtis contributed to the design of your latest implant?
SCOFIELD: Yes, she proposed several ideas for the circuitry. 
INTERVIEWER: Do residents typically assist in implant design?
SCOFIELD: Not usually, no, but I’m not necessarily opposed to it. All of our designs go through rigorous pre-clinical testing. Curtis was very interested in the project, and her ideas were sound, so I allowed her to have a hand in it. 
INTERVIEWER: And that new prototype that she helped produce, did you test it on the rats?
SCOFIELD: Yes, extensively. 
INTERVIEWER: And the results were promising?
SCOFIELD: Very. The animals had an almost complete turnaround of their symptoms. It was incredible. 
INTERVIEWER: Did you ever suspect Dr. Curtis of falsifying data prior to this incident?
SCOFIELD: No. God, no. 
INTERVIEWER: Was the prototype tested in humans?
SCOFIELD: Yes. It was tested on my son, Henry Scofield, earlier this year. 
INTERVIEWER: What was your son’s official diagnosis?
SCOFIELD: Acute fulminant multiple sclerosis. 
INTERVIEWER: Is that a disease with a good prognosis?
SCOFIELD: What do you think? No. 
INTERVIEWER: Sorry.
SCOFIELD: My son was diagnosed when he was four years old. He had a classical aggressive presentation: seizures, atrophy, loss of sensation, so on. The seizures were the worst. They were debilitating. They precluded him from everything else. 
INTERVIEWER: Why did you opt to try the implant?
SCOFIELD: We had had success with the implant when treating epileptic patients in the past. I was hopeful. 
INTERVIEWER: And what was the outcome of the surgery?
SCOFIELD: Henry suffered a sudden intracranial hemorrhage and lost a significant amount of blood. Eventually, he lost consciousness and fell… fell into a persistent vegetative state, which then progressed to a coma. 
INTERVIEWER: What is your son’s current prognosis, Dr. Scofield?
SCOFIELD: It is extremely unlikely that he will ever come out of this coma. 
INTERVIEWER: I’m sorry for your loss. 
SCOFIELD: Thank you. Excuse me…
INTERVIEWER: Take your time. 
SCOFIELD: Sorry, go on. 
INTERVIEWER: That’s quite all right. When the surgery was over, what did you report as the outcome regarding the success of the implant?
SCOFIELD: I wrote that the implant had failed, and that the surgery had resulted in what was likely the patient’s d… the… what was likely my son’s death.
INTERVIEWER: And what did Dr. Curtis report as the outcome of the surgery?
SCOFIELD: She reported that the surgery was a success, and the patient had made a full recovery. 
INTERVIEWER: Dr. Curtis’ status as a physician is currently under investigation. Do you think she should be allowed to retain her license?
SCOFIELD. Under no circumstances. She’s a danger to public safety. I think her license should be suspended with no possibility of ever practicing or operating again. Or going into a hospital. She should be in jail or kept in some kind of facility. And if she tries to spin it as if this is happened because of those diagnoses or disorders, don’t believe her, for God’s sake. She knew what she was doing. She wanted to do it. She’s a criminal. 
INTERVIEWER: Thank you, Dr. Scofield. 
SCOFIELD: Thank you.
