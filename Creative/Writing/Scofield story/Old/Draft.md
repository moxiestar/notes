# PART ONE.

## *1983. Scofield performs a resection.*

“I just can’t figure him out.”
The circulator looks up from her desk. She frowns. “Dr. Scofield?”
The neurosurgical operating room is dark: monitors and nameless students fade into blue oblivion. On the operating table, an old man is hidden under surgical drapes, a dark green sea capped with bright steel. A small window in the sterile cloth glows red. A piece of the man’s skull lies in a plastic cup nearby. Above the pulsating wound, manning a bipolar cautery pen, stands a surgeon in a green gown. The lighthead pours down on his face, picking him out like a psychic with a beaming crystal ball. 
“Forceps,” he says, holding out a priceless hand. He has a deep voice, academic and confident, yet somehow soothing. The sort of voice heroic doctors on television have. 
“Think about it, Emma. He comes in from Yale with an MD-PhD, invents a new cure for epilepsy, and in ten years he’s the chief of neuro,” the scrub tech whispers to the circulator. “It doesn’t add up.”
“Think he’s the next Dr. Death? Cool it.”
The tech rolls her eyes. “That’s not what I meant. It’s just funny, that’s all. And then he had that big break with the implants, and then it’s like he’s lost his mind or something. He goes back to tumor resections, he sulks all day in his office, doesn’t speak to anyone.”
Veiled in a corner, the anesthesiologist coughs. Dr. George Taylor is uptight by trade and impatient by nature. A newspaper, earmarked at the Saturday morning cartoons, is sprawled across his scrubs. On a tray to his right, a small radio sits lopsided, singing The Doors. With two narrow eyes, Taylor flashes the women a look. They fall silent. 
The surgeon stops his work, squinting over his loupes. “Bonnie, can we get a suction in here?”
Bonnie leaves Emma at her computer. The suction, a clear vacuum hooked to a blood bag, lies waiting on the drapes. As she leans over the incision, she glances up at the surgeon. Covered almost head to toe in his operating gear, only his eyes are visible, bright and blue, still boyish despite the wrinkles that frame them. She’s seen him during grand rounds, and roughly remembers his face: handsome, self-assured, piercing. His hair, she thinks, is dark, but tending towards grey. He’s always well-dressed, always on time, pulling up to the Duke parking deck in his black Mercedes-Benz, with a North Carolina vanity plate that spells out CEREBRAL. Sometimes he rides in on a Harley. In his corner office, he listens to Beethoven on a black CD player, the muffled strings radiating through the thick wooden door. The perfect doctor, the perfect man. 
Catching his eye, Bonnie smiles. The surgeon nods.
“Okay,” he says. “Let’s soak this.”
“What is this, Nick, the fifth hour?” Taylor interjects.
The forceps glint in the white light. The surgeon removes a piece of reddish gauze and discards it on the drapes. “You can’t rush genius, George,” he quips.
The nurses laugh. Taylor does too. “The stage lost a great actor when Nicola Scofield went into functional neurosurgery,” he says.
In the background, a news broadcast has taken over Taylor’s radio. “In recent news,” a chipper man begins, “heavy rain has led to disastrous flooding in Spain, where forty-four lives have been claimed. Today…”
The surgeon groans. “Christ, read the room. George, turn it off.”
Taylor spins the dial. A snippet gets through: *You’re everywhere, but you’re so hard to find….*
“Oh, fuck yeah.” He jacks the volume. “Love Foreigner.”
“George, this man’s life is on the line here,” the surgeon pitches in. 
“Really? I had no idea. I guess the rest of us are just here for fun.”
Another round of laughter. The surgeon, clicking his tongue, turns to his resident and beckons. 
“Your turn, Dr. Simon,” he says, holding out a driver and a pack of sutures. 
The resident, a nervous twenty-something with black hair, nods. Doctor is a strong word for a kid, even a kid who graduated from medical school top of his class. “Yes, sir.” 
With his trademark nod, the surgeon travels the periphery of the room, taking off his mask and his two sets of gloves as he goes. He passes the table where the bone flap floats in saline; next to it, a small tumor lurks at the bottom of a specimen jar. He picks it up and turns it over. 
“Happy, George?” he says, glancing over his shoulder. 
“I’ve never seen such a beautiful resection in my life,” Taylor says. There’s a wry smile on his face. 
“That’s better,” the surgeon laughs. 
Taylor isn’t finished. “I think you might have just saved this man’s life.”
“I aim to please.”
“No shit,” Emma mutters, scrolling through the case list on her boxy computer. Turning on her nurse voice, she smiles up at the surgeon. “Your case tomorrow morning is in OR 21. Lateral craniotomy for another partial tumor resection. You’re taking over the case from Dr. Burlingame. There’s a med student from UCSF rotating — Tom Howard.” She holds out a printed copy of the file. “Here, in case you need it.”
“Thanks.” He looks back at the surgical team before he pushes open the door. They can see his face now, his confident smile. He’s about to say something, but he shuts his mouth, and just nods. 
The door shuts. Taylor sighs. 
“This was his last case for the day, wasn’t it?” Bonnie says.
Emma checks her computer. “Yeah, that’s it.”
“I think he did pretty well, all things considered,” one of the nurses pipes up.
“Everyone says that. *All things considered.*”
“Well, you know,” the nurse says.
Bonnie makes a face. “I don’t.”
The nurse leans in. “He’s got to go home to his wife after this. The lawyer. After everything that happened with the implants, with Patient H.S., apparently they haven’t been getting along. And didn’t you hear…”
Taylor clears his throat. “For crying out loud, ladies, that’s enough. Let the poor man rest. I don’t see any of you up here doing brain surgery.”
“Good thing, too — we don’t have any lawsuits either,” Emma says. “If you left Bonnie alone with a scalpel we’d be back to the days of the frontal lobotomy in five minutes.”

In the hall, Scofield throws his surgical gown in the trash. He leaves the mask hanging around his neck, half-tied. A nurse runs after him, holding a stack of papers in her hands. 
“Dr. Scofield!” she calls.
He stops. 
“Dr. Scofield…” She’s out of breath. “Sorry. There’s a resident waiting for you in your office.”
“Just one?” They’re an invasive species.
“Just one. A young lady.”
Scofield looks down. “Say what her name was?”
“No, sir.”
He shakes his head. The nurse, still short of air, smiles.
“Steady on, Mary,” he says, smiling too.

## *Curtis leaves the hospital.

He’s situated between two clinical professors, all the way at the end of the neurosurgery hall. A large dieffenbachia plant stands in a thick pot by the door. A plaque reads DR. NICOLA SCOFIELD, M.D., Ph.D., CHAIR OF NEUROSURGERY. The door is ajar. 
He steps in, switching on the lights. In the afterimage, the room fades into view. A large, mahogany desk covered in papers; several ample windows, the blinds neatly lowered; and, flanking his chair, his rack of diplomas and a tall portrait completed during his residency. Sitting in a corner, he sees the student. 
Out of all the rumors that have spread about Christina Curtis, few are accurate. She isn’t even as attractive as the jealous nurses claim. She’s clean, beautiful, with a sharp jawline and fair hair scattered across her broad shoulders. But she’s disappointingly ordinary. She knows it, too: there’s a subtle, angry desperation in her face. It’s dangerous. That, if anything, is the thing that makes her so appealing.
Scofield stops in the doorway. For a while, before moving again, he watches her. Then he crosses the room, puts on his fleece, and sits down. Still he’s looking at her. A mug stands on the end of his desk, decorated with a cartoonish drawing of a neuron: from it, he takes one of his many fountain pens and begins to twirl it between his fingers.
“Chris,” he says. “Been all right?”
She scoffs.
“Please, sit down.” He gestures to the chair opposite him.
“I can’t stay.”
“It’s six o’clock on a Friday. Our cases are done for the day. Please, I insist.”
Curtis stands up and brushes the wrinkles out of her grey skirt. “Nick, I’m quitting.”
He takes a deep breath and taps the pen on his desk. He isn’t surprised. Maybe he’s been expecting this. Dreading it. 
“You’re leaving Duke?” he finally says.
“I found another position. At a lab near Hillsborough. Far away. Where I won’t bother you.”
“Hillsborough?” Scofield laughs. “What are you gonna do up there, psychoanalyze the tobacco farmers? Tell them about Freud and Jung?” He stops. “Wherever you go, Chris, you’ll make a great impression. You’re a great student. They’ll be lucky to have you.”
“Don’t… Look, I came to ask you a question. Before I leave.”
He sighs. “I think it’s best we put all this behind us.”
She catches his eye. Feeling guilty, he reaches into a drawer and takes out a stack of papers. Something to distract him. “I’ll be happy to help in any way I can,” he says, thumbing through an old case file, “but the work on the implants has been concluded. The project is over.”
“Nick, I… listen, I really would like to stay in touch with you.”
He pulls out a report on a craniectomy, scans the details.
“Nick, please, talk to me.”
“Between my work here and Allison’s cases at the firm, I just don’t think…”
“You’d talk to me before you’d talk to Allison, wouldn’t you?”
Scofield flashes her a warning glance. “Christina…”
“I really need you, Nick. You know that. I don’t know how else to tell you.”
“Christina, we’ve talked about this.”
“Call me Chris.” Her hands are shaking. “Please call me Chris.”
“Dr. Curtis, I think it would be best if you left.”
She paces back and forth in front of the door, wringing her thin hands. He studies her hands, her slender fingers; hands he once guided through incisions, hands he’s seen covered in blood. He wants to say: you were my favorite student. He wants to say: if there was a way I’d known to help you, I would have stopped at nothing. 
“When I came to Duke,” she begins, “you were the only person who trusted me. You gave me responsibility when no one else would. You saw potential in me when no one else did. And we produced incredible work.”
“No, you wanted to believe that we produced incredible work. Our project failed. And I’m sick of talking about this.”
“We could try again.”
“No.” Scofield shakes his head. “Christina, I think you should leave.”
“I’ll give up everything for you, if you’ll just let me.”
“For God’s sake, Chris, this’ll be so much easier if you just walk away.”
Curtis scoffs. “Just walk away? Nick…” She looks away. Tears fill her eyes. She’s losing the fight. “Here,” she says, reaching into her bag. “I want you to have this.”
She steps forward and holds out a small black box. Scofield thinks. He takes it and turns it over, like it’s a Rubik’s cube, a problem he can solve. “Thank you.”
“Least I could do,” she says. She tries to think of something else to say, a way to hold on. “Nick, if I’m out there all on my own, and something happens…”
“You need help that I can’t give you. You are beyond helping. Since you came here, you have embarrassed me and the hospital. If it was up to me, you’d be spending the rest of your life behind bars. Don’t forget that.”
She tries to nod. “Please call me,” she says, her hand on the door. She reaches down but misses the handle. Tucking her hair behind her ears, she waits, lingering. Scofield won’t look at her. Then she walks away. 
Alone, Scofield puts on his glasses — narrow wire rims, an academic look. He sets the box on his desk and carefully opens it: a watch, a Swiss chronograph with a blue face. He slips it off its casing and holds it up to the light. It glistens like his surgical tools, cold and unflinching. He slides it onto his wrist and clicks the chain-link band into place. 

## *1984. Scofield is losing his mind? His wife thinks so.*

At home, Scofield can’t sleep. He’s lying on his side, facing away from his wife. Their bed is cold, but somehow, he’s feverish, almost unwell. It’s dark. The room is black and white, blurry, like he’s looking through the viewfinder in his father’s old camera. Without his glasses, indistinct shapes appear in his periphery, pieces on a chessboard, moving closer and then slipping away. On his wrist, the watch is softly ticking, taunting him. He needs to sleep. He has an operation tomorrow, a difficult one, a day-long open craniotomy for an aneurysm clipping. But that isn’t what’s keeping him up. It’s something else, something he doesn’t know the word for — he wouldn’t want to say it, even if he did. 
He goes downstairs. 
There, at least, he can’t hear the watch. The ticking is drowned under the hum of the fridge. Outside, it’s storming, and the raindrops are dancing on the skylight. The house was built in the fifties, nestled atop a sprawling garden. The windows are tall, the ceilings are taller: hardwood flooring below, elegant lights above. It’s a family home. With just the two of them, it feels empty — hungry, almost. 
Sitting at one of the kitchen stools, he twirls his pen in his hand. Back and forth, back and forth. He searches the papers on the counter, finds an article covered with notes. As he spins the pen, the watch chain softly rattles. In the dark he can just make out the hands on the dial, with their strange green glow. 
“Nick?”
He turns around.
Allison stands at the bottom of the stairs, pulling her robe over her nightgown. Her black hair is still ruffled from bed. Her eyes are nervous, disappointed. 
“Nick, come back to bed.”
He tries to smile. “Sorry,” he says. “Didn’t mean to wake you up.”
She comes closer, puts a hand on his shoulder. Glancing down at the article, she sees the title, the scrawled reminders. Her face falls.
“I’m worried about you,” she tells him. “I know you’re stressed about what happened at the hospital, but you need to sleep. You’ve got a big case tomorrow.”
He isn’t here with her. He’s somewhere else. “If I had…”
“No. There’s nothing you could’ve done differently. You tried everything you could. You did everything right.” She waits. “Please, honey, come back to bed.”
It’s like he’s looking right through her, straight past the back of her head. Something like a smile comes onto his troubled face. As if he’s going to say, Allison, you just don’t understand. 
“I’m… not sure,” he says instead. “I can’t stop thinking about it. I can’t…” He lowers his eyes, breathing hard. 
Allison reaches up to cup his face. His skin is cold. 
“Please,” he says, turning away, “don’t touch me.”
“I think you should see a psychologist.”
Scofield scoffs. “What’s a shrink gonna do? Tell me I’m losing my mind. I know that. Everyone fucking knows that.” He turns sharply away. “Excuse me.” Always a gentleman. It ticks her off, his correctness. 
“I understand,” she says.
“No, you…” He stops himself, though he’s right, she doesn’t. “I should quit,” he says, sitting back down and twirling his pen. “They’ll find someone else. They don’t need me.”
“Nick, what are you talking about? You’re a department head. You can’t just walk away from the hospital. Anyway, where would you go? UNC? Wake Med? Give me a break.”
“There are places,” he says slowly. “There’s this lab out near Hillsborough.”
Allison looks down. She sees the hands of the watch, imagines him playing with it between his cases, his thumb brushing over the crown. 
Scofield isn’t sentimental, not one to keep things around for the sake of it. He’s never reminisced fondly about his trainees over a glass of wine, never hesitated about throwing their thank-you cards in the trash. He’s never been a forgiving teacher, either, never been slow to point out his students’ failings. Once, after a trivial slip-up during a closing, he told a third-year resident to his face that he’d be a danger to public safety if he ever picked up a scalpel again. 
He’s trained many interns in the ten-odd years he’s worked at the hospital: the best they can hope for is a letter of recommendation and a tight-lipped nod of approval. He catalogues his students like livestock, gives them unflattering nicknames that he shares with his colleagues. There was the rodent, whose indiscrete and pointed nose made him famous; the politician, whose clever speeches were well-known across the neurosurgical floor; the butcher, whose trigger-happy mistake during a trauma rotation cost him his reputation. But there was, Allison knows, only one Christina Curtis. He has no nickname for her. Sometimes, out of habit, he calls her Chris.
Allison makes a face. She takes a deep breath and points her gaze towards the windows. She has tried hard to be easy. Curtis, she knows, is younger, better, smarter. She knows that it was Curtis’ mind that Scofield fell in love with. Scofield, of course, would never call it love — he would say it was a lapse in judgement. Allison imagines how he complains about her to his colleagues, dreading the drive home. She doesn’t tell anyone, but writes in her journal, *Christina probably dissects him better than any surgeon he knows, probably talks casually about things I could never understand.* 
“This has nothing to do with Curtis,” he breaks in. “I was never interested in her, Allison. I don’t know what else I can say to convince you.”
“Forget it.”
“I don’t understand…”
“You are so exhausting to be around sometimes,” she says. “You wanna play the victim, the poor doctor who just can’t handle the pressure anymore? Fine. But you are not getting my pity.”
“I’m not asking for your pity. I don’t want your pity. I don’t want anyone’s pity. But that’s all I get now. Everyone at the hospital. Collins, Simon, Taylor, Carson, Burlingame. I can feel it when they look at me. It’s driving me insane.”
Allison stops. “When I married you, I...”
“Oh, for fuck’s sake,” he snaps. “I don’t want to have this conversation again. I am not having this conversation again.”
“Rather pretend everything’s fine?”
He shakes his head. 
“Then what the hell are we gonna do? ’Cause I can’t keep living like this, Nick. I can’t. And I love you, honey. I really do. But I can’t do this.” Her voice gets quiet. “I can’t, Nick. I can’t.”
Scofield won’t face her. When he speaks, it’s painful. “I don’t know.”
“Okay,” she says. “Okay.”
He doesn’t say anything. Maybe he can’t.
“I’m going back to bed. I have a headache.”
She watches him, waiting for him to follow her. Waiting for him to turn and say, No, don’t go, I need you more than anything. He doesn’t move. 
“Goodnight, Nick,” she says. 
He can hear her soft footsteps as she goes up the stairs. He listens until they fade, then listens a little longer. Eventually, he knows, she will fall back asleep, no matter how much tossing and turning it takes. Scofield hasn’t slept in months. He’s back to the days of residency, those hundred-hour weeks, short nights lying in the hospital cot, holding the pager in his raw hands, waiting. 
He’s waiting again.
Picking up his pen, he turns to the next page of the article and begins to write a note.

## **1999. Diana knocks on the Zhangs’ door.

Mrs. Zhang’s house is small, dark, and claustrophobic. Stepping inside is like walking into a phone booth: it’s dirty, it smells like other people, and there’s nowhere to put your elbows. Old family portraits, dead relatives from Shanghai adorn the walls — a different time, a different place. The sun has set, and the blinds are drawn crooked over the narrow windows. Down the street, a dog is barking at the clouds, and somewhere, further away, sirens roll by. 
Adela Zhang is sitting at one side of the dinner table, under a dying yellow light. She’s glaring down at her English homework: who needs essays when we have phones we can carry around in our pockets? Soundgarden is blaring on her hand-me-down Walkman, the music coming out half static and half all-American anger. Adela is the kind of girl who thinks Nirvana is mainstream. There’s something about her face that you’d never forget: that angry, stubborn look, her long black hair falling across her forehead, the bangs her mother keeps cutting. She has everything to prove. 
Across the table, her older brother, Leonard, is reading. Even home alone, he needs everyone to think he’s cool. He’s worked too hard to make his thick black hair seem messy. He wants the kids on the block to think he listens to French music and watches movies by David Lynch.
“Adela,” he says, not looking at her. 
She grunts. 
“Adela.”
She grunts again, a little louder.
“That essay’s not gonna write itself.”
She pulls out her headphones and sneers at him. “Fuck you. I’ve been working on this stupid thing for two hours.” She holds up the assignment like it’s a warrant. “Consider the following text from chapter two of *The Scarlet Letter* by Nathaniel Hawthorne. ‘Dreadful as it was, she was conscious of a shelter in the presence of… of these thousand witnesses. It was better to stand thus, with so many…’” Adela hesitates. “‘*Betwixt* him and her, than to greet him, face—’”
“Okay,” Leo interrupts. “I get it. It’s a dumb assignment. But if you don’t pass this class, you’re gonna have to repeat eighth grade.”
“I will kill myself and everyone in this house if they make me take this piece of shit class again.” 
Leo groans. “Adela, watch it. You sound like a gangster.”
Adela puts her headphones back in her ears. “At least I don’t sound like a pretty boy with a stick up my ass.”
“That’s it, I’m taking your Walkman.”
“What the hell? You don’t get to boss me around just ’cause Ma’s out.”
“Yeah? Who else is gonna make sure you get your work done? *You* sure aren’t. What are you even listening to, ACDC?”
“None of your business,” Adela hisses.
“You are such a little brat.”
“I’m the brat? You were out with Lila for three hours last night and Ma didn’t say a thing. She didn’t bat a fucking eye. But I stay out for one minute past nine, and I’m dead to her. You don’t get to ride my ass just ’cause she likes you better.”
Leo scoffs. “You know why I’m *riding your ass*, Adela? Because if you don’t do your homework, I’m the one who’s gonna get in trouble. Not you.”
“Oh, please. Ma takes everything out on me. You could rob a bank and Ma would still bring you a plate of apple slices before you went to bed.”
“That’s it. You’re done.” He stretches across the table and snatches Adela’s Walkman in his hands.
“Are you fucking kidding me? Give that back.” Adela stands up, lunging at him. 
It’s as she leans on the corner of the table and swats at the headphones that they hear it: a soft knock. 
They jump. 
“Oh my God,” Leo whispers. “It’s Jeffrey Dahmer.”
“Jeffrey Dahmer’s dead, you idiot,” Adela says. 
Another knock. Adela steps forward. Leo stops her. 
“Are you out of your mind? Stranger danger. I’m calling the cops.”
“It could be one of those old women who lives down the street.” She reaches into her pocket and takes out her prize possession — a black-handled Italian switchblade — and clicks it open. Her hands anticipate the quick, harsh swing of the blade. She’s done this a couple times before. “If they come at me, I’ll skin ’em.”
“Put that away,” Leo hisses. 
Again the knocking. Through the stained glass insert, they can see a figure. Adela looks over and Leo and slowly approaches the door. With her free hand, she unlocks the deadbolt. 
“I’m armed,” she says, hopefully loud enough for the guest to hear her. “If you pull anything, you’re dead, punk.” A showing of *Dirty Harry* at the local drive-in has enlightened Adela’s vocabulary. 
Her hands are shaking. She turns the handle and pulls back. 
Standing on the other side of the door is a girl. 
She’s tall, rail thin. Her choppy blondish hair is thrown across her face. Her eyes are unusual and mesmerizing: one blue, one green, both cold and bright. On her cheek is a star-shaped scar, snaking across her pale skin, stretching from her jaw to slice the bridge of her nose. She’s wearing a dull hospital gown, ugly blue fabric polka-dotted with little flowers. Stranger still, her arms are plastic, white and silver, with joints made of metal. She’s like a mechanical angel. 
Adela doesn’t know what to make of her. She isn’t scared anymore, just puzzled. When Leo comes over, his eyes get wide. 
“Um, can we help you?” Adela says. “Are you lost?”
The girl stares at her, not moving, hardly even blinking.
“Uh, okay.” Adela purses her lips. “What’s going on? You sick or something? Come from the ER?”
Slowly, as if doing it for the first time, the girl shakes her head. “Not sick,” she says.
Adela glances at her brother. “Okay. That’s good. Know where your parents are?”
Again the girl shakes her head. “Cold.”
“What?”
The girl puts out her robotic arm. She’s upset. “Cold.”
Folding up her knife, Adela reaches out and touches her hand. She smiles. “You sure are cold. What’s all this? You a robot or something? Paranoid Android?”
The girl takes her hand away, shaking her head. Apparently it’s the only thing she knows how to do. “No.”
Leo steps in. “Where’d you come from, kid?”
“America?” the girl says, apparently giving her best guess. 
“Well, I sure hope so,” Leo mutters. “Bet you came straight from Cherry.”
Adela smacks him. “She’s not a psycho.” She fixes the girl with her best Clint Eastwood smolder. “How old are you, kid?”
“Don’t know.”
“Really?” Adela can’t believe it. “You fucking with me?”
“Don’t know,” the girl repeats, angrier.
“Okay, sorry. Jesus. What’s your deal, then? You’re not sick, you’re not lost. I’ll call the cops on you if I have to, you know.”
“No police.” The girl panics. “No police. I need to stay somewhere. I need a place.”
Adela frowns. “Just doing some casual house-shopping at nine o’clock? Cool, I dig it.” 
Leo is less impressed. He grabs Adela’s arm and flashes the girl an awkward smile. “She’s crazy, right?” he says, pulling Adela aside. 
“She’s totally crazy.”
“Maybe she’s a spy.”
Adela eyes the girl, the way she’d watch an unruly bowl of mac-n-cheese through the microwave door. “I don’t think she’s a spy. She seems kinda… not all there.”
“No kidding.” He waits for a second. “What are we gonna do, then?”
“I don’t know. Let her crash here? She doesn’t seem dangerous.”
“She’s — she’s a robot, Adela. Are you insane?”
Adela turns back to the girl before he has a chance to argue. “Hi,” she says, holding out her hand. “I’m Adela. This is my brother Leo.”

## **Leo and Adela try to take care of Diana.**

It’s going to be a long night. 
The girl is sitting on the couch, an old plaid blanket over her shoulders and a cup of tea in her hands — Adela’s reassured her twice that, yes, the tea is supposed to be hot. Leo’s posted at the table, monitoring their guest. His sister’s in the kitchen, digging around for scraps. 
“Do you like apples?” she calls over her shoulder.
“If you want,” the girl says. 
Leo puts his head in his hands. His whole day’s off schedule. Adela’s supposed to be in bed by now, and her essay, lying lonely by her placemat, should be finished. He should be brushing his teeth while his Jeff Buckley CD plays in the background and his mother pulls into the driveway. Instead, there’s an escaped patient from an insane asylum with plastic arms in his living room, drinking his tea and distracting his sister.
“Look,” he says, “who are you?”
She stares at him, frowning, like this is all his fault. Sniffing cautiously at the tea, she tries to take a bite out of the cup. 
“Did you hear me? Who are you?”
She points at something behind him. 
“What?” he says. He arches around, tries to follow her hand. The calendar: September 7. “Yeah, it’s fall. School just started.”
“Lucky number seven,” she says, in the same monotone.
“What?”
“Seven,” she repeats. “Seven, seven, seven.”
From the kitchen, Adela says, “Can you even eat?”
The girl thinks about it. She shakes her head. “Don’t know.”
Adela walks over, carrying a plate: apple slices. Leo almost laughs. His sister takes the girl’s cup, puts it on the floor, and passes Leo the plate. 
“Guess I made these for nothing.”
“Hold on, you and I need to talk,” Leo says, pushing back his chair. He drags Adela over to a corner, glancing over his shoulder as he goes. “Either I’m losing the edge,” he whispers, “or she’s totally nuts.”
“She’s a little weird,” Adela admits.
“That girl is not staying in our house, Adela. Do you know what Ma would say if she found out we were hiding some kind of robot under her nose? She’d kill us. We’d be dead. Dead meat.”
“No, she’d kill *me,* and I’m dead anyway. What, you wanna send her back into the cold?”
Leo gives her a look. “Yes! You don’t?”
Adela rolls her eyes. “She could sleep on the floor for a night.”
“Oh, yeah, great idea!” Leo throws up his hands. “Let the half-mute robot girl stay in your bedroom overnight. She’ll get inside your brain. Like *The Thing*.” Another feature at the drive-in: one that’s kept Leo up since he saw it. “She looks like a science experiment, Adela. She’s a robot and a spy. This is straight out of the *X-Files*.”
They look back. The girl’s watching them. 
“Are you a spy?” Adela says. 
The girl thinks. She shakes her head. The slow, even, inhuman motion.

*Rain down... rain down... come on, rain down...*
She puts her face up to the CD player, her plastic hands pressed against the display. She wishes she could go inside of the music. Sliding her finger across the screen, she makes the music move: faster, slower, higher. This was her favorite trick, back then. They could never figure out how she did it. “What are you doing?”
She jumps back. Standing in the doorway, backpack over one shoulder, is Leo. He looks like he’s just seen a ghost.

As soon as their eyes meet, he shifts his gaze to the wall, which has suddenly become very interesting. He runs his hands through his hair. Takes a big breath and lets it out. ## *Dr. Scofield contemplates suicide.

At home, the kitchen is bathed in light. The counters are glowing, the cutlery sparkling in the sink. The sun, pouring through the window, blurs the room with its yellow haze. Scofield sits at the kitchen counter, a cup of coffee half-empty in his hands. These days, he needs the coffee just to stay awake. Not during surgery — he has no trouble with the scalpel — but as soon as he leaves the hospital. It’s as if he’s a different person inside than out. There’s something eating away at him. Something stagnant, like algae blooming across a dead pond.
He doesn’t remember how it happened, or when it began — maybe last month, last year, maybe before Allison left. He hardly remembers how long it’s been since the divorce. It could have been three weeks or six years and it wouldn’t make a difference. It’s as if he’s watching himself, watching as he operates, watching as he talks to his colleagues, watching as he goes home, gets in bed, wakes up with his alarm, drinks his coffee, drives back in. Operates again. Different patient, same procedure, same blood on his gloved hands. The cases go longer than they used to. He leaves the hospital at the same time but feels as if he’s been in the OR for weeks. 
Sometimes, he takes his mind off it. A cigarette, a night out, a new prescription. But it never leaves. It never changes. In his life, there has been a clear before and after. He didn’t notice as he moved from one to the other. But slowly, he became aware of the change. All the best memories, the biggest awards, the kindest people — all of it belongs to the before. The after is just one day, the same meaningless day, that keeps replaying over and over again. There’s nothing after the after. This is it — Scofield knows that. And recently, he’s started to have this feeling, a feeling he can’t quite put into words. Like he’s waiting for something. A quiet night, the right time. Recently, he’s been so tired. It would be nice to sleep, not have to wake up for a while: a week, a month. A year. That’s what’s next, that’s what comes after the after. That’s what he’s waiting for. 
The morning passes at the kitchen counter. At half past noon, he has lunch. Later, he showers, throws his scrubs in the laundry, cleans his glasses. He stands on the porch, above the garden and the driveway, where his Ferrari is sitting, dented and a little pathetic. He smokes: always Camel. Then he goes back in, and waits.

## *Nightmare #2. Who is this kid, anyway?*

He’s back here again. The operating room, the blood, the walls a little too close, the ceilings a little too low. He can already see the red pooling around his feet. And the boy on the table, his head rolling back on his neck, his cadaver-pale hands lying limp beside his body. The drapes are on the floor — the boy’s skull is crackled crudely open, a shard of bone left carelessly close to the wound. The techs and nurses are crowded around him, the doctors circling the room. 
Scofield looks down at his hands. It’s as if they belong to someone else. In his right hand is a slim metal pick. In his left, a small hammer. Stepping in, he watches the surgical team. Their faces are strange — their voices are unfamiliar. This isn’t his team. 
One of the nurses turns around, beckons him over. “Dr. Scofield, it’s time,” she says. 
The blue gowns disperse. Suddenly, he is alone. He peers at the boy, but his features keep shifting, changing. He reaches down, checks for a pulse. The boy’s heart is beating, but the rhythm is irregular, almost inhuman. 
“It’s time,” the nurse repeats.
When he looks over his shoulder, the room is empty. “Hello?” he calls out. “This boy is in critical condition. We can’t continue the operation.”
Silence. 
“What is the operation, anyway?” He searches for a monitor, a scan, a clue. A piece to the puzzle. “Who performed the craniectomy? Was there a bleed?”
Still, silence. 
“Great,” he mutters. He focuses on the boy. He can’t make out his face. When he tries to touch the boy’s hands, they slip away across the table. 
“He’s still awake,” Scofield says. “We’ll need to up the dose.” He turns around again. The walls have fallen away. Every way he looks, the room stretches out into infinite blackness: no beginning, no end. His hands are shaking, his heart is up in his throat. There’s only one thing he can do. 
He stands over the boy’s open head, watching the blood drip off the table. The boy stirs, twitches, like a dying deer fighting for its last moment. Scofield positions the pick above the incision and slowly lowers it. The steel cuts through the layers of cortical tissue, severing the boy’s consciousness. He raises the hammer. 

The blood pours over Scofield’s gloves. A piece of the boy’s skull cracks and slips onto the table. Again, the hammer comes down, crushing a sliver of the boy’s brain. In the background, he hears an alarm echoing through the hospital, beyond walls he cannot see. Code blue. The rhythmic beeping, getting louder and quieter, louder and quieter. Again he slams the hammer down onto the pick, driving further into the incision. 
Squirming on the table, the boy opens his eyes. Frozen at first, they dart from left to right, then fix on Scofield. For the first time, Scofield can see him clearly. At least, he can see his eyes, wide and blue and full of fear. 
“Jesus Christ,” Scofield mutters. He tries to step back, but he can’t move his legs, can’t stop his hands from striking again. 
The boy tries to lift his arm. He’s trembling. Slowly he raises his hand, reaches for Scofield. 
“What the hell….”
“Stop,” the boy says. 
Again the clink of metal against metal, again the blood spattering across Scofield’s gown. 
“Stop…” The boy grabs Scofield’s arm, clenches his fist around his sleeve. “Stop….”
“I can’t, I can’t. Work with me here, kid. Stay with me.”
“Stop it, please.” The boy’s eyes are filled up with tears. His mouth slips into a terrified scowl. “Please, just stop.” There’s blood running over his face, trickling down the end of his nose. “Please.” He pulls Scofield’s hand away, shakes it. Seizing up, he begins to sob. “Please, Dad.”

“No, no.” She’s backing herself into the corner.

“No.”

Could she have thrown something at him? Diana, with her big eyes like mismatched socks and her nervous hands? He doubts it. And then again, why would she? He leans on the sink, both hands on the porcelain, and feels something else. Anger.

## *McLaughlin saves Scofield.*

After the third knock, McLaughlin begins to get suspicious. He puts his face up against the glass slit in the big wooden door and shuts one eye, squinting with the other. The house is quiet and dark. Purple streaks of evening fall across the foyer, painting the spotless floor. He can just see the edge of the kitchen, suspended in a bluish haze. One corner of the thin woven carpet that runs along the hall is wrinkled, crimped, like someone tripped over it on their way to bed. 
McLaughlin steps away. “Scofield?” he calls, knocking again. “Doc, I don’t got all day here.”
The door swings open, almost into his face. He jumps back, a little stunned, and opens his mouth to say something. Then he stops, frowning. Scofield’s face is pale, his bloodshot eyes framed by mauve shadows. His hair, usually combed back from his face in slow waves, is strewn across his forehead, matted and parted by sweat. 
“Everything all right here?” 
Scofield nods, wiping the beads of sweat off his lip. “Yeah, fine.”
“I was just comin’ over to ask about my mailbox. Was hopin’ we could come to an agreement.” McLaughlin shifts his weight. “But seein’ as you… uh….” He gestures. “What’s goin’ on?”

As he makes to shut the door, McLaughlin holds it open. “What’s the matter with you?”

But we don’t have to keep doing this act where we pretend we actually want to talk to each other. It’s been a long week. Just….” He holds up a hand and draws a long breath. “Just go back home.”
McLaughlin hesitates. “Fuck off. You ain’t right in the head.”
“I told you I’m fine. Go back across the street and leave me the hell alone.”
“Let me in.”
Scofield throws up his hands. “Jesus Christ,” he mutters, but steps aside for McLaughlin to come in. He watches the officer’s back as he walks past, his eyes darting from shoulder to shoulder, the way a deer looks across a highway. “Look,” McLaughlin says, voice echoing from the kitchen, “I’ll make this quick. What are you on?”
“Not what you think I’m on.”
“What, snow?”
“No.” Scofield almost laughs. “I wish.”

“I’m not worried about that.”

“The room is spinning. Please.”

“You don’t need to say that to make me feel better.”
“Do I look like the kind of person who would say shit to make you feel better?”
Scofield shrugs. 
Smiling, McLaughlin turns on the faucet. He passes a towel under the water and wrings it out above the sink, droplets spattering on the mirror. Scofield watches him, trying not to move his head, but looks away when McLaughlin comes closer and holds the towel up to his face.

“Back at the station, this one time,” McLaughlin begins, speaking low, his accent winning, “we brought this guy in for a DUI. A constellation of punctures lies in the crease between Scofield’s forearm and bicep. “No,” McLaughlin groans. “Jesus, you are a fuckin’ pill. I don’t believe in God or any of that shit. I don’t see any fuckin’ beauty in life. Life is hard. Life sucks. Every day, you suffer cause of things that ain’t your fault. But when you give up, you let them win.”

“Somethin’ like that, I reckon. I don’t know. I’m no fuckin’ philosopher.” McLaughlin shifts his weight, folding one leg to sit further back. “All right, what’s your accident? I know you got one.”

But there ain’t much out there about him, your son. And there’s even less about you, just a bunch of medical shit, and Lord knows that’s all above my head.”
For almost a minute, Scofield is silent. He draws his lips into a thin line, his teeth catching on the slightly chapped skin. “My son didn’t die,” he says, “I killed him.”
McLaughlin scoffs. “Jesus Christ, you kiddin’?”

“I killed my son. What else is there to say?” Scofield’s breathing is ragged again. “You want me to snap or something? Start crying, tell you how sorry I am about all this, how I’ve made all these mistakes and I’ll never be able to forgive myself? I’m not going to do that. This isn’t your problem, and you don’t want it to be your problem.”
“Yes, I do.” McLaughlin shakes his head, incredulous. “I do.”
“Why? Why not just leave me alone?”
“Tell me about your son.”
Groaning, Scofield covers his face with his hands and rubs his eyes with his palms. “You are an asshole, Leslie.”
“That’s real original,” McLaughlin drawls. “I mean, come on, doc, what the hell are you doin’? Jesus, sometimes I think you just like to talk. “Shut up.” Scofield flashes a glare. “Get the hell out of my house.”

Scofield sits up. “I never tried to cover my tracks. I took the implants out of commission right after my son’s operation.”
“When I looked you up, before I was movin’ in, I heard about the big articles you published. Who was Patient H.S.?”

“He was born sick?”

Asking me whether I loved my wife? What are you getting at? What are you implying about me?” Again, he waits. “What do you want me to say?”

“It’s late,” Scofield says.
McLaughlin nods.
“Look at me.”
His eyes flit up. 
“What do you want me to say?”
McLaughlin lowers his eyes again, cocking his head to one side and sighing. Scofield reaches across, takes the cigarette from his hand, and throws it into the sink. As he’s turning back, McLaughlin catches his arm, the sleeve of his button-up. He studies Scofield for a minute: his tired eyes, his strong nose, his sweaty hair. Scofield tenses when he feels the cold tiles on his back, and he throws McLaughlin over and holds him down. McLaughlin flinches, shivering, the hairs on his arms standing up, and he keeps his eyes set on Scofield’s. 


“And your wife? When did you split up?”
He leans back, like he’s counting the years in his head. “Oh, I don’t know. A while ago.”

“Not really. I don’t know. We weren’t unhappy.”
